# Affordances: Gibson's Ecological Psychology

## Overview

**Affordance** is the central concept of James J. Gibson's ecological psychology — the view that organisms perceive the world not as a collection of physical properties but as a structured field of *action possibilities*. An affordance is a relational property: it is what the environment *offers* a particular organism given that organism's body, capacities, and needs. The concept has been enormously influential, migrating from ecological psychology into phenomenology, enactivism, HCI/design, robotics, and biosemiotics — though often with significant reinterpretation along the way.

---

## Historical Origins: Against Indirect Perception

### The Standard View Gibson Rejected

By the mid-twentieth century, the dominant view of perception was **indirect**: the sense organs deliver raw data (retinal images, frequency patterns, pressure gradients), and the brain constructs a representation of the world through inference, computation, and hypothesis-testing. This picture runs from Helmholtz's "unconscious inference" through classical information-processing cognitive science, and remains influential in predictive processing frameworks today.

Gibson spent his career building an alternative. His key insight: the environment contains *more* information than the indirect view assumes, and perception is organized to exploit that information *directly* — without mediation by internal representations.

### The Ecological Approach

Gibson's ecological approach, fully articulated in *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception* (1979), rests on three foundational claims:

1. **The ambient optic array** — the structured pattern of light reaching an organism from all directions — contains sufficient information to specify the layout and properties of the environment. The environment is informationally rich, not impoverished.
2. **Optical flow** — the pattern of apparent motion across the visual field as the organism moves — directly specifies the organism's movement through space. No computation is required.
3. **Resonance** — perception is a process of the organism *resonating with* or *picking up* information in the optic array, not inferring the world from impoverished retinal signals.

This ecological realism contrasts both with naive direct realism (which Gibson never fully endorsed) and with representationalism. Gibson's world is real and structured, but the *structure* that matters is *ecological* — relative to organisms and their capacities — not purely physical.

## Affordances: The Core Concept

### Gibson's Definition

> "The affordances of the environment are what it *offers* the animal, what it *provides* or *furnishes*, either for good or ill."
> — *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception* (1979), p. 127

An affordance is neither a subjective projection of the animal's mind nor an objective property of the physical environment. It is a **relational property** — it exists only in the coupling between a particular kind of organism and a particular kind of environment. Neither the organism alone nor the environment alone has affordances; they emerge from the *fit* between the two.

### Key Properties of Affordances

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Relational** | A surface affords walking-on for a human but not for a fish. The same physical substrate affords different things for different organisms. |
| **Directly perceived** | Affordances are not inferred from physical properties; they are perceived directly. We see the climbability of a ladder, the graspability of a handle, the sit-on-ability of a chair. |
| **Action-oriented** | Affordances are defined in terms of what the organism *can do*, not what the environment *is* in physical terms. |
| **Body-scaled** | Whether a step affords climbing depends on the step height relative to the climber's leg length. Whether a gap affords passing through depends on shoulder width. Affordances are calibrated to the organism's morphology. |
| **Objectively present** | Crucially, affordances are *real properties of the environment* — they exist whether or not any organism perceives them. A chair affords sitting even in an empty room. They are neither purely physical nor purely mental. |
| **Complementary** | Every affordance implies a corresponding effectivity in the organism — the sensorimotor capacity to exploit it. |

### The Ontological Status of Affordances

One of the most contested aspects of Gibson's account is whether affordances are *objective* (fully in the world), *subjective* (in the mind), or *relational* (in the animal-environment system). Gibson clearly intended the relational reading: affordances are real features of the world, but their existence as affordances depends on the type of organism in relation to which they are defined. This is analogous to mass (objective) vs. weight (relational — depends on gravity). The physical substrate is objective; its affordance-character is relational.

This position creates puzzles:
- Can the same physical object have multiple affordances simultaneously for different organisms?
- Are affordances *identical* to physical properties or *supervenient* on them?
- If affordances depend on organisms, does extinguishing all organisms eliminate affordances from the universe?

Gibson's own view seems to be that affordance-properties are *real ecological properties* — as real as chemical or optical properties — but defined at the level of organism-environment systems, not sub-personal physics.

## Perception of Affordances: Direct vs. Inferential

### Gibson's Direct Perception Claim

Gibson insists that affordances are *perceived directly* — they are features of the optic array that organisms are tuned to resonate with. This claim is philosophically strong: it denies that perception of affordances involves any step of inference, hypothesis-testing, or representation-construction.

This is plausible for routinized affordances in familiar environments: an experienced climber perceives the climbability of a rock face directly, without reasoning. Motor experts (athletes, craftspeople) perceive action possibilities immediately, not through conscious deliberation.

### Critiques of Direct Perception

**Fodor & Pylyshyn (1981)** — the most influential critique: They argued that ecological optics cannot explain how the perceptual system picks up affordance-information without internal representations and inferential processes. The optic array is ambiguous; different world-states can produce the same optic array. Resolving this ambiguity requires prior knowledge — representations. Direct perception, on this view, is an explanatory sleight of hand.

**Vera & Simon (1993)** — argued that affordances are mental representations of action possibilities, not properties of the environment. This representationalist co-optation of the concept contradicts Gibson's intent but shows how useful the word is independent of the ecological framework.

**Reply from ecological psychologists**: Gibson's defenders argue that Fodor & Pylyshyn presuppose the framework they're criticizing. If you assume that perception works by recovering a symbolic description of the world from impoverished retinal images, of course you need inference. But if the optic array is informationally rich enough to *specify* affordances, no inference is required. The debate is partly terminological and partly empirical.

### Novel Affordances: The Limits of Direct Perception

Even friendly commentators grant that ecological psychology handles *novel affordances* — perceiving that an object affords an unfamiliar use — poorly. When Wolfgang Köhler's chimpanzee Sultan stacked boxes to reach bananas, he was not directly perceiving a pre-formed affordance. He was *discovering* that boxes afford stacking-to-reach. This insight process looks more like hypothesis-testing or analogical reasoning than direct ecological resonance.

Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization (see `cognitive-science/relevance-realization/`) addresses this gap: novel affordance perception requires a background capacity for generating and revising relevance assignments — the meta-level capacity to notice *new* action possibilities in the environment — that ecological psychology alone does not explain.

## Social and Linguistic Affordances

Gibson's original account focused on physical affordances — surfaces, objects, layouts. Later work extended the concept:

### Social Affordances

**Reed (1996)** argued that other organisms are the richest sources of affordances. Animals afford:
- Mating (for conspecifics)
- Predation (for predators)
- Flight (for prey)
- Communication (for social species)
- Tool-mediated cooperation (for humans)

Social affordances are particularly interesting because they are *dynamic* — they depend on the other organism's current state (a predator that is sated affords different things from a hungry one). This makes social affordances inherently temporal and relational in a more complex sense than physical affordances.

### Linguistic and Symbolic Affordances

**Costall (1995)** and others have examined **conventional affordances** — affordances that depend on cultural convention, not just physical structure. A door handle affords pulling (physical affordance) but a "PUSH" sign affords a conventionally specified action. The difference is that conventional affordances are mediated by shared normative frameworks, not just bodily-environmental coupling.

This creates a sharp divide between two interpretations of symbolic environments: are symbols perceived as affording certain interpretive actions (a Gibsonian account), or do they operate through an entirely different, representational mechanism (the cognitivist account)?

## Affordances in Design and HCI

### Norman's Redesign of the Concept

Donald Norman popularized "affordance" in design through *The Design of Everyday Things* (1988), but significantly altered the concept. For Norman, a perceived affordance is a property of an object that *suggests* how it should be used — the "push-pull" relationship communicated by a door's design. Norman later distinguished:

- **Affordances**: Actual action possibilities (Gibsonian sense)
- **Signifiers**: Perceptible signals that communicate where/how to act

Norman's use became standard in HCI, divorced from Gibson's anti-representationalist framework. Gibson intended affordances to be directly perceived, not signaled or inferred. Norman's "perceived affordance" is closer to a cue or signal — a representation-mediated relationship.

### Ecological Interface Design

Vicente & Rasmussen's **Ecological Interface Design (EID)** attempts to bring the genuine Gibsonian concept back into design: interfaces should make the actual action possibilities (work domain constraints and possibilities) directly perceivable, rather than requiring operators to compute possibilities from abstract data displays. This has been applied to nuclear power plant control rooms and medical device design.

## Chemero and Radical Embodied Cognitive Science

Anthony Chemero (*Radical Embodied Cognitive Science*, 2009) develops the most philosophically sophisticated defense of affordances in the post-Gibson literature. Key moves:

1. **Affordances as relations, not properties**: Affordances are not properties of environments or organisms but *relations* between abilities of organisms and features of situations. This formally separates affordances from physical properties.

2. **Anti-representationalism**: Drawing on dynamical systems theory, Chemero argues that cognition — including affordance perception — should be explained *dynamically* (in terms of coupled agent-environment systems evolving over time) rather than computationally (in terms of representation and inference).

3. **Affordance perception is skill**: Perceiving affordances is an exercise of perceptual-motor skill — an ability that develops through practice and that is constituted by the coupled organism-environment dynamics, not by internal representations of external properties.

4. **Scale of analysis**: Chemero argues that the right level for describing affordance-perception is the whole organism-environment system, not the organism's internal mechanisms. This is a methodological commitment that makes affordance-science continuous with ecology.

## Connections to Adjacent Frameworks

### Merleau-Ponty: Motor Significance

Merleau-Ponty's concept of **motor significance** is the phenomenological twin of Gibson's affordance. The perceived world solicits bodily responses — a chair *calls* for sitting, a steep slope *calls* for caution — and this call-response structure is given in perception itself, not added by judgment. The convergence is striking given that Merleau-Ponty and Gibson developed their ideas independently.

Key difference: Gibson grounded affordances in ecological physics (the optic array, objective properties of the environment). Merleau-Ponty grounded them in the phenomenology of embodied subjectivity (the lived body, motor intentionality). Ecological psychology is third-person; phenomenology is first-person. Enactivism attempts to integrate both.

### Umwelt: Species-Specific Affordance Landscapes

Uexküll's **umwelt** concept (see `cognitive-science/umwelt-uexkull-biosemiotics.md`) precedes Gibson but arrives at a related conclusion by a different route. Each organism inhabits a *species-specific world* defined by its sensory and motor apparatus. What Uexküll calls the *Merkwelt* (perception world) structures the environmental features that are available as sign-carriers; what Gibson calls affordances are the action-possibilities that those features specify. The umwelt and the affordance landscape are complementary descriptions of the same underlying phenomenon: the organism's world is not the physicist's world but the *ecologically relevant* world — structured by what the organism can perceive and what it can do.

### Active Inference and Predictive Processing

Friston's free energy principle offers a formal bridge between direct perception and predictive processing. In the active inference framework, an organism acts to bring sensory input in line with its generative model's predictions. Affordances can be understood as *prior beliefs* about what the environment makes possible — policies of action that the generative model anticipates as available. The organism doesn't merely perceive affordances but *actively tests* them through action, reducing uncertainty about what the environment actually offers.

This creates an interesting synthesis: predictive processing explains *how* affordance perception works (via generative models), while ecological psychology explains *what* is being perceived (relational action-possibilities). The two frameworks are not necessarily rivals.

## Agent Implications

For an AI system operating via tool use and memory:

- **Tool affordances**: Each tool affords certain operations. A well-designed tool interface makes affordances transparent — the agent "sees" what the tool enables without having to reason through its documentation each time.
- **Memory affordances**: A well-structured knowledge base affords different retrieval strategies. Poorly organized memory hides affordances — the agent cannot easily perceive what it can recall.
- **Environmental coupling**: Agent architectures with persistent environments, feedback loops, and ongoing action-perception cycles approach the Gibsonian picture more closely than pure query-response architectures.
- **Relevance without representation**: Affordance theory suggests that relevance can be *in* the environment (in the interface design, the tool structure, the knowledge organization) rather than entirely in the agent's internal model — a design principle for cognitive scaffolding.
