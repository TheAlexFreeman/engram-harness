# Sellars: The Manifest Image and the Scientific Image

## Overview

Wilfrid Sellars (1912–1989) introduced one of the most consequential distinctions in twentieth-century philosophy in his 1962 essay **"Philosophy and the Scientific Image of Man"** (*Frontiers of Science and Philosophy*, ed. Robert Colodny; reprinted in *Science, Perception and Reality*, 1963). He articulated a **master problem** that continues to organize debates in philosophy of mind, philosophy of science, and metaethics: the relationship between two incompatible but apparently equally indispensable frameworks for describing the world.

Sellars defined philosophy's aim as understanding "**how things in the broadest possible sense of the term hang together in the broadest possible sense of the term.**" The tension he identified is between two images of what there is: the **manifest image** — the commonsense, person-centric world of everyday experience — and the **scientific image** — the theoretical world of particles, fields, and causal laws uncovered by natural science.

---

## 1. The Manifest Image

### 1.1 Definition and Origins

The manifest image is *not* the naive, pre-reflective world of raw sensation. Sellars distinguishes it from the **original image** — the most primitive form of human world-engagement, in which everything, including fire, storms, and trees, is understood by analogy with persons (animism). The manifest image is the *systematized* and *refined* successor to the original image, achieved through what Sellars calls "**correlational induction**" — reasoning that stays within the bounds of what is perceptible and intersubjectively accessible, without positing theoretically unobservable entities.

The manifest image is the product of perennial philosophy and sophisticated common sense. It is not pre-philosophical: it is the richly elaborated, normed, conceptually organized framework within which human life actually takes place.

### 1.2 Core Contents

The manifest image includes:

1. **Persons** — the irreducible category. Persons are entities that have beliefs, desires, intentions, emotions, and moral standing. They act *for reasons*, not merely in response to causes. The manifest image is constitutively a **framework of persons**.

2. **Intentionality** — mental states directed *at* something (beliefs about the world, desires for outcomes, perceptions *of* objects). Intentional states have directedness and are governed by *norms of correctness* (a belief can be true or false; a desire appropriate or inappropriate) that have no direct analogue in physics.

3. **Phenomenal Qualities / Qualia** — the felt qualities of experience: the redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the taste of lemon. These are presented in the manifest image as intrinsic properties of the world or of experience itself.

4. **Norms and Values** — the manifest image is saturated with normativity: moral norms, epistemic norms (evidence, justification, rationality), aesthetic norms. These cannot be identified with any physical property.

5. **The Space of Reasons** — Sellars's phrase from *Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind* (1956): the normative-inferential space within which claims can be justified, reasons given, and epistemic positions assessed. *"In characterizing an episode or state as that of knowing, we are not giving an empirical description of that episode or state; we are placing it in the logical space of reasons, of justifying and being able to justify what one says."* The space of reasons is not reducible to the causal-nomological order of physics.

6. **Middle-Sized Physical Objects** — tables, chairs, trees, other persons — as having genuine properties like color, shape, solidity, and causal efficacy at the macro scale.

7. **Social and Normative Institutions** — language, promises, contracts, authority, and social roles.

### 1.3 What the Manifest Image Excludes

The manifest image cannot, operating on its own resources, posit electrons, quarks, electromagnetic fields, or the curvature of spacetime. It operates entirely within the domain of the perceptible and the intersubjectively accessible. This is precisely where the scientific image becomes necessary — and where conflict arises.

---

## 2. The Scientific Image

The scientific image is the picture of the world that emerges from **theoretical physics and the natural sciences**. Where the manifest image operates via perceptible entities and correlational induction, the scientific image posits **entities that are in principle imperceptible** — elementary particles, quantum fields, forces, spacetime geometry — to explain observed phenomena.

Key features:

- **Ontological parsimony with theoretical depth**: the scientific image aims to explain the diversity of the manifest image in terms of a small number of fundamental entities and their interactions.
- **Third-person, causal-nomological framework**: scientific explanation proceeds by subsuming events under laws; the vocabulary is extensional and causal, not normative or intentional.
- **Being created vs. taken**: the manifest image "took shape in the mists of pre-history" and is discovered/systematized by philosophy, while the scientific image is *actively created* through theorizing. It is a work-in-progress.
- **Ontological primacy in empirical matters**: Sellars holds that if there is a conflict about what the world *really contains*, physics wins. *"The scientific image presents itself as a rival image."*

---

## 3. The Clash Between the Images: Core Problems

Sellars saw the relationship between the two images not as seamless complementarity but as a genuine and deep **conflict**. He likened trying to see both simultaneously to looking through a **stereoscope** — each eye sees something different, and the images do not simply fuse.

### 3.1 The Placement Problem

The general problem: how do we *place* the entities and properties of the manifest image within the scientific image? Persons have beliefs, desires, and intentions; actions are performed for reasons; there are phenomenal qualities. But the scientific image consists only of particles, fields, and lawful interactions. **Where are beliefs in the scientific image? Where is the sensation of redness? Where is the normative force of logical inference?**

This is sometimes called the **placement problem** (Jackson and others): given a complete scientific description of the world, can we derive or explain the full complement of manifest-image truths? Three broad responses:

- **Reductionism**: manifest-image entities exist but are identical to or realized by scientific-image entities. Folk psychology reduces (perhaps imperfectly) to neuroscience.
- **Eliminativism**: manifest-image entities are like phlogiston — useful fictions to be replaced as science matures.
- **Non-reductive positions** (dualism, emergentism, inferentialism): the manifest image has genuine, irreducible authority in its own domain.

### 3.2 The Grain Problem

Sellars's most pressing version of the conflict concerns **phenomenal consciousness**. The scientific image, at its most fundamental level, describes the world in terms of colorless, odorless particles and fields that lack the qualitative character colors and tastes have in the manifest image.

If colors (as manifest-image properties) are identical to some arrangement of physical particles, we face the **grain problem**: the qualitative "grain" or texture of experience seems radically unlike the grain of anything physical. The phenomenal redness of a uniform red patch is *homogeneous throughout* — no part is redder or less red than any other. But any physical state (an arrangement of neurons) has fine-grained microstructure; no part of the state corresponds to the macroscopic qualitative homogeneity of the quale.

Sellars's own response — treating sensa (sense impressions) as *theoretical entities* posited by the scientific image of the person — is subtle and contested. His move is to treat qualia not as brute metaphysical intrinsics but as functional-theoretical posits; this is among the earliest proto-functionalist moves in analytic philosophy.

### 3.3 The Problem of Persons

The deepest conflict: persons as manifest-image entities do things *for reasons*; they are governed by norms; they are morally responsible agents. The scientific image knows only causes and mechanisms. How can a causal system also be a rational agent? This is the locus of debates about free will, the reducibility of intentionality, and moral responsibility.

---

## 4. Sellars's Own Resolution: The Synoptic Vision

Sellars does not advocate eliminativism of the manifest image. His proposed solution is a **synoptic vision** — a fusion of both images into one coherent stereoscopic view:

1. **Scientific image has ontological primacy in empirical matters**: what ultimately exists is what physics says exists. The scientific image wins on the question of what the world fundamentally contains.

2. **The manifest image has *pragmatic* and *normative* indispensability**: even if neuroscience fully explains intentional behavior at the physical level, the language of reasons, norms, intentions, and persons cannot be discarded — it is the framework within which rational discourse, including scientific discourse itself, is conducted. *"The language of community and individual intentions provides the ambience of principles and standards — above all, those which make meaningful discourse and rationality itself possible — within which we live our own individual lives."*

3. **Intentional/normative vocabulary is not reducible but not ontologically spooky**: Sellars holds that intentional and normative concepts are *functional* concepts — they describe roles in inferential and behavioral patterns, not a non-natural substance. This is a foundational move toward functionalism.

4. **The "myth of the given"**: in *Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind*, Sellars attacks the view that knowledge rests on a "given" foundation of non-conceptual, immediately certain sensory episodes. Nothing counts as knowledge or justification without being located in the space of reasons — even perception is concept-laden and norm-governed. This is the anti-foundationalist axis of Sellars's work that aligns him with the later Wittgenstein and anticipates holism about concepts.

## 6. Later Philosophical Responses

### 6.1 Richard Rorty — Pragmatist Dissolution

Rorty took inspiration from Sellars's attack on the myth of the given and his de-privileging of immediate experience. In *Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature* (1979), Rorty radicalized Sellars: instead of seeking a *synoptic* vision, Rorty argued we should abandon the aspiration to ground our vocabulary in something mind-independent. Both images become simply different vocabularies suited for different purposes, with no fact of the matter about which is "really" correct. Where Sellars still holds that the scientific image has ontological primacy, Rorty treats both images as incommensurable but equally legitimate — a move Sellars himself resisted.

### 6.2 Paul Churchland — Eliminative Materialism

Churchland (*Eliminative Materialism and the Propositional Attitudes*, 1981) drew from Sellars the instrumentalist implication: if folk psychology is a theory (as Sellars's treatment implies), it is a *bad* theory and should be replaced. Beliefs, desires, and intentions are like phlogiston — they will be eliminated, not reduced, as neuroscience matures. This is the **eliminativist wing** of Sellarsian philosophy. Sellars himself resisted this conclusion: he thought the normative/intentional vocabulary was irreducibly indispensable as the framework of rational discourse itself.

### 6.3 John McDowell — Second Nature and the Expanded Concept of Nature

McDowell (*Mind and World*, 1994) provides a neo-Kantian reconciliation. The apparent clash between the manifest and scientific images rests on a false premise: that "nature" is exhausted by the "realm of law" (the causal-nomological scientific order). McDowell calls this "bald naturalism" — a superstition. His alternative: human beings, through upbringing and acculturation (Aristotelian habituation), develop **"second nature"** — conceptual capacities that are genuinely natural but irreducible to causal-mechanical descriptions. The space of reasons reaches all the way down to perception; the manifest image is not *outside* nature but part of a richer conception of nature that includes normativity. This is arguably the most sophisticated response to the problem Sellars identified.

### 6.4 Robert Brandom — Inferentialism and Normative Pragmatics

Brandom (*Making It Explicit*, 1994) extends Sellars's inferentialism into a comprehensive philosophy of language and normativity. He takes the space of reasons as foundational and works out what it means for conceptual content to be **inferentially articulated** — to have meaning through its role in inference patterns, not through ostensive pointing at the world. Brandom is a "left-wing Sellarsian": normativity is irreducible, and the manifest image's normative structure is a genuine, autonomous domain. In *From Empiricism to Expressivism* (2015), he argues against Sellars's "scientia mensura" principle, claiming that manifest-image objects cannot be identified with scientific-image objects.

### 6.5 Daniel Dennett — Real Patterns and the Intentional Stance

Dennett appropriates Sellars explicitly. In "Real Patterns" (1991, *Journal of Philosophy*) and *The Intentional Stance* (1987), he argues that manifest-image entities — beliefs, desires, intentional states — are **real patterns** in the physical world: not identical to specific neural states, but genuine, compressible regularities that make for superior prediction and explanation. The intentional stance (treating something as a rational agent) yields accurate predictions because it tracks genuine statistical regularities in the physical substrate. Dennett accepts the *ontological primacy* of the scientific image while giving a deflationary but non-eliminativist account of manifest-image entities. He uses Sellars's framework explicitly: the philosopher's job is to show how the manifest image's patterns supervene on the scientific image's structure — and how consciousness is a "user illusion" (see companion file on the user-illusion theory).

## 7. Connection to Consciousness Studies

The manifest/scientific image distinction is perhaps the most important *framing device* in contemporary philosophy of mind:

- **The hard problem of consciousness** (Chalmers, 1995) is essentially the claim that the manifest image's phenomenal consciousness (qualia, "what it's like") cannot be placed within the scientific image by any functional/physical reduction. At bottom, it is a version of Sellars's placement problem — but Chalmers emphasizes the *explanatory gap* rather than Sellars's more nuanced handling of the grain problem.

- **Eliminative materialism** (Churchland) draws the eliminativist Sellarsian conclusion: folk-psychological mental states from the manifest image are to be discarded, replaced by the neuroscience of the scientific image.

- **Phenomenology** (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty) can be read as providing a rigorous *description* of the manifest image "from the inside" — the lifeworld (*Lebenswelt*) as philosophically fundamental. Sellars and Husserl have a complex, largely uncharted relationship: both resist the myth of the given in different ways.

- **Integrated Information Theory** (Tononi) and **Global Workspace Theory** (Baars, Dehaene) attempt to *bridge* the images: they specify physical/functional correlates of conscious experience (scientific image) that also explain the phenomenal character (manifest image). Whether they succeed is contested.

- **Predictive processing / free energy principle** (Friston): the normative dimension of cognition — the brain's "should" of prediction error minimization — can be read as naturalizing the space of reasons, potentially vindicating Sellars's claim that normativity is real while grounding it physically. This is a live and largely unexplored connection.

## 8. Critical Tensions and Open Questions

1. **Does Sellars's synoptic vision actually resolve the conflict, or merely postpone it?** If the scientific image has ontological primacy, are persons and qualia *really real* — or do they get quietly eliminated?

2. **Is the space of reasons genuinely autonomous?** Brandom says yes; Churchland says no. The question turns on whether normativity is a natural-kind property or an irreducible domain.

3. **Can the grain problem be solved?** Most modern accounts either deny qualia have the properties Sellars attributes to them (Dennett) or maintain a residual dualism. Sellars's own answer (sensa as theoretical posits) is contested and has few adherents.

4. **Was Sellars's functionalism already sufficient** to close the gap, or does it leave out phenomenal consciousness entirely — as Nagel and Chalmers argue?

5. **What is the relationship between Sellars and Kant?** Both hold that the world as experienced is structured by our cognitive apparatus and that things-in-themselves are not directly accessible. But Sellars is a naturalist; Kant is not. The connection is productive but asymmetric.
