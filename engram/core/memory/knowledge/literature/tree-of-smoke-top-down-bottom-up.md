# Tree of Smoke: Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up Processing

## Overview

Denis Johnson's *Tree of Smoke* (2007, National Book Award winner) is structured, at its deepest level, as a sustained meditation on two irreconcilable ways of knowing: the top-down imposition of conceptual frameworks onto a recalcitrant reality, and the bottom-up accumulation of raw, often shattering experience that refuses to be organized by those frameworks. The Vietnam War serves as the novel's crucible not merely as historical backdrop but as the site where these epistemological modes collide — and where the collision proves catastrophic for virtually every character.

The terms "top-down" and "bottom-up" are borrowed from cognitive science and perception theory. In top-down processing, prior knowledge, expectations, and high-level conceptual schemas shape how lower-level data is interpreted — the mind projects meaning onto the world. In bottom-up processing, raw sensory data builds upward toward meaning without the guidance of pre-existing schema — the world forces itself onto the mind. Johnson dramatizes the war as an environment in which top-down frameworks (American strategic doctrine, intelligence theory, religious faith, ideological conviction) are systematically overwhelmed by bottom-up reality (violence, confusion, moral disorder, unabsorbable grief).

---

## The Intelligence Apparatus as Top-Down Epistemology

The CIA machinery and the military intelligence complex are the novel's primary figures for top-down processing. Colonel Francis Sands — charismatic, visionary, possibly brilliant, certainly unhinged — incarnates this mode in its most extreme form. His "Tree of Smoke" project is a disinformation operation of extraordinary ambition: a scheme to deceive the Viet Cong by feeding them false intelligence through a carefully cultivated double agent (Trung), which will in turn corrupt their own intelligence picture.

The project is a perfect structural emblem of top-down cognition: it works by inserting a conceptual layer (the false picture) between the enemy and reality. The Colonel believes that if you control the frame, you control the world. His intelligence vision is essentially Platonic — the real action happens at the level of abstraction; the war on the ground is secondary, even epiphenomenal, to the war of information and interpretation.

Johnson carefully shows the grandiosity and self-sealing quality of this top-down system. The Colonel builds elaborate dossiers, speaks in the cadences of a prophet, and accumulates a personal mythology that his nephew Skip gradually recognizes as a kind of madness masquerading as insight. The system cannot incorporate disconfirming information — any evidence against the scheme can be reinterpreted as enemy counter-intelligence, which is itself proof the scheme is working. This is the diagnostic feature of pathological top-down processing: the framework becomes unfalsifiable.

## Skip Sands: The Disillusionment Arc as Epistemological Journey

Skip is the novel's central consciousness for much of its length, and his arc is precisely the arc of a top-down believer being broken down by bottom-up reality. He arrives in Southeast Asia as a true convert to the Colonel's vision — educated, earnest, and invested in the idea that intelligence work is a form of deep understanding, that the right conceptual framework can unlock the war's meaning.

His disillusionment is gradual and richly rendered. Key stages:

- **The dossiers:** Skip's work compiling files on individuals — including the dead — begins to feel like a parody of understanding. The files accumulate detail without producing knowledge. The gap between the thickness of the folder and the thinness of genuine comprehension becomes unbearable.
- **Trung's opacity:** The Vietnamese double agent Trung is the novel's most pointed figure for the limits of top-down knowing. The Americans believe they understand him — his motivations, his loyalties, his use. Johnson systematically withholds Trung's interiority, making him unreadable in a way that mirrors the American failure to "read" Vietnam itself. Top-down processing requires that the object conform to the schema; Trung refuses.
- **The Psalms and religious seeking:** Skip's intermittent engagement with scripture represents a parallel top-down move — the attempt to find a transcendent framework that would organize his experience. It fails in the same way the Colonel's project fails, for the same structural reason: the framework cannot absorb the brute particulars.
- **The novel's closing decades:** Skip's postwar life in the Philippines is a study in what remains after the top-down project collapses — a man unmoored, moving through the world without organizing schema, picking up fragments of religious practice that never quite cohere.

## The Houston Brothers: Bottom-Up Experience Without Framework

James and Bill Houston represent the opposite pole. They are grunts — not ideologues, not intelligence operatives — and their experience of the war is almost entirely bottom-up: a succession of sensory events, violent encounters, and survival decisions that arrive without conceptual scaffolding and resist retrospective organization.

Johnson's prose when inhabiting the Houston brothers' perspectives is characteristically clipped, paratactic, impressionistic. Sentences don't build arguments; they accumulate perceptions. This stylistic shift is not accidental — it enacts the bottom-up phenomenology of their situation. They are not processing the war through frameworks; the war is processing them.

What is striking is that the Houston brothers do not achieve the redemptive clarity that a simpler narrative might grant them as a corrective to top-down hubris. Their bottom-up experience does not coalesce into wisdom. James's postwar trajectory (drug addiction, crime, institutional cycling) and Bill's aimless drift suggest that raw experience without adequate frameworks is its own catastrophe. Johnson refuses the Romantic suggestion that immediate experience is truer or healthier than conceptual mediation. Bottom-up processing without any organizing structure is not liberation — it is dissolution.

This is the novel's most challenging and morally serious move: neither mode works. Top-down frameworks impose false coherence; bottom-up experience without frameworks produces incoherence. The war destroys both.

## The Title as Figure

"Tree of Smoke" is overdetermined as an image, but one of its most productive readings is epistemological. Smoke is the consummate bottom-up sensory phenomenon: it has texture, volume, color, movement — it is emphatically present — but it resists the stable objecthood that top-down processing requires. You cannot organize smoke the way you organize a tree. Yet the phrase inverts this: it is a *tree* of smoke — the imposition of a top-down organizing structure (the tree, with its hierarchy, its root-to-crown intelligibility) onto something constitutively resistant to that structure.

The Colonel's disinformation project is literally called "Tree of Smoke" — a hierarchy of controlled meaning built on something that billows, shifts, and disperses. This is the novel's thesis in a figure: top-down projects erected on bottom-up reality are smoke given the appearance of wood.

## Narrative Structure as Epistemological Form

Johnson's formal choices reinforce the thematic argument. The novel's structure is fragmented, multi-perspectival, and temporally non-linear, spanning from 1963 to 1983. No single consciousness is privileged. The reader cannot achieve the panoptic view that the Colonel imagines — cannot see the whole board from above. Instead, the reading experience is itself bottom-up: we accumulate partial perspectives, conflicting impressions, and irresolvable ambiguities.

This is particularly pointed given the novel's subject. Intelligence work promises the top-down view — the file, the dossier, the complete picture. Johnson's form refuses us that view, implicating us in the same epistemological predicament as the characters. We are, as readers, trying to process the war from below, building upward toward meaning that the novel deliberately withholds.

## Kathy Jones: Faith as Top-Down Processing Under Siege

Kathy, the Canadian aid worker and missionary nurse, provides the novel's most sustained examination of religious belief as top-down processing. Her faith is an organizing schema — a framework that should, in principle, render suffering meaningful, locate individual experience within a providential order, and supply moral guidance in conditions of extreme disorder.

The war systematically pressures this schema. The suffering Kathy witnesses does not fit within redemptive narratives. Her romantic and spiritual entanglements — with Skip, with the Psalmist's language — represent attempts to maintain the top-down framework under assault from bottom-up reality. Her arc ends in ambiguity: faith neither vindicated nor definitively lost, but worn, patched, no longer seamless.

Johnson's treatment of faith is notable for its lack of irony or condescension. He does not present Kathy's framework as simply naive. The question the novel asks is not whether top-down frameworks are foolish, but whether any framework can survive contact with this particular reality — and whether we can survive without one.

---

## Hao / Trung: The Vietnamese Perspective as Structural Irony

The novel's Vietnamese characters — particularly Trung — serve a crucial structural function. They are the objects of American top-down processing (the subjects of the dossiers, the targets of the disinformation scheme) who are never fully captured by those processes. Trung's motivations remain genuinely opaque: is he loyal to the North, the South, himself, some other principle entirely? Johnson refuses to resolve this.

This opacity is not a failure of characterization but a deliberate argument: the top-down American intelligence project has constructed an elaborate picture of Trung that may bear no relationship to who Trung actually is. The gap between the picture and the person is the gap between top-down projection and bottom-up reality. Johnson suggests — quietly, without melodrama — that the entire American enterprise in Vietnam may be a version of this error at scale: a conceptual projection onto a reality that was never asked, never obliged, and never organized itself accordingly.

## Key Passages for Further Analysis

- Colonel Sands's speech about "the other war" and the nature of intelligence (early sections): exemplary top-down manifesto
- Skip's work with the dossiers of the dead: the pathos of information without understanding
- James Houston in combat: the phenomenological texture of bottom-up experience
- Kathy's prayers and her readings of the Psalms: top-down framework under stress
- The Colonel's breakdown / disappearance: the collapse of the top-down project
- The 1983 epilogue: the afterlife of both modes, neither vindicated

## Connections and Comparanda

- **Tim O'Brien, *The Things They Carried*** (1990): Similar preoccupation with the gap between story-making (top-down) and raw happening (bottom-up), but O'Brien is more sanguine about narrative's power to redeem experience. Johnson is darker.
- **Graham Greene, *The Quiet American*** (1955): Alden Pyle's ideological confidence as top-down processing; Fowler's weary empiricism as bottom-up. Johnson's Colonel Sands inherits something from Pyle.
- **Thomas Pynchon, *Gravity's Rainbow*** (1973): The conspiratorial schema as top-down project; the Zone as bottom-up reality. Structurally analogous, if tonally different.
- **Cognitive science / perception theory:** David Marr's levels of analysis; Karl Friston's predictive processing framework (the brain as a prediction machine constantly updating top-down priors against bottom-up prediction errors) offers a surprisingly productive lens for the novel's dynamics.

## Open Questions for Further Research

1. Does Johnson finally privilege either mode, or does the novel maintain genuine equipoise between them?
2. How does Johnson's own biography (addiction, conversion, recovery) inflect the epistemological drama? Is there an autobiographical dimension to the top-down/bottom-up conflict?
3. How does the novel's treatment of language — particularly the Colonel's ornate, prophetic speech vs. the Houston brothers' clipped vernacular — map onto the top-down/bottom-up distinction?
4. The 1983 epilogue features characters who have lived through the collapse of both modes. What, if anything, does Johnson suggest is left? Is there a third mode he gestures toward?
5. Compare with Johnson's *Jesus' Son* (1992): the bottom-up experience of the addict, without framework, as an earlier treatment of the same problem.
