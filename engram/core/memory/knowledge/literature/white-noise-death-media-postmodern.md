# White Noise: Death, Media, and the Postmodern Sublime

Don DeLillo's eighth novel, winner of the 1985 National Book Award, follows Jack Gladney, chairman of the department of Hitler Studies at the College-on-the-Hill, through the routines of his blended family, a catastrophic industrial accident called the Airborne Toxic Event, and his desperate attempt to obtain a black-market drug called Dylar that promises to eliminate the fear of death. The novel is very funny, structurally disciplined, and philosophically relentless. Its subject is what happens to death — and to the fear of death — when experience is mediated by television, supermarkets, and the continuous low-frequency hum of information that gives the novel its title.

## 1. The White Noise Itself

The title names something that pervades the novel without ever being the focus of a scene. Television mutters in the background of nearly every domestic moment. Brand names surface mid-sentence like involuntary speech. The radio announces disasters in the cadences of weather reports. The supermarket aisles radiate a liturgical order. DeLillo renders this ambient information not as content to be processed but as an environment to be inhabited — a constant, low-grade signal that fills the silence where other kinds of awareness might otherwise occur.

This is McLuhan's insight dramatized: the medium has become the environment. The Gladneys do not watch television for information. They live inside its field. The white noise is not background to their lives; it *is* the texture of their lives, and its removal — during the evacuation for the Airborne Toxic Event — produces not clarity but panic. The silence is worse than the hum because the hum was what stood between them and the questions they cannot answer.

The novel anticipates by decades the condition that media theory would later call ambient information — the state in which people are never fully attending to media and never fully free of it, in which the distinction between signal and noise has become a matter of attention rather than content. DeLillo understood in 1985 what the smartphone would make universal: that the most powerful effect of media is not what it says but that it is always there.

## 2. Death and the Supermarket

Jack Gladney is afraid of dying. His wife Babette is afraid of dying. Their conversations about who is more afraid of dying are among the funniest and most harrowing passages in postwar American fiction. The fear is not prompted by any particular threat — it precedes the Airborne Toxic Event and survives it. It is constitutional, existential, irreducible.

Heidegger argued that authentic existence requires confronting one's own death — that the awareness of being-toward-death is what rescues Dasein from the anonymous chatter of das Man, the "they-self" that absorbs individual existence into conventional roles. DeLillo's characters are Heideggerian subjects who cannot manage the confrontation. They know they will die. The knowledge is unbearable. And instead of the authentic resolution Heidegger described, they reach for every available buffer: pharmaceutical, commercial, informational, institutional.

The supermarket is DeLillo's central image for this buffering. The A&P scenes are written with a reverence that borders on the sacred — the stacked goods, the fluorescent light, the implied promise that everything is available and everything is organized. The supermarket is a temple of consumer abundance whose real function is not to provide food but to provide the sense that the world is orderly, legible, and under control. It is a defense against the chaos that death introduces — a secular liturgy whose repeated rituals (shopping, choosing, purchasing) substitute for the religious rituals that once did the same work.

## 3. The Airborne Toxic Event

The novel's second section departs from domestic comedy into something closer to disaster narrative. An industrial accident releases a cloud of Nyodene D. over the Gladney family's town. They evacuate. Jack is briefly exposed to the cloud and is told the substance may have long-term health consequences — though the exact nature and timeline remain uncertain.

The Toxic Event functions on multiple levels. As plot, it introduces genuine mortal threat into a novel that had been circling death abstractly. As satire, it exposes the absurdity of institutional response — the SIMUVAC organization, which exists to simulate evacuations, decides to use the real evacuation as an opportunity to rehearse its simulations. The real event becomes the rehearsal for its own representation.

This is the novel's deepest joke and its most serious insight. In a media-saturated culture, the relationship between event and representation has inverted. The Gladneys experience the toxic cloud partly as spectacle — they evaluate the sunsets it produces, they compare it to television disasters, they narrate their own experience in the cadences of news coverage. The event is real (the chemical exposure is genuinely dangerous to Jack) but its reality is accessible only through layers of representation that simultaneously communicate and insulate.

DeLillo is not making the simple poststructuralist claim that there is no reality behind the signs. He is making the more unsettling claim that there *is* a reality — the chemical is in Jack's body, the death is coming — but that the culture's apparatus of representation has made it nearly impossible to experience that reality directly. The signs do not replace the real; they surround it so thoroughly that the real can only be approached through them.

## 4. Dylar and the Pharmacology of Fear

Babette's secret: she has been taking an experimental drug called Dylar, obtained through a sexual arrangement with the project manager, that is supposed to eliminate the fear of death. The drug does not work — or rather, it works on the wrong level. It damages the capacity to distinguish between words and things. A person on Dylar who hears the word "fire" dives to the floor. The drug does not remove the fear of death; it removes the mediating layer of symbolic representation that allows humans to think about death without being destroyed by it.

This is DeLillo's most precise philosophical move. The fear of death is not a bug in human consciousness. It is a feature of the same symbolic capacity that makes language, thought, and culture possible. To be a creature that uses symbols is to be a creature that can represent its own nonexistence — and therefore a creature that can be terrified by its own nonexistence. Dylar's failure demonstrates that you cannot surgically remove death anxiety without destroying the symbolic apparatus that makes you human. The cure is worse than the disease because the disease is coextensive with the capacity for meaning.

This connects to the meaning crisis in reverse. Vervaeke describes the meaning crisis as the loss of frameworks that once made life meaningful. DeLillo shows the complementary problem: even when meaning-frameworks are functioning (the Gladneys have careers, family, community, intellectual life), the fear they were meant to address — the raw fact of mortality — bleeds through. The crisis is not only that we have lost meaning but that meaning was never quite sufficient to the task it was built for.

## 5. Hitler Studies and the Aesthetics of Mass Death

Jack Gladney invented the field of Hitler Studies. He does not speak German. This is played for comedy, but the comedy is load-bearing. Hitler is the figure in the twentieth century who most completely converted mass death into spectacle — who made death into an aesthetic experience on an industrial scale. Jack's professional immersion in Hitler is another form of death-buffering: by studying the most overwhelming instance of death in modern history, he attempts to master his own fear through intellectual domestication.

DeLillo is tracking something real about academic culture and, more broadly, about the way knowledge institutions process threatening material. The university converts Hitler into a subject, complete with conferences, bibliographies, and departmental politics. The conversion is not dishonest — genuine scholarship happens — but it serves a secondary function: making the unbearable into the manageable by giving it a syllabus. This is the same operation the supermarket performs on hunger and the television performs on catastrophe. Institutions metabolize fear into routine.

## 6. The Novel's Place in American Postmodernism

*White Noise* is often grouped with Pynchon, Barth, and the postmodernist metafictionists, but DeLillo's relationship to postmodernism is more diagnostic than participatory. He does not celebrate the free play of signs or the dissolution of grand narratives. He anatomizes a culture in which signs have proliferated to the point where they constitute the primary environment, and he finds this condition terrifying rather than liberating.

Where Wallace, a decade later, would diagnose the same cultural condition and prescribe sincerity as the remedy, DeLillo offers no prescription. *White Noise* ends in the supermarket — the aisles have been rearranged, the familiar order disrupted, and the shoppers wander in confusion. There is no resolution because DeLillo does not believe the problem admits of one. Death is real. The fear is rational. The culture's mechanisms for managing the fear are elaborate and insufficient. The white noise continues.

The novel's lasting power is its refusal to resolve this tension. It does not comfort the reader with the possibility that authenticity or sincerity or spiritual practice can overcome the fundamental terror of being a mortal creature in a world of signs. It simply describes the condition with extraordinary precision and allows the reader to recognize it as their own.
