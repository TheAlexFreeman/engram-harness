# Ulysses — Consciousness, the Body, and the Ordinary Epic

## 1. June 16, 1904

*Ulysses* takes place in a single day. Leopold Bloom — advertising canvasser, Hungarian-Irish Jew, cuckold, father of a dead son — wakes, makes breakfast, uses the outhouse, attends a funeral, does business, eats lunch, visits a library, walks along the beach, goes to a pub, checks on a woman in labor, gets into an altercation, accompanies Stephen Dedalus to a brothel, takes the young man home for cocoa, goes to bed beside his wife Molly, who has spent the afternoon with her concert manager Blazes Boylan.

Nothing happens. Everything happens. The novel contains no dramatic climax, no revelation that changes the course of a life, no villain defeated or prize won. It contains, instead, the fullest representation of a human consciousness in a single day that literature has achieved — and in achieving it, argues that the single ordinary day is epic enough.

## 2. The Homeric Scaffold

Each of the novel's eighteen episodes corresponds to an episode in Homer's *Odyssey*. Bloom is Odysseus, the wanderer trying to get home; Stephen is Telemachus, the fatherless son; Molly is Penelope, the wife besieged by suitors. The correspondences are sometimes precise (the Cyclops episode takes place in a pub where a one-eyed nationalist rages), sometimes oblique (the Sirens episode is set in a bar where barmaids sing and Bloom writes a letter to another woman), and sometimes almost invisible.

The parallel is not allegory. Joyce does not retell the *Odyssey* in modern dress; he uses the *Odyssey* as a structural armature that reveals, through contrast, both what modernity has lost and what it has retained. Odysseus slaughtered the suitors and reclaimed his wife; Bloom does not confront Boylan at all. The deflation is the point. Bloom's heroism is not martial but domestic, not dramatic but patient — the heroism of getting through the day, of feeding a cat, of remembering a dead son, of returning to a bed his wife has shared with another man and deciding, without grand gestures, that the marriage continues.

Joyce's argument, implied by the Homeric structure itself, is that the epic scale of human experience has not diminished. What has changed is the lens. Homer's world externalizes the struggle — monsters, wars, divine interventions. Joyce's world internalizes it — the battlefield is consciousness, the monsters are memories and anxieties, the divine intervention is a moment of kindness from a stranger. Bloom's odyssey is as real as Odysseus's. The novel dares the reader to disagree.

## 3. Stream of Consciousness

The technique is usually attributed to Joyce (along with Woolf and Faulkner), but what Joyce actually does in *Ulysses* is more various and stranger than the label suggests. "Stream of consciousness" implies a continuous flow; what Bloom's interior monologue delivers is something more like a series of associative jumps — a word overheard triggers a memory, the memory triggers a bodily sensation, the sensation triggers an advertisement, the advertisement triggers a thought about death, and the whole sequence occupies the time it takes to walk half a block.

The technique's philosophical significance is enormous. It demonstrates that consciousness is not a theater watched by a homunculus but a field of continuous, overlapping processes — perceptual, mnemonic, affective, somatic — that cohere without being organized by a central executive. Bloom does not decide to think about his dead son Rudy; the thought arrives, triggered by an associative chain he did not initiate. He does not choose to feel hungry; the body inserts itself into the stream.

This anticipates both Husserl's time-consciousness (the primal impression embedded in a flow of retentions and protentions, the "thick present" in which past and future interpenetrate the now) and Merleau-Ponty's insistence that perception is always already embodied, that consciousness does not hover above the body but is saturated by it. Bloom's inner monologue is the literary equivalent of the phenomenological claim that the self is not a substance but a process.

## 4. The Body Speaks

*Ulysses* is the most thoroughly embodied novel ever written. Bloom eats — "Mr Leopold Bloom ate with relish the inner organs of beasts and fowls. He liked thick giblet soup, nutty gizzards, a stuffed roast heart." He defecates — the Calypso episode ends in the outhouse. He masturbates — the Nausicaa episode, on the beach, watching a girl lean back to show her underwear. He bathes, urinates, farts, and falls asleep. Molly's final monologue, the great "yes" of the last chapter, moves through menstruation, sexual memory, pregnancy, and desire with a candor that got the book banned for over a decade.

This is not provocation for its own sake. Joyce understood that the Western literary tradition had been systematically dishonest about the body — that Hamlet thinks but does not digest, that Emma Bovary desires but does not menstruate. By insisting on the body's full presence, *Ulysses* makes a philosophical claim: consciousness is not separable from its biological substrate. Bloom's thoughts about death at Paddy Dignam's funeral and his thoughts about lunch are not different registers of experience; they are the same consciousness, operating simultaneously, and the meal is as real as the meditation.

The body in *Ulysses* is not the soul's prison; it is the soul's medium. Bloom is kind, observant, forgiving, curious, and melancholy, and none of these qualities exist apart from the body that eats, walks, and aches. His moral life is not a set of principles upheld against bodily temptation; it is a pattern of attention enacted through bodily engagement with the world — stopping to help a blind man cross the street, buying soap, feeding a stray cat. The body does not interrupt consciousness; it completes it.

## 5. Eighteen Styles

Each episode of *Ulysses* is written in a different style, and the stylistic variation intensifies as the novel progresses. The early episodes use relatively conventional narrative techniques; by the middle of the book, Joyce deploys fugal structure (Sirens), gigantism and catalogue (Cyclops), scientific catechism (Ithaca), and a phantasmagoric drama (Circe) that stages Bloom's and Stephen's unconscious fears as hallucinatory theater. The Oxen of the Sun episode traverses the entire history of English prose style, from Anglo-Saxon alliterative verse to twentieth-century slang, in a single chapter.

The effect is to make the medium itself unstable. The reader cannot settle into a single relationship with the narrative because the narrative keeps changing its terms. This is not ornamental virtuosity; it is an argument about representation. No single style is adequate to reality. The stream-of-consciousness technique of the early chapters, for all its intimacy, captures only one dimension of experience; the catechism of "Ithaca" captures another; Molly's unpunctuated monologue captures yet another. The novel's encyclopedic form — its famous aspiration to include everything — is a recognition that totality requires multiplicity, that the one day in Dublin can only be rendered by an instrument that is itself as various as the day.

## 6. Bloom's Decency

The novel's moral center is not its technique but its hero. Leopold Bloom is a good man — not heroically, not demonstratively, but persistently. He feeds cats. He worries about his daughter. He attends the funeral of a man he barely knew. He helps a blind man. He follows a woman in labor to the hospital. He takes Stephen Dedalus home when the young man is drunk and in danger. He does not strike Blazes Boylan, does not confront Molly, does not indulge in self-pity beyond what is reasonable.

Bloom's goodness is secular, embodied, and imperfect — he is a voyeur, a fantasist, an occasional coward — and it is precisely this imperfection that makes it real. He is Odysseus without the god-given certainty that he will triumph, without Athena's protection, without the categorical assurance that getting home is the right thing to do. He goes home anyway. He gets into bed beside a wife who spent the afternoon with another man, and his final thought, rendered in the catechism of "Ithaca," is an inventory of the cosmic scale of the universe that reduces his personal drama to proper size — not nothing, but not everything either.

## 7. Yes

Molly's monologue closes the book: eight sentences without punctuation, moving through memory, desire, grievance, and affirmation. It ends: "and yes I said yes I will Yes." The affirmation is not moral (Molly is not a virtuous character by conventional standards), not intellectual (there is no argument), not religious (though it echoes prayer). It is biological, erotic, human — the body's yes to being alive, uttered by a woman lying in bed in Dublin in the dark.

*Ulysses* begins with Stephen Dedalus, the intellectual who cannot affirm, and ends with Molly Bloom, the body that does. Between them stands Leopold Bloom, the ordinary man who navigates the single ordinary day and returns home. The epic is complete. It turns out that it was always about getting home, feeding the cat, and going to bed — and that these things, rendered with sufficient attention, are enough.
