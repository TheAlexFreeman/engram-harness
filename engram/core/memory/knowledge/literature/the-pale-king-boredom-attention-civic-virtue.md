# The Pale King: Boredom, Attention, and Civic Virtue

David Foster Wallace's unfinished novel, published posthumously in 2011 from drafts and notes assembled by his editor Michael Pietsch, is set in an IRS Regional Examination Center in Peoria, Illinois, in 1985. Its characters are tax examiners — people whose job is to read tax returns, line by line, all day, every day, looking for errors and fraud. The novel is, by design, about the least entertaining subject Wallace could find.

Where *Infinite Jest* asked what happens when a culture optimizes for pleasure, *The Pale King* asks the complementary question: what happens when you remove pleasure entirely? What remains when the stimulus is gone? What kind of person can sustain attention on something that offers no reward, no narrative satisfaction, no dopamine — and why might that capacity be the most important thing a person can develop?

## 1. Boredom as the Central Problem

Wallace called the novel's subject "the really central question of the American civic compact," and the question is boredom. Not boredom as a trivial complaint but boredom as a fundamental feature of human consciousness — the experience of time when nothing is happening that the organism judges to be relevant.

The cognitive science of boredom confirms Wallace's intuition. Boredom is not the absence of stimulation; it is the failure of the current environment to engage the attention system. The mind-wandering and vigilance-decrement research both demonstrate that sustained attention on monotonous tasks is not a default state that external stimulation disrupts — it is an achievement that requires continuous effort and that degrades predictably over time. The radar operators in Mackworth's clock test experienced the same thing the IRS examiners experience: the steady, measurable erosion of the capacity to notice what matters when nothing is dramatic enough to capture attention automatically.

Wallace understood that this is not a personal failing. It is a design feature of human cognition. The attentional system evolved to track novelty and salience, not to maintain focus on stable, repetitive inputs. To sustain attention on a tax return — to actually read the numbers, to think about what they mean, to notice the small inconsistency in line 37 — is to work against the grain of the attentional system itself. This requires not merely effort but a particular kind of effort: the effort of choosing, moment by moment, to remain present with something the mind is actively trying to escape.

## 2. The Heroism of the Mundane

The novel contains a passage, widely quoted since its posthumous publication, in which a character delivers what amounts to the book's thesis:

> "Enduring tedium over real time in a confined space is what real courage is. The thing is that most of us will never know the extent of our own courage. The thing about it is that people who can withstand boredom can do anything."

This is not motivational rhetoric. It is a philosophical claim about the relationship between attention and character. If the central challenge of modern life is the engineering of distraction — if the attention economy works by making sustained focus on unrewarding tasks increasingly difficult — then the person who can sustain that focus possesses a form of freedom that pleasure-seekers and distraction-consumers do not.

The IRS examiners who are good at their jobs are not good because they find tax returns interesting. They are good because they have developed the capacity to remain attentive in the absence of interest. This is a trained ability, not a personality trait. It is closer to the contemplative traditions — meditation, ascetic practice, the monastic discipline of lectio divina — than to anything the entertainment culture recognizes as valuable.

Wallace is explicit about this connection. Several characters in the novel have backgrounds in meditation, religious practice, or extreme physical discipline. The suggestion is that the modern bureaucrat who can actually pay attention to a tax return is the secular heir of the monk who could sustain attention on a sacred text for hours — and that both are exercising a capacity that the broader culture has largely abandoned.

## 3. Civic Virtue and the Tax System

The novel is set in 1985, during the early years of the Reagan revolution's transformation of the IRS. Wallace uses this historical moment to frame a question about civic life: what happens to a democratic society when it can no longer sustain attention on the boring but essential machinery of collective self-governance?

Taxation is the paradigmatic civic boring thing. It is necessary, unglamorous, and requires enormous administrative labor to function even adequately. The people who do this labor — the examiners, the auditors, the processors — are doing work that the society depends on and that the society despises. Wallace sees in this a parable: the democratic compact requires citizens to attend to things that are tedious and unrewarding, and the culture's accelerating inability to sustain such attention threatens the compact itself.

This is Lewis's argument in *The Abolition of Man* translated into a different register. Lewis warned that the modern project of debunking — treating all values as mere sentiment, all commitments as projections — would produce "men without chests," people with appetites and intellects but no formation of character to mediate between them. Wallace's IRS examiners are the people who still have chests — who can subordinate personal comfort to institutional obligation — and the novel asks whether such people can continue to exist in a culture that has systematically devalued the virtues they embody.

## 4. Attention as Spiritual Practice

*The Pale King* treats attention not merely as a cognitive function but as a spiritual discipline. The ability to be present — fully, continuously, without escape into fantasy or distraction — is presented as something close to the contemplative ideal. Wallace was familiar with both Zen Buddhism and the Christian contemplative traditions, and the novel's most striking passages describe attention itself as a form of prayer: the sustained, choiceful orientation of consciousness toward what is actually there rather than toward what the mind would prefer to be there.

This connects directly to the four kinds of knowing and the meaning crisis framework. Propositional knowledge of tax law is necessary but not sufficient. The examiner needs procedural knowledge (the skill of reading returns efficiently), perspectival knowledge (the capacity to see the return from the filer's point of view, to notice what someone trying to hide income would do differently), and — most provocatively — participatory knowledge. The good examiner *participates* in the return, enters into a relationship with the document that is neither detached analysis nor absorbed entertainment but something that the contemplative traditions would recognize as attention.

The meaning crisis, as John Vervaeke characterizes it, is the loss of exactly this kind of engaged presence. A culture that has optimized for stimulation and ironic distance has forgotten how to attend to what is simply there, without requiring that it be exciting, novel, or personally rewarding. The Pale King argues that the recovery of this capacity is not just a cognitive skill but a moral achievement, and that the places where it still exists — however unglamorous — are the places where something essential about human agency is being preserved.

## 5. The Unfinished Form

The novel is unfinished, and this fact cannot be separated from its meaning. Wallace died in 2008, and the book was assembled from drafts in various states of completion. Some chapters are polished; others are fragmentary. The reader experiences the book as a series of brilliant sections that do not fully cohere — beginnings without endings, characters who appear and vanish, threads that promise resolution and never deliver it.

This is, accidentally or otherwise, the formal experience of boredom itself. The reader who stays with the book must tolerate incompleteness, must sustain attention without the assurance that a payoff is coming, must find value in the quality of attention the book demands rather than in the satisfaction of narrative closure. *The Pale King* asks of its readers exactly what it argues the modern world asks of its citizens: can you stay with something difficult, unresolved, and unrewarding — and can you find in that staying a form of meaning that the entertainment model has taught you to think does not exist?

## 6. The Two Novels as a Diptych

Together, *Infinite Jest* and *The Pale King* form a complete argument. The first shows the pathology: a culture enslaved to the optimization of pleasure, in which freedom has been reduced to the absence of friction and attention has been hijacked by stimulus. The second shows the cure — or at least the direction of the cure: the deliberate, disciplined, unglamorous practice of sustaining attention on what does not reward it. The Entertainment kills because it offers too much. The tax return saves because it offers nothing at all, and the person who can remain present with nothing is the person who is actually free.
