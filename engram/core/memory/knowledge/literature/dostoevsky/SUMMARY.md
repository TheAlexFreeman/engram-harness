# Dostoevsky — Collection Summary

Five works spanning Dostoevsky's major phase (1864–1880), read as a single sustained argument about consciousness, freedom, and the consequences of ideas.

## Unifying Arc

The five novels trace a single trajectory: from the isolated consciousness rebelling against rational order (Underground Man), through the individual who tests an idea with his life and is destroyed by the result (Raskolnikov), to the perfectly good man who is destroyed by the world he enters (Myshkin), through the civilization-scale demonstration that ideas divorced from love become instruments of destruction (the revolutionaries of *Demons*), to the final polyphonic synthesis that refuses to resolve the tension between Ivan's unanswerable rebellion and Alyosha's inarticulate faith (*Karamazov*).

The through-line is the relationship between ideas and embodied existence. Dostoevsky's characters do not merely hold philosophical positions; they live them, and the living reveals what the theorizing conceals. The Underground Man's freedom produces paralysis; Raskolnikov's theory produces guilt; Myshkin's compassion produces catastrophe; Stavrogin's liberation produces the void; Ivan's clarity produces madness. In every case, the idea is sound and its consequences are devastating.

## Cross-Domain Connections

- **Existentialism**: The entire sequence anticipates Kierkegaard's reception, Heidegger's ontological guilt, Sartre's bad faith, Camus's absurd (→ phenomenology-existentialism, kierkegaard-nietzsche, heidegger-care-temporality)
- **Meaning crisis**: Dostoevsky diagnoses the post-religious predicament more precisely than any of his contemporaries (→ meaning-crisis-psychotechnologies)
- **Moral epistemology**: The novels argue that moral knowledge is not propositional but experiential — earned through suffering, not derived from theory (→ moral-epistemology, virtue-ethics)
- **Literary parallels**: Hamlet's paralysis (→ hamlet), Faust's destructive striving (→ faust), Zarathustra's diagnosis of nihilism (→ zarathustra), Chesterton's comic theodicy vs Ivan's tragic one (→ man-who-was-thursday)
- **Ideology and narrative**: *Demons* provides a structural anatomy of ideological capture that connects to narrative cognition and conceptual blending (→ cognitive-linguistics-metaphor-blending, tree-of-smoke)
