# Joyce and McLuhan — Medium, Consciousness, and the Compression of Experience

## 1. The Dissertation

Marshall McLuhan's intellectual career begins with James Joyce. His 1943 Cambridge PhD thesis, supervised by Muriel Bradbrook, was titled *The Place of Thomas Nashe in the Learning of His Time* — a study of Renaissance rhetoric and the medieval trivium (grammar, logic, rhetoric). But the thesis was animated by a conviction McLuhan had formed while reading *Ulysses* and *Finnegans Wake*: that the formal properties of language — its rhythms, its material textures, its patterns of juxtaposition — are not ornamental additions to meaning but the primary carriers of it. Joyce had demonstrated this more radically than anyone in literary history. McLuhan spent the rest of his life working out the implications.

The connection was not incidental. McLuhan read the *Wake* aloud in study groups at St. Louis University in the late 1930s, before he had any theory of media at all. What he found there became the template: a work in which the *how* overwhelms the *what*, in which form is not a vehicle for content but is itself the message. When McLuhan later announced that "the medium is the message," he was generalizing a principle he had first encountered as a literary fact in Joyce. The slogan is a critical reading of *Finnegans Wake* disguised as a theory of technology.

## 2. The Medium Is the Message — As Literary Principle

Joyce's career can be read as a progressive demonstration that medium precedes and constitutes content. In *Dubliners*, a deliberately impoverished prose style — what Joyce called "scrupulous meanness" — produces the experience of paralysis more effectively than any amount of thematic statement could. The reader feels trapped in narrow sentences because the sentences *are* the trap. In *A Portrait of the Artist*, the prose style changes with each chapter, tracking Stephen Dedalus's cognitive development from infant babble to Scholastic abstraction. The content of each chapter is whatever Stephen thinks about; the *medium* — the sentence structure, the vocabulary, the rhythm — is what actually changes and what the reader experiences as growth. In *Ulysses*, eighteen episodes use eighteen different styles, and the effect is to reveal that no single representational mode is adequate to a single day of human experience. Style is not a transparent window onto events; it is a frame that determines what events can appear.

By *Finnegans Wake*, the principle has been pushed to its limit. The "content" of the *Wake* — the Earwicker family, the fall, the river, the cycle — is almost impossible to extract from the medium because the medium has absorbed it. You cannot paraphrase a portmanteau. "Bababadalgharaghtakamminarronnkonnbronntonnerronntuonnthunntrovarrhounawnskawntoohoohoordenenthurnuk" *is* the thunder; it does not *represent* the thunder. McLuhan recognized this as the literary equivalent of what electric media were doing to print culture: collapsing the distinction between message and medium that Gutenberg's technology had made seem natural.

## 3. Visual Space, Acoustic Space

McLuhan's distinction between visual and acoustic space is the conceptual architecture that makes the Joyce connection structural rather than anecdotal. Print, McLuhan argued, organizes perception into visual space: linear, sequential, bounded, perspectival, with a fixed point of view and a clear separation between figure and ground. Manuscript culture and oral culture, by contrast, operate in acoustic space: simultaneous, multi-directional, without fixed boundaries, with figure and ground in constant interchange.

Joyce's formal experiments map precisely onto this transition — and reverse it. *Dubliners* and *Portrait* still operate largely within visual space: sequential prose, linear narrative, perspectival point of view. *Ulysses* begins to dismantle visual space from within. Its stream of consciousness passages restore simultaneity — Bloom's mind does not proceed linearly but radiates outward from sensory stimuli, catching memory, desire, hunger, music, and advertising copy in a single perceptual field. The "Sirens" episode renders Dublin as an acoustic environment, its language organized by musical rather than logical principles. The "Ithaca" episode's catechism format parodies the visual-space pretension that questions can isolate discrete facts from a continuous experiential field.

*Finnegans Wake* completes the transition into acoustic space. Its puns operate by sound, not by sight — they must be read aloud to release their meaning, which means they require the participatory, communal engagement of oral culture rather than the private, silent processing of print culture. McLuhan called the *Wake* "the greatest guide to the media ever devised on this planet" not because it discusses media (it barely does) but because it *is* a medium that has shed the assumptions of print while being composed in print. It retrieves orality, etymology, multilingual resonance, and cyclical time — everything that the Gutenberg era had suppressed — using the technology of the book against itself.

## 4. The Probe and the Portmanteau

McLuhan was frequently criticized for being unsystematic, contradictory, and impossible to pin down. His preferred unit of thought was the "probe" — a compressed, paradoxical statement designed to illuminate by collision rather than by argument. "The medium is the message." "We shape our tools and thereafter our tools shape us." "The global village." These are not propositions to be tested but cognitive devices to be detonated.

Joyce's portmanteau operates by the same logic. A word like "chaosmos" (chaos + cosmos) does not argue that the universe contains both disorder and order; it compresses the two into a single phonemic event so that the reader experiences their inseparability rather than evaluating a claim about it. McLuhan understood this. He called the *Wake*'s method "applied knowledge," meaning knowledge that has been processed through formal compression until it acts on the reader's perception directly rather than passing through propositional evaluation. The probe and the portmanteau are the same cognitive technology operating at different scales.

This has implications for understanding compression as a general principle. In information-theoretic terms, compression reduces description length while preserving structure. Kolmogorov complexity measures the shortest program that can reproduce a string; a good compression algorithm finds the regularities and encodes them efficiently. Joyce's portmanteau performs a version of this: "cropse" (corpse + crops + crop up + Fr. *corps*) encodes the entire death-and-resurrection cycle of the *Wake* in six letters. The compression ratio is extraordinary. But it approaches the limit where compression degrades into noise — where the encoded signal is so dense that the receiver cannot decode it without near-total knowledge of the encoder's reference set. This is the tension the *Wake* inhabits deliberately: maximum meaning per phoneme, at the cost of accessibility that trends toward zero.

McLuhan's probes face the same problem at a lower intensity. "The medium is the message" compresses an entire theory of cultural cognition into five words. Its power is its density; its weakness is that most people decode it as a clever paradox rather than a substantive claim about the constitutive role of representational form.

## 5. Consciousness Shaped by Its Containers

The deepest point of convergence is the shared conviction that consciousness does not exist prior to the media through which it is expressed. For Joyce, language is not a tool the self uses to communicate pre-formed thoughts; it is the material from which the self is assembled. Stephen Dedalus does not *have* thoughts that he then puts into Scholastic vocabulary — the vocabulary *is* the thinking; change the vocabulary and you change the mind. Bloom's consciousness in *Ulysses* is constituted by the urban sensorium of 1904 Dublin: tram sounds, soap smells, newspaper headlines, popular songs. Remove the media environment and there is no Bloom.

McLuhan generalized this into a theory of civilization. Each dominant medium — speech, manuscript, print, telegraph, television — reorganizes the sensorium, the ratio of the senses, and thereby reconstitutes what it is possible to think, feel, and perceive. The content of any medium is always another medium (the content of print is writing; the content of writing is speech), which means that each new technology wraps the previous one inside itself, altering the user's consciousness in the act of apparently just delivering familiar content.

Joyce foresaw this nesting. *Ulysses* contains within itself the entire history of English prose style — from Anglo-Saxon alliterative verse through Bunyan, Pepys, Gibbon, Dickens, and Pater — each style functioning as a medium that shapes the reality it ostensibly reports. *Finnegans Wake* goes further: it contains multiple languages simultaneously, as though the container of English has been cracked open and the babel of its etymological sources allowed to speak at once. The book performs what McLuhan would later describe as the retrieval phase of the tetrad: electric media retrieving the oral, simultaneous, participatory consciousness that print had suppressed.

## 6. What the Connection Illuminates

Reading Joyce through McLuhan makes visible what is otherwise easy to miss: that Joyce's formal experiments are not aesthetic self-indulgence but a systematic investigation of how representational media constitute human experience. Reading McLuhan through Joyce reveals the literary roots of a theory too often reduced to technological determinism. McLuhan was not primarily interested in gadgets. He was interested in what Joyce had shown him: that every medium is a form of consciousness, and that changing the medium changes the mind.

The convergence also marks a limit. Both Joyce and McLuhan push compression toward its boundary condition — the point where a communication so dense, so layered, so resistant to linear decoding that it risks becoming opaque. *Finnegans Wake* has never had more than a few thousand serious readers. McLuhan's reputation collapsed in the 1970s when academics demanded the linear argumentation his method was designed to supersede. Both were vindicated not by being decoded but by the emergence of a media environment — hypertext, the internet, algorithmic feeds, large language models — that made their compression legible by making simultaneity, non-linearity, and participatory construction the default conditions of experience. We did not learn to read them. The world became what they had already described.
