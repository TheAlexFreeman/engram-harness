# C.S. Lewis: *The Abolition of Man* (1943)

*The Abolition of Man* — subtitled "Reflections on education with special
reference to the teaching of English in the upper forms of schools" — is a
slim book of three lectures delivered at Durham University in February 1943.
It is Lewis's most concentrated piece of moral philosophy, and arguably his
most important non-fiction work. It was written simultaneously with *Perelandra*
and *That Hideous Strength*, and the three works are best read as a single
argument in two registers: the fiction dramatizes what the philosophy argues.

---

## Background and framing

The occasion is a grammar textbook for schoolchildren — Lewis calls it "The
Green Book" and withholds the real title and authors' names to avoid
personalizing what he treats as a representative cultural symptom. The book
contains a passage about the poet Coleridge at a waterfall: a tourist calls it
"sublime," and Coleridge agrees. The Green Book's authors remark, as a
passing aside, that what the tourist said was really only a statement about his
own feelings. The waterfall is not sublime; the person felt sublimity.

Lewis devotes his first lecture to showing that this aside — apparently
innocuous, even sophisticated — is in fact philosophically catastrophic, and
that its casual insertion into a children's textbook is a form of cultural
vandalism whose effects are not yet visible because they won't be felt for a
generation.

## Chapter 1: "Men Without Chests"

### The subjectivist claim and its self-refutation

The Green Book's position is that value judgments — statements of the form "X is
beautiful," "this is noble," "that is wicked" — do not describe the world but
express the speaker's emotional state. They have no cognitive content; they are
not true or false. This is the standard Humean/emotivst view: values are
projections, not perceptions.

Lewis's first objection is that the position is self-undermining. Any attempt
to argue that students *ought* to adopt a more careful, empirically accurate
relationship to their language has smuggled in a value claim the position
supposedly rules out. The Green Book authors proceed as though some things
matter — honesty, intellectual rigor, correct grammar — while insisting that
nothing objectively matters. The position cannot be stated without refuting
itself.

### The head-chest-belly framework

Lewis proposes a tripartite picture of the human person drawn from Plato and
classical tradition. The *head* is reason — intellect, abstract analysis. The
*belly* is appetite and instinct — raw desire. Between them is the *chest*:
trained sentiment, emotion brought into alignment with what reason recognizes
as good. The chest is what makes a person specifically *human* rather than
merely clever animal or reasoning machine.

Education, in the classical conception, trains the chest: it cultivates
appropriate emotional responses to what is genuinely good, genuinely beautiful,
genuinely just. The goal is not to suppress emotion with reason but to produce
a person whose feelings track reality — who is moved by what deserves to move
them.

The Green Book's pedagogical effect is to dissolve the chest. By teaching
children that their emotional responses have no cognitive validity — that
"sublime" means only "I felt sublimity" — it produces what Lewis calls **Men
Without Chests**: intellectually developed, appetitively functional, but
morally hollowed out. And then, Lewis notes with bitter precision:

> "We make men without chests and expect of them virtue and enterprise. We
> laugh at honour and are shocked to find traitors in our midst. We castrate
> and bid the geldings be fruitful."

## Chapter 2: "The Way"

### The Tao

Lewis introduces the term *Tao* — borrowed from Chinese philosophy but used as
a label for something he finds in every major civilization — to name the
doctrine of objective value: the belief that certain attitudes are *true*, and
others *false*, to the kind of thing the universe is and the kind of things we
are.

He is not claiming that Chinese philosophy, Christian ethics, Stoic natural law,
and ancient Egyptian moral instruction all say the same thing in every detail.
He is claiming that they all take moral realism for granted — that they all
treat the questions "what is good," "what is owed," and "how should one live"
as having answers that are not merely matters of preference or cultural
convention. The appendix to the book provides cross-cultural textual evidence
for this claim: passages from the Egyptian Book of the Dead, Hindu scripture,
Confucian texts, Greek philosophy, Roman law, Old Norse poetry, Australian
Aboriginal tradition, and early Christian writing all converging on the same
core moral recognitions.

The Tao, in Lewis's usage, is not specifically Christian. It is the common
moral inheritance of humanity — natural law by another name. His argument is
that you cannot reject it by appealing to something outside it, because any
criterion you use to evaluate it is itself a value judgment, and you will have
smuggled the Tao back in through the back door.

### The debunking dilemma

The sophisticated move of Lewis's opponents is to *debunk* the Tao: to explain
why people have the values they do (evolutionary pressure, social conditioning,
economic interest) in ways that seem to undermine the authority of those values.
If your sense that cruelty is wrong is just an evolutionary heuristic for
reciprocal cooperation, why should it bind you?

Lewis's response is that the debunking move is available for any value, including
the values the debunkers invoke. If you debunk pity by showing it's irrational
sentiment, you are appealing to reason — but reason is itself a value, available
for the same debunking treatment. If you debunk tradition by appealing to
progress, progress is itself a value claim. The chain of debunking doesn't
terminate in a bedrock of non-value facts; it terminates in some value the
debunker has refused to debunk because they need it to stand.

The result is that the only stable position is to accept *some* moral claims as
basic — self-evident in the way that logical axioms are self-evident, not
provable from more basic premises but recognizable to a properly functioning
moral intelligence. This is the classical position: some things are simply good,
and the capacity to recognize this is part of what it means to be a rational
being.

## Chapter 3: "The Abolition of Man"

### The Conditioners

If Lewis is right that the Tao cannot be replaced by anything derived from
purely factual or scientific premises, then those who seek to "go beyond" it
face a problem: by what do they guide their construction of a new morality?

Lewis imagines a future in which a ruling class — the *Conditioners* — has
acquired the scientific and political power to shape the values of all subsequent
generations. They can use psychology, propaganda, education, and biotechnology
to instill whatever "values" they choose in those they govern. The question is:
what values will they choose, and on what basis?

The answer, Lewis argues, is that they have none. Having rejected the Tao,
they have no objective criterion. Their choices will be driven by whatever
impulses happen to survive in themselves — by caprice, by their own conditioning,
by power-seeking, by whim. The Conditioners, who styled themselves as free of
the superstition of objective value, find that they are simply ruled by impulse
without even the check that the Tao would have imposed. They have not conquered
nature; nature has conquered them.

> "It is not that they are bad men. It is not that they are not clever. It is
> that their heads are in the air and their feet below the earth, and between
> the two there is nothing — nothing but an elaborate structure of apparatus
> which works and serves... but which embodies no wisdom."

(Lewis paraphrases Chesterton in the vicinity of this passage; the thought is shared.)

### The conquest that reverses itself

Lewis's central paradox is that humanity's progressive conquest of nature —
each generation extending human power over the physical world and over human
nature — eventually becomes nature's conquest of humanity. Each step toward
mastery over nature requires surrendering more of what it means to be a person
standing within the Tao. The final step — mastery over human nature itself,
over the values of future generations — is the step at which there is no longer
a "humanity" doing the mastering. There are only Conditioners and the
conditioned.

> "Man's conquest of Nature, if the dreams of some scientific planners are
> realized, means the rule of a few hundreds of men over billions of men. There
> neither is nor can be any simple increase of power on Man's side. Each new
> power won by man is a power over man as well."

### The magician's bargain

Lewis uses the phrase *the magician's bargain* — selling the soul for power —
to describe the transaction the Conditioners have made. They have given up their
embeddedness in the Tao (which would constrain their power but also give it
direction and justification) in exchange for the ability to shape others without
constraint. What they receive is genuine power. What they lose is everything
that would make the exercise of that power good rather than merely effective.

Lewis draws an explicit parallel between the Baconian scientist of the
seventeenth century (who "tortures Nature to discover her secrets") and the
magician (who also seeks power over nature by techniques divorced from
wisdom). Both, he argues, represent the same disposition: the will to power
over natural order, rather than the will to understand and participate in it.

## The Appendix: Illustrations of the Tao

Lewis includes an extensive appendix of cross-cultural quotations demonstrating
the universality of certain moral recognitions. Organized by category — duties
to parents, children, and ancestors; sexual ethics; justice; honesty; mercy —
the quotations show that while the content of moral codes varies in detail,
the underlying framework (that some things are owed, that some acts are
intrinsically wrong, that persons deserve respect) is consistent across cultures
separated by vast distances and centuries.

This is not the genetic fallacy in reverse (arguing that because everyone
believes X, X is true). It is an argument that the consensus is *evidence*:
if something is recognized everywhere, across vastly different cultural contexts,
as self-evident, this gives us reason to think we are tracking a real feature of
moral reality rather than a local prejudice. The argument is similar to the way
Lewis would defend the reliability of sense-perception: the fact that the senses
are not infallible does not mean they are not tracking reality at all.

## Critical objections

### The is-ought gap

Critics note that Lewis does not fully bridge the is-ought gap. Establishing
that the Tao exists as a widespread human moral intuition does not, by itself,
establish that it tracks moral reality. The naturalistic fallacy (Moore) remains
a challenge: even if *all* cultures recognize that cruelty is wrong, this could
still be a universal human bias rather than a recognition of objective fact.

Lewis's response — that *all* moral reasoning must start somewhere, and that the
Tao's self-evidence is no different in structure from the self-evidence of
logical axioms — is philosophically defensible but not knockdown. He is less
making a proof than a diagnosis: the attempt to get behind the Tao produces
nothing but impulse, and the choice between impulse and the Tao is itself a
moral choice that can only be made from within the Tao.

### The conservatism charge

Critics from the left argue that Lewis's defense of the Tao is effectively a
defense of whatever moral consensus happens to exist — that it provides no
resources for moral reform. Lewis explicitly addresses this: the Tao can
develop, and particular formulations can be criticized from within it. What
cannot be done is to reject it wholesale from some external vantage point.
The abolitionist who argued that slavery was wrong was not abandoning the Tao;
he was applying one part of it (the recognition of universal human dignity)
against another part's practical application (legal sanction of property in
persons). Reform within the Tao is possible; destruction of the Tao is
self-defeating.

### The pluralism problem

If cultures disagree about important moral questions, how does appeal to the Tao
adjudicate? Lewis's appendix documents areas of convergence but strategically
avoids areas of divergence. His critics (including some sympathetic ones) note
that the cross-cultural consensus is strongest at the most abstract level
(honor parents, do not murder arbitrarily) and breaks down under pressure
(which killings count as murder, what counts as honoring parents). Lewis
does not provide a method for resolving these disagreements from within the Tao.

## Relationship to the Space Trilogy

*The Abolition of Man* and *That Hideous Strength* are companion works published
in the same year. The N.I.C.E. is the Conditioners given institutional form:
a technocratic organization that has explicitly rejected the Tao and is now
reshaping human nature according to the impulses of its leadership (who are,
in the fiction, being directed by demonic forces — Lewis's way of saying that
the vacuum left by the Tao is filled by something).

Mark Studdock's trajectory in *That Hideous Strength* is a case study in how
the Conditioner mentality propagates: he is recruited not by a direct appeal to
wickedness but by the desire for insider status, the pleasure of being one of
those who understand that the old values are naïve, the intellectual vanity of
believing oneself beyond conventional morality. He becomes an accessory to
atrocity not through malice but through the progressive hollowing of his chest.

## Contemporary relevance

### AI alignment

The connection to AI alignment discourse is precise and has been noted by several
serious commentators (Joe Carlsmith, 2024, is the most sustained). The alignment
problem is in part a Conditioner problem: a group of humans, working from some
combination of utilitarian ethics and technical judgment, must decide what values
to instill in AI systems that will then shape the future for billions of people.
They are, structurally, Conditioners — exercising influence over the values of
future generations from a position outside (or at least upstream of) those values.

Lewis's warning is not that the alignment researchers are wicked but that they
face an intrinsic problem: having rejected (or bracketed) the Tao as a guide
(because it doesn't cash out in the mathematical form required for optimization),
they have no basis for their conditioning beyond their own preferences and
intuitions — which are themselves products of conditioning they did not choose.
The "human feedback" in RLHF is not the Tao; it is the aggregated preferences
of a particular pool of raters at a particular historical moment, treated as a
proxy for human values.

Whether Lewis's moral realism is correct determines whether this is a solvable
problem (find the Tao, encode it) or a permanent one (all value-encoding is
Conditioner work, and the only check on it is political accountability).

### The Men Without Chests and modern education

Lewis's diagnosis of the Green Book has proven durable. The view that value
judgments are merely expressions of feeling — and that a rigorous education
should strip away such irrational sentiment — has become more institutionalized,
not less, since 1943. STEM-dominant educational cultures that systematically
devalue the humanities, and humanities cultures that teach literary texts
primarily through the lens of power-critique, are both, in Lewis's analysis,
training Men Without Chests: the former by neglect, the latter by systematic
debunking.

### Technocracy and the administrative state

The Conditioner analysis maps onto critiques of technocratic governance: expert
classes who believe they have "seen through" popular moral intuitions and
therefore feel licensed to override democratic processes for technical reasons.
Lewis's warning is that the moment you accept that governance can be conducted
from outside the Tao — that the values of the governed are raw material to be
shaped by those who know better — you have accepted the logic of abolition.
