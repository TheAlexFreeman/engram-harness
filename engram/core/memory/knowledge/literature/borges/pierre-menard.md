# Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote: Identity, Context, and the Production of Meaning

Pierre Menard, a minor French symbolist writer of the early twentieth century, undertakes the most ambitious literary project imaginable: to produce several chapters of Don Quixote. Not to copy Cervantes. Not to transcribe. Not to produce a contemporary version or a translation. To produce, word for word, several chapters of the Quixote — the same text, in the same Spanish, arriving at each sentence not by copying but by the independent creative effort of a twentieth-century French intellectual who happens, through immersion in Cervantes's world and language, to arrive at the same words.

Menard succeeds. His text is identical to Cervantes's text, word for word. And, the narrator argues — and this is the story's detonation — Menard's version is "almost infinitely richer."

## 1. The Same Words, Different Meaning

The narrator compares a passage from Cervantes and the identical passage from Menard. Cervantes writes that history is "the mother of truth." In Cervantes's mouth, this is conventional — a seventeenth-century Spaniard affirming a commonplace about the value of historical knowledge. Menard writes the same phrase: history is "the mother of truth." In Menard's mouth — the mouth of a contemporary of William James, of pragmatism, of the critique of naive realism — the same words become a radical philosophical claim: not that history records truth, but that history *produces* truth, that truth is a function of historical context rather than a stable property of propositions.

The words are identical. The meaning is different. The difference is produced entirely by the context of production — who said it, when, in what intellectual milieu, against what background of available ideas. Borges is demonstrating, through a thought experiment of absurd precision, that meaning is not intrinsic to text. Meaning is a function of the relationship between a text and its conditions of production and reception. Change the author and the moment, and the same text means something else.

## 2. The Death of the Author Before Barthes

Barthes published "The Death of the Author" in 1967. Foucault published "What Is an Author?" in 1969. Both argued that the author is not the origin or guarantor of a text's meaning — that meaning is produced by the reader, the interpretive community, the intertextual field, rather than by an authorial intention that stands behind the text and controls it.

Borges said all of this in 1939, and said it better, because he said it as a story rather than as a theory. Pierre Menard is the demonstration that makes the theoretical argument unnecessary. If two identical texts can have different meanings depending on their authors, then authorial identity is a meaning-producing context, not a meaning-originating cause. The author does not inject meaning into the text like a syringe into an arm. The author is one of the frames through which the text is read, and changing the frame changes the meaning.

The advantage of Borges's approach over Barthes's is that Borges does not need to "kill" the author. He multiplies them. Menard is a different author of the same text, and the result is not the absence of meaning but the proliferation of meaning. The text becomes richer, not emptier, when it has two authors — because each author provides a different context, and each context activates different readings of the same words.

## 3. The Impossibility of Repetition

Menard's project is, strictly speaking, impossible — and Borges knows this. The joke is that Menard has (allegedly) accomplished something that cannot be accomplished. No amount of immersion in seventeenth-century Spain can make a twentieth-century Frenchman *be* Cervantes. The creative effort required to produce the Quixote's sentences "independently" is either infinite (because the goal is incoherent) or trivially satisfied (by simply copying the text and claiming independent derivation).

But the impossibility is the point. The thought experiment does not need to be realizable to be illuminating. Its illumination lies in what it reveals about the texts we do produce: every text is written by a specific person at a specific time, and those specificities are not accidental features that can be subtracted from the text's meaning. They are constitutive. A sentence written in 1605 and the same sentence written in 1934 are not the same sentence, because sentences do not exist in a vacuum. They exist in history, and history is part of what they say.

This has implications that extend beyond literary theory. Every communication — every utterance, every document, every line of code — acquires meaning partly from its context of production. The same function written by a junior developer and by a senior architect communicates different things to a code reviewer, even if the characters are identical. The same policy statement issued in peacetime and in crisis carries different force. Menard's impossibility is a hyperbolic version of an everyday reality: meaning is always contextual, and context is always specific.

## 4. Reading as Creation

The narrator's claim that Menard's Quixote is "richer" than Cervantes's is absurd on its face. But the absurdity conceals a serious proposition: every act of reading is an act of creation. The reader does not receive a fixed meaning from the text. The reader produces meaning by bringing to the text their own framework of knowledge, expectations, and concerns. The "same" text read in 1605 and in 1939 (or in 2026) is not the same text, because the reader is different and the reading is different.

This is not relativism — the claim that all readings are equally valid. It is the claim that meaning is relational rather than intrinsic. A text means something *to someone in a context*, and the meaning changes as the someone and the context change. Borges is not saying that texts can mean anything. He is saying that what they mean depends on more than the words on the page. Pierre Menard demonstrates this by holding the words constant and varying everything else, and the variation produces genuinely different meaning.

For a knowledge base built by human-AI collaboration — a system in which the "author" of any given text is ambiguous, in which the same words may have been generated by a language model and edited by a human, in which context of production is layered and complex — Menard's lesson is directly relevant. The meaning of a document is not fully determined by its content. It is determined by the relationship between its content and the conditions under which it was produced, curated, and read.
