# On Exactitude in Science: Maps, Models, and the Paradox of Perfect Representation

The entire story is one paragraph. An empire's cartographers, in their zeal for accuracy, produce maps of ever-increasing detail until they create a map at 1:1 scale — a map the size of the empire itself, coinciding with it point for point. Succeeding generations, finding the map useless, abandon it, and its tattered fragments can still be found in the deserts of the west, "inhabited by Animals and Beggars."

Borges attributed the paragraph to a fictional seventeenth-century author, Suárez Miranda. It was published in 1946. In one paragraph, it says everything there is to say about the relationship between models and what they model.

## 1. The Map That Is the Territory

A map works by leaving things out. A road map omits trees, weathering, and birdsong. A topographic map omits roads, buildings, and people. The utility of a map is directly proportional to its selectivity — a map that includes everything is a map of nothing, because it has discarded the only function that made it a map: the function of abstraction.

A 1:1 map is not a map. It is a duplicate. It provides no information that the territory itself does not provide, because it *is* the territory, restated. The empire's cartographers, by pursuing accuracy to its logical limit, destroyed the thing they were trying to create. The project's perfection is its annihilation.

This is the same insight that governs the Library of Babel (exhaustive completeness = uselessness), Funes's memory (total recall = cognitive paralysis), and Daneri's poem about the earth (exhaustive description = literary failure). Borges returns to this principle compulsively because it is, for him, the fundamental law of representation: compression is not a compromise. Compression is the mechanism by which representation acquires value. A map that is smaller than the territory is more useful than the territory precisely because it is smaller. What it leaves out is what makes it work.

## 2. Korzybski's Dictum and Its Inversion

Alfred Korzybski coined the phrase "the map is not the territory" in 1931, fifteen years before Borges published the story. Korzybski's point was that representations are always incomplete — that confusing the map for the territory leads to errors of reasoning and perception.

Borges takes Korzybski's dictum and inverts it: what if the map *were* the territory? What if you resolved the incompleteness by making the representation exhaust the reality? The result is not enlightenment but absurdity. The 1:1 map, far from solving the map-territory problem, reveals that the problem was never about insufficient accuracy. It was about the nature of representation itself, which requires divergence from the thing represented in order to be representation at all.

A perfect simulation of a weather system is not a weather forecast. It is a second weather system. A language model trained on all human language does not understand language; it duplicates the surface patterns of language at a scale that may be isomorphic to understanding or may be a 1:1 map — impressive, coextensive with the territory, and missing the point.

## 3. Models, Science, and the Dignity of Reduction

Science works by compression. A physical law is a map — a drastic reduction of the territory to a few variables and a relationship between them. $F = ma$ does not describe every force in the universe. It describes the relationship between any force, any mass, and any acceleration, and its power lies in what it omits (friction, material composition, temperature, color, smell) rather than in what it includes.

Borges's cartographers represent the anti-scientific impulse: the desire to include everything, to leave nothing out, to make the model so complete that it replaces the reality. This impulse is not confined to fiction. Every discipline contains a version of it — the database that must record every field, the simulation that must model every particle, the documentation that must cover every case. The result is always the same: the product becomes unusable under its own weight, and the deserts of the west accumulate its fragments.

The story's lesson is not that accuracy is bad. It is that accuracy and completeness are different goals, and that pursuing completeness past a certain point actively undermines usefulness. The good map, the good model, the good representation is the one that captures the right regularities at the right level of abstraction and accepts, without apology, that it is leaving things out. The cartographers failed not because they lacked skill but because they lacked the willingness to be incomplete.

## 4. One Paragraph

The story is one paragraph. This fact is itself the story's final argument. The most economical Borges fiction — one paragraph, a few hundred words — contains the same insight that the Library of Babel explores in eight pages and that a 1:1 map of the empire cannot convey at all. Compression works. Borges proves it by the act of writing: the shortest story in his corpus is also one of the most information-dense. The paragraph *is* the anti-map — a representation so compressed that it acquires, through its compression, a specificity and force that the exhaustive version (were it possible) could never achieve.

Animals and beggars live in the ruins of the 1:1 map. The paragraph survives.
