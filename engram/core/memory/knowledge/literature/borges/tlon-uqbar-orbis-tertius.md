# Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius: Idealism, Language, and the Construction of Reality

The narrator discovers a mysterious encyclopedia article about a country called Uqbar, which exists in some copies of the Anglo-American Encyclopedia but not others. The article references the literature of Uqbar, which concerns two imaginary regions — one of which is Tlön. Years later, a complete volume of the *First Encyclopaedia of Tlön* surfaces, describing in exhaustive detail the culture, philosophy, languages, and sciences of an entire world.

Tlön is not merely imaginary. It is *idealist* — its inhabitants reject materialism entirely. Their philosophy holds that the universe consists of mental acts, that objects do not persist when unperceived, that the material world is a secondary effect of consciousness rather than its cause. Their languages have no nouns, only verbs or adjectives: one language says not "the moon rose above the river" but something closer to "upward behind the onstreaming it mooned." The absence of nouns is not an accident. It reflects the metaphysics: if there are no persistent objects, there is nothing for nouns to denote.

The story's final section reveals that Tlön was invented — deliberately, over centuries, by a secret society of encyclopedists. And then the invented world begins to seep into the real one.

## 1. Berkeley's Revenge

George Berkeley argued in the eighteenth century that material objects do not exist independently of perception — *esse est percipi*, to be is to be perceived. The argument was logically rigorous and almost universally rejected, because it feels absurd: surely the table continues to exist when no one is looking at it. (Samuel Johnson's famous "refutation" consisted of kicking a stone.)

Borges does not argue for Berkeley. He does something more interesting: he constructs a world in which Berkeley is simply correct, and then shows how the inhabitants of that world think, speak, and organize knowledge differently than we do. The effect is not to prove idealism but to denaturalize materialism — to show that our own metaphysics (objects persist, matter is primary, language names things) is not the only coherent framework but one framework among others, held in place by habits of thought and language that could, in principle, be otherwise.

Tlön's philosophy departments contain no idealist-vs-materialist debates because there is no materialism to debate. Their entire intellectual culture operates within an idealist framework, and it works — they have arts, sciences (of a kind), logic (of a kind), and a complete civilization. The framework produces different knowledge than ours, but it is not incoherent. It is simply organized around different primitive assumptions.

## 2. Language and Ontology

The most striking feature of Tlön is its languages. In the languages of Tlön's southern hemisphere, nouns do not exist. Instead of saying "the moon," speakers combine verbs and modifiers to indicate a transient process: "it mooned" or "airy-bright over dark-round." The thought experiment asks: if your language cannot name persistent objects, can you think about persistent objects? Can you even perceive them?

This is the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis taken to its logical extreme — not as a mild claim about framing effects but as a radical claim about the constitution of reality through language. Borges does not endorse strong Sapir-Whorf; he uses it as a device. But the device illuminates something real: the categories embedded in a language shape what its speakers can easily think, and a language built on radically different ontological assumptions would produce a radically different phenomenology.

The cognitive linguistics research on conceptual metaphor and frame semantics confirms that language structure shapes cognition, though not as totally as the Tlönian extreme implies. English speakers who use spatial metaphors for time think about time differently than Mandarin speakers who use vertical metaphors. The categories available in a language are not prisons, but they are defaults, and defaults shape the distribution of thought even when they do not determine it. Tlön is the limit case: a language whose defaults are so different from ours that the resulting civilization is alien in its basic categories.

## 3. The Encyclopedia as World-Building

Tlön was created by encyclopedists. A secret society, funded by a millionaire, produced an encyclopedia of an imaginary world so detailed, so internally consistent, and so intellectually compelling that it began to displace reality. People started finding artifacts from Tlön. Tlönian ideas infiltrated academic discourse. The encyclopedia rewrote the world.

This is Borges's most direct statement about the power of comprehensive, systematic description to constitute rather than represent reality. An encyclopedia is supposed to be a neutral record of what exists. Borges inverts this: the encyclopedia of Tlön does not describe a pre-existing world. It *produces* one. The act of describing Tlön in sufficient detail, with sufficient consistency, with the authority that comes from encyclopedic comprehensiveness — this act brings Tlön into being.

The implication extends to all encyclopedic projects, including the one you are reading from now. Every knowledge system — every encyclopedia, every database, every curated collection of documents — does not merely record a world. It participates in constituting one. The categories it uses, the relationships it encodes, the boundaries it draws between domains — these are not neutral reflections of a pre-existing structure. They are choices that shape what can be found, what can be thought, and what appears to be real.

## 4. The Contagion of Ideas

The story's final pages describe the intrusion of Tlön into our world. Objects from Tlön appear — a compass inscribed with Tlönian letters, a cone of impossibly dense metal. Intellectual life begins to reorganize itself along Tlönian lines. "The truth is that it longed to yield," Borges writes. The world *wanted* to be overwritten by a more elegant, more internally consistent system.

This is the story's deepest observation. Ideas do not require truth to propagate. They require coherence and comprehensiveness. A system of ideas that is internally consistent, aesthetically satisfying, and exhaustively detailed will attract minds regardless of whether it corresponds to anything external. Tlön conquers not because it is true but because it is *complete* — because it offers a total explanation, a framework that accounts for everything, a world without gaps or loose ends.

The history of intellectual movements — Marxism, Freudianism, certain strains of economic theory, certain AI paradigms — confirms this pattern: coherence is more seductive than correspondence. A framework that explains everything (within its own terms) will persist long after its empirical failures accumulate, because the human mind prefers completeness to accuracy. Tlön is the thought experiment that isolates this preference and shows where it leads: to the displacement of the real by the consistent.

## 5. The Narrator's Quiet Resistance

Amid the general capitulation to Tlön, the narrator retreats to his hotel and works on a translation of Sir Thomas Browne's *Urn Burial*. He does not expect to publish it. He works on it because the act of attending to a specific, particular, real text — a seventeenth-century English meditation on mortality — is a form of resistance against the totalizing abstractions of Tlön. The detail, the idiosyncrasy, the historical specificity of Browne's prose is precisely what Tlön's system cannot absorb.

This ending is Borges's quiet defense of the particular against the total, of the specific against the systematic. Tlön is seductive because it is complete. Reality is resistant because it is particular, messy, and inexhaustible. The translator working on Browne is doing the opposite of the encyclopedists who created Tlön: instead of building a system, he is attending to a specific object in all its historical contingency. The story suggests — without insisting — that this attention to the particular may be the only reliable defense against the seduction of beautiful, coherent, and ultimately fictitious totalities.
