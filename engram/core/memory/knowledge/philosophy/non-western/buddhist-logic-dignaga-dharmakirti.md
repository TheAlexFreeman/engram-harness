# Buddhist Logic: Dignāga, Dharmakīrti, and Apoha Theory

**Buddhist logic and epistemology** (pramāṇavāda) represents one of the most technically sophisticated traditions in world philosophy, comparable to Aristotelian logic and analytic epistemology in rigour and scope. Developed primarily by **Dignāga** (c. 480–540 CE) and systematised by **Dharmakīrti** (c. 600–680 CE), it built a self-contained epistemological system from Buddhist metaphysical premises, engaging in sustained debate with rival schools — particularly Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika.

---

## Background and Context

The impetus for the pramāṇavāda project was Buddhist metaphysics: the doctrines of **impermanence** (anicca), **no-self** (anātman), and **momentariness** (kṣaṇavāda — each entity exists for only an instant) generate pressure on conventional epistemology, since standard accounts of perception and inference presuppose relatively stable objects and persistent selves.

**Dignāga's foundational move:** Accept only two valid sources of knowledge (*pramāṇas*) — direct perception (*pratyakṣa*) and inference (*anumāna*). Reject the Nyāya school's additional pramāṇas (comparison, testimony) as reducible to these two. This minimalism was methodologically radical.

---

## Dignāga's Epistemology

### The Two Pramāṇas and Their Distinct Objects

| Pramāṇa | Object | Character |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| **Pratyakṣa** (perception) | Particular (*svalakṣaṇa*) — unique, momentary, causally efficacious particular | Non-conceptual, non-erroneous |
| **Anumāna** (inference) | Universal (*sāmānyalakṣaṇa*) — generic, conceptually constructed | Conceptual, involves superimposition |

The fundamental division: **particulars are real (causally efficacious)** and **universals are constructs** (mental/linguistic fictions). This is Dignāga's Buddhist nominalism.

**Svalakṣaṇa** (particular): that which has its own individual defining mark; occupies a unique spatiotemporal location; is causally efficacious (it can produce effects); is apprehended by perception without conceptual mediation.

**Sāmānyalakṣaṇa** (general/universal): that which is grasped by conceptual cognition; shared across instances; not independently causally efficacious; its apprehension always involves elimination (apoha) rather than positive grasp.

### Non-Conceptual Perception (nirvikalpaka pratyakṣa)

Dignāga defines **valid perception** as:

$$\text{Pratyakṣa} = \kappa\text{alpanāpoḍham abhrāntam}$$

"Free from conceptual construction (*kalpanā*) and non-erroneous (*abhrāntam*)."

**Kalpanā** (conceptual construction) involves the association of names (*nāma*), universals (*jāti*), properties (*guṇa*), actions (*kriyā*), and substances (*dravya*). Valid bare perception is *prior* to all such conceptual overlays.

The challenge: if bare perception is pre-conceptual, how does it ever deliver cognitive content useful for inference and action? Dignāga's answer: perception delivers the **particular's unique causally-relevant nature**; inference then constructs the conceptualised presentation of that particular as-if-universal.

**The practical paradox:** Most of what we call "seeing a fire," "perceiving a cow," or "recognising my friend" already involves the conceptual-perceptual liaison. Dignāga treats these as inference-laden constructions built on top of the raw perceptual datum, not as pure perception itself. Pure perception is, in a sense, pre-cognitive.

## Apoha Theory: Meaning by Exclusion

### The Problem of Universals

Dignāga must explain how language works if universals don't exist as real entities. If "cow" refers to no real shared property, what does it mean?

### Apoha (*apoha* = exclusion/difference)

Dignāga's solution: **words do not refer to positive universals; they refer by excluding non-instances**.

$$\llbracket \text{"cow"} \rrbracket = \{x : x \text{ is not a non-cow}\}$$

The meaning of "cow" is not a positive entity (such as cow-ness as a universal) but the **exclusion of everything that is not a cow**. This is not the same as "everything that is a cow" — it is a negative/relational characterisation that does not require universals to be real.

**Formal structure:** The apoha of $F$ is $\neg\neg F$ (double negation), which is logically weaker than $F$ itself — it allows for the conceptual grouping of similar things without positing a real shared property.

**The three-apoha structure:**
1. Words produce concepts of *apoha* (exclusion of the opposite)
2. Perception delivers the particular
3. Conceptual cognition then applies the apoha-concept to cognise the particular-as-F

### Dharmakīrti's Refinement

Dharmakīrti completes the apoha theory by explaining *why* certain exclusions group things that are functionally similar:

- Objects grouped under the same apoha-concept share **causal-productive capacity** (arthakriyā-sāmarthya) — they produce similar effects
- The grouping is not arbitrary: "fire"-apoha groups things that produce heat/light/burning, sharing this causal profile
- The universal is purely mental (*buddhibheda*) — a cognitive, not ontic, category

This gives apoha theory a **pragmatic realist** dimension: conceptual groupings track real causal similarities in particulars without requiring shared universals.

## Dharmakīrti: The Pramāṇaviniścaya Tradition

### The Three-Aspect Criterion (Trirūpa-hetu)

Dharmakīrti systematised Buddhist inference around the **three conditions that a valid inferential reason (hetu) must satisfy**:

For inference "The mountain has fire — because it has smoke," the reason "smoke" is valid iff:

| Aspect | Sanskrit | Condition |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Pakṣadharmatā** | Property of the subject | Smoke is present on the mountain (the inferential subject) |
| **Sapakṣa-vṛtti** | Presence in similar cases | Smoke is present wherever there is fire (e.g., the kitchen) |
| **Vipakṣa-vyāvṛtti** | Absence in dissimilar cases | Smoke is absent wherever there is no fire (e.g., the lake) |

This is formally equivalent to: $(\text{hetu} \subset \text{pakṣa}) \land (\text{hetu} \subset \text{sādhya})$ — the reason is a subset of the subject, and the reason is a subset of the probandum (what is to be established).

The trirūpa-hetu is more demanding than Aristotelian syllogistic: it requires both positive co-presence (sapakṣa) and negative co-absence (vipakṣa) — analogous to requiring both sensitivity and specificity.

### Two Types of Valid Inference

Dharmakīrti distinguishes two fundamentally different grounding relations for valid inference:

#### 1. Svabhāvahetu (Identity/Nature Inference)

The reason stands in an **essential identity relation** to the probandum:

*"This is a tree — because it is a śiṃśapā [a type of tree]"*

The validity rests not on causal connection but on **definitional/essential inclusion**: śiṃśapā is a type of tree by its very nature. The trirūpa conditions hold analytically.

These are roughly analytic inferences — valid by the nature of the conceptual scheme.

#### 2. Kāryahetu (Effect/Causal Inference)

The reason is an **effect** of the probandum; their connection is causal:

*"There is fire — because there is smoke"*

Smoke is causally produced by fire (one of few genuine causes of smoke), so smoke is a reliable indicator of fire. Validity rests on **real causal nexus** (tadutpatti or pratītyasamutpāda — dependent origination) between particular smoke-instances and fire-instances.

**Key asymmetry:** Kāryahetu inferences are contingent (causal connections are empirically discovered), while svabhāvahetu inferences are necessary (given the conceptual scheme). But both are *genuine knowledge* because both satisfy the trirūpa.

**A third type** — Anupalabdhihetu (inference from non-perception): "There is no pot here — because it is not perceived under conditions where it would be perceived if present." This grounds inference from absence.

### Causal Theory of Perception

Dharmakīrti develops a sophisticated **causal-representationalist** theory of perception:

- Perception is caused by the object-particular (svalakṣaṇa) through direct causal contact
- The perceptual experience is a cognition whose content is **determined by the causal particular**
- The cognition has a "form" (*ākāra*) that represents the object — Dharmakīrti debates whether this form is the same as the object (*sa-kārāvāda*) or merely represents it

**The representationalist challenge:** if perception is of a mental representation (ākāra), how do we know it corresponds to the external particular? Dharmakīrti's sāhopalambhaniyama ("the rule of co-appearance") argument: external objects and their cognitions are invariably co-appeared — but this threatens to collapse into idealism (*vijñānavāda*), which Dharmakīrti officially rejects but flirts with in his later works.

### Two Levels of Truth (Saṃvṛti / Paramārtha)

Inherited from Nāgārjuna's Madhyamaka, Dharmakīrti applies the two-truth framework to epistemology:

- **Paramārthasat** (ultimately real): causally efficacious momentary particulars (svalakṣaṇa); the objects of valid perception
- **Saṃvṛtisat** (conventionally real): conceptually constructed universals, persons, composite objects; the objects of inference

This maps onto the pramāṇa distinction: perception gives us the ultimately real; inference operates over the conventionally real.

## Comparison with Nyāya

| Feature | Buddhist (Dignāga-Dharmakīrti) | Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika |
|---------|-------------------------------|-----------------|
| Valid pramāṇas | Perception + Inference (2) | Perception + Inference + Comparison + Testimony (4) |
| Universals | Mental constructs (apoha) | Really existing (sāmānya, inherence relation) |
| Perceptual object | Particular (svalakṣaṇa) | Universal-particular complex |
| Inference grounding | Causal nexus / essential relation | Universal concomitance (vyāpti) |
| Self | No-self (anātman) | Permanent, unified ātman |
| Valid perception | Strictly non-conceptual | Includes conceptual "determinate perception" |

The central debate: Nyāya argues that perception is *of* universals as well as particulars (we perceive "cow" not just a particular instance), and that testimony is a genuine irreducible source of knowledge. Dignāga-Dharmakīrti deny both, generating one of the most sustained philosophical debates in world intellectual history.

## Tibetan Reception and Debate

Dharmakīrti's *Pramāṇavārttika* became the central epistemology text in Tibetan Buddhist curricula, generating centuries of commentary:

- **Tibetan Sautrāntika tradition:** accepts Dharmakīrti's framework; debates the extent of his idealist commitments
- **Gelug school** (Tsongkhapa): complex interpretation that reconciles Dharmakīrti with Prāsaṅgika Madhyamaka; extensive debate on the status of svabhāvahetu in a Madhyamaka context
- The **dharma-debate tradition** (gcod log) trained Tibetan monastics in formal inference rules derived from Dharmakīrti — a living logical tradition

## Contemporary Relevance

1. **Philosophy of language:** Apoha theory anticipates semantic externalism debates and prototype theory (Rosch) by arguing that reference works through exclusion and causal similarity rather than necessary-and-sufficient conditions
2. **Artificial concept formation:** The two-object distinction (particular/universal) maps onto the feature-learning / symbolic abstraction dichotomy in AI; apoha foreshadows how neural networks represent categories through gradient descent on similarity boundaries rather than explicit universals
3. **Causal inference:** Dharmakīrti's kāryahetu and svabhāvahetu distinction maps roughly onto empirical vs analytic inference — anticipating debates in causal graph theory about whether causation is an additional ontological primitive or derivable from associations
