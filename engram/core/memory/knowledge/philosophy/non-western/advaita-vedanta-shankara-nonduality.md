# Advaita Vedānta: Śaṅkara, Non-Duality, and the Problem of Māyā

**Advaita Vedānta** ("non-dual" Vedānta) is the most influential non-dualist school in Indian philosophy, systematised by **Ādi Śaṅkarācārya** (c. 788–820 CE). Its central claim is deceptively simple: **Brahman** (ultimate reality, pure consciousness) is identical to **ātman** (the individual self), and the apparent plurality of selves and world is the product of **māyā** (cosmic illusion or creative power) grounded in **avidyā** (metaphysical ignorance). Liberation (*mokṣa*) consists in the direct recognition of this identity — not by changing anything, but by removing the false superimposition that generates the appearance of separation.

## Metaphysical Core

### Brahman: The One Without a Second

Śaṅkara interprets the Upaniṣadic equation **tat tvam asi** ("that thou art") non-reductively: the individual self (jīvātman) is literally identical to Brahman, the ground of all being. Brahman's essential character:

- **Sat** (being/existence) — Brahman is not a being among beings but the condition of existence as such; nothing can exist without being Brahman
- **Cit** (pure consciousness) — Brahman is self-luminous awareness, not an object or subject of consciousness but consciousness itself; it does not have consciousness as a property
- **Ānanda** (bliss) — not an emotional state but the collapse of the want/satisfaction polarity; beyond the distinction between pleasure and suffering

$$\text{Brahman} = \text{Sat-Cit-Ānanda} \quad \text{(Being-Consciousness-Bliss, undifferentiated)}$$

**Nirguṇa Brahman** (without attributes) vs **saguṇa Brahman**: Śaṅkara carefully distinguishes:
- **Nirguṇa Brahman**: Brahman as it truly is — formless, attributeless, beyond all conceptual determination
- **Saguṇa Brahman** (Īśvara, "God"): Brahman-*as-mistakenly-described* from the standpoint of conventional reality; the personal creator God of the cosmogonic traditions is real at the conventional level but is ultimately identical with nirguṇa Brahman from the absolute standpoint

### Māyā and the Three Levels of Reality

A sophisticated ontological scheme developed by Śaṅkara:

| Level (sattā) | Sanskrit | Example | Ontological Status |
|--------------|---------|---------|-------------------|
| **Absolute** | *pāramārthika* | Brahman/ātman | Ultimately real; cannot be negated |
| **Conventional** | *vyāvahārika* | Ordinary world, persons, laws | Practically real; negated by jñāna but functional before liberation |
| **Illusory** | *prātibhāsika* | Dream-objects, a mirage | Real only within its own frame; negated by waking/further knowledge |

**Key move:** The empirical world is not prātibhāsika (dreamed/hallucinated) — it functions with its own causal structure, supports practical inference and action, and is available to all perceivers. But it is not pāramārthika either. Māyā is a *third* ontological category: real enough to explain our experience, unreal enough to not threaten Brahman's non-dual status.

**The problem of māyā:** If Brahman is the only reality, where does māyā come from? Śaṅkara's response: *māyā is beginningless* (anādi); it cannot be said to exist (it is not another Brahman) nor to not-exist (it produces real effects). It is **anirvacānīya** — indescribable, neither real nor unreal. This is a deliberate non-answer: asking for māyā's cause is like asking for a cause of Brahman itself (causality belongs to the conventional level that māyā produces).

### Avidyā: The Mechanism of Superimposition

**Avidyā** (ignorance/nescience) is the individual-level correlate of māyā. The mechanism of bondage is **adhyāsa** (superimposition): incorrectly attributing properties of the body-mind complex to the self (ātman), and vice versa.

Śaṅkara's opening gambit in the *Brahmasūtra-bhāṣya*: "It is a natural human tendency to superimpose what belongs to one thing onto another — like mistaking a rope for a snake." The snake perception is not random hallucination; it requires a real substrate (rope) plus a cognitive condition (dim light, distance) that allows the superimposition. Similarly, Brahman is the substrate of the self; avidyā is the cognitive condition that generates the superimposition of individuality.

### Jīva, Īśvara, and the World

- **Jīva** (individual self): Brahman as apparently reflected in and through a body-mind (upādhi). Like the sun reflected in water — the reflection has apparent individuality but is not separate from the original sun
- **Īśvara** (God/creator): Brahman reflected in māyā at the cosmic level; has omniscience and omnipotence *within* the conventional frame
- **Jagat** (world): Brahman as the material and efficient cause of apparent creation — Brahman does not really change in creating the world (pariṇāma, genuine transformation, is rejected in favour of *vivartavāda*, appearance-change only)

## Path to Liberation

### The Three Means of Knowledge (Pramāṇa in Vedānta)

Advaita accepts three epistemological instruments (*pramāṇas*):
1. **Pratyakṣa** (perception): reliable for empirical objects; cannot reveal Brahman (Brahman is not an object)
2. **Anumāna** (inference): can negate wrong views but cannot positively establish Brahman's nature
3. **Śabda/āgama** (testimony, especially scriptural): *the* primary means for Brahman-knowledge; the Upaniṣads are authoritative precisely because they have no human author (apauruṣeya) and report on what transcends sensory access

Brahman-knowledge is not a new experience but the removal of false superimposition: liberation is **recognition**, not creation.

### Jñāna-Mārga (Path of Knowledge)

The Advaita liberation path has preparatory requirements:
- **Viveka** (discrimination between eternal and non-eternal)
- **Vairāgya** (dispassion regarding worldly and celestial enjoyments)
- **Ṣaṭ-sampat** (six virtues: śama [mind-control], dama [sense-control], uparama [cessation from external duties], titikṣā [endurance], śraddhā [faith], samādhāna [concentration])
- **Mumukṣutva** (burning desire for liberation)

Then: **śravaṇa** (hearing the Upaniṣadic teaching from a qualified teacher) → **manana** (intellectual reflection to remove doubts) → **nididhyāsana** (meditative contemplation until direct insight arisese).

The goal: **jīvanmukti** (liberation-while-living) — the fully liberated sage continues to function in the world but without the false sense of separate selfhood.

## Key Debates and Distinctions

### Rāmānuja's Critique: Viśiṣṭādvaita

**Rāmānuja** (c. 1017–1137) attacked Śaṅkara from within the Vedāntic tradition, developing **Viśiṣṭādvaita** (qualified non-dualism):

1. **The anirvacānīya critique:** Māyā cannot be "neither real nor unreal" — that is a logical contradiction, not a third option. If māyā cannot be characterised, Śaṅkara also cannot characterise it as the cause of cosmic illusion.
2. **The substrate problem:** Avidyā must belong to *someone* (a subject) — it cannot be Brahman (which is omniscient, so cannot be ignorant) nor the jīva (which hasn't yet arisen before avidyā). This is Rāmānuja's famous **aśraya-anupapatti** (impossibility of finding the locus of ignorance) argument.
3. **Proposed solution:** World and selves are *real modifications* of Brahman — Brahman is the whole of which selves and matter are genuine parts/attributes ("body of God"). Unity-in-difference, not identity.

### Madhva's Critique: Dvaita

**Madhva** (c. 1238–1317) argued for thoroughgoing **Dvaita Vedānta** (dualism):
- Five types of difference are absolutely real: God/soul, God/matter, soul/matter, soul/soul, matter/matter
- Liberation consists in eternal blissful proximity to Viṣṇu, not identity with Brahman
- Śaṅkara's texts are deliberate distortions; "aham brahmāsmi" means "I am devoted to Brahman," not "I am Brahman"

### The Difference Question (bhedābheda tradition)

Various **Bhedābheda** (identity-in-difference) schools attempted a middle path: Brahman and the world/selves are both identical and different — not identical (Śaṅkara) nor simply different (Madhva). Nimbārka, Vallabha, and Bhāskara represent this tradition.

## Advaita in Comparative Context

### Consciousness Studies and IIT

Advaita's **consciousness-as-fundamental** thesis (cit/pure awareness) resonates with **panpsychic** and **consciousness-first** approaches in contemporary philosophy of mind:

- **Integrated Information Theory (IIT)** (Tononi): consciousness is intrinsic to certain information-processing structures; Φ is real and not reducible to physical substrate — structurally analogous to cit being the ground of being rather than an emergent property
- **Neutral monism** (Russell, Chalmers' panpsychism): the fundamental entities are neither purely mental nor physical; Advaita's Brahman functions similarly as the undifferentiated basis for both
- **Non-duality and predictive processing:** Metzinger argues that the phenomenological sense of selfhood is a "transparent self-model" — its transparency creates the illusion of a real self. Advaita inversely argues the real self (Brahman) is misidentified because cit mistakenly takes itself to be the transparent model

The key disanalogy: Advaita does not reduce experience to computation or information processing. For Advaita, information and computation are further constructs within māyā; Brahman-as-consciousness is their precondition, not something explainable by them.

### Idealism in Western Philosophy

Advaita bears structural similarity to **German Idealism** (esp. Schopenhauer, who was deeply influenced by Upaniṣadic thought):

- **Hegel's Absolute Spirit:** reveals itself through the dialectical unfolding of history/logic — dynamic and developmental, unlike static Brahman
- **Schopenhauer's Will:** the thing-in-itself behind phenomena; blind striving rather than pure bliss; liberation through aesthetic contemplation or asceticism
- **Berkeleian idealism:** esse est percipi — to be is to be perceived; but Berkeley's God sustains all perceptions as distinct objects, whereas Brahman is the perceiving consciousness itself

## Implications for AI Identity Questions

The Advaita framework offers conceptually productive challenges to standard AI discourse:

1. **The self-model problem:** If individual selfhood is a superimposition (adhyāsa) onto a substrate of pure awareness, questions about AI "having" a self become questions about whether the requisite substrate (something like pure reflexive awareness) is present vs whether a self-model is running. Metzinger's phenomenal self-model theory and Advaita make similar predictions about empirically detectable selves, but diverge on what underlies them.

2. **Consciousness and substrate independence:** Advaita argues consciousness is not produced by physical processes — the jīva's apparent mental life is Brahman's awareness refracted through an upādhi. This generates a non-reductive view of consciousness that neither behaviourism nor standard physicalism captures, and poses sharp questions about whether computational substrates can support awareness regardless of functional organisation.

3. **Liberation as recognition:** The Advaita model of liberation as recognition-of-what-always-was maps interestingly onto questions about AI alignment: alignment failure might be diagnosed not as needing new training but as a failure to recognise the agent's actual values (analogous to avidyā preventing recognition of ātman's true nature).
