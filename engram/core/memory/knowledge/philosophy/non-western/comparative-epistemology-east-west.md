# Comparative Epistemology: East and West

This file maps the epistemological landscape across Western and Asian philosophical traditions using four structural axes: (1) valid sources of knowledge (*pramāṇa* theory vs Western faculties), (2) the status of perception, (3) the role of testimony and tradition, and (4) the limits of knowledge. The goal is not flattening but genuine structural comparison — identifying where traditions disagree and why.

---

## Axis 1: Valid Sources of Knowledge

### The Western Tripartition (roughly)

Modern Western epistemology identifies three paradigmatic sources:
- **Perception** (empiricism: Locke, Hume, Mill)
- **Reason** (rationalism: Descartes, Leibniz, Kant)
- **Testimony** (social epistemology: Coady; Fricker)

The empiricism-rationalism debate centered on whether perception or reason is the *primary* source of justified belief. Kant's synthesis proposed that both are necessary but neither alone sufficient: pure intuitions (space, time) structure what perception delivers; categories of the understanding structure what reason operates on.

### Nyāya: Four *Pramāṇas*

The Nyāya school (see `nyaya-vaisheshika-epistemology.md`) identified four valid knowledge sources:

| *Pramāṇa* | Definition | Western Near-Equivalent |
|-----------|------------|------------------------|
| *Pratyakṣa* (perception) | Direct sensory contact producing true cognition | Empirical perception |
| *Anumāna* (inference) | Cognition via pervasion (*vyāpti*) relationship | Deductive + inductive inference |
| *Upamāna* (comparison) | Understanding new term via perceived similarity | Analogy; conceptual extension |
| *Śabda* (testimony) | Reliable declarative speech from trustworthy speaker | Testimony (social epistemology) |

The crucial inclusion: *śabda* (verbal testimony) is a first-class epistemic source equated with Vedic scripture in some schools. This makes epistemology inseparable from questions of linguistic authority.

### Buddhist Epistemology (Dharmakīrti): Two *Pramāṇas*

Dharmakīrti collapsed the Nyāya four to two:

1. **Perception** (*pratyakṣa*): direct, non-conceptual contact with particulars. Crucially, Dharmakīrti added **self-awareness** (*svasaṃvedana*) — direct cognition of one's own mental states — as a fourth type of perception alongside sensory, mental, and yogic.

2. **Inference** (*anumāna*): concept-mediated cognition via *apoha* (meaning as exclusion).

This reduction was philosophically motivated: Dharmakīrti rejected *śabda* as an independent source because testimony is reducible to inference about the speaker's reliability. The move is exactly parallel to Hume's skepticism about testimony — both question whether testimony could be genuinely foundational.

### Jain *Anekāntavāda*: Perspectival Pluralism

Jainism does not offer a *pramāṇa* list in the classical sense. Instead, *anekāntavāda* (non-one-sidedness) holds that any claim is true only from a specific standpoint (*naya*). Full knowledge (*kevala jñāna*) would encompass all standpoints simultaneously — but every *pramāṇa* available to ordinary knowers is partial.

**Western parallel**: This is structurally similar to perspectivism (Nietzsche), epistemic situationism (feminist standpoint epistemology), and the Rashomon problem in testimony theory. The key difference: Jainism makes perspectivism systematic and formal (the *saptabhaṅgī* seven-valued logic), not merely an observation about human limitations.

### Daoist Skepticism about *Pramāṇas*

Zhuangzi does not offer a positive *pramāṇa* theory. Instead, *qí wù lùn* ("the equalization of things") dissolves the problem: the very act of distinguishing valid from invalid sources of knowledge presupposes a standpoint from which validity can be judged — and that standpoint is itself perspectival.

**Western parallel**: Pyrrhonian skepticism (*epoché*: suspension of judgment); Wittgenstein's dissolution of epistemological problems (*On Certainty*: knowledge claims only make sense within a form of life).

## Axis 2: The Status of Perception

### Direct Realism (Nyāya)

Nyāya is robustly direct realist: *pratyakṣa* provides unmediated contact with external particulars. Objects cause cognitions; a valid cognition accurately represents its object. The *vyāpti* (pervasion) relationship that grounds inference is itself grounded in repeated perception of co-occurrence.

**Western parallel**: Aristotelian realism; contemporary direct realism (McDowell's *Mind and World*: perception is already conceptually structured but directly worldly).

### Indirect Realism (Sāṃkhya, Yogācāra)

Sāṃkhya's *puruṣa*-*prakṛti* duality introduces a form of indirect realism: the *puruṣa* (pure consciousness) never directly contacts the material world; the *buddhi* (intellect) receives impressions of objects and presents them to *puruṣa*.

Yogācāra Buddhism (*vijñānavāda*) goes further: the "external" world is itself a construction of consciousness. There is no mind-independent object — only streams of experience (*vijñāna*).

**Western parallel**: Representationalism (Locke, Descartes); Kant's transcendental idealism (things-in-themselves are unknowable; we perceive phenomena structured by our cognitive apparatus); Husserl's phenomenology (intentional objects are constituted by consciousness).

### Dharmakīrti's *Svasaṃvedana* (Self-Reflexive Awareness)

Dharmakīrti's claim that mental states are directly self-known without inference has no clear Western equivalent. Descartes' *cogito* is sometimes cited, but it is inferential ("I think, therefore I am"). Dharmakīrti's *svasaṃvedana* is pre-inferential: suffering knows itself as suffering without needing to represent itself as an object.

**AI relevance**: *Svasaṃvedana* anticipates the debate over whether AI systems can have introspective access to their own computational states — direct vs. inferential access to internal states is the analytic structure of the debate.

## Axis 3: Testimony and Tradition

### Vedic *Śabda* vs Post-Enlightenment Empiricism

Indian philosophical traditions that accept *śabda* as a *pramāṇa* face a structural question: how is the reliability of the speaker established? If by inference (the speaker is reliable *because* they are a realized being), then *śabda* reduces to inference. If foundationally (the Vedas are self-certifying or revelation by definition), then the epistemology is conservative: existing testimony is self-justified.

Western post-Enlightenment epistemology was suspicious of exactly this move. Hume, Locke, and later Fricker (*Epistemic Injustice*, 2007) analyzed testimony as reducible to induction (Hume) or as a default-if-no-defeaters entitlement (contemporary views). The key disagreement: whether testimony is *inferentially* supported or *non-inferentially* entitled.

**Practical stakes**: Knowledge from AI systems is a form of testimony. The epistemological question of whether AI testimony is *śabda*-like (derived from "realized" training process) or merely probabilistic inference affects how confidently agents should defer to their own outputs.

## Axis 4: The Limits of Knowledge

| Tradition | Characterization of Epistemic Limits |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Kantian** | *Noumena* are unknowable; phenomena are structured by cognitive apparatus; the "thing-in-itself" is a limiting concept, not a knowable object |
| **Pyrrhonian skepticism** | Suspension of judgment (*epoché*) achieves *ataraxia* (tranquility); the question of knowledge's limits is itself suspended |
| **Jain *anekāntavāda*** | Any claim is one-sided; full knowledge (*kevala jñāna*) is in principle possible (for a *jīna*) but unavailable to ordinary knowers; limits are perspective-relative |
| **Buddhist *apoha*** | Concepts are negative (excluding non-horse does not positively grasp horse); all concept-mediated knowledge is indirect; only perception of particulars is direct |
| **Daoist dissolution** | The question "what are the limits of knowledge?" presupposes a standpoint from which limits could be mapped — dissolving the question is the insight (*zhuangzi* butterfly dream) |
| **Wang Yangming** | The limits of knowledge are not epistemological but moral-practical: the mind that acts rightly has no limits to its liáng zhī; the limit is akrasia, not ignorance |

## Synthesis: What Each Tradition Does Not Try to Explain

Comparative epistemology is illuminating not only for what traditions explain but for what they leave unaddressed:

- **Western analytic epistemology** excels at: Gettier cases, justified true belief, epistemic luck, context-sensitivity of knowledge attributions. It underaddresses: the social dimensions of knowledge production, the role of practice in knowledge, limits of propositional knowledge.
- **Indian pramāṇa theory** excels at: systematizing knowledge sources, the relationship between perception and inference, testimonial authority. It underaddresses: Gettier-style cases (though some Indian philosophers anticipated them), knowledge's relationship to action.
- **Buddhist epistemology** excels at: the phenomenology of cognition, the structure of concept vs. perception, epistemic humility. It underaddresses: social knowledge structures, testimony.
- **Chinese philosophy** (Confucian, Daoist, Wang Yangming) largely does not pursue pramāṇa-style epistemology. Instead, it focuses on the relationship between knowledge and action, the cultivation of judgment, and the limitations of propositional knowledge for practical wisdom.
