# Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika: Epistemology, Inference, and Ontology

**Nyāya** (logic/theory of knowledge) and **Vaiśeṣika** (particularism/ontology) are two of the six classical **āstika** (orthodox, Veda-affirming) schools of Indian philosophy. Though historically distinct, they were paired early in the tradition (by at least the 10th century CE) because they share metaphysical commitments: a **pluralist realist ontology** (the world consists of many real, distinct entities), a **correspondence theory of truth**, and an **empiricist epistemology** that takes perception and inference as the primary roads to knowledge. Together they developed Indian philosophy's most systematic logic, theory of inference (*anumāna*), and ontological category scheme (*padārtha*), which became the shared "common sense realist" background against which Buddhist and Jain epistemologists defined their critiques.

## Nyāya Epistemology

### The Four Pramāṇas

Nyāya accepts **four valid sources of knowledge** (*pramāṇas*), distinguished by the kind of cogniser-object relation that produces them:

| Pramāṇa | Sanskrit | Mechanism | Paradigm case |
|---------|---------|-----------|--------------|
| **Pratyakṣa** | Perception | Direct sensory contact: sense organ + sense object → percept | Seeing a blue pot |
| **Anumāna** | Inference | Cognition through a mark (*liṅga*) — universal concomitance + particular case | Fire inferred from smoke |
| **Upamāna** | Comparison/analogy | Similarity to a previously known thing | Recognising a gavaya (wild cow) by similarity to a domestic cow |
| **Śabda** | Testimony | Reliable speaker (*āpta*) assertion | Learning astronomical facts from a trustworthy astronomer |

Dignāga-Dharmakīrti argued that Upamāna and Śabda reduce to the first two; Nyāya defended their irreducibility.

### Perception (Pratyakṣa)

Nyāya distinguishes two stages:

- **Nirvikalpaka pratyakṣa** (indeterminate perception): immediate but pre-linguistic contact with the object; registers the particular and its universal without yet predication
- **Savikalpaka pratyakṣa** (determinate perception): linguistically articulable cognition; "This is a pot made of clay" — involves predication, identification of universals, and ready for assertion

**Key contrast with Dignāga:** Nyāya holds that *savikalpaka* perception is genuinely perceptual (not inference-laden); we really perceive universals as instantiated in particulars. Dignāga denies this — anything involving universals/language is inference, not perception.

**Alaukika pratyakṣa (extraordinary perception):**
- **Sāmānya-lakṣaṇa**: perception of universals through their exemplified instances
- **Jñāna-lakṣaṇa**: perception of an object's properties through prior cognition (e.g., cotton "looks soft" because past touch-perception informs current visual experience)
- **Yogaja**: perception through yogic powers; direct perception of subtle, distant, or past objects by advanced practitioners (Nyāya accepts this to explain veridical claims by sages)

### Inference (Anumāna): The Five-Membered Syllogism

The Nyāya **five-membered syllogistic** (*pañcāvayava vākya*) is the canonical form for inference presentation in debate:

| Member | Sanskrit | Content | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Pratijñā** | Thesis | Assertion to be demonstrated | "The mountain has fire" |
| **Hetu** | Reason | Inferential mark | "because it has smoke" |
| **Udāharaṇa** | Illustration | Universal rule with example | "wherever there is smoke there is fire, as in the kitchen" |
| **Upanaya** | Application | Apply rule to subject | "the mountain has smoke, which is this sort of thing" |
| **Nigamana** | Conclusion | Restate thesis as established | "therefore the mountain has fire" |

**Comparison with Aristotelian syllogistic:**

| Feature | Nyāya 5-member | Aristotelian 3-member |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| Form | Includes example + application | Pure schematic |
| Direction | From particular to universal to particular | From universals to particular |
| Pragmatic role | Debate/persuasion (includes illustration for witness) | Deductive demonstration |
| Epistemological grounding | Perceptual basis (vyāpti from induction) | Foundational axioms |

The five-member form was later reduced to a two-member or three-member form by Dignāga and later Navya-Nyāya thinkers for logical purposes; the five-member form was preserved for rhetorical/debate contexts.

### Vyāpti (Universal Concomitance)

The validity of inference depends on **vyāpti**: the universal and invariable concomitance between reason (hetu) and probandum (sādhya):

$$\forall x: \text{smoke}(x) \Rightarrow \text{fire}(x) \quad (\text{vyāpti})$$

Vyāpti is established through **inductive perception** (*bhūyodarśana*): repeated co-perception of smoke and fire in many instances, combined with the non-observation of any counterexample (*vyabhicāra*).

**The problem of induction (vyāpti's foundation):** How is vyāpti established from finite cases? Nyāya offers:
1. Perception of repeated co-presence is itself a pramāṇa (bhūyodarśana as valid)
2. Absence of counterexample strengthens (non-observation under appropriate conditions)
3. Knowledge of causal-essential connection in some cases licenses universal extension

This anticipates Humean concerns, which Buddhist epistemologists pressed vigorously: Dharmakīrti argued vyāpti can only be established through essential identity (svabhāvahetu) or causal connection (kāryahetu) — mere co-occurrence is insufficient for universal generalisation.

## Vaiśeṣika Ontology: The Seven Padārthas

The Vaiśeṣika school, founded by Kaṇāda (c. 2nd century BCE, the *Vaiśeṣika-sūtras*), offered the most systematic **category ontology** (padārthavāda) in Indian philosophy. A padārtha ("that which a word refers to") is an irreducible type of thing that exists and can be known:

| Padārtha | Sanskrit | Examples | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|
| 1. **Dravya** | Substance | Earth, water, fire, air, ether (ākāśa), time, space, soul (ātman), mind (manas) | Nine fundamental substances; everything else inheres in substances |
| 2. **Guṇa** | Quality | Colour, taste, smell, touch, number, size, separateness, conjunction, disjunction, priority, posteriority, pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, volition | 24 qualities; inhere in substances; do not have qualities themselves |
| 3. **Karma** | Action/motion | Upward, downward, lateral, radial, composite motion | Inheres in substances; produces conjunction and disjunction |
| 4. **Sāmānya** | Universal | Cowness, treehood, substance-hood | Real entity; produces "one in many"; basis of generic cognition |
| 5. **Viśeṣa** (*Vaiśeṣika*'s namesake) | Ultimate particular | The irreducible individuating mark of eternal simples (atoms, souls, ākāśa) | Explains what makes one eternal atom distinct from another |
| 6. **Samavāya** | Inherence relation | The relation between quality and substance, between universal and particular | Eternal, one relation connecting many pairs |
| 7. **Abhāva** | Absence (later addition) | Prior absence (before creation), posterior absence (after destruction), absolute absence, mutual absence | Prābhākara Mīmāṃsā had argued absence is a seventh category; Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika eventually incorporated |

### Key Ontological Commitments

**Atoms:** Vaiśeṣika developed a sophisticated **atomism**. The four material elements (earth, water, fire, air) have irreducible eternal atoms (*paramāṇus*) as their smallest units. Physical objects are dyads (atoms combined), triads, and larger aggregates. Atoms are spherical, dimensionless (technically), and eternal — they are never created or destroyed, only recombined.

**Samavāya (Inherence):** The crucial glue-relation of Vaiśeṣika ontology. Samavāya connects:
- Quality and the substance it belongs to (redness *inheres in* this rose)
- Universal and its instances (cowness *inheres in* this cow)
- Parts and whole (threads *inhere in* the cloth)
- Atoms and their aggregates

Samavāya is itself a real entity distinct from conjunction (*saṃyoga*, which is a quality, reversible by separation) — inherence is permanent and asymmetric.

**Buddhist critique of sāmānya:** Dignāga and Dharmakīrti argued that sāmānya (universals) are not real entities — they are apoha constructs. The Vaiśeṣika response: sāmānya is required to explain why knowledge of one cow-instance supports expectations about all cows; if sāmānya were not real, inference from similar cases (sāmānya-at-work in vyāpti) would have no ontological basis.

## Navya-Nyāya: The New Logic

From the 13th century CE, the **Navya-Nyāya** ("New Nyāya") school — primarily associated with **Gaṅgeśa** (c. 1325, *Tattvacintāmaṇi*) and subsequently Raghunātha Śiromaṇi and Gadādhara — introduced a rigorous technical vocabulary for expressing logical and ontological analyses with precision:

**Key innovations:**
- **Technical language of relational abstracts:** A relation is described by its "conditioner" (*anavacchedaka*) and "limitor" (*avacchedaka*); "the smoke on the mountain" is formalised as "smokeness-delimited smoke borne by mountain-ness-delimited locus"
- **Paraphrase method:** Any claim can be paraphrased into this technical language to eliminate ambiguity
- **Formal analysis of vyāpti:** Vyāpti is now precisely defined as: "smoke-ness is the pervaded, fire-ness is the pervader — wherever the pervaded is present, the pervader is present, and the absence of the pervader does not co-occur with the presence of the pervaded"

This highly formalised language — sometimes called "Sanskrit formal logic" — was studied across religious traditions in India and was able to state and resolve debates that ordinary Sanskrit could not express unambiguously. It has been argued to anticipate features of modern predicate logic and relation theory.

## Udayana's Proofs for the Existence of God

**Udayana** (c. 1050 CE) developed Nyāya's most sophisticated arguments for the existence of Īśvara (God) in the *Nyāyakusumāñjali*, deploying standard Nyāya inference methodology:

1. **From the effect:** The world is an effect (it has parts, is limited, is arranged toward ends) → therefore it has an agent cause (kartṛ) → that agent must have adequate knowledge/power → therefore Īśvara
2. **From inorganic combination:** Atoms, being inert, cannot spontaneously combine into ordered structures → they require a knower-director → Īśvara
3. **From the authority of the Vedas:** Vedas are authorless (*apauruṣeya*) in the sense of having no human author → but all words are produced by speakers → therefore a superhuman speaker, Īśvara
4. **From numericality:** Numbers above one (twoness, threeness, etc.) are qualities produced in substances by a knower's cognition of the substances → for eternal atoms, this cognizer is Īśvara

These arguments were extensively debated by Buddhist and Mīmāṃsaka critics (who denied God's existence or denied the authority argument), generating one of the richest philosophical debates in world theism.

## Epistemological Legacy

**Comparison with Western epistemology:**

| Feature | Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika | Western analogue |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Four pramāṇas | Plural foundationalism | Reid's common-sense, BonJour |
| Vyāpti/induction problem | Explicitly solved by co-perception + non-counterexample | Hume's problem (less explicitly solved) |
| Universals as real | Medieval realism (universalia ante rem) | Armstrong's universal realism |
| Inherence (samavāya) | Distinctive real relation | Armstrong on instantiation; distinct from Aristotelian immanent universals |
| Atomism | Vaiśeṣika physical atomism + substance dualism | Democritus + Descartes (hybrid) |
