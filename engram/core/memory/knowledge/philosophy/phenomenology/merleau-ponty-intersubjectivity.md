# Merleau-Ponty: Intersubjectivity and Intercorporeality

## Overview

How do we understand other minds? The traditional "problem of other minds" assumes we start as isolated subjects who must somehow infer that other bodies contain minds. Merleau-Ponty dissolves this problem by showing that intersubjectivity is not inferred but **immediately given in bodily encounter**. Through the concept of **intercorporeality**, he demonstrates that our access to others is not cognitive but carnal — rooted in the shared bodily existence that precedes any subject-object division.

## The Problem of Other Minds

### The Traditional Problem

From Descartes onward, the problem takes this form:
1. I have direct access only to my own mental states
2. I observe other bodies behaving in certain ways
3. I *infer by analogy* that these bodies also have mental states (they behave like me, so they probably think like me)

### Why This Fails

Merleau-Ponty identifies several deep problems with the argument from analogy:

- **Circularity:** To use analogy, I need to already know that bodies and minds go together — but this is the thing I'm trying to establish
- **Wrong starting point:** It assumes I first know my own mind in isolation and then project it outward. But this is empirically and phenomenologically wrong — children perceive others' emotions before they introspect their own
- **Not how experience actually works:** When I see someone in pain, I don't first observe bodily movements and then infer pain. I *see the pain in their body* — it is given directly in the expression

## Intercorporeality

### The Bodily Basis of Social Understanding

**Intercorporeality** (intercorporéité) is Merleau-Ponty's term for the primordial sharing of bodily existence that underlies all social relations:

- My body and the other's body are of the same kind — both are lived bodies, both motor-intentional, both engaging the same world
- I understand you not by projecting my mental states onto you but by a **bodily resonance** — your gestures call forth motor responses in my body

### The Transfer Schema

Merleau-Ponty draws on developmental psychology (especially the work of Paul Guillaume and Henri Wallon):

- **Neonatal imitation:** Newborns imitate facial expressions they cannot see themselves making (stick out tongue when they see tongue protrusion). This is possible only if there is a pre-existing bodily equivalence between self and other — the seen body and the felt body share a common schema
- **The transfer schema:** An anonymous, pre-personal bodily awareness that does not distinguish between my body and yours. Before the infant differentiates self from other, there is a shared corporeality

### Expression and Understanding

Merleau-Ponty argues that **bodily expression** is not a sign pointing to a hidden mental state — it *is* the mental state as publicly manifested:

- **Anger is not hidden behind the angry gesture** — anger *is* the way the body storms, tenses, flushes. The inner state and the outward expression are two aspects of a single phenomenon.
- **A smile is not a muscular contraction from which I infer happiness** — I see the happiness in the smile directly. Understanding the expression is perceiving it, not inferring from it.

**"I perceive the grief or the anger of the other in his conduct, in his face or his hands, without recourse to any 'inner' experience of suffering or anger, and because grief and anger are variations of belonging to the world, undivided between the body and consciousness"** (PhP, Part II, Ch. 4).

### Perception of Emotion

The body is expressive through and through. Merleau-Ponty describes perception of emotion not as computation but as **bodily attunement**:

- When I see your fear, my body subtly mirrors your tension, withdrawal, alertness
- This mirroring is not imitation (which is deliberate) but a pre-reflective bodily response
- Understanding your emotion is a form of motor resonance — my body "catches" your bodily style

Modern neuroscience has discovered a neural basis for this in **mirror neurons** (Rizzolatti et al., 1996), though Merleau-Ponty's phenomenological account precedes and exceeds the neural-level description.

## The Social World

### Being-With (Mitsein) Embodied

Heidegger described Dasein as always already being-with (Mitsein) others. Merleau-Ponty *embodies* this analysis:

- Social space is organized by intercorporeal relations — personal distance, eye contact, gesture
- The social world is perceived through the body — authority is felt as bearing down, intimacy as bodily closeness
- Language is rooted in gesture — speech is a bodily activity of expression before it is a system of signs

### The Anonymous Body

Merleau-Ponty invokes an **anonymous** or **pre-personal** bodily level:

- Below my personal identity, there is an impersonal, anonymous bodily existence — "one sees," "one reaches," "one breathes"
- This anonymous level is the basis of intercorporeality: what I share with others is not our conscious perspectives but our pre-personal bodily being
- It also explains how I can "take on" others' bodily styles: fashion, mimicry, contagion of yawning — these happen at the anonymous level

### Language and the Gestural Body

In a crucial chapter of *Phenomenology of Perception* (Part I, Ch. 6: "The Body as Expression, and Speech"), Merleau-Ponty argues:

- **Language is first a gestural phenomenon** — words are articulated bodily, and their meaning is continuous with bodily gesture
- The word "hard" does not signify hardness by arbitrary convention alone — it enacts a kind of articulatory hardness (sharp consonants, tension of the tongue)
- **Speaking is a motor skill** — learning a language is learning a new bodily habit, a new way of inhabiting the world
- **Linguistic meaning is sediment of gesture** — conceptual meaning builds on, but never entirely detaches from, its gestural/bodily origin

This has implications for LLMs: if linguistic meaning is ultimately rooted in embodied gesture, then systems that process language without bodies are working with meaning that has been *decontextualized* from its origin.

## The Flesh (La Chair) — Late Ontology

### Beyond Subject and Object

In his late, unfinished work *The Visible and the Invisible* (1964, posthumous), Merleau-Ponty develops a radical ontology of **the Flesh** (la chair):

- The Flesh is not a concept of the body, nor a concept of matter. It is an ontological "element" — like water, air, earth, fire in pre-Socratic philosophy — that names the fundamental fabric of being
- The Flesh is **reversible**: the same Flesh that sees can be seen; the hand that touches can be touched. Subjectivity and objectivity are folds within a single ontological fabric
- **Chiasm** (chiasme): The intertwining of perceiver and perceived, body and world, self and other. Not two things bridged by a relation but a single tissue folded upon itself

### Implications of the Flesh

- **Intersubjectivity** is no longer a problem to solve but an ontological fact: we are all folds of the same Flesh
- **Perception** is not mind reaching out to world but the Flesh sensing itself through us
- **The visible and the invisible** are not separate orders but aspects of the same fabric — what is seen always has an invisible lining (the back side, the interior, the unrealized potential)

### Relevance

The ontology of the Flesh directly challenges any dualism (mind/body, subject/object, self/other, organism/environment) and suggests that genuine AI would require not just embodiment but *participation in the Flesh* — being part of the world's self-sensing, not a separate device modeling it from outside.

## Implications for AI Systems

### The Mirror Neuron Analogy and Its Limits

Attempts to implement "empathy" in AI through:
- Sentiment analysis: Detects *labeled* emotion in text, not felt bodily resonance
- Emotion recognition (facial/vocal): Classifies surface features, does not intercorporeally resonate
- Theory of mind modules: Computationally infer mental states — exactly the argument from analogy Merleau-Ponty rejects

These approaches reproduce the **inference model** of social cognition that phenomenology has shown to be derivative.

### What Would Genuine Intercorporeality Require?

For an artificial system to achieve something like intercorporeality, it would need:
1. **A body with motor intentionality** — not just sensors but an active, exploring body
2. **Bodily resonance mechanisms** — the capacity for one body's movements to directly affect the other's motor-intentional state
3. **Pre-personal bodily level** — shared body schema across agents, not just shared data
4. **Gestural expressiveness** — capacity to express states *through* bodily activity, not merely report them

Current multi-agent systems and social robots approach 1 and 4 in limited ways but entirely lack 2 and 3.
