# Embedded, Enacted, Ecological Cognition: Gibson's Affordances and the 4E Synthesis

## Overview

The **4E framework** organizes contemporary non-Cartesian approaches to cognition under four headings: **Embodied**, **Embedded**, **Enacted**, and **Extended**. This file covers the remaining two E's (Embedded and Enacted, with ecological psychology woven in) and provides a synthesis of the entire 4E landscape. The central unifying figure is James J. Gibson, whose ecological approach to perception provides the concept of **affordance** — which structures the intersection between phenomenology, ecological psychology, and enactivism.

## Embedded Cognition

### The Thesis

**Embedded cognition** claims that cognitive processes are deeply dependent on the environment in which they occur. The environment is not merely a source of input but a *scaffold* that shapes, supports, and partially constitutes cognitive activity.

This is weaker than the extended mind thesis (which claims the environment is literally part of the cognitive system) but stronger than orthodox internalism (which treats the environment as merely providing inputs to a self-contained brain).

### Environmental Scaffolding

Key examples:

- **Epistemic actions** (Kirsh & Maglio, 1994): Tetris players rotate pieces on-screen rather than mentally — not because they can't rotate mentally, but because the environmental manipulation is faster and more reliable. The action changes the environment to make cognition easier.
- **Spatial organization:** Physical arrangement of one's workspace (placing related documents near each other, using sticky notes, whiteboard diagrams) structures thought without being "part of" the mind.
- **Social scaffolding:** Other people serve as cognitive scaffolds — reminding, correcting, complementing one's own cognitive capacities. Joint problem-solving distributes cognitive load across individuals.
- **Cognitive artifacts:** Maps, calendars, lists, calculators — designed objects that scaffold specific cognitive tasks.

### Situated Cognition

A related tradition, **situated cognition** (Suchman, 1987; Lave & Wenger, 1991), emphasizes that cognitive processes are always embedded in:
- Physical situations (office, workshop, kitchen)
- Social situations (institutional roles, cultural norms)
- Historical situations (prior experience, accumulated practices)

Abstracted, context-free cognition (as studied in laboratory experiments or AI benchmarks) is a special, artificial case — not the default.

## Enacted Cognition

### Beyond Varela et al.

While Varela, Thompson, and Rosch launched enactivism (covered in the Embodied Mind file), the tradition has developed significantly:

#### Autopoietic Enactivism (Thompson, Di Paolo)

- Cognition is grounded in the **autonomy** of living systems
- **Adaptive behavior** requires a normative dimension: the organism evaluates its own activity relative to its viability
- Di Paolo (2005): Adaptivity — the capacity to *regulate* structural coupling in relation to norms — is the missing element in basic autopoiesis

#### Sensorimotor Enactivism (O'Regan & Noë)

- Perception is constituted by **sensorimotor contingencies** — the lawful relationships between movements and changes in sensory stimulation
- To perceive is to *master* these contingencies — to know (implicitly, bodily) how sensory input will change given motor activity
- "Visual awareness" is not having an internal picture but exercising sensorimotor mastery of visual contingencies

**O'Regan & Noë (2001):** "Vision is not something that happens *to* us. It is something we *do*."

#### Radical Enactivism (Hutto & Myin)

- Hutto & Myin (2013): There is no contentful representation in basic minds. Basic cognitive activities (perception, motor control, emotional response) involve *contentless* sensorimotor engagements.
- **Content** — semantic, truth-evaluable representation — arises only in linguistically scaffolded, socially embedded practices. It is not a feature of all cognition but a late, culturally dependent achievement.
- This "radical" version pushes enactivism further from mainstream cognitive science by denying that basic perception involves any kind of representation or intentional content.

## Gibson's Ecological Psychology

### James J. Gibson (1904–1979)

Gibson spent his career attacking the prevailing view that perception is indirect — that we perceive "sense data" (sensations, retinal images) and then infer or construct the world through cognitive processing. His alternative:

### Direct Perception

**Perception is direct** — we perceive the world, not mental intermediaries. There are no internal representations between perceiver and perceived:

- The ambient optic array (structured light reaching the eyes from all directions) contains sufficient information to specify the layout and properties of the environment
- **Optical flow** (the pattern of motion across the visual field as the observer moves) directly specifies the observer's movement through space — no computation needed
- **Texture gradients** directly specify surface orientation and distance — no depth computation from binocular disparity

### Affordances

Gibson's most influential concept: **affordances** are the possibilities for action that the environment offers to a particular organism.

> "The affordances of the environment are what it *offers* the animal, what it *provides* or *furnishes*, either for good or ill" (Gibson, 1979, p. 127).

Key features:

| Property | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Relational** | Affordances are neither objective properties of the environment nor subjective projections of the organism — they exist in the *relation* between the two. A surface affords walking-on for a human but not for a fish. |
| **Directly perceived** | Affordances are not inferred from physical properties — they are perceived directly. We see the climbability of stairs, the graspability of handles, the sit-on-ability of chairs. |
| **Action-oriented** | Affordances are defined in terms of what the organism *can do*, not what the environment *is*. The world is perceived as a field of possible actions. |
| **Scaled to the body** | Whether a surface affords walking depends on its height relative to the walker's leg length. Whether a gap affords passing through depends on the passer's shoulder width. Affordances are body-scaled. |
| **Organism-environment mutuality** | Affordances exist only in the animal-environment system. Neither the animal nor the environment alone has affordances. |

### Resonance with Phenomenology

Gibson's affordances converge powerfully with Merleau-Ponty (though Gibson developed them independently):

| Merleau-Ponty | Gibson |
|---------------|--------|
| Motor significance | Affordance |
| Body schema | Effectivity (organism's action capabilities) |
| Lived space | Ecological niche |
| Maximum grip | Perceiving affordances optimally |
| Perception as skill | Direct perception through active exploration |

**The key connection:** Both Gibson and Merleau-Ponty reject the perception-as-computation model. Both see the perceiver as an active, embodied explorer. Both treat the meaningful world as constituted in the organism-environment relation, not in the organism's head.

### Points of Agreement

All four E's share:
1. **Anti-Cartesianism:** Cognition is not disembodied symbol manipulation
2. **Anti-representationalism** (to varying degrees): Internal models are not the whole story (or not the story at all)
3. **Organism-environment coupling:** Cognition happens in the relation, not just in the head
4. **Emphasis on action:** Cognition is fundamentally for doing, not for contemplating
5. **Continuity of life and mind:** Cognitive capacities grow out of biological capacities (at least for the enactivists)

### Points of Tension

| Tension | Positions |
|---------|-----------|
| **Representations?** | Extended mind accepts representations (functionally defined); radical enactivism rejects all content; other positions are intermediate |
| **Functionalism?** | Clark is a functionalist (parity principle); enactivists reject functionalism (autonomy matters, not function) |
| **Boundaries of mind?** | Extended mind: mind includes external resources. Embedded cognition: mind depends on but excludes external resources. Enacted: boundaries are dynamic, drawn by the organism's sense-making |
| **Phenomenology?** | Embodied and enacted traditions are deeply phenomenological; extended mind is functionalist and less concerned with experience; embedded is eclectic |

### Reconciliation Attempts

- **Gallagher (2017):** *Enactivist Interventions* argues that enactivism can absorb the valid insights of the other three E's
- **Newen, De Bruin, & Gallagher (2018):** *The Oxford Handbook of 4E Cognition* — comprehensive attempt at integration
- **Rowlands (2010):** *The New Science of the Mind* proposes "amalgamated mind" combining all four

## Implications for AI

### The 4E Diagnostic for AI Systems

Each E identifies a specific dimension of embodiment/situatedness that AI systems can possess (or lack):

| E | What it requires | Current LLM status |
|---|------------------|--------------------|
| **Embodied** | Body with motor capacities shaping cognition | ✗ (no body; multimodal extends but doesn't embody) |
| **Embedded** | Genuine environmental scaffolding | Partial (tool use scaffolds, persistent files, but no continuous environment) |
| **Enacted** | Autonomy, sense-making, structural coupling | ✗ (no metabolic autonomy, no organismal norms) |
| **Extended** | Cognitive processes spanning brain-body-world | Partial (Engram-style memory systems approximate this) |

### Design Implications

1. **Maximize embedding:** Give AI systems rich, persistent environments (not just prompt windows)
2. **Approximate enaction:** Create feedback loops where the agent's actions modify its future inputs
3. **Embrace extension:** Design memory and tool systems as cognitive prosthetics, not just data stores
4. **Accept embodiment limits:** Some cognitive capacities may remain permanently unavailable without genuine embodiment

### Gibson and AI Tool Use

Affordances provide a useful design framework for AI tool systems:
- Tools should be "perceived" (presented) in terms of what they afford — not what they are
- The agent's "body" (its capabilities: text generation, tool calls, memory access) determines what the environment affords
- Good tool design makes affordances transparent; poor design hides them or creates misleading ones
