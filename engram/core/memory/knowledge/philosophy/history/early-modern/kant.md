# Kant: The Copernican Revolution in Philosophy (1781–1800)

**Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804) stands at the hinge point of the entire history of modern philosophy. He synthesizes the rationalist and empiricist traditions, transforms the terms in which philosophy is conducted, and makes possible — by resolving some of their tensions and generating new ones — virtually every subsequent philosophical movement: German Idealism (Fichte, Schelling, Hegel), phenomenology (via the Marburg Neo-Kantians and directly to Husserl and Heidegger), analytic philosophy (the neo-Kantian roots of Frege and early Wittgenstein), and contemporary ethics (Kantian constructivism: Rawls, Korsgaard, O'Neill).

Kant's three *Critiques* form an architectonic:
- **Critique of Pure Reason** (1781, revised 1787): What can reason know? What are the conditions of the possibility of experience and knowledge?
- **Critique of Practical Reason** (1788): What must reason do? What are the conditions of moral action?
- **Critique of Judgment** (1790): How does reason relate to beauty, the sublime, and biological purpose?

The unifying theme: **reason's self-examination** — reason legislating to itself the limits of its legitimate use.

---

## 1. The Copernican Revolution in Epistemology

Kant describes his revolution as analogous to Copernicus's: instead of the mind conforming to objects (the pre-Kantian assumption), objects conform to the mind — that is, the mind contributes the *structure* through which objects are experienced; objects as experienced already bear the mark of the mind's organizing activity.

The problem Kant inherits: Hume showed that necessary causal connection cannot be found in experience. But causation is essential to Newtonian physics, which is the most successful knowledge-claim in history. How can something be both a necessary truth (math and physics) and about the empirical world? This is the problem of **synthetic a priori knowledge**: knowledge that is (a) not merely logical truth (it's not true by definition — the denial of "every event has a cause" doesn't generate a logical contradiction) and (b) not derived from experience (it has necessity and universality that experience cannot provide).

Kant's answer: synthetic a priori knowledge is possible because the mind imposes its own structures on raw experience — space, time, and the twelve categories (including causation) — and these structures are the conditions of the possibility of experience itself. We don't *learn* that every event has a cause because we observe it; we *know* it because causation is the lens through which we organize any coherent experience. Causation isn't a feature of things-in-themselves; it's a structure the understanding brings to the material of sensation.

This is the **transcendental** method: to investigate not objects but the conditions of the possibility of experience and knowledge of objects. The *Critique* is not an empirical inquiry but a *a priori* investigation of what must be true for experience to be possible at all.

## 2. Critique of Pure Reason: The Architectonic

### Transcendental Aesthetic: Space and Time

Before even applying concepts, experience requires **intuition** — the immediate presentation of objects. Kant argues that space and time are not features of objects in themselves but forms of human sensibility: the organizing structures through which we receive any sensory manifold whatsoever. This is why geometry and arithmetic are synthetic a priori: they are truths about the forms of our intuition, not about things-in-themselves.

This has immediate consequences: we know nothing about what space and time are "out there" (in things-in-themselves); space and time are *ours*. Things-in-themselves are not spatiotemporally located; objects *as experienced* are necessarily so.

### Transcendental Analytic: Categories of the Understanding

Pure concepts of the understanding — **categories** — are not derived from experience but are the *a priori* rules by which the understanding organizes the manifold of intuition into coherent experience. Kant derives twelve categories from the logical forms of judgment (the table of judgments from Aristotelian logic): causation, substance, unity, plurality, possibility, actuality, necessity, etc.

The **transcendental deduction** (the most difficult part of the *Critique*) argues that these categories must apply to all experience because experience itself — the synthesis of manifold intuition into coherent, objective, intersubjective reality — requires these categories. To have experience at all is to have causally organized, substantively structured, spatio-temporally unified experience.

The **schematism**: categories are pure and non-temporal; intuitions are temporal. The schema is the time-determination that mediates between category and intuition: the schema of causation is "rule-governed temporal succession," not bare logical implication.

Key results:
- **Analogies of Experience**: three analogies prove that substance is permanent, that every event has a cause (second analogy), and that substances mutually interact (third analogy). These are not empirical generalizations but transcendental proofs of the conditions of any possible experience.
- **Refutation of Idealism**: against Berkeley (inner experience presupposes outer experience) and against Descartes (the certainty of the external world is as basic as the certainty of the inner).

### Transcendental Dialectic: The Limits of Reason

When reason tries to extend the categories *beyond* possible experience — to the soul as a whole (rational psychology), the world as a whole (rational cosmology), and God (rational theology) — it generates **illusions** (dialectical illusions). This is the "negative" part of the *Critique*: showing the limits of theoretical reason.

**Paralogisms**: arguments purporting to prove the rational soul is simple, immortal, identical, and distinct from the body — all rest on misapplying the category of substance to consciousness beyond the limits of experience. The Cartesian substantial self is a transcendental illusion.

**Antinomies of Pure Reason**: when reason tries to think the world as a completed totality, it can prove both (a) that the world had a beginning in time and (b) that it did not; both (a) that matter is composed of simple parts and (b) that it is infinitely divisible; both (a) that there is fre will and (b) that everything is causally determined; both (a) that there is a necessary being and (b) that there is no necessary being. These paired contradictions demonstrate that the world-as-totality is not a legitimate object of theoretical knowledge.

**Ideal of Pure Reason**: the idea of God as the totality of all possible predicates — a regulative ideal for systematic knowledge, not a constitutive fact. The ontological, cosmological, and design arguments all fail as theoretical proofs: they either illegitimately apply categories beyond experience, or they smuggle in the existence of God as a hidden premise.

The result: theoretical reason cannot establish souls, immortality, free will, or God's existence. This clears the ground for practical reason to establish free will and God on moral rather than theoretical grounds — the primacy of practical over theoretical reason.

## 3. Critique of Practical Reason: Moral Autonomy

Kant's ethics is the *other* great turning point of modern moral philosophy (alongside Hume's naturalism). The *Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785) and second *Critique* establish what Kant regards as the one unconditional moral principle.

### The Good Will

The *Groundwork* opens: "Nothing in the world — indeed nothing even beyond the world — can possibly be conceived which could be called good without qualification except a **good will**." Health, intelligence, happiness, courage — all these are good only conditionally, valuable when used well. The good will is good without qualification because it acts from duty regardless of consequences or inclination.

This is the **deontological** character of Kantian ethics: the moral worth of an action lies in the motive (conformity to duty) rather than in consequences. The physician who prescribes correctly from self-interest acts lawfully but not morally; she who prescribes correctly from respect for her duty to the patient acts morally.

### The Categorical Imperative

The moral law must be **a priori** (independent of empirical desires and inclinations) and **categorical** (binding unconditionally, not just "if you want X, do Y"). It cannot depend on what anyone desires, because desires are contingent; it must express what reason demands universally.

Kant offers three formulations of the single moral law — the **Categorical Imperative**:

**Formula of Universal Law**: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law." Test any proposed action by asking: could you universalize the maxim — could all rational beings act this way without contradiction? A lying promise fails: if everyone made lying promises, promising would be impossible (no one would trust promises), so a world of universal lying promises is logically self-defeating.

**Formula of Humanity**: "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never as a means only." Rational beings have dignity — not merely price (what can be exchanged) but intrinsic worth. To use a person purely as a tool (deceiving them, coercing them, treating them as an object to be managed) violates their dignity as a rational agent.

**Formula of Autonomy / Kingdom of Ends**: "Act according to the maxims of a universally legislating member of a merely possible kingdom of ends." Moral agents are simultaneously the legislators and the subjects of the moral law; the moral community is a kingdom of ends (all rational beings treated as ends) to which we give the law we give ourselves.

**Autonomy** is the key concept: the moral will gives itself its own law, rather than receiving it from external authority (heteronomy). To act on desire, self-interest, happiness, or divine command is heteronomous — the will is determined by something outside itself. To act from reason — from the categorical imperative — is autonomous: the will acts in accordance with the law it legislates as a member of the rational community.

### Freedom, Immortality, and God: Postulates of Practical Reason

The antinomy of freedom showed that theoretical reason can prove both that freedom exists and that determinism is universal. Kant's resolution in the second *Critique*:
- As **phenomena** (as objects of natural science), human actions are causally determined — there is no exception to the laws of nature.
- As **noumena** (as things-in-themselves), human agents may be free — the causality of freedom is an unknowable noumenal capacity.

We cannot prove freedom theoretically; we must postulate it for practical reason — moral responsibility presupposes that agents could have done otherwise. The postulates of practical reason extend beyond theoretical knowledge:
- **Freedom**: practical reason requires it.
- **Immortality**: realizing the goal of perfect conformity of will with moral law (*holiness*) is infinitely demanding; we must postulate infinite time.
- **God**: practical reason postulates God as guarantor of the supreme good — the coincidence of virtue and happiness that reason requires but cannot bring about on its own.

These postulates are not theoretical knowledge but rationally necessary moral beliefs — the basis for Kant's "moral religion" that replaces speculative theology.

## 4. Critique of Judgment: Beauty, Sublimity, and Purpose

The third *Critique* bridges the gap between the realm of nature (theoretical reason) and the realm of freedom (practical reason) through two investigations: **aesthetic judgment** and **teleological judgment**.

### Aesthetic Judgment: The Beautiful and the Sublime

A judgment of taste (e.g., "This rose is beautiful") is neither purely subjective (a matter of mere personal preference) nor purely objective (a theoretical claim about properties of the rose). It claims **universal validity** — I don't say "this rose pleases me" but "this rose IS beautiful" — while being based on feeling rather than concept. It is a judgment that demands agreement (universalizing), but cannot be proved by argument.

The **four moments** of the judgment of taste:
1. It is disinterested (not a desire for the object; contemplative pleasure)
2. It is universally valid without a concept
3. It is purposive without a purpose (the beautiful appears as if designed for our pleasure, but we don't think it has a literal purpose)
4. It is necessarily pleasing to everyone

The **sublime** (Burke before Kant, Longinus in antiquity) is distinct from the beautiful: the sublime is the overwhelming experience of vastness or power — a mountain, a storm, the starry sky — that initially threatens to overwhelm human cognition but ultimately elicits a feeling of our own rational dignity. The sublime demonstrates that reason exceeds nature; confronting our cognitive limitations, we discover something in ourselves that transcends nature.

Kant's aesthetics will be decisive for Romanticism (the aesthetic as bridge between nature and freedom), for Schiller (aesthetic education as the road to moral freedom), for Schopenhauer (art as escape from the will), and for 20th-century aesthetic theory (Adorno, Gadamer, Derrida's reading of the parergon).

### Teleological Judgment: Organisms and Natural Purposes

The final sections of the *Critique* investigate the concept of natural purpose as applied to organisms. Mechanistic physics (Newtonian) explains everything in terms of efficient causation; but organisms seem to require **teleological explanation** — parts existing for the sake of the whole, the whole for the sake of the parts. A kidney cannot be understood without reference to its function in the organism; the organism cannot be understood as a mere aggregation of parts.

Kant's conclusion: teleological judgment is **regulative** (a heuristic principle for investigating nature) rather than **constitutive** (a theoretical truth about organisms). We cannot know whether organisms are "really" purposive (we can't access nature-in-itself); we must investigate them *as if* they had purposes — because this is the only way to understand them.

This is profoundly important for the philosophy of biology: it resists both crude mechanism (organisms are just complicated machines) and naive vitalism (there is a special life-force), proposing instead a functional perspective that is practically indispensable and epistemically humble. It anticipates the debates over teleological explanation in evolutionary biology, systems biology, and computational cognitive science.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

The Kantian theoretical philosophy transforms the metaphysics of mind. The self as a substantial Cartesian subject is revealed as a transcendental illusion (the paralogisms). The empirical self (the self of introspection and psychology) is an object of inner sense, given in time, observable through self-awareness. The **transcendental unity of apperception** — "the 'I think' which must be able to accompany all my representations" — is not a substance or an entity but a formal condition of experience: the logical unity required for the manifold of intuition to be organized into a single coherent experiential field.

This anticipates Husserl's transcendental reduction and the phenomenological account of consciousness as intentional structure rather than Cartesian substance. It anticipates debates in cognitive science about whether there is a unified "self" (rejected by Metzinger and many neuroscientists — consciousness is constructed, not found) or whether some minimal self-structure is necessary (defended by Zahavi).

### Language and meaning

The *Critique* does not primarily address language, but the transcendental account of concepts inaugurates a tradition. Categories are pure concepts — conceptual forms without sensible content — and the question of how concepts apply to intuitions is the question of how thought connects to world. The schematism is Kant's answer: through time-determinations that mediate between general concept and particular intuition.

Subsequent language philosophy will radicalize this: Frege, Husserl, and Wittgenstein will all begin from the question of how meaning connects to world. The neo-Kantian tradition (Cassirer's *Philosophy of Symbolic Forms*) will extend the Kantian account of categories to language and culture generally: different symbolic forms (language, myth, science, art) organize experience differently, and there is no "view from nowhere" outside all symbolic forms.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Kantian ethics remains the most developed and widely influential deontological framework. The categorical imperative — especially the Formula of Humanity — has been applied to virtually every practical ethical question: lying, promise-breaking, using persons as mere means, the demands of justice. Rawls builds *A Theory of Justice* (1971) on Kantian foundations: the original position is a Kantian device for deriving principles of justice from what rational agents would choose under conditions that express our nature as free and equal persons.

The concept of **autonomy** — legislating for oneself the moral law — becomes the defining concept of liberal political theory and bioethics: respect for persons means, above all, respect for their autonomous agency; paternalism is a violation of the dignity of the rational agent.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

The Kantian settlement about knowledge has a complex legacy. Positive: it explains how mathematical physics is possible without positing a pre-established harmony (Leibniz) or a divine creator (Descartes). Negative: it limits knowledge to the phenomenal domain, making the traditional metaphysical questions (soul, God, free will) theoretically undecidable and placing them under the jurisdiction of practical reason.

The limitation of knowledge to make room for faith is both Kant's most celebrated and most contested move. Hegel will attack it: if we know that the thing-in-itself is unknowable, we seem to know something about it after all; the distinction between phenomenon and noumenon collapses. The logical positivists will attack from the other direction: if we cannot even formulate claims about things-in-themselves in empirically meaningful terms, they are not a limit on knowledge but mere pseudo-problems.

## The Arc Forward

The *Critique of Pure Reason* generates the agenda for 19th-century German philosophy:
- **Fichte**: the thing-in-itself is an incoherent residue; eliminate it — the absolute is the self-positing I.
- **Schelling**: the absolute must be the identity of subject and object, the ground of both.
- **Hegel**: the absolute is Spirit (Geist) articulating itself through history — the phenomenology of consciousness culminating in absolute knowledge.

The *Critique of Practical Reason* generates the agenda for 19th-century ethics and political philosophy:
- **Schiller**: the aesthetic mediates between sensuous nature and moral freedom; the *Aesthetic Education of Man* is the program.
- **Hegel**: the categorical imperative is "empty formalism" without content; actual ethical life (*Sittlichkeit*) is the concrete expression of ethical substance in family, civil society, and state.
- **Marx**: Kantian freedom is merely formal; real freedom requires material conditions.
- **Kierkegaard**: the ethical stage (Kantian duty) is not the highest; it must be surpassed through the religious leap.

The *Critique of Judgment* generates:
- **Romanticism**: the aesthetic imagination as the supreme human faculty, bridging nature and freedom.
- **Schopenhauer**: art as the momentary escape from the will's tyranny, the sublime as the confrontation with will-less knowledge.
- **Hermeneutics**: Gadamer roots the fusion of horizons and the question of understanding in Kantian aesthetics.

Everything after Kant is either an extension, a synthesis, or a critique of the Kantian framework — the three *Critiques* together constitute the definitive statement of the questions that 19th- and 20th-century philosophy will struggle to answer.
