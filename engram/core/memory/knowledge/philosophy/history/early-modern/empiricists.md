# The Empiricists: Locke, Berkeley, and Hume (1689–1776)

The empiricist tradition begins from the opposite pole to rationalism: not the certainty of mathematical reason but the primacy of sensory experience. Where the rationalists held that the mind comes equipped with innate ideas and principles, and that the most fundamental truths are discoverable by reason alone, the empiricists hold that the mind begins as *tabula rasa* — a blank slate — and all knowledge is derived from experience. The program is to trace every idea back to its origin in sensation or reflection, and to reject any idea that cannot be so traced as confused or empty.

The logic of empiricism is ruthless: pursued consistently, it disallows not just the Cartesian innate ideas but also causation, the substantial self, and the external world. Hume sees this clearly and follows it through. The skeptical results of consistent empiricism — rather than defeating empiricism — become the problem that Kant undertakes to solve.

## 1. Locke: Ideas, Experience, and the Social Contract

**John Locke** (1632–1704) is the founding empiricist and one of the most politically influential philosophers in history. The *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) is the first systematic empiricist epistemology; the *Two Treatises of Government* (1689) established the framework for liberal political theory and influenced the American founding documents directly.

### The Origin of Ideas

The *Essay* opens with an attack on innate ideas (aimed at Descartes): there are no ideas present at birth in the mind. The *Essay* then gives the positive account: all ideas come from two sources — **sensation** (information from the five senses) and **reflection** (awareness of our own mental operations: thinking, doubting, willing). These are the two fountains of knowledge; from them, by combination, abstraction, and association, all complex ideas are derived.

Locke distinguishes:
- **Simple ideas**: received passively from experience (the idea of red, of hot, of pain). The mind cannot produce or destroy simple ideas, only receive them.
- **Complex ideas**: formed by the mind from simple ideas through combination (the idea of a golden mountain — combining gold and mountain), abstraction (the idea of a triangle — the common form of all particular triangles), and relation (the idea of "taller than").

He further distinguishes **primary** and **secondary qualities** in bodies, following Galileo:
- **Primary qualities**: those in bodies that resemble the ideas they produce — extension, solidity, shape, number, motion. A round ball produces the idea of roundness; the roundness is in the ball.
- **Secondary qualities**: those in bodies that produce ideas that *don't* resemble the qualities — colors, sounds, tastes, temperatures. These are powers in objects to produce sensations in minds; the redness is in the mind, not in the apple.

The primary/secondary distinction raises the **veil of perception** problem: if we immediately perceive only ideas (sense representations) and not objects directly, how do we know that our primary-quality ideas resemble the actual properties of bodies? Locke asserts the resemblance for primary qualities but cannot prove it; Berkeley and Hume will push on this.

### The Limits of Knowledge

Locke is remarkable for his modesty about the extent of human knowledge. Knowledge, he holds, is the perception of the agreement or disagreement of ideas — and most of the time we fall far short of genuine knowledge, having instead only judgment and probability. The consequence: on most matters, including theology, natural philosophy, and ethics, we must tolerate uncertainty and difference of opinion.

This epistemological modesty feeds directly into Locke's political philosophy: if no one has certain knowledge of the one true religion, states have no authority to enforce religious conformity. The *Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) argues for separation of church and state and freedom of religious practice (with contested exceptions for Catholics and atheists, reflecting historical anxieties rather than principled limits).

### Political Philosophy: Consent, Property, and Revolution

The *Two Treatises* are a direct response to Robert Filmer's *Patriarcha* — the royalist argument that kings inherit absolute authority from Adam. Locke's alternative:

**State of nature**: before political society, humans live in a state of nature — not Hobbes's war of all against all, because the **law of nature** (discernible by reason) establishes natural rights: life, liberty, and property. The law of nature requires that no one harm another in their natural rights.

**Property**: the foundation of individual rights to property: my body is mine; my labor is mine; when I mix my labor with an unowned natural resource, I make it mine. This **labor theory of property** is influential and widely contested (why does mixing give ownership? why doesn't mixing lose rather than gain?). With money, unlimited accumulation becomes possible (money doesn't spoil), creating the inequality we find in civil society.

**Social contract**: people enter political society by consent, transferring to government the power to enforce natural rights — but *not* absolute power. Government's authority is conditional: if it violates natural rights rather than protecting them, **the right of revolution** arises. This is directly subversive of divine-right monarchy.

Locke's political theory defines the liberal framework: natural rights, government by consent, limited state power, rule of law, toleration. Its influence runs through Montesquieu to the American Constitution, through Jefferson ("life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" — Lockean list with "happiness" replacing "property") to contemporary liberal political philosophy.

## 2. Berkeley: Idealism as Empiricism

**George Berkeley** (1685–1753), Bishop of Cloyne, is the most radical and most misunderstood of the empiricists. His conclusion — that there is no mind-independent material world, that "to be is to be perceived" (*esse est percipi*) — strikes most first readers as obviously absurd. Johnson famously "refuted" Berkeley by kicking a stone. But Berkeley's arguments are more powerful than they appear, and the idealist tradition he inaugurates runs through Kant and German Idealism to contemporary phenomenology and philosophy of mind.

### The Argument for Idealism

Berkeley accepts Locke's empiricism fully: all we ever immediately perceive are ideas. But he attacks the primary/secondary quality distinction:

1. The argument against primary qualities: Locke holds that secondary qualities (colors, etc.) are ideas in the mind, not properties in bodies — because they vary with the perceiver's condition. But primary qualities (size, shape, motion) *also* vary with the perceiver's condition. A tower looks smaller from a distance; a moving hand in cold water feels different than in warm water. If variation with the perceiver disqualifies secondary qualities from being in objects, it equally disqualifies primary qualities.

2. The conceivability argument: Locke holds that primary qualities resemble the ideas they produce. But we can only compare ideas to other ideas, never to non-ideas. The notion of an external world "resembling" our ideas is incoherent — what would it mean for a shape-in-the-world (not perceived) to resemble a shape-idea (perceived)?

3. The abstraction argument: the idea of matter-as-such (extension, shape without any particular color, temperature, etc.) is an abstracted idea. Berkeley argues (following his critique of abstract ideas) that no such idea is genuinely coherent — we cannot conceive of extension without any color.

The conclusion: there is no coherent notion of matter as something existing independently of all perception. What exists are **minds** (*spirits*) and **ideas**. Material objects are collections of ideas regularly produced in minds — by God for the ordinary world; by God in waking coherent experience (the difference between hallucination and perception is the coherence and lawfulness of sensory experience, which God guarantees). This is the esse est percipi: to be an object is to be perceived (or to be a perceiver).

The advantages claimed for idealism: it eliminates the veil of perception (there is no veil — we do perceive objects, because objects ARE idea-collections); it eliminates the interaction problem (no mind-body interaction, only God and minds); it vindicates science (the laws of nature are God's consistent language for communicating with minds); it refutes skepticism about the external world (the external world IS ideas, and ideas are certain).

The objection Berkeley cannot avoid: how is the regularity of sensory experience explained without positing God? And can we verify that God (rather than some other mechanism or the mind's own activity) is producing the regular idea sequences? Berkeley's God is doing exactly the work that Descartes's matter did — providing a stable, lawful, intersubjective world — but now without material substance.

## 3. Hume: The Limits of Reason and the Science of Human Nature

**David Hume** (1711–1776) is the most important empiricist and, in many philosophers' judgment, the most important philosopher in the English language. His *Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40), *Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748), *Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751), and *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* (published posthumously 1779) pursue empiricism to its logical conclusions so ruthlessly that the results constitute a philosophical crisis — one that Kant describes as waking him from "dogmatic slumber."

### The Theory of Ideas

Hume follows Locke in grounding all knowledge in experience. He distinguishes:
- **Impressions**: vivid, forceful perceptions — sensations from the world or passions/emotions from within.
- **Ideas**: fainter copies of impressions — thoughts, memories, imaginings.

The **copy principle**: all simple ideas are derived from impressions they copy. Any idea that cannot be traced to an underlying impression is spurious — the word it expresses has no genuine content. This is Hume's empiricist criterion of meaning, a more radical version of Locke's program.

### The Problem of Causation

Hume's most influential and devastating analysis is of causation — the concept on which all reasoning about matters of fact and all natural science depend. We believe that cause necessarily produces effect; that given the cause, the effect must follow. Where does this idea of *necessity* come from?

Hume examines cases of apparent causation and finds three things:
1. **Priority**: the cause precedes the effect.
2. **Contiguity**: cause and effect are spatially/temporally adjacent.
3. **Constant conjunction**: causes are always followed by effects (fire always produces heat).

What he does not find: **necessary connection** — any observable feature of the world (impression) corresponding to the idea that the cause must produce the effect. We see the billiard ball strike; we see the other billiard ball move. We do not see any force or necessity passing between them.

The origin of the idea of necessary connection: it is not, as we thought, a feature of the external world but a feature of **the mind itself**. After regularly observing conjunction, the mind comes to expect the effect when it perceives the cause; this expectation — this feeling of the mind being carried forward from cause to effect — is the impression from which the idea of necessary connection is derived. Causation is **constant conjunction + customary transition in the mind**. It is a natural belief formed by habit, not a rational inference from what we observe.

This has devastating consequences:
- **No justified belief in the uniformity of nature**: the belief that the future will resemble the past is neither demonstrably true nor empirically justified — justifying it empirically would be circular (assuming the future resembles the past in order to prove that the future resembles the past). **The problem of induction** is here stated in its definitive form.
- **No necessary causal connection**: science discovers constant conjunctions, not real necessities. What Leibniz called the PSR — there must be a sufficient reason why things are as they are — is groundless; things might have been otherwise.
- **No justified cosmological argument**: the argument from causation to God's existence relies on the principle that everything has a cause (why not the universe?); and on the assumption that causal reasoning can extend to what lies beyond all possible experience (why?).

### The Self: Bundle Theory

Locke and Descartes assumed a persistent substantial self — a thing that does the experiencing, thinking, and willing. Hume applies the copy principle: what impression does the idea of "the self" derive from? When I introspect, I find only a succession of perceptions — pain, pleasure, thoughts, sensations — following each other rapidly. I never find a persistent "self" behind them; just the bundle.

**Bundle theory**: the self is not a substance distinct from its experiences but a bundle or collection of perceptions. Personal identity over time is a matter of psychological continuity and natural tendency to regard connected perceptions as belonging to one thing — a fiction that is useful and natural but not metaphysically substantiated.

This prefigures Buddhist no-self doctrine (which Hume may have known through Jesuit reports from Asia), Mach's neutral monism, the narrative theory of self (Ricoeur, MacIntyre — the self is constituted by narrative rather than by substance), and contemporary cognitive science (there is no unified "I" in the brain; the sense of unified selfhood is constructed, not found).

### Moral Philosophy: Sentiment over Reason

The *Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* and the *Treatise* Book III make the central empiricist claim in ethics: moral judgments are not rational inferences but expressions of **sentiment** — specifically, of moral approval and disapproval, which are a form of feeling (related to sympathy and sentiment of humanity) rather than a rational perception of moral facts.

**Is-ought gap** (*Treatise* Book III, Section i): Hume notices that most moral arguments move from premises about what *is* the case to conclusions about what *ought* to be done, without explaining why "is" implies "ought." The gap between descriptive and normative claims must be explicitly bridged; it cannot be assumed.

This observation defines the **fact-value distinction** and motivates much subsequent ethical theory: naturalists (G.E. Moore's open question argument), non-cognitivists (moral statements express attitudes, not describe facts), and constructivists (Kant, Rawls — norms are constructed by rational agents, not read off nature) all respond to it.

**Virtue ethics**: despite the anti-rationalist framework, Hume's ethics is not nihilistic. The *Enquiry* argues that virtues are qualities useful or agreeable to the person and to others — a naturalistic account of why we approve what we approve. This is more empirically adequate than Kant's purely rational ethics, though it loses the categorical force of moral claims.

### Philosophy of Religion

The *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* present three characters — Cleanthes (the design argument theist), Demea (orthodox Christian), and Philo (Hume's mouthpiece) — debating the design argument (the universe resembles an artifact, therefore it has a designer). Philo's critique is methodologically impeccable:
- The analogy between the universe and a human artifact is too weak — we have no experience of universe-making.
- The evidence could equally support a finite, incompetent, or multiple-designer theory.
- The problem of evil: an omnipotent, omniscient, omnibenevolent God would not create a world with the suffering actually found in nature.

The *Dialogues* effectively end the design argument as a piece of philosophy until its 20th-century reformulation as the fine-tuning argument.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

The empiricist tradition's central epistemological crisis is Hume's skeptical results: we cannot justify the uniformity of nature, the necessary connection of cause and effect, the existence of the external world, or the persistence of the self — and we cannot find a non-circular route out of these conclusions from experience alone.

The philosophical situation after Hume: either accept the skepticism (as Hume himself does, cheerfully — we live by custom and natural belief, not by justified rational inference), or reconstruct the foundations of knowledge. Kant's critical philosophy is the reconstruction attempt; it begins from Hume's problem and attempts to show how synthetic a priori knowledge is possible — how the understanding can impose necessary structures on experience without deriving those structures from experience.

### Language and meaning

The empiricist theory of meaning — all genuine ideas are derived from impressions — is the earliest systematic version of what will become the logical positivists' **verification criterion of meaning**: a sentence is meaningful only if there is an empirical procedure for determining its truth or falsity. Hume's application of his criterion is radical: the idea of necessary causation, the substantial self, and God all fail the test; the sentences ostensibly expressing them are meaningless or at least empty.

This criterion of meaning becomes the tool of early analytic philosophy (Russell, the Vienna Circle, Ayer's *Language, Truth and Logic*) for distinguishing genuine claims from metaphysical pseudo-problems. Its self-refutation (the verification criterion is not itself verifiable) becomes the locus of its eventual abandonment by the late Wittgenstein and by Quine.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Hume grounds ethics in sentiment and naturalizes moral psychology. The Humean tradition in ethics (emotivism, prescriptivism, expressivism, Humean motivational internalism) holds that moral judgments are fundamentally conative rather than purely cognitive — they express and motivate, not merely describe.

Locke's political philosophy remains the canonical statement of liberal rights-based politics. The Lockean social contract + natural rights framework defines constitutional democracy; its influence on the American and French revolutions makes it arguably the most practically consequential philosophical tradition.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

Hume's critique of causation, induction, and the cosmological/design arguments constitutes the definitive empiricist challenge to the rationalist ambition of a science-supporting metaphysics. His results: the foundations of science are in habit and natural belief, not in demonstrative reason; the metaphysical claims of theology cannot be sustained; and reasonable persons should embrace a modest, experience-bounded epistemology.

This Humean naturalism about science — science proceeds by the observation of regularities, without access to underlying necessities — anticipates the philosophy of science of Ernst Mach and logical positivism and remains influential in contemporary anti-metaphysical naturalism (Quine's naturalized epistemology).

## The Arc Forward

The empiricist tradition leaves Kant with a precisely articulated crisis:
- Experience (Hume shows) cannot generate necessary truth — all it yields is constant conjunction and habit.
- Reason alone (the rationalists showed) tends to overreach — applying concepts beyond the limits of possible experience generates the antinomies and paralogisms Kant will analyze.

Kant's task: find a middle path — neither pure rationalism (reason legislating independent of experience) nor pure empiricism (experience without rational structure). His answer: the mind imposes necessary *structure* (categories of the understanding, forms of intuition — space and time) on raw experience, yielding synthetic a priori knowledge of the phenomenal world. But this structure is only available *within* the domain of possible experience — it does not extend to things in themselves (the noumenon). The Critique of Pure Reason (1781) is written as a response to precisely the impasse that rationalism and empiricism have reached.
