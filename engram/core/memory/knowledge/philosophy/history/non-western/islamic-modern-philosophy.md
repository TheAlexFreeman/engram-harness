# Islamic Philosophy in the Modern Period (and Broader Revisit)

The medieval Islamic philosophical tradition (Al-Kindi, Al-Farabi, Avicenna, Averroes, Al-Ghazali) was surveyed in the earlier file on transmission to the medieval West. This file takes a broader view: (1) the later classical tradition within Islamic philosophy that the earlier file underemphasized; (2) the encounter with Western modernity from the 19th century onward; (3) contemporary Islamic philosophy and theology; and (4) the genuinely underappreciated extent to which Islamic philosophy independently developed ideas later appearing in Western philosophy.

---

## 1. The Later Classical Tradition

### Suhrawardi and the Illuminationist School (12th century CE)

Al-Suhrawardi (Shahab al-Din Yahya Suhrawardi, 1154–1191 CE) founded the school of Ishrāq (Illuminationism) — a synthesis of Platonic light-metaphysics, Zoroastrian cosmology, and Sufi mysticism with the Neoplatonic-Aristotelian heritage of Islamic philosophy.

Against the Aristotelian primacy of essence: Suhrawardi holds that light (Nūr) is the fundamental ontological category — pure self-manifestation, self-presence, self-luminosity. The cosmos is structured as a hierarchy of lights, from the Light of Lights (God) down through intelligences, souls, and material things (which have light only insofar as they are illuminated from above). Knowledge is not abstraction (the Aristotelian-Avicennan model) but the self-presence of light to light — direct illuminative knowledge (*ishrāqi ilm*) in which the knowing subject and the known object are modes of a single luminous self-manifestation.

This is philosophically significant: Suhrawardi's critique of the Aristotelian-Peripatetic model of knowledge, and his positive account of self-luminous awareness, anticipates arguments appearing much later in Western philosophy (the phenomenological critique of representation; Brentano's intentionality; and even arguments about the irreducibility of first-person perspective in consciousness studies). Whether this is convergence or influence would require detailed historical tracing.

### Mulla Sadra and Transcendent Wisdom (17th century CE)

Sadr al-Din Muhammad Shirazi (Mulla Sadra, 1572–1640 CE) is the culminating figure of Islamic philosophy's classical period. His *Asfar* (The Four Journeys) undertakes a synthesis of the entire tradition — Avicennan philosophy, Suhrawardian Illuminationism, Ibn Arabi's Sufi metaphysics, and Quranic theology — around a new foundational insight.

**Primacy of existence** (*aṣālat al-wujūd*): against Avicenna's (implicit) primacy of essence, Mulla Sadra argues that existence is the primary ontological category — essences are abstractions from the concrete act of existing. This is closely comparable to Heidegger's distinction between Being and beings, and to Sartrean existentialism's "existence precedes essence." Mulla Sadra arrives at this conclusion three hundred years before Heidegger, in a completely different cultural context.

**The intensification of existence** (*tashkīk al-wujūd*): existence is not univocal (the same in all instances) but graded — different things participate more or less intensely in being. The universe is a dynamic continuum of being-intensification, from the most attenuated (pure matter) to the most intense (God as the pure, self-subsistent Act of Being). This is a process ontology — being as dynamic, not static — closer to Whitehead or Bergson than to scholastic substance metaphysics.

**Substantial motion** (*ḥarakat jawhariyya*): against the Aristotelian-Scholastic view that substances (the ultimate subjects of predication) are static and change only accidentally, Mulla Sadra holds that substances themselves are in constant motion — existence is a process of becoming, not a static state. This is a remarkable anticipation of process philosophy and of Bergson's *durée*.

### Ibn Arabi and the Unity of Being (12th–13th century CE)

Muhyi al-Din Ibn Arabi (1165–1240 CE) is the greatest systematic mystic-philosopher in the Islamic tradition — his *Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam* (Bezels of Wisdom) and *Futūhāt al-Makkiyya* (Meccan Openings) are among the most complex texts in any tradition.

**Waḥdat al-Wujūd** (Unity of Being / Oneness of Existence): there is only one Being — God's Being. The cosmos is not created out of nothing and placed next to God; it is a self-unfolding of the Divine Being in forms that are not exactly the same as the Divine (which is beyond all determination) and not exactly other than the Divine (since only Being is). Individual things exist as *theophanies* (self-manifestations, *tajalliyyāt*) of the Divine Being through the infinite number of possible "forms" (*ṣuwar*).

This doctrine was controversially close to pantheism (Spinoza's *Deus sive Natura* is the closest Western parallel) and was criticized by orthodox Islamic theology. Ibn Arabi's defense: God's transcendence is maintained because the Divine Essence (*al-Dhāt*) is beyond all manifestation — it is in the self-manifestations, not the Essence itself, that the cosmos consists.

## 2. The Modern Encounter with Western Philosophy

### The Challenge of Modernity

From the 18th century onward, Islamic civilization faced the challenge of Western scientific, technological, and eventually military dominance. The philosophical questions this raised: (1) What is the relationship between Islam and Western modernity (science, democracy, individual rights)? (2) Is the Islamic philosophical heritage a resource for engaging with or critiquing modernity, or an obstacle? (3) What is the appropriate relationship between reason and revelation in Islamic thought?

Different responses generate different intellectual-political movements:

### Islamic Modernism (19th–early 20th century)

**Jamal al-Din al-Afghani** (1838–1897): pan-Islamic unification as the response to Western colonial dominance; emphasis on reason (*ʿaql*) within Islam; critique of both uncritical traditionalism and uncritical Westernization. Al-Afghani wrote a response to Ernest Renan's claim that Islam is inherently hostile to science — pointing out that European science built on Islamic foundations.

**Muhammad Abduh** (1849–1905, Egypt): the restoration of reason in Islamic thought — arguing that the Quranic tradition is compatible with modern science and that the apparent conflict between Islam and reason is a historical distortion. A key reformist claim: the "closing of the gates of *ijtihad*" (independent legal reasoning) was a historical contingency, not a theological necessity — rational interpretation of Islamic sources is legitimately required.

**Muhammad Iqbal** (1877–1938, India/Pakistan): the most philosophically sophisticated Islamic modernist, trained in Western philosophy (Bergson, Hegel, Nietzsche). His *Reconstruction of Religious Thought in Islam* argues for a synthesis of Islamic philosophical spirituality with what he takes to be the most advanced insights of Western philosophy: the dynamic, creative self (influenced by Bergson and Nietzsche), operating within a relational cosmos, oriented toward active engagement with the world rather than passive contemplation. Iqbal's engagement with Bergson is particularly illuminating: he finds in Bergson's *élan vital* a Western philosophical articulation of what the Quran calls the divine creative breath (*nafkha rūh*).

### Islamic Traditionalism / Perennialist Philosophy

**Rene Guenon** (1886–1951, French convert to Islam) and **Seyyed Hossein Nasr** (born 1933, Iranian philosopher) represent a traditionalist-perennialist current: there is an eternal wisdom (*primordial tradition / sophia perennis*) that underlies all authentic religious traditions; modernity is a deviation from this universal wisdom; the Islamic philosophical tradition (especially Suhrawardi and Mulla Sadra) preserves resources that Western modernity has lost.

Nasr's work is also significant for its sustained critique of the ecological and spiritual consequences of the Western scientific worldview — the mechanization of nature is not a neutral project but an act of ontological violence. This places him in dialogue with Heidegger's critique of technology and with environmental philosophy.

## 3. Contemporary Islamic Philosophy

### The Revival of Islamic Philosophy in Iran

The Islamic Revolution in Iran (1979) created a context in which Islamic philosophy — especially Mulla Sadra and Suhrawardi — became state-sponsored, academically institutionalized, and politically inflected. Contemporary Iranian Islamic philosophy includes sophisticated engagement with Western philosophy (Heidegger in particular has been enormously influential — Dariush Shayegan's comparative work; Ahmad Fardid's critique of Western modernity from a Heideggerian-Islamic perspective).

The ambivalence: Heidegger's critique of Western modernity and its technological-metaphysical drive is taken as support for anti-Western political positions — an alignment whose intellectual legitimacy is contested.

### Political Philosophy: Democracy, Rights, and the Islamic State

The most practically urgent debates in contemporary Islamic philosophy concern political philosophy:

**Theocratic vs. democratic**: the *wilāyat-e faqīh* doctrine (guardianship of the jurist, the theoretical basis of the Iranian political system) holds that in the absence of the Imam, religious jurists have authority over temporal government. This is controversial within Islamic jurisprudence.

**Islam and human rights**: the relationship between the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (grounded in natural law / liberal political philosophy) and Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*). Multiple positions: (a) Islamic law is compatible with universal human rights properly interpreted; (b) Islamic law is an alternative foundation for human rights; (c) there is genuine conflict that requires either reform of Islamic law or critique of Western liberal assumptions.

**Abdolkarim Soroush** (born 1945, Iranian philosopher): the most important contemporary Muslim philosopher of democracy. His distinction between the essence of religion (unchanging) and the interpretation of religion (historically conditioned and revisable): the *shari'a* (Islamic law) as we have it is a human interpretation, fallible and improvable, not the direct word of God. Democratic governance and pluralism are compatible with authentic Islamic faith; indeed, they are required by intellectual honesty once we recognize the historical conditionedness of all interpretation.

## 4. What Islamic Philosophy Independently Developed

One of the aims of this file is to resist the narrative in which Islamic philosophy is merely the conduit for Greek ideas to the West, with nothing original to add.

**Avicennan modal logic and metaphysics**: the distinction between necessary, possible, and impossible existence — and the argument that there must be a Necessary Being as the ground of all contingent existence — is developed by Avicenna with logical sophistication that goes beyond anything in Aristotle. The parallel with Leibniz's cosmological argument (which independently uses modal categories) illustrates the parallel development.

**The floating man thought experiment (Avicenna)**: a thought experiment in which a newly created person floats in space, deprived of all sensory input, is asked whether they would still be aware of their own existence — and the answer is yes, they would be aware of themselves as a simple, unified subject. This anticipates Descartes' *cogito* and is a precursor to phenomenological arguments for the irreducibility of first-person awareness. Avicenna probably worked this out in the 11th century, independent of Descartes (17th century) and definitively independent of Husserl (20th century).

**Al-Ghazali's occasionalism**: the *Incoherence of the Philosophers* argues against the Aristotelian-Avicennan doctrine of necessary causal efficacy in nature — causes do not necessitate their effects by their own nature; all apparent causal connections are maintained by God's direct action (occasionalism). This position (also held by Malebranche in the 17th century West) raises the same questions about causation that Hume raises from a completely different direction: what is the ground of our confidence in causal connections?

**Illuminationist philosophy of mind** (Suhrawardi): self-luminous awareness as philosophically fundamental, distinct from and irreducible to the Aristotelian model of knowledge as abstraction — this anticipates phenomenological themes centuries before Brentano and Husserl.

**Mulla Sadra's primacy of existence and substantial motion**: as noted above, these positions are philosophically comparable to Heidegger, Bergson, and process philosophy — but arrived at in a completely different conceptual-historical context.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

The debate in Islamic philosophy between Avicennan representationalism (knowledge as abstraction of form from matter, culminating in the Active Intellect) and Suhrawardian Illuminationism (knowledge as self-luminous presence) generates an epistemological polarization comparable to the analytic/continental divide: the representationalist tradition (which also runs through Averroes into Latin Scholasticism and thence into Descartes and the modern epistemological tradition) vs. the presence/immediacy tradition (which runs through Suhrawardi, Ibn Arabi, Mulla Sadra, and in some respects connects to phenomenology).

Avicenna's **floating man** is the clearest example of an Islamic philosophical contribution to what became the Western mind-body debate: the first-person awareness of self cannot be accounted for by the body or its senses — there is a formal unity of self-awareness that is not constituted by sense-data. This is an independently developed argument for the irreducibility of the first-person perspective.

### Language and meaning

Islamic philosophy's approach to language is primarily hermeneutical (how to interpret the Quran and *hadith*) and jurisprudential (*usul al-fiqh*, the principles of Islamic legal interpretation). The most sophisticated hermeneutical theory is developed by Medieval Islamic jurists, who create elaborate systems for dealing with ambiguity, specification, general principles, conflicts between sources — comparable in some ways to modern legal philosophy and to the philosophical hermeneutics of Gadamer.

The *kalam* tradition (Islamic speculative theology) develops technical metalanguage for theological argument comparable to that developed in Christian Scholasticism — and some of the same logical tools.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

The central ethical concepts of Islamic philosophy:
- **Tawḥīd** (divine unity): the affirmation of God's oneness as the foundation of all ethical obligation — the human being is God's *khalīfa* (steward/vicegerent) in the world, responsible for justice and the maintenance of the created order.
- **Maṣlaḥa** (public welfare/interest): in Islamic jurisprudence, the concept that law should serve human welfare — used by reformists to justify adaptation of Islamic law to modern conditions.
- **ʿAql** (reason): the role of reason in ethics — Muʿtazilite rationalism (reason independently establishes the good; God commands what is good *because* it is good) vs. Ashʿarite voluntarism (what is good is what God commands; there is no independent rational standard). This exactly parallels the Euthyphro dilemma in Plato and the debates about divine command theory in Western philosophical theology.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

The relationship between philosophy (*falsafa* — Greek-based rational inquiry) and theology (*kalam* — Quranic-based speculative theology) is the central institutional tension in the Islamic intellectual tradition. Al-Ghazali's *Incoherence of the Philosophers* represents orthodox theology's challenge to philosophy; Averroes's *Incoherence of the Incoherence* is philosophy's response. The outcome: in the Sunni heartland, formal *falsafa* largely ceased as a living discipline after Al-Ghazali; in Shiite Persia, it survived and flourished (Mulla Sadra).

The relationship between Islamic philosophy and modern science: Nasr's traditionalist critique holds that modern science, in desacralizing nature, has made an ontological error with catastrophic consequences. The more prevalent Islamic modernist position: modern science studies natural regularities; this is compatible with theism; the Quran encourages investigation of nature as signs (*āyāt*) of God.

## The Arc Forward

Contemporary Islamic philosophy continues to develop across multiple registers:

- **Academic Islamic philosophy** in Western universities: Oliver Leaman, Seyyed Hossein Nasr (Harvard), Fazlur Rahman, Zailan Moris.
- **Continental encounters**: Heidegger in the Muslim world (Dariush Shayegan, Ahmad Fardid, Hamid Dabashi); Derrida's engagement with religion; Soroush's democratic theory.
- **Comparative philosophy proper**: bringing Islamic philosophy into genuine engagement with Western and Indian philosophy — not as a curiosity but as an equal interlocutor (Nasr, Leaman, Adamson).
- **Islamic bioethics, environmental ethics, AI ethics**: applying Islamic jurisprudential and philosophical resources to contemporary practical problems.
