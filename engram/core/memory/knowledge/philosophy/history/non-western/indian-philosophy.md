# Indian Philosophy: From the Vedas to Contemporary Thought

Indian philosophy is among the oldest sustained intellectual traditions in the world, beginning roughly with the Rigveda (~1500 BCE) and continuing uninterrupted to the present. It encompasses a range of positions that overlap with all four of the through-lines: rigorous epistemology (Nyaya, Buddhist logic), sophisticated metaphysics (Vedanta, Madhyamaka), systematic ethics (Dharmaśāstra, Buddhist ethics), and philosophy of mind (Yogacara, Samkhya). Indian philosophers independently developed notions of atomism, linguistic theory, formal logic, and theories of consciousness that are richly comparable — not reducible to — their Western counterparts.

The tradition is internally diverse and marked by intense debate among schools. The classical "orthodox" (āstika) schools accept the authority of the Vedas; the "heterodox" (nāstika) schools — Buddhism, Jainism, Cārvāka — do not. This distinction shapes the debates, though all schools share many philosophical preoccupations.

## 1. Vedic and Upanishadic Foundations

### The Vedic Worldview

The four Vedas (Rigveda, Samaveda, Yajurveda, Atharvaveda, ~1500–800 BCE) are ritual texts organized around the sacrifice (*yajña*) as cosmogonic act — the re-enactment and maintenance of cosmic order (*ṛta*). The gods (devas) and the cosmic order are not external to humanity but are sustained by human ritual action. *Dharma* (righteous order, moral and cosmic law) and *karma* (action and its consequences) emerge here as foundational concepts.

### Upanishadic Philosophy

The Upanishads (~800–200 BCE) represent a philosophical turn within and beyond Vedic ritual: what is the permanent ground of the self and of the cosmos? Two concepts become central:

**Ātman**: the self, understood progressively more abstractly. In the Chandogya Upanishad, Yajnavalkya leads his student Maitreyi through a series of identifications — the waking self, the dreaming self, the sleeping self — to the conclusion that the real self is a pure witnessing awareness that cannot be grasped as an object because it is the subject of all grasping. "It is not this, not this" (*neti neti*).

**Brahman**: the ultimate ground of all existence — not the Vedic creator-god but the impersonal, infinite, non-dual ground of being-consciousness-bliss (*sat-cit-ānanda*). The central Upanishadic formula: *tat tvam asi* ("that art thou") — Brahman and ātman are one. Consciousness and the ground of the universe are the same. Individual consciousness is not separate from cosmic consciousness; the apparent separateness is *avidyā* (ignorance).

This is not mysticism unsupported by argument — the Upanishadic dialogues are philosophical exchanges in which the identity claim is *argued*, using methods analogous to thought experiments and reductio ad absurdum.

## 2. The Six Orthodox Schools (Ṣaḍdarśanas)

### Nyāya

Nyāya (founded by Akṣapāda Gautama, ~2nd century CE, but with much older antecedents) is the school of logic and epistemology. Its primary concern: what constitutes a valid source of knowledge (*pramāṇa*)?

Four pramāṇas: *pratyakṣa* (perception), *anumāna* (inference), *upamāna* (comparison/analogy), and *śabda* (testimony — including Vedic testimony). Of these, inference receives the most systematic treatment.

The **Nyāya syllogism** has five members (unlike the Aristotelian three): *pratijñā* (statement of what is to be proved), *hetu* (reason), *udāharaṇa* (general example — the vyāpti/universal concomitance), *upanaya* (application), *nigamana* (conclusion). Example: "There is fire on the hill (pratijñā) / because there is smoke (hetu) / wherever there is smoke there is fire, as in a kitchen (udāharaṇa) / this hill has smoke (upanaya) / therefore there is fire on the hill (nigamana)."

**Vyāpti** (universal concomitance, invariable accompaniment) is the crucial concept — the relationship between the inferential sign (smoke) and the inferred property (fire). Nyāya analyzes the conditions under which vyāpti is established, and Buddhist logicians (Dharmakīrti) mount the most devastating objection: how do we establish universal concomitance from finite observations? This is the Indian problem of induction, argued with remarkable sophistication.

Navya-Nyāya (new Nyāya, from Gaṅgeśa, ~12th century, through 16th-17th century Bengal) develops a sophisticated formal metalanguage — a near-formal logic — to analyze inference, property-relations, and the anumāna schema. This tradition is arguably the most technically sophisticated logic in any tradition prior to Frege.

### Vaiśeṣika

Vaiśeṣika (Kaṇāda, ~2nd–1st century BCE) is the school of ontological categories and atomic theory. Its ontology posits six (later seven) categories: *dravya* (substance), *guṇa* (quality), *karma* (motion/action), *sāmānya* (generality/universal), *viśeṣa* (particularity), *samavāya* (inherence). Matter is ultimately composed of eternal, partless atoms (*paramāṇu*) — different for each element (earth, water, fire, air). The universe is produced by atomic combination.

### Sāṃkhya-Yoga

Sāṃkhya (systematized by Iśvarakṛṣṇa, ~3rd–4th century CE) is dualist: reality consists of two ultimate categories — *puruṣa* (pure consciousness, witness, completely inactive) and *prakṛti* (primordial nature, matter-energy in its unconfigured state). The cosmos emerges from the interaction of puruṣa and prakṛti — prakṛti, in the presence of puruṣa's witnessing, generates the universe through a sequence of emanations.

Prakṛti is composed of three *guṇas* (strands/qualities): *sattva* (clarity, luminosity, intelligence), *rajas* (activity, passion, agitation), *tamas* (inertia, darkness, mass). All psychological and physical phenomena are combinations of the three guṇas in varying proportions.

**Yoga** (Patañjali's *Yoga Sūtras*, ~3rd–4th century CE) is the practice-oriented companion to Sāṃkhya: through systematic practice (the eight-limbed path: yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma, pratyāhāra, dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi), the mind's modifications (*vṛttis*) are stilled, and puruṣa's pure witnessing nature is revealed. The psychological analysis of mental modifications in the Yoga Sūtras is remarkably empirical and systematic.

### Mīmāṃsā

Mīmāṃsā (Jaimini, Prabhākara, Kumārila Bhaṭṭa) is the hermeneutical school: its primary concern is the correct interpretation of Vedic injunctions (primarily ritual injunctions). This makes it the school most directly concerned with language and meaning. Key doctrine: the **eternal validity of the Vedas** (*apauruṣeyatva*) — the Vedas were not composed by any person and are self-validating. Language itself is eternal (*Sphota* doctrine in the grammatical tradition of Bhartṛhari — the meaning-bearer is a unitary whole, not a sequence of sounds).

Kumārila's epistemology: *intrinsic validity* (*svataḥ prāmāṇya*) — knowledge is self-certifying unless specifically invalidated. This is a strong foundationalist position that Nyāya and Buddhist epistemologists challenge.

### Vedānta

Vedānta ("end of the Vedas" — the Upanishads and their commentaries) becomes the dominant Hindu philosophical tradition, encompassing the most systematic development of the Upanishadic insights.

**Advaita Vedānta** (Śaṅkara, 8th century CE) is the most radical non-dual school. Main theses:
- Only Brahman truly exists (*sat*) — infinite, non-dual, consciousness.
- The phenomenal world has a lower ontological status: it is *vyāvahārika satya* (practical/conventional reality) but not *pāramārthika satya* (ultimate reality).
- The appearance of individual selves (*jīva*) and the phenomenal world is *māyā* — not simply "illusion" (things appear) but a reality of a lower kind than Brahman. Māyā is the power of Brahman to appear as multiplicity.
- *Avidyā* (ignorance) superimposes (*adhyāsa*) the qualities of non-self onto the self and vice versa — the self is taken to be the body/mind complex; the body/mind complex is taken to have the self's pure witnessing nature.
- Liberation (*mokṣa*) is the removal of avidyā through study (*śravana*), reflection (*manana*), and meditation (*nididhyāsana*) on the Upanishadic great sayings (*mahāvākya*), especially "Brahman is consciousness" (*prajñānam brahma*) and *tat tvam asi*.

**Viśiṣṭādvaita** (Rāmānuja, 11th–12th century CE) qualifies Śaṅkara: Brahman is one but qualified by the individual selves and matter, which are real and constitute Brahman's body. This preserves a real plurality within ultimate unity, and allows for a personal relationship between the individual and God (Viṣṇu/Nārāyaṇa).

**Dvaita** (Madhva, 13th century CE) is emphatically dualist: God (Viṣṇu), individual selves (jīvas), and matter are all ultimately real and distinct. This is the most theist of the Vedānta schools.

## 3. Buddhist Philosophy

Buddhism's philosophical tradition is extraordinarily rich and internally diverse, engaging all four through-lines with sustained rigor. Only the main philosophical schools can be sketched here.

### The Early Teaching

The Buddha's philosophical contribution: the doctrine of *anātman* (no-self) — what we ordinarily take to be a permanent self is in fact a collection of five *skandhas* (aggregates: form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness) in constant flux. There is no permanent substance underlying experience.

*Dependent origination* (*pratītyasamutpāda*): all phenomena arise in dependence on causes and conditions. Nothing exists independently, from its own side; all phenomena are relational.

The four noble truths and the eightfold path constitute the practical-ethical core.

### Madhyamaka (Nāgārjuna, ~2nd century CE)

Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* is among the most argued-about philosophical texts in any tradition. Main doctrine: **śūnyatā** (emptiness). All phenomena are empty of *svabhāva* (own-nature, intrinsic existence, inherent existence). This refines the early Buddhist anātman doctrine: not just persons but *all phenomena* (including causation, motion, time, Nirvāṇa itself) lack inherent existence.

The argument proceeds through systematic *prasaṅga* (reductio ad absurdum): if motion had inherent existence, it would have to be in the mover but not in the moved, or in neither, or in both — each option generates contradiction. If causation had inherent existence, causes would have to be the same as effects or different — each option generates contradiction. Therefore nothing has inherent existence; everything is empty.

This is *not* nihilism — Nāgārjuna insists on the **two truths**: *saṃvṛti satya* (conventional truth, in which things exist, function, and causally interact) and *paramārtha satya* (ultimate truth, in which things are empty of inherent existence). Conventional truth and ultimate truth are complementary, not competing; emptiness is the emptiness of inherent existence, not the emptiness of conventional existence.

The connection to Advaita Vedānta's two-truth theory and the connection to Derrida's deconstruction (Derrida was aware of Buddhist philosophy and noted both convergences and divergences) are among the most philosophically interesting cross-tradition questions.

### Yogācāra (Asaṅga and Vasubandhu, 4th–5th century CE)

The Yogācāra ("mind-only") school holds that the objects of experience are not external to consciousness — all phenomena arise within consciousness. The foundational doctrine: *vijñaptimātratā* — "only representations" or "only consciousness." External objects as such are not established; what exists is the experiential presentation within consciousness.

This is not solipsism: there is a shared intersubjective reality, accounted for by the **ālayavijñāna** (storehouse consciousness) — a foundational layer of consciousness (controversial: is this a self? the later Mādhyamika critics argue that Yogācāra smuggles back a subtle self) into which karmic impressions are deposited and from which arising experience draws.

Yogācāra Buddhist philosophy develops highly sophisticated accounts of perception, cognition, and the structure of consciousness — rivaling and in some ways surpassing the 20th-century phenomenological tradition.

### Buddhist Logic (Dignāga and Dharmakīrti, 5th–7th century CE)

Dignāga's *Pramāṇasamuccaya* and Dharmakīrti's *Pramāṇavārttika* establish a two-pramāṇa epistemology (perception and inference only — the Nyāya four are rejected) that is among the most technically rigorous epistemological systems in the pre-modern world.

*Apoha* theory (Dignāga): the meaning of a word is not a positive universals but the negation of its negation — the word "cow" means "not non-cow." Universals do not exist; they are useful cognitive constructions (*kalpanā*). Language latches onto particulars via exclusion rather than positive class-membership. This is a sophisticated nominalism with implications for semantics and cognitive science.

Dharmakīrti refines *vyāpti* theory: universal concomitance must be grounded in real causal connections (*svabhāva pratibandhā*) — either same-nature or causal relations. Without this grounding, inference would be mere correlation. This anticipates modern discussions of the connection between causation and inductive inference.

## 4. Jain Philosophy

### Anekāntavāda (Many-Sidedness)

Jain philosophy (systematized by Umāsvāti, ~2nd–5th century CE, based on Mahāvīra's teaching) is notable above all for its epistemological theory of **anekāntavāda** (many-sidedness of truth): reality is complex and can be truly described from multiple perspectives; no single perspective captures the whole truth.

**Syādvāda** (conditional predication): every statement about reality should be qualified by *syāt* ("in some respect" / "conditionally") — "in some respect, the pot exists"; "in some respect, the pot does not exist"; "in some respect, the pot both exists and does not exist"; etc. The seven-fold predication (*saptabhaṅgī*) systematizes the possible conditioned assertions.

This is not relativism or incoherence — Jain logic holds that contradictory properties can be true of the same subject in different respects. Its comparison with perspectivism (Nietzsche, Langacker's construal theory in cognitive linguistics) and with contemporary views of semantic context-sensitivity is philosophically rich.

### Ethical Theory: Ahiṃsā

Jain ethics is grounded in *ahiṃsā* (non-harming) extended to all living beings — the Jains are the most consistent practitioners of non-harm in any tradition, including meticulous avoidance of harm to insects and plants. This is grounded in the doctrine that all living beings have souls (*jīva*) with intrinsic value.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Indian philosophy's contribution to epistemology is among the most systematic in any tradition. The pramāṇa ("source of valid knowledge") framework organizes the debate: what counts as a legitimate means of knowing? Buddhist apoha theory, Nyāya syllogistic, Dharmakīrti's causal theory of perception — these engage directly (and would engage productively, if the language barrier were bridged) with contemporary cognitive science and philosophy of mind.

Yogācāra philosophy of mind presents a rich alternative to both substance dualism and physicalism: consciousness as the ground of all phenomena, with a detailed analysis of the structure of conscious experience — multiple layers of awareness, the interaction of attentive and pre-attentive processing, the storage and retrieval of experiential traces — that anticipates aspects of phenomenology and cognitive science.

The ātman/anātman debate runs through the entire tradition: is there a permanent self? Indian philosophy explores this with greater systematic depth than the Western tradition manages until Hume, and the Buddhist anātman arguments anticipate Hume's bundle theory while going significantly further.

### Language and meaning

The Sanskrit grammatical tradition (Pāṇini's *Aṣṭādhyāyī*, ~4th century BCE — the most complete grammar of any language before the modern period; Patañjali's *Mahābhāṣya*; Bhartṛhari's *Vākyapadīya*) constitutes an independent systematic philosophy of language.

Bhartṛhari's *Sphota* theory: meaning is grasped as a unitary whole (sphota), not as a sequence of sounds. The sentence-meaning (*vākya-sphota*) is primary; words and sounds are abstractions from it. This is a holistic theory of meaning closely comparable to later 20th-century holism (Quine, Davidson), arrived at from a completely different direction.

Apoha theory and its debate with rival views (Kumārila's Mīmāṃsā critiques, Dharmakīrti's defense) constitute a sustained technical debate about the semantics of general terms and the existence of universals — one of the most sophisticated debates in the history of semantics.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

The concept of *dharma* is not adequately translated as "duty" or "law" — it encompasses cosmic order, moral norm, essential nature, and social role. The *Dharmaśāstra* tradition (Manu, Yājñavalkya, etc.) systematizes legal and ethical norms; the Bhagavad Gītā is the most influential single text on the ethics of action — Kṛṣṇa's teaching to Arjuna combines elements of Sāṃkhya-Yoga, Vedānta, and a distinctive doctrine of **niṣkāma karma** (action without attachment to results) that anticipates Kant's deontological emphasis on the will rather than consequences.

Buddhist ethics: the *bodhisattva* path of Mahayana Buddhism — the commitment to the enlightenment of all beings before oneself — represents a universal ethics based on the suffering and liberation of all sentient beings. The Madhyamaka analysis of emptiness has ethical implications: recognizing the lack of inherent selfhood dissolves the basis for rigid self-other distinction.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

The Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika schools constitute a realist empiricism: the world consists of real, independently existing substances and properties, and we can have genuine knowledge of them through perception and inference. This is a metaphysics that is compatible with scientific inquiry in a way that Vedāntic idealism is not.

The question of the āstika/nāstika distinction (acceptance or rejection of Vedic authority) is not simply a religious question but grounds the epistemological debate: is revealed testimony (*śabda*) a valid pramāṇa, and if so, what are its scope and limits? Buddhist and Jain philosophers reject Vedic authority and must ground all knowledge in perception and inference — this forces them to develop unusually rigorous empirical epistemologies.

## The Arc Forward

Indian philosophy's later history includes:
- The **Navya-Nyāya** technical development of formal logic (16th–17th century Bengal) — one of the most sophisticated pre-modern logical systems anywhere.
- The encounter with Western colonial modernity: **Ram Mohan Roy** (neo-Vedanta, engagement with Unitarianism), **Vivekananda** (universalizing Advaita, the Parliament of Religions 1893), **Sri Aurobindo** (evolutionary Vedanta), **Radhakrishnan** (comparative philosophy).
- **Ambedkar**: the philosophical critique of caste from a Buddhist standpoint — among the most significant political philosophers of modern India.
- **Contemporary analytic-Indian engagement**: Bimal Krishna Matilal (Cambridge-trained, brought Indian epistemology into analytic dialogue); Mark Siderits, Jay Garfield, Graham Priest (working on Madhyamaka and paraconsistent logic).
- The connection to embodied cognition and cognitive science (Varela, Thompson, Rosch — *The Embodied Mind* — explicitly draw on Buddhist Madhyamaka and Yogacara); and the comparison with phenomenology (Zahavi's work on Buddhist-phenomenological convergences).
