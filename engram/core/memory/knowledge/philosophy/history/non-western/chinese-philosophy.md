# Chinese Philosophy: From the Hundred Schools to Contemporary Thought

Chinese philosophy constitutes one of the world's great independent philosophical traditions, emerging roughly in the same period as the Greek Presocratics (6th–5th century BCE) and developing continuously to the present. Its central preoccupations are often different from Western philosophy's: where Western ethics asks "what is the right action?" Chinese philosophy more often asks "what is the fulfilled person or the well-ordered society?" Where Western epistemology asks "how do we justify our beliefs?", Chinese philosophy more often asks "how do we cultivate wisdom?" This is not to say that logic and epistemology are absent — they appear prominently in Mohism and in Neo-Confucian thought — but the practical-ethical and politico-social orientation is primary.

The classical period (the "Hundred Schools of Thought," ~600–221 BCE, during the Warring States period) is the most philosophically fertile: Confucianism, Taoism, Mohism, Legalism, and the School of Names all emerge in this period. The Han dynasty unification institutes Confucianism as official state ideology, but philosophical diversity continues. Neo-Confucianism (Song–Ming dynasties) synthesizes Confucianism with Buddhist and Taoist ideas. The modern period brings the encounter with Western philosophy, culminating in present-day Chinese philosophy.

## 1. Confucius and the Confucian Tradition

### Confucius (551–479 BCE)

The *Analects* (Lunyu) preserves the conversations of Confucius (Kǒngzǐ) with his students — a collection of aphorisms, brief dialogues, and observations that is one of the most influential texts in human history.

**Rén** (仁, benevolence, humaneness, goodness): the central Confucian virtue — the quality of the fully human person. Rén is not a single act but a stable character orientation oriented toward the genuine good of others. Its precise content is notoriously difficult to pin down — when asked directly, Confucius gives different answers in different contexts. This is deliberate: rén cannot be captured in a definition; it is a form of practical wisdom (*zhì*) that perceives what each situation requires.

**Lǐ** (禮, ritual propriety, rites, customs): the traditional rituals and norms of behavior — greeting forms, mourning practices, sacrificial procedures, codes of conduct in different social roles. Confucius insists on the continued importance of *lǐ* despite the social upheaval of the Warring States period. The point: ritual structures shared social life and expresses and cultivates proper social relationships.

The **rectification of names** (*zhèng míng*): social order depends on words meaning what they should mean — a father being truly a father, a ruler truly a ruler. This is not merely semantic but ethical: if names are used carelessly, behavior becomes confused. Related to Aristotle's claim that political philosophy requires correct descriptions of social roles.

**Five relationships** (*wǔlún*): ruler-minister, father-son, husband-wife, elder-younger brother, friend-friend. Each relationship involves role-specific virtues and responsibilities; their proper ordering constitutes social harmony. The family is the model for all social relationships (filial piety as the root of benevolence).

### Mencius (372–289 BCE)

Mencius (Mèngzǐ) systematizes and defends Confucianism against objections, especially from Mohist and Yangist critics.

**Human nature is good** (*xìng shàn*): all humans are born with "four beginnings" (*sìduān*) — the beginnings of benevolence (compassion), righteousness (shame), ritual propriety (deference), and wisdom (right/wrong discrimination). These are not yet fully developed virtues but natural tendencies that moral education cultivates. Example: anyone who sees a child about to fall into a well will feel alarm and compassion — not from self-interest, not from social training, but from an innate tendency.

The Mencian argument against psychological egoism: our natural responses (the well-case) reveal genuine other-regarding motivation as constitutive of human nature. Moral failure is the failure to develop what is already naturally present — like a mountain that has been deforested by bad practice.

**The kingly way** (*wángdào*) vs. the hegemon's way (*bàdào*): the legitimate ruler governs through moral persuasion and the people's genuine affirmation, not through force. Mencius argues for a right of revolution against tyrannical rulers — if the ruler fails in his duty to the people, he forfeits the Mandate of Heaven and may legitimately be deposed. This is among the earliest explicit accounts of political legitimacy grounded in the people's welfare.

### Xunzi (~310–220 BCE)

Xunzi agrees with Confucius on the importance of *lǐ* and moral education but disagrees with Mencius on human nature.

**Human nature is bad** (*xìng è*): our natural inclinations — toward pleasure, profit, and social domination — will produce conflict and disorder if unchecked. Moral virtue is not the development of natural tendencies but their transformation through the external imposition of *lǐ*. The ritual system is a cultural achievement that works against, not with, nature.

This is not pessimism but a different account of the mechanism of moral development. Xunzi's ethics has a Kantian flavor: virtue is the deliberate re-shaping of nature in accordance with rational principle, not the flowering of inborn tendencies. His epistemology (the *Jie Bi* chapter — "Dispelling Blindness") is the most consistently empirically-oriented of the early Confucians.

## 2. Taoism

### Laozi and the Tao Te Ching

The *Tao Te Ching* (Dào Dé Jīng, "Classic of the Way and Power," attributed to Laozi, ~6th–4th century BCE) is the foundational Taoist text — 81 short chapters, dense, paradoxical, and among the most translated books in history.

**Tao** (道, Way): the Tao is the ineffable ground of all being — not a god, not a substance, not a force in the physical sense, but the source and pattern of all things. "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao" — any positive characterization is immediately inadequate. The Tao is the nameless (chapter 1), the uncarved block (*pǔ*), the unhewn simplicity from which all things emerge and to which they return.

**Te** (德, Power/Virtue/Integrity): the Tao's creative potency as it manifests in individual things — the virtue that makes a thing what it fully is. The sage who embodies the Tao possesses *de* — virtue not as moral achievement but as existential integrity.

**Wu wei** (無為, non-action, effortless action): acting in accordance with the natural flow of things rather than forcing outcomes. Not passivity but perfect responsiveness — action without the interference of imposing ego, deliberation, or force. Water as the paradigm: it yields, flows around obstacles, is soft, yet carves the Grand Canyon. The ruler who governs with *wu wei* achieves more than the one who rules by force.

Laozi's political philosophy is a critique of the Confucian emphasis on culture, ritual, and moral strenuousness: these are artificial, secondary, symptomatic of loss. When the Tao is lost, benevolence appears; when benevolence is lost, righteousness appears; and so on down through convention and ritual. Return to the simple, the natural, the small.

### Zhuangzi (~369–286 BCE)

The *Zhuangzi* is one of the most philosophically playful, literarily brilliant, and philosophically serious texts in any tradition. Where the *Tao Te Ching* is cryptic and austere, Zhuangzi is exuberant, funny, and formally experimental.

**Perspectivism and the relativity of viewpoints**: Zhuangzi is the most systematic rellativist in the Chinese tradition (comparable to Nietzsche but arrived at independently). Each being has its own perspective, its own "this" and "that" — what is big from one perspective is small from another; what is useful from one perspective is useless from another. The butterfly dream: Zhuangzi dreams he is a butterfly and wonders, on waking, whether he is a man who dreamed he was a butterfly or a butterfly dreaming he is a man. The question is not answered — the distinction itself may be a limitation of perspective.

The **Cook and the Ox** parable (one of the most famous in Chinese literature): the perfect cook does not hack at the ox but follows the natural joints and cavities, moving in accordance with the structure of the thing itself. His knife never dulls because it never presses against resistance. This is the paradigm of *wu wei* and of *zhi* (skill/knowledge as embodied competence) — related to Ryle's knowing-how and to Dreyfus's critique of cognitivism.

**The Tao as equalizer**: from the Tao's perspective, all distinctions dissolve — right/wrong, large/small, life/death. The sage does not discriminate (conceptually-egoically) but responds to each situation with undivided attention. The philosophical implications: a critique of the Confucian (and Mohist) project of ethical systematization. Values are practical orientations from particular perspectives, not universal necessities.

## 3. Mohism

**Mozi** (~470–391 BCE) and the Mohist school are the most systematic philosophers of the early period in terms of logic and ethics.

**Impartial caring** (*jiān ài*): Mozi's central ethical doctrine — we should care for all people equally, not privileging family members over strangers. This is an explicit critique of the Confucian prioritization of family-based ethics. The argument is consequentialist: partial caring — caring more for one's own — produces conflict; universal caring produces mutual benefit.

**Consequentialist ethics**: actions are right insofar as they increase the welfare of the people — measured roughly in terms of population increase, material welfare, and social order. This is the most explicitly consequentialist ethics in classical Chinese philosophy.

**Logic**: the later Mohist *Canons* develop the most rigorous logic and epistemology of the classical period — defining terms, distinguishing types of inference, analyzing the conditions of valid argument. The Mohist school developed a logic partly comparable to Aristotle's but independently. It disappeared as a living school by the Han dynasty and was not rediscovered until the 20th century.

## 4. Legalism

**Legalism** (Fǎjiā): Han Feizi (~280–233 BCE) is the most systematic Legalist. The Legalist position: neither Confucian moral education nor Mohist consequentialism can produce reliable social order. What works is a system of clear laws, consistent enforcement, and purely formal incentives (reward and punishment). The ruler rules through *shì* (power/position), *shù* (technique/method), and *fǎ* (law).

Han Feizi's political philosophy is explicitly amoral — not nihilistic, but refusing to ground political philosophy in virtue or benevolence because these cannot be reliably produced or measured. The comparison to Machiavelli (roughly contemporary in the Western tradition) is apt: a realist science of political organization that brackets moral considerations.

The Qin dynasty's unification of China (221 BCE) was implemented under Legalist principles — and the Qin's rapid collapse after Qin Shi Huang's death is taken as evidence of Legalism's limitations.

## 5. Neo-Confucianism (Song–Ming Dynasties, 960–1644 CE)

Neo-Confucianism is the synthesis of Confucian ethical-political philosophy with Buddhist metaphysics (especially Hua-yen Buddhist cosmology) and Taoist metaphysics. The Confucian tradition needed to provide a metaphysical foundation it had previously lacked, because Buddhist philosophy had produced a systematic metaphysics that Confucianism could not simply ignore.

### Zhu Xi (1130–1200 CE)

Zhu Xi is the systematizer of Neo-Confucianism — his commentaries on the Four Books became the official curriculum of the Chinese state examination system for 600 years.

**Lǐ** (理, principle/pattern — a different character from the *lǐ* of ritual) and **Qì** (氣, vital force/matter-energy): everything that exists is constituted by *lǐ* (principle — the reason why things are what they are, the normative structure) and *qì* (the vital force/physical stuff through which *lǐ* is actualized). *Lǐ* and *qì* are not two separate things but two aspects of all existing things — the principle of a thing and the material through which that principle is expressed. This is closely comparable to the Aristotelian form/matter distinction.

**Investigation of things** (*géwù*): the moral and cognitive project of attending carefully to things — natural phenomena, texts, social situations — to discern their *lǐ*. This is both an empirical and a moral practice: understanding the principle of things cultivates moral knowledge.

### Wang Yangming (1472–1529 CE)

Wang Yangming develops Neo-Confucianism in a more internalist direction: the mind is principle (*xīn jí lǐ*). The principles of things are not to be found through external investigation but through the cultivation of the mind itself. His doctrine of the **unity of knowledge and action** (*zhīxíng héyī*): genuine moral knowledge is always motivationally effective — if you truly know that the right thing to do is to help someone in need, you will do it; the failure to act reveals the failure to truly know. This is a sophisticated critique of the intellectualist assumption that moral knowledge is theoretical, separable from motivation.

## 6. Modern Chinese Philosophy

The encounter with Western philosophy in the 19th–20th centuries: Chinese intellectuals engage with the challenge of Western modernity, technology, and political thought. The result is a series of syntheses and debates:

**The New Culture Movement** (1919 onwards): young intellectuals, reacting to China's military and political weakness, call for radical cultural transformation — abandoning Confucian tradition ("Confucius and sons" as the obstacle) and embracing science and democracy. Figures include Hu Shi (pragmatism; trained under Dewey) and Chen Duxiu.

**Marxism and Chinese philosophy**: Mao Zedong synthesizes Marxist-Leninist political philosophy with Chinese conditions; the Cultural Revolution is in part an attempt to eradicate the entire Confucian tradition. The philosophical aftermath: how to reconstruct Chinese philosophy in the post-Mao period?

**Contemporary New Confucianism** (Xiong Shili, Tang Junyi, Mou Zongsan): a movement asserting that Confucianism contains the philosophical resources to engage with and even criticize Western modernity — not as traditionalist conservatism but as recovering genuine philosophical insights that the Western tradition has missed. Mou Zongsan's engagement with Kant is the most technically sophisticated: he argues that Confucian moral experience demonstrates the possibility of intellectual intuition — what Kant denied to humans and reserved for God.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Chinese philosophy is less epistemology-focused than either Western or Indian philosophy, but it is not without epistemological content. The Neo-Confucian *géwù* program (investigation of things) implies an empirical orientation toward the world; Wang Yangming's critique implies a different account of knowledge — knowledge as active and inseparable from commitment and responsiveness.

Zhuangzi's perspectivism raises the deepest epistemological questions: can there be a view from nowhere? If every perspective is partial, is there a Taoist meta-perspective that sees the partiality of all? Zhuangzi seems to embrace the consequence: philosophical argument cannot escape perspective; what can be done is to cultivate a more flexible, responsive, less ego-bound perspective.

The concept of *zhì* (practical wisdom, skill, the knowledge of the expert craftsperson) — exemplified by Cook Ding — is one of the most important contributions of classical Chinese philosophy to the cross-cultural discussion: knowledge as embodied competence, inseparable from practice, is primary; propositional knowledge is abstracted from it. This connects directly to Ryle's knowing-how, Dreyfus's critique of AI, and the embodied cognition program.

### Language and meaning

The School of Names (Míngjiā — Gongsun Long, Hui Shi) developed sophisticated logical puzzles about language and predication: "A white horse is not a horse" (Gongsun Long) — because the term "horse" picks out shape while "white" picks out color; the compound does not have the same extension as either term. This has been analyzed as a form of Platonic realism about universals or as a sophisticated puzzle about type/token distinctions.

Confucius's doctrine of the **rectification of names** (*zhèng míng*) implies a philosophy of language: language is not merely descriptive but normative — words carry expectations about how things should be, not merely how they are. This is closer to Austin's speech-act theory (words as doing things, not merely stating things) than to the referentialist picture of meaning.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

The central question of Chinese ethics is what constitutes the fulfilled person (*jūnzǐ*, the exemplary person in Confucianism; the perfected one in Taoism). This grounds ethics in virtue and character rather than in rules or consequences — though Mohist consequentialism and Legalist rule-based systems are also present as alternatives.

The political philosophy of the Confucian-Taoist tradition is persistently communitarian: the self is constituted by its relationships, not prior to them. There is no "original position" behind a veil of ignorance because there is no self independent of its social roles. The comparison to communitarian critiques of Rawlsian liberalism (Sandel, MacIntyre) is apt.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

Chinese philosophy never developed a sharp science/philosophy distinction comparable to the one that crystallized in Western Europe in the 17th century. The investigative program of Neo-Confucianism (*géwù*) encompasses both what we would call natural history and what we would call ethics/metaphysics.

The concept of *qì* — vital force, matter-energy, the dynamic stuff of natural processes — provides a basis for a non-dualist naturalism: mind/body, heaven/earth, human/natural are not opposed pairs but integrated aspects of one dynamic-processual reality. This is a genuinely distinct ontological picture from either Cartesian dualism or reductive physicalism — closer to process philosophy (Whitehead) or to some versions of the philosophy of biology and dynamical systems.

## The Arc Forward

Chinese philosophy's ongoing engagement with Western thought includes:

- **Comparative philosophy** as a discipline: Roger Ames and David Hall (Confucian process thought and Dewey); Bryan van Norden (Confucian virtue ethics); Chad Hansen (Chinese philosophy of language and semantics); Graham (Mohist logic).
- **Contemporary New Confucianism** continues as a live academic movement in East Asia and among diaspora Chinese philosophers.
- **Chinese political philosophy** in the contemporary context: debates between Confucian communitarianism and liberal individualism; the "Confucian meritocracy" arguments (Daniel Bell) vs. democratic theory.
- The connection to **embodied cognition** through the concept of *zhì* and *wu wei*: Dreyfus's *What Computers Can't Do* draws explicitly on Zhuangzi; the comparison of embodied skill knowledge in Zhuangzi and in Merleau-Ponty is developed by Erin McCarthy, Roger Ames, and others.
