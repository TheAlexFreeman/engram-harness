# Later Wittgenstein and Ordinary Language Philosophy (1945–1970)

The later Wittgenstein and the Oxford ordinary language philosophers represent a decisive turn within the analytic tradition: from the ideal of a logically perfect language that analysis would reveal to the actual, functioning language of everyday life. Where the early Wittgenstein sought the hidden logical structure beneath ordinary language, the later Wittgenstein finds in ordinary language the very richness that philosophy has systematically ignored, distorted, and then generated pseudo-problems from. Philosophy's task is not to replace ordinary language with something better but to bring philosophy itself to peace — to dissolve the confusions that arise when language goes on holiday.

---

## 1. The Later Wittgenstein: Language Games and Forms of Life

**Ludwig Wittgenstein** (1889–1951) repudiated — more explicitly than almost any major philosopher — the errors of his own earlier work. He returned to Cambridge in 1929 and spent the rest of his life working through a complete reorientation of his philosophy. The *Philosophical Investigations* (written in the 1940s, published 1953) is the result — a collection of remarks, dialogues, and thought experiments organized around a loose set of interrelated themes.

### Language Games

The central concept: **language game** (*Sprachspiel*). Language is not a single, unified system with a hidden logical structure; it is a family of diverse practices — each a "language game" — embedded in corresponding forms of life. Giving orders, describing appearances, reporting an event, speculating about events, making up stories, playing in a theater, translating, asking, thanking, cursing, greeting, praying — all are language games, and they differ as radically as chess differs from checkers or poker.

The significance: meaning is not a Platonic abstract object ("the logical form") nor an inner mental image (the empiricist picture); it is **use** — the pattern of activity comprising a language game. "For a large class of cases in which we employ the word 'meaning' — though not for all — this word can be explained thus: the meaning of a word is its use in the language."

This dissolves the traditional philosophy of meaning: it's not that "red" means the property Redness (Frege's sense) or the inner sensation (the empiricist picture) — it means what it does through its pattern of use. Different language games use the same words differently; philosophy errs by extracting words from their native language game and applying them in another (e.g., using "know" in the language game of empirical knowledge and then asking "but do I *really* know?").

### Meaning as Use and Family Resemblance

The picture theory of the *Tractatus* required that every genuine name have a definite referent and every proposition have a definite sense. Wittgenstein now attacks the underlying assumption: that a concept must have a single, shared essence.

**Family resemblance** (*Familienähnlichkeit*): the concept of "game" — board games, card games, ball games, Olympic games, war games, children's games — has no single feature shared by all instances. Instead, there are overlapping and criss-crossing similarities: some games are competitive (but not all); some have rules (but not all); some are played for entertainment (but not all). The concept is held together by a network of resemblances, like the overlapping resemblances among members of a family — nose, build, gait, temperament — with no feature present in every member.

This directly anticipates and grounds **prototype theory** in cognitive psychology (Rosch): natural categories are organized around prototypes with graded membership, not defined by necessary and sufficient conditions. The "classical theory" of concepts (every concept has defining features) is the philosophical assumption that Wittgenstein's family resemblance argument undermines from within.

The connection extends to cognitive linguistics: Lakoff's categories are organized by prototype, with fuzzy edges and family resemblance structure. Wittgenstein's philosophical analysis and Rosch's psychological experiments converge on the same conclusion.

### Private Language Argument

One of the most consequential arguments in 20th-century philosophy: can there be a genuinely private language — a language whose terms refer to private inner sensations accessible only to the speaker?

The argument is not a single compact proof but a complex of considerations:
- A term without application-conditions is not a word but a name without a meaning.
- To use a term correctly requires a norm of correctness — a standard against which uses can be checked.
- A merely private sensation cannot provide such a standard: if I say "I will call this sensation 'S'" and thereafter apply S to some sensation, what determines whether I have applied it correctly? Memory? But memory is not an independent check on memory.
- Therefore, there is no genuinely private language — no language whose meaning is fixed solely by a private inner ostension.

The philosophical import: **there is no Cartesian inner theater** whose contents are perfectly determinate and incorrigible. Even our talk about inner states, sensations, and experiences gets its meaning from public practices — from the forms of life in which expressions of inner states are embedded. This does not deny inner experience; it claims that the language with which we describe inner experience is publicly grounded.

The private language argument is the philosophical ancestor of Ryle's demolition of the Cartesian ghost in the machine, of functionalism in philosophy of mind (mental states defined by their public-functional relations, not by inner intrinsic properties), and of the social externalism in philosophy of language (Putnam, Burge — meaning is not in the head).

### Rule-Following

The rule-following considerations (*Investigations* §§138–242, extended in *Remarks on the Foundations of Mathematics*) raise a problem: what is it to follow a rule? If I follow the rule "add 2" and compute 2, 4, 6, 8... 1000, 1002, 1004 — what makes this correct? Not merely my intention (I might have "misremembered" the rule); not the rule itself (any pattern of responses is compatible with infinitely many rules); not the grammatical structure of the rule (that can always be reinterpreted).

**Kripke's Wittgenstein** (*Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language*, 1982): there is no fact of the matter about which rule is being followed — what makes me right is not a fact about my inner state or the rule itself, but my agreement with the community in a practice. Meaning and rule-following are communal, not individual, phenomena.

Wittgenstein's own view is more deflationary: the demand for a *fact* that makes rule-following determinate is itself a philosophical confusion — we follow rules in practice; that practice does not require a further guarantee. The philosopher's craving for certainty — the demand for a super-fact that grounds normative practice — is itself the source of the confusion.

## 2. Gilbert Ryle: The Category Mistake and the Deconstruction of the Cartesian Mind

**Gilbert Ryle** (1900–1976): *The Concept of Mind* (1949) — the most systematic attack on Cartesian dualism from within ordinary language philosophy. Ryle argues that the Cartesian picture of the mind as a "ghost in the machine" — an inner mental substance behind observable behavior — is a **category mistake**: treating mental terms as if they referred to inner occurrences of the same ontological category as physical processes, but in a private mental theater.

Category mistake: a visitor to Oxford is shown the colleges, libraries, playing fields, and administrative offices, and then asks "But where is the University?" — they have mistaken the category; the University is not an additional thing *alongside* these but is their organizational unity. Ryle argues that when we say "Jones decided, and then moved," we are not referring to two events — a mental decision-event and a subsequent bodily movement — but to one thing under two aspects (or the disposition and its exercise).

**Dispositions and knowledge-how**: Ryle distinguishes **knowing-that** (propositional knowledge: knowing that 7 × 7 = 49) from **knowing-how** (practical competence: knowing how to ride a bicycle). Intellectualist legend holds that knowing how requires first knowing that — you ride a bicycle by first forming propositions about what to do and then executing them. But this leads to an infinite regress (applying the rules itself requires knowing how). Most of our cognitive life is knowing-how — embodied competence that does not proceed through propositional formulation. This anticipates Dreyfus on expertise (expert action is not rule-following but embodied skill), Polanyi's tacit knowledge, and the embodied cognition tradition.

## 3. Austin: Speech Acts and the Performative

**J.L. Austin** (1911–1960): *How to Do Things with Words* (1962, lectures). Austin's central insight: not all utterances describe states of affairs; many utterances *do* things.

**Performative utterances**: "I name this ship the *Queen Elizabeth*" (spoken while breaking the bottle), "I promise to pay you tomorrow," "I hereby declare war" — these are not descriptions of namings, promises, or declarations; they *are* the naming, promising, and declaring. Getting caught in the performative utterance does not affect its truth (it has no truth-value); it can be **infelicitous** (done in the wrong circumstances, by the wrong person, without the right conventions in place).

Austin eventually generalizes: every utternace has a **locutionary** act (saying something with a meaning), an **illocutionary** act (doing something *in* saying: asserting, promising, warning, requesting...), and a **perlocutionary** act (producing an effect by saying: convincing, frightening, persuading...). This three-level analysis is the foundation of **speech act theory**.

**Speech act theory** (developed by Searle): different kinds of illocutionary acts have different **felicity conditions** — the conditions that must hold for the act to succeed. This framework:
- Grounds **pragmatics** in formal linguistics — the systematic study of speaker meaning vs. sentence meaning.
- Grounds **Gricean implicature** (what is communicated beyond what is literally said) — the cooperative nature of communication.
- Grounds **relevance theory** (Sperber and Wilson).
- Connects to **cognitive linguistics** (Sweetser's extension of speech acts to diachronic semantics — the deontic, epistemic, and speech-act domains as conceptual metaphorical extensions of each other).

## 4. Strawson: Descriptive Metaphysics

**P.F. Strawson** (1919–2006): *Individuals: An Essay in Descriptive Metaphysics* (1959) — an attempt to use careful conceptual analysis to uncover the basic conceptual structure implicit in the most general features of our thought and language, without committing to either the idealism of a Kant or the logical atomism of a Russell.

**Descriptive metaphysics** vs. **revisionary metaphysics**: Strawson proposes to describe the structure of our actual conceptual scheme (persons and material bodies as basic particulars, space and time as the organizing framework of reference) rather than replace it with an ideal scheme grounded in some prior philosophical commitment.

The concept of **person**: a person is not a Cartesian mind plus a body; the concept of person is primitive — more basic than either mind or body. A person is a being to which we can attribute *both* M-predicates (mental: experiences, thoughts, desires) and P-predicates (physical: weight, location, bodily movements). The primitive concept of person is necessary for applying the concept of experience to anything at all — experience without a subject attributed to is no experience at all.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

The later Wittgenstein and ordinary language philosophy produce the most significant shift in philosophy of mind of the 20th century — from the Cartesian picture (private inner mental theater) to a publicly embedded, norm-governed account of mind. Key contributions:
- Private language argument → no purely private mental content; mentality is grounded in public practice.
- Ryle's category mistake → the "ghost in the machine" is a grammatical confusion, not a metaphysical fact.
- Knowing-how vs. knowing-that → most cognition is embodied practical competence, not propositional theoretical knowledge.
- Strawson's person → the concept of person is primitive; neither mind nor body is the fundamental.

These moves clear the philosophical ground for:
- **Functionalism** (mental states defined by functional roles in a public system — Putnam)
- **Embodied cognition** (cognition as practical engagement, not inner representation — Dreyfus, Merleau-Ponty)
- **Eliminative materialism** (the folk-psychological vocabulary should be replaced by neuroscience — Churchland)
- **Phenomenal consciousness** debates (whatever is right about the Cartesian picture survives as the "hard problem" — Chalmers)

### Language and meaning

The later Wittgenstein is the most radical philosophy of language developed in the analytic tradition. **Meaning is use** — the pattern of deployment in a practice — rather than a relation between symbols and objects, or a mental image, or a Fregean abstract sense. This:
- Grounds the **proto-pragmatics** that Austin and Grice will formalize.
- Grounds the **cognitive linguistic** account of meaning as use-pattern (Langacker's usage-based model; Tomasello's acquisition theory).
- Grounds Quine's **behaviorist semantics** (meaning is behavioral disposition) which, though Quine goes too far, captures something of the use-theoretic insight.
- Defines the **family resemblance** structure of concepts that prototype theory confirms empirically.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Ryle, Austin, and Wittgenstein are less directly engaged with ethics than with philosophy of mind and language. But the later Wittgenstein's account of forms of life has deep ethical implications: moral practices are embedded in forms of life; ethical claims do not stand outside forms of life as neutral universal truths but are the expressions of those forms of life. This is neither moral relativism (the claim that any form of life is as good as any other) nor moral realism (the claim that moral truths are independent of all forms of life) but a third position: moral discourse is meaningful and normative within the practices and forms of life in which it is embedded, and changed through those practices.

Strawson's "Freedom and Resentment" (1962): the concept of moral responsibility is not a theoretical thesis to be refuted by determinism but a practice constituted by **reactive attitudes** (resentment, gratitude, indignation, love) that we are incapable of adopting the "objective stance" toward in normal life. Strawson grounds ethics in the irreducible participant standpoint — the stance of engagement with persons as persons, not as objects.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

The ordinary language tradition is deeply suspicious of both scientism (the reduction of all genuine knowledge to science) and traditional metaphysics. Philosophy's proper task is neither to extend science (Quine's naturalism goes too far) nor to deliver a priori truths about the nature of ultimate reality (the idealist and rationalist ambition goes too far) but to clarify the logic of our language — to map the forms of our actual practice of talking and thinking.

This methodological modesty has been criticized as sterile; it has also generated remarkable clarity about the structure of our actual conceptual practices. The post-Gettier epistemology (starting from the counterexample to justified true belief as the analysis of knowledge) is a paradigm of ordinary language analysis generating productive research programs.

## The Arc Forward

The later Wittgenstein has generated remarkably diverse programs:
- **Social constructivism and the sociology of knowledge** (Bloor's Edinburgh school — the use of Wittgenstein to argue that even scientific knowledge is socially constituted).
- **Therapeutic philosophy** (the Wittgensteinian tradition that continues to use both early and late methods to dissolve philosophical confusions rather than answer philosophical questions — Cavell, Conant, Diamond).
- **Inferentialism** (Brandom's *Making It Explicit* — a Wittgensteinian pragmatist account of meaning as constituted by inferential roles and normative practices of giving and asking for reasons).
- **Embodied cognition** and cognitive linguistics (indirect but deep — through Ryle and through the philosophical framework's resonance with empirical findings in prototype theory and motor cognition).
- **Contextualism in epistemology** (knowledge-attribution varies with conversational context — epistemic standards are context-relative, not absolute — a Wittgensteinian insight extended into formal epistemology).
