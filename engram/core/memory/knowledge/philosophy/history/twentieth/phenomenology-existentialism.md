# Phenomenology and Existentialism (1900–1960)

Phenomenology and existentialism emerge from the same philosophical moment — the crisis of neo-Kantian academic philosophy at the turn of the 20th century — and share a common orientation: return to the *things themselves* (*zu den Sachen selbst*), to the structures of lived experience as they actually present themselves, rather than to theoretical constructs, logical posits, or physiological explanations. But they diverge dramatically in method and outcome: phenomenology (Husserl) is a rigorous descriptive science of consciousness; existentialism (Kierkegaard's heirs — Heidegger, Sartre, de Beauvoir, Camus) engages the concrete human situation — freedom, anxiety, death, bad faith, and the possibility of authentic existence.

## 1. Husserl: Intentionality and the Structure of Consciousness

**Edmund Husserl** (1859–1938) is the founder of phenomenology and one of the most technically demanding philosophers of the 20th century. His project: establish philosophy as a rigorous science by investigating the **a priori structures of consciousness** — the invariant features of experience that hold independently of particular empirical facts. Husserl's phenomenology is not psychology (which is empirical, contingent) but eidetic science (concerned with essences, necessary structures).

### Intentionality

The foundational concept, inherited from Husserl's teacher **Franz Brentano** (who inherited it from the Scholastics): **intentionality** — consciousness is always *consciousness of something*. Every mental act has an object: perception perceives something, thought thinks something, desire desires something, fear fears something. The "of" is not a spatial or causal relation; it is a structural feature of consciousness itself.

Brentano's claim (largely discredited in his original form): mental phenomena are uniquely characterized by "intentional inexistence" — the object is in the act in a distinctive way not found in physical phenomena. When I think of a unicorn, there is no unicorn in the world; yet my thought is *of* a unicorn. Husserl refines this: the object of a mental act is the *noema* — the intentional object as presented in a particular way — which must be distinguished from the actual object in the world. The same object (Mount Blanc) can be intended in different ways (as "the highest mountain in Europe," as "the mountain Nietzsche climbed," as a visual percept); the noema captures the mode of givenness.

### The Phenomenological Reduction (Epoché)

Husserl's method: **bracket** (put out of play) all presuppositions about the existence and nature of the external world — not deny them, but suspend judgment temporarily. The Husserlian epoché (borrowed from Pyrrhonian skepticism) is a methodological neutralization: not asserting or denying that trees and tables exist, but turning attention to the *structural features of the experience of trees and tables*. What remains after the reduction is **pure consciousness** and its **intentional structure** — the transcendental field that is the a priori condition of all experience.

By this method, Husserl aims to investigate what cannot be subjected to naturalistic or empiricist explanation: the connection between consciousness and world is not a causal connection (perception isn't caused by objects) but an intentional connection — the world presents itself *as* such-and-such through the intentional acts of consciousness.

### Time-Consciousness

Husserl's *Lectures on the Phenomenology of the Consciousness of Internal Time* (ca. 1905–1910, published 1928) is one of the most technically extraordinary texts in phenomenology and directly relevant to Alex's existing knowledge files on narrative cognition.

The problem: how is it possible to hear a melody — rather than just a sequence of disconnected notes? Each note sounds and then is gone; the present moment replaces it. Yet we hear the notes as *belonging together* — as a temporal sequence with retained past and anticipated future.

Husserl's analysis: **primal impression** (the punctual "now"), **retention** (the fading "just-past" note still actively present in modified form), and **protention** (the anticipated "about-to-come" note dimly present in expectation). These three are not three separate acts but aspects of the single living present — the **thick present** that extends somewhat into past and future through retention and protention. Time-consciousness is thus never punctual but always this structured three-fold present.

This analysis connects to:
- **Ricoeur's narrative time** (*Time and Narrative*): the Husserlian analysis of the micro-structure of time-consciousness is the foundation for Ricoeur's account of how narrative gives human time its meaning — narrative extends the Husserlian living present into the cultural-historical macrostructure of meaningful life.
- **Augustine's *distentio animi*** (*Confessions* XI) — the stretching of the soul across past memory, present attention, and future expectation. Husserl's analysis is the rigorously phenomenological version of what Augustine described psychologically and theologically.
- **Connectionist and dynamical models in cognitive science**: the way retention and protention structure cognition without explicit representation anticipates recurrent neural network dynamics.

### The Life-World and the Crisis of European Sciences

The late Husserl (*The Crisis of European Sciences and Transcendental Phenomenology*, 1936) develops the concept of the **life-world** (*Lebenswelt*) — the pre-scientific, pre-theoretical world of everyday experience that is the ground from which all science arises and to which it ultimately refers. Modern science has forgotten this ground — it takes its mathematized abstractions (Galileo's geometry of nature, Newton's abstract space) as the real world and treats the life-world as a secondary, subjective appearance.

The "crisis of European sciences" is not a technical failure but a crisis of meaning: science can describe the causal structure of nature but cannot address the questions most urgent for human life — questions of meaning, value, and the good life. The return to the life-world is the return to the foundation that gives science its sense.

## 2. Heidegger: Dasein and the Question of Being

**Martin Heidegger** (1889–1976) is both one of the greatest and most politically disgraced philosophers of the 20th century: his *Being and Time* (1927) transformed philosophy of existence, transformed hermeneutics, generated deep resonances with cognitive science and ecology; his collaboration with the Nazi regime (1933–34 as Rector of Freiburg) and his subsequent failure to adequately repudiate it are a permanent shadow on his legacy.

### Being-in-the-World

The fundamental move of *Being and Time*: against Cartesian dualism (subject vs. object, mind vs. world), Heidegger argues that the basic structure of human existence (which he calls **Dasein** — literally "being-there") is **being-in-the-world** — a single, inseparable structure, not a mind that is then related to a world. We are always already *in* a world; there is no pre-given Cartesian subject from which we then reach out to world.

The Cartesian picture requires us to explain how an inner mental substance gets access to an outer physical world — the epistemological problem of the external world. Heidegger dissolves this pseudo-problem by showing that it arises from the **derivative** (theoretical) standpoint in which we stand back and observe things as objects, rather than the **primary** (practical, engaged) standpoint in which we are always already *dealing* with things as tools.

**Equipment and the ready-to-hand**: things in our world are first encountered not as neutral physical objects (the "present-at-hand" theoretical stance) but as **equipment** (*Zeug*) — as "ready-to-hand" (*zuhanden*) — hammers for hammering, roads for driving, eyes for seeing. Equipment comes in a **totality of involvements**: the hammer is for nailing, the nail for the wood, the wood for the desk, the desk for writing, writing for the project. This network of instrumental references is *disclosed* through engaged practice, not theoretically constructed.

When equipment **breaks down** — the hammer slips, the key doesn't fit — it becomes conspicuous, prominent, obtrusive, and only then does it show up as a *thing with properties* (the present-at-hand stance of science and theory). Theoretical cognition is not the primary relation to the world but a **deficient mode** of practical engagement — what happens when practice is interrupted.

This analysis closely anticipates **embodied cognition** and **situated robotics** (Brooks's subsumption architecture, the notion that cognition is manipulation in an environment rather than representation inside a head) and the phenomenological approach to cognitive science of Dreyfus, Varela, Thompson, and Evan Thompson.

### Care, Thrownness, and Projection

Dasein's fundamental structure is **care** (*Sorge*): being ahead of itself (projection into possibilities — always already projecting toward a future), being already in (thrownness — always already factically situated: in a body, in a language, in a culture, in a time, in a family), and being alongside (falling — absorbed in everyday life, in the world).

**Thrownness** (*Geworfenheit*): I did not choose to be born, to be born in this time and place and culture and body. I am always already *thrown* into a situation I did not choose — a historical epoch, a language, a set of culturally transmitted possibilities. This is not a deficiency but the condition of being a finite being in a historical world. Understanding is always from this thrown position.

**Projection** (*Entwurf*): Dasein is always running ahead of itself toward possibilities — it is always projecting toward what it might be. We are fundamentally temporal, futural beings: our "being" is always ahead of us — we are the beings for whom being is an issue, who understand ourselves in terms of possibilities.

**Falling** (*Verfallenheit*): everyday Dasein is absorbed in the public world — in what "one" does, thinks, says (das *Man* — the "they-self"). The *Man* flattens everything to the average — authentic stances are replaced by what "they" do; anxiety is covered over by idle talk and the busyness of everyday life. This is not a moral failing but the structural tendency of Dasein to lose itself in the world of others.

### Death and Authenticity

Death as Heidegger's term: **Dasein's ownmost possibility** — the possibility that is non-relational (no one else can die my death for me), certain (will come), and indefinite (when unknown). Running ahead to death (*Vorlaufen*) — anticipating one's own finitude — is the condition for living as one's own self rather than as the they-self. The call of conscience (*Gewissensruf*) summons Dasein from its lostness in das *Man* back to its ownmost possibility — not a divine voice but the structure of Dasein calling itself from its own ground of thrownness and guilt.

Authenticity (*Eigentlichkeit*) — literally "ownness" — is not a moral achievement or a special mode of experience but a modification of Dasein's engagement with its own possibilities: owning its thrownness, projecting resolutely from its own possibilities, rather than dispersing itself in the they-self.

### Language, Truth, and the Fourfold (Late Heidegger)

After the *Kehre* (turn, ca. 1930s), Heidegger's focus shifts from the analysis of Dasein to the question of **Being itself** — how Being discloses itself through epochs of Western history and particularly through language and art.

"Language is the house of Being" — language is not a tool for expressing pre-given thoughts but the medium in which Being discloses itself. The poet (Hölderlin, Rilke, Trakl) most purely lets Being speak — not through propositional statements but through the gathering of world in word. The philosopher's task is to think more originarily, to recover what has been covered over by the metaphysical tradition's focus on entities (beings) and its forgetting of the question of Being itself.

**Technology** (*Gestell* — "enframing"): the modern technological world reveals beings exclusively as **standing-reserve** (*Bestand*) — resources to be exploited, optimized, ordered. The forest becomes standing timber, the river becomes hydroelectric power. This is not a contingent misuse of technology but the deepest structure of modern thought — the culmination of the Western metaphysical tradition that began with Plato's subordination of beings to essence and form. The danger of *Gestell* is that humans themselves become standing-reserve — optimizable resources.

## 3. Merleau-Ponty: The Lived Body and Motor Intentionality

**Maurice Merleau-Ponty** (1908–1961) is the phenomenologist most directly relevant to embodied cognition, cognitive linguistics, and the philosophy of perception. His *Phenomenology of Perception* (1945) is the most important work in the philosophy of body and perception in the 20th century.

### The Lived Body

Against Descartes: the body is not an object in the world among other objects (the body as *Körper* — the physical body studied by biology). The body is the **site of subjectivity** — the body-subject (*corps propre*, the lived body) — the field from which perception, action, and intentionality always already proceed.

The body-schema: not a representation of the body (another mental image), but the pre-reflective, practical field through which we orient ourselves and move in the world. My hand knows where the keyboard is not by first forming a spatial representation and then consulting it, but through its own motor habitude — its practical readiness. The phenomenal body is **motoric intelligence** before it is reflective knowledge.

**Motor intentionality**: perception is not a passive registration of inputs but a motor engagement with the environment — perception is oriented toward possible action, and action creates the perceptual field. Merleau-Ponty's primacy of perception is the primacy of motoric engagement over cognitive representation.

This directly anticipates:
- **Embodied cognition** (Lakoff, Johnson): conceptual structure is grounded in bodily interaction with the world.
- **Enactive cognition** (Varela, Thompson, Rosch): cognition is the enactment of a world, not the processing of pre-given information.
- **Gibsonian affordances**: the environment is perceived in terms of possibilities for action, not as value-neutral physical array.
- **Cognitive linguistics' construal**: language encodes perspectival, motoric engagements — spatial terms, figure-ground structure, image schemas — all grounded in embodied experience.

## 4. Sartre, de Beauvoir, and Camus

### Sartre: Radical Freedom and Bad Faith

**Jean-Paul Sartre** (1905–1980): the most publicly visible philosopher of the 20th century, whose political engagement, fiction (*Nausea*, the plays), and philosophy combined to make existentialism a cultural as well as philosophical force.

*Being and Nothingness* (1943): the distinction between **being-in-itself** (*être en soi*) — self-identical being, what things simply are — and **being-for-itself** (*être pour soi*) — self-conscious being, the human mode of being that is always ahead of itself, never coinciding with itself. The for-itself is the being through which **nothingness** enters the world: only conscious beings can negate (imagine what is not, deny, refuse, project past the given).

**Radical freedom**: the for-itself is "condemned to be free" — there is no human nature that determines what we are; we are what we make ourselves through our choices. Even to refuse to choose is a choice. There is no escape from freedom.

**Bad faith** (*mauvaise foi*): the attempt to deny one's freedom by treating oneself as a thing (in-itself) — identifying with a fixed role, denying one's transcendence, pretending that one "had to" do what one did. The waiter who plays the waiter too completely — who has made "waiter" into an essence rather than a role chosen and maintained — is in bad faith. So is the person who says "I can't help it — it's just my nature."

### Simone de Beauvoir: Situated Freedom and the Second Sex

**Simone de Beauvoir** (1908–1986: *The Second Sex* (1949): the foundational text of second-wave feminism. "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman" — gender is not a natural fact but a social construction, historically achieved through the practices and institutions that constitute "woman" as the Other against which the masculine universal subject is defined.

*The Ethics of Ambiguity* (1947): against Sartre's atomistic radical freedom — freedom is situated, social, and intersubjective. My freedom is conditioned by the freedom of others; genuine freedom requires the liberation of others. The ethical stance is the recognition of the ambiguity of human existence — neither pure transcendence nor pure facticity — and the embrace of that ambiguity as the ground of genuine engagement.

### Camus: The Absurd and Revolt

**Albert Camus** (1913–1960): *The Myth of Sisyphus* (1942) — the absurd is the confrontation between the human demand for meaning and the world's silence. The honest response to absurd: neither the religious leap (Kierkegaard: bad faith for Camus) nor suicide (physical or philosophical abandonment of the project) but **revolt** — continued engagement in full consciousness of the absurd. "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Phenomenology is the most rigorous analysis of the structure of mind and its relation to the world in the 20th century. Key contributions:
- **Intentionality** as the structural feature of consciousness — not inner mental images corresponding to outer things but the intrinsic directedness of consciousness toward objects.
- **Time-consciousness** as the deepest layer of subjective experience — Husserl's retention/protention analysis remains the most detailed philosophical account of temporal experience.
- **Being-in-the-world** as the replacement of Cartesian subject-object dualism — consciousness is always already engaged in a world.
- **Motor intentionality** (Merleau-Ponty) as the embodied ground of all cognition — the body-subject before the reflective ego.
- **Intersubjectivity** — for Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, and Sartre, consciousness is not the solitary Cartesian cogito but is always already in relation to others; the other is co-constitutive of the self.

### Language and meaning

Heidegger's linguistic turn (late work): language as "the house of Being" — not a tool for expressing pre-given thoughts but the opening through which beings are disclosed. The philosopher and poet are the guardians of language because language is the site of Being's self-disclosure.

Merleau-Ponty's linguistic phenomenology (posthumous *The Prose of the World* and *Signs*): language is not a system of conventional signs imposed on a pre-linguistic experience but is the deepening — the sedimentary taking-hold — of lived experience in an intersubjective medium. His account of speech as gesture (*la gesticulation*) — expression as bodily enactment — anticipates embodied approaches to language.

Sartre's *What is Literature?* (1948): the distinctions between prose (which pursues meaning through transparent reference) and poetry (which treats words as things, not transparent signs) address the politics and phenomenology of literary language.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Sartre's radical freedom generates a distinctive ethical problem: if there is no given human nature and all values are chosen, what grounds the choice? Sartre's late answer (*Notebooks for an Ethics*): authentic freedom is the freedom that acknowledges the freedom of others — a proto-intersubjective ethics that de Beauvoir develops more rigorously. Sartre's political engagement (communism, anti-colonialism, *Critique of Dialectical Reason*) is the existentialist's attempt to live the implication that theory without practice is bad faith.

Heidegger's ethics is largely latent in *Being and Time* — authenticity and the call of conscience provide a basis for a non-moralistic account of ethical seriousness, but Heidegger is notoriously reluctant to develop it explicitly. His political failure (Nazism) is a catastrophic exemplification of the gap between existentialist authenticity and political judgment.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

Heidegger's diagnosis of modern science and technology (*Gestell*) as the culmination of Western metaphysics' forgetting of Being is the most philosophically developed critique of the scientific worldview in the 20th century — and the most controversial. It is simultaneously insightful (the reduction of all relation to the world to resource-extraction and optimization is a genuinely alarming cultural tendency) and suspect (the alternative he offers — the poetic language of Being, rural peasant life — has a reactionary character that critics rightly note).

Husserl's life-world analysis provides a more sober critical framework: any adequate account of human life requires the life-world as its ground, and a philosophy that evacuates it (in favor of pure formalism or pure mechanism) is self-undermining.

## The Arc Forward

Phenomenology generates:
- **Hermeneutic phenomenology** (Heidegger's late work, **Gadamer's** *Truth and Method*, **Ricoeur's** narrative philosophy): understanding as interpretation, always from a historical standpoint; the fusion of horizons; narrative as the medium of temporal self-understanding.
- **Embodied cognitive science** (Dreyfus, Varela, Thompson, Maturana): phenomenological insights operationalized in cognitive science.
- **Poststructuralism** (Derrida's deconstructive reading of Husserl, Foucault's archaeology as a critique of phenomenology): both emerge from and against the phenomenological tradition.

Existentialism generates:
- **Existential psychology and therapy** (Frankl, Laing, May, Yalom): the structure of authentic existence applied to clinical practice.
- **Feminist philosophy** (de Beauvoir's situated freedom as the foundation for intersectional accounts of embodied identity).
- **Postcolonial theory** (Fanon's *Black Skin, White Masks* — phenomenology of the colonial body; Sartrean and de Beauvoirian frameworks applied to racialized experience).
