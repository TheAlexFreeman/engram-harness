# Philosophy of Mind and Language in the 20th Century (1950–2000)

The 20th century's analytic philosophy of mind and language converge on a set of problems that are ultimately post-Cartesian: not whether mind is a separate substance (Descartes), but what kind of thing a mind is within a broadly naturalist metaphysical picture; not whether language has meaning (it obviously does), but how meaning is constituted and how it connects word to world. The central debates range from the nature of mental states (functionalism, physicalism, eliminativism) to the semantics of reference (Kripke, Putnam) to the nature of intentionality (Brentano, Husserl, Searle, Dennett). These debates directly connect to cognitive science and cognitive linguistics.

---

## 1. The Mind-Body Problem After Ryle

The Cartesian mind-body problem — how does a non-physical substance interact with a physical body — is dissolved by Ryle's category mistake (the ghost in the machine) and the ordinary language critique. But dissolution of the Cartesian formulation does not dissolve the underlying problem: how does the mental (consciousness, intentionality, semantic content) relate to the physical (brain states, causal processes)?

### Identity Theory: Smart, Place

The first physicalist response: **type-type identity theory** (Place, Smart, mid-1950s): mental state types are identical to physical (neural) state types. Pain is a neural process (C-fibers firing). Mental properties are physical properties, given that the mind is the brain.

Advantage: metaphysically parsimonious — no separate mental substance.  
Problem: **multiple realizability** (Putnam, 1967): pain can be realized by C-fibers in humans, by different neural structures in octopi, by silicon circuits in a hypothetical robot. If pain is defined by a specific physical type, then octopus pain and human pain are different things — but intuitively they are the same functional state. Identity theory is too chauvinistic about physical implementation.

### Functionalism

**Functionalism** (Putnam, 1960s): mental state types are defined by their **functional roles** — by their typical causes (damage to tissue causes pain), their typical effects (pain causes avoidance behavior, verbal report), and their relations to other mental states (pain and belief that the dentist can help typically cause the desire to go to the dentist). Mental states are functional states, and functional states can be multiply realized — the same function can be implemented in many different physical substrates.

Functionalism solves the multiple realizability problem and grounds the possibility of **machine minds**: if mental states are functional states, there is no principled reason why an appropriately organized silicon system cannot have mental states.

Functionalism connects to: cognitive science's computational models (mental states as computational/functional states of an information-processing system), artificial intelligence (the mind as an algorithm), and the debate about machine consciousness.

Problems:
- **Qualia and the inverted spectrum**: functionalists account for mental states by their relations to other states and to inputs/outputs. But qualia — the intrinsic qualitative character of experience (the "redness" of seeing red, the "hurtfulness" of pain) — seem to be more than just functional role. If two systems have identical functional organization but one has inverted qualia (what my "red" looks like is your "green"), functionalism cannot distinguish them.
- **The Chinese Room** (Searle, 1980): a person in a room who follows rules for manipulating Chinese symbols (without understanding Chinese) implements the same input-output function as a Chinese speaker, but does not *understand* Chinese. Syntactic manipulation is not sufficient for semantic understanding.

### Functionalism and Cognitive Science

The functional/computational picture of mind grounds most of mainstream cognitive science:
- **Classical AI** (Newell, Simon): the mind is a general-purpose symbol manipulator; cognition is computation over symbolic representations.
- **Connectionism**: the mind is a neural network — a particular kind of functional organization implemented in parallel distributed processing.
- **The computational theory of mind** (Fodor): there is a **language of thought** (*mentalese*) — a system of internal symbolic representations with compositional syntax and semantics; propositional attitudes (beliefs, desires) are relations to sentences in mentalese.

## 2. Intentionality: Semantics and the Philosophy of Mind

**Intentionality** — the directedness of mental states toward objects and states of affairs — is Brentano's mark of the mental. The philosophical problem: how does a physical state of a brain come to be *about* anything?

### Dennett: Heterophenomenology and the Intentional Stance

**Daniel Dennett** (1942–): *The Intentional Stance* (1987), *Consciousness Explained* (1991). Dennett's strategy: **intentional stance** — we adopt the intentional stance when we predict behavior by attributing beliefs, desires, and rationality to a system. The stance is successful when it works — when it predicts behavior. It applies to humans, animals, thermostats, and chess computers equally; what distinguishes genuine belief from mere "as-if" belief is a pragmatic/functional question, not a categorical one.

**Heterophenomenology**: what people report about their own experience (the "phenomenological" data) is important evidence about cognitive architecture, but it is not incorrigible — introspection can be wrong. There is no Cartesian theater where the "real" qualia live; the appearance of being an appearance is all there is.

**Multiple drafts** (the model of consciousness): there is no single "central Cartesian theater" where consciousness happens; instead, multiple parallel processing streams create narrative drafts, none of which is the "real" consciousness. This connects to neuroscience and challenges the intuition that there is a single, unified moment of conscious awareness.

### Chalmers: The Hard Problem

**David Chalmers** (1966–): *The Conscious Mind* (1996). The **hard problem** of consciousness: why does any physical/functional process give rise to *subjective experience* — to there being *something it is like* to be in that state? The "easy" problems (how the brain integrates information, responds to stimuli, controls behavior) are hard scientifically but in principle reducible to functional explanation. The hard problem is why functional solutions to the easy problems don't automatically solve the question of phenomenal experience.

The conceivability of **philosophical zombies** — beings physically and functionally identical to us but with no inner experience whatsoever — suggests (if zombies are metaphysically possible) that functional organization is not sufficient for consciousness. This argument challenges physicalism.

Chalmers's resolution: **naturalistic dualism** — phenomenal properties are fundamental features of nature (not reducible to the physical), related to functional organization by psychophysical laws analogous to natural laws. Or: **panpsychism** — proto-phenomenal properties are present even in simple physical systems and combine into the full phenomenal consciousness of complex systems.

## 3. Philosophy of Language: After Frege and Russell

### Grice: Meaning and Implicature

**H.P. Grice** (1913–1988): *Meaning* (1957), *Logic and Conversation* (1975). Grice distinguishes:
- **Natural meaning**: "those spots mean measles" — a causal/evidential relation.
- **Non-natural meaning**: speaker meaning — what a speaker intends to communicate by an utterance.

Non-natural meaning involves a complex intention: the speaker intends (1) to produce an effect in the hearer, (2) by the hearer recognizing that intention. Meaning is a form of communicative intention — not just signaling but making transparent one's own communicative intent.

**Conversational implicature**: what is communicated beyond what is literally said. Cooperative conversation is governed by the **Cooperative Principle** (make your contribution appropriate to the purpose of the conversation) and four **maxims**: Quantity (be as informative as needed, not more), Quality (be truthful, don't say what you believe false), Relation (be relevant), Manner (be clear, avoid obscurity and ambiguity). Hearers interpret utterances by assuming conformity with these maxims — or by inferring that apparent violation is deliberate, generating pragmatic "implicatures."

Classic example: A says "How's the new employee?" B says "He's always on time." B has said nothing unfalse (Quantity+Quality maintained) but has implicated (by what B says being relevant under Relation) that there is little else positive to say. The implicature is cancellable (unlike logical entailment), non-detachable (any way of saying the same thing would carry the same implicature), and conventionally generated by the conversational context.

Grician implicature is foundational for:
- **Formal pragmatics** (the study of meaning beyond literal sentence meaning)
- **Relevance theory** (Sperber and Wilson — a cognitive reformulation of Grice)
- **Cognitive linguistics** (the pragmatics-semantics interface — how construal and context shape interpretation)

### Kripke and Rigid Designation

**Saul Kripke** (1940–): *Naming and Necessity* (1970/1980) — the most influential work in philosophy of language since Frege.

Against the Fregean/Russellian description theory of names: a proper name like "Aristotle" does not mean "the teacher of Alexander the Great" or any other description — if it did, then "Aristotle was not the teacher of Alexander" would be analytically false (false by meaning), which it isn't. Names are **rigid designators** — they pick out the same individual in all possible worlds (all counterfactual scenarios). "Aristotle might not have taught Alexander" is a coherent counterfactual; "The teacher of Alexander might not have taught Alexander" is incoherent.

Names pick out individuals through a **causal-historical chain** of reference — not through associated descriptions. When Aristotle was first named, the name was attached to the individual in a baptism; subsequent uses of the name refer to that same individual through a chain of transmission, even as descriptions associated with the name change (if we discovered that Aristotle didn't actually teach Alexander, we wouldn't conclude there was no Aristotle).

Consequences for the metaphysics of necessity:
- "Aristotle is Aristotle" is necessarily true (trivially).
- "Hesperus is Phosphorus" (the morning star is the evening star) is **necessarily true** (they are the same object — Venus — in all worlds), though the identity was an **empirical discovery** (not knowable a priori). This shows that necessity (metaphysical) and a-priority (epistemological) come apart — some necessary truths are only knowable a posteriori.
- "Water is H₂O" is necessarily true (water just is H₂O), though this too was an empirical discovery.
- "This lectern is made of wood" — if it is, is it necessarily wood? Kripke's **essentialism**: some properties are essential to objects — this particular lectern could not have been made of ice (even if it looks just the same); its material origin is essential to it.

**Semantic externalism** (with Putnam): the meaning of natural-kind terms like "water" is not constituted by speakers' descriptions (everyone's description might pick out a different liquid on a twin earth) but by the actual nature of the substance referred to. "Meaning ain't in the head" — semantic content is constituted by the world and the causal-historical connections between words and world.

### Putnam: Twin Earth and the Division of Linguistic Labor

**Hilary Putnam** (1926–2016): the **Twin Earth** thought experiment (*The Meaning of "Meaning"*, 1975). Imagine a planet exactly like Earth in 1750 except that what fills rivers and rain on Twin Earth is not H₂O but some other liquid (XYZ) with all the same macroscopic properties (colorless, tasteless, drinkable). Oscar on Earth uses "water" and Twin Oscar on Twin Earth uses "water" — they are in identical mental states (this is 1750 — no one knows molecular chemistry). Yet their words refer to different substances: Oscar's "water" means H₂O; Twin Oscar's "water" means XYZ.

Conclusion: **mental states (in the Cartesian, individualist sense) do not determine extension** (what a term refers to). Two people can be in identical mental states yet their words have different meanings. The environment (and causal-historical connections to it) is partly constitutive of meaning.

This is semantic externalism — the opposite of Descartes's internalism (what the mind contains determines meaning). Meaning and mental content are partly constituted by the world.

**Division of linguistic labor**: ordinary speakers defer to experts (chemists, botanists) for the fixation of reference of natural-kind terms. When I use "gold," I defer to metallurgists to fix what counts as gold — the meaning is not fully in my head but distributed through the linguistic community.

## 4. Anomalous Monism and the Philosophy of Mind

**Donald Davidson** (1917–2003): **anomalous monism** — every mental event is identical to some physical event (monism), but there are no strict psychophysical laws connecting mental event types to physical event types (anomalism). The **rationality constraints** that govern mental concepts (consistency, coherence, rationality) are irreducibly normative — they cannot be reduced to the causal-mechanical laws of physics.

This is a sophisticated physicalism that preserves the irreducibility of the intentional without positing non-physical entities. Mental properties supervene on physical properties (no mental difference without a physical difference) but are not reducible to them. The **principle of charity** (we must interpret speakers as largely rational and correct if we are to interpret them at all) is a holistic constraint on interpretation that has no analogue in physics.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

The 20th-century philosophy of mind traces a trajectory from Cartesian dualism (two substances) through Ryle's dispositional behaviorism through type-identity theory to functionalism and then to the fragmentation of functionalism (by qualia, by semantic externalism, by neuroscience). The convergence point is: mental states are multiply realized, semantically externalist, intentionally characterized, and involve phenomenal features that resist functional reduction.

This convergence is directly relevant to cognitive science: the computational theory of mind (Fodor) is challenged by connectionism (no explicit symbolic representation), by embodied cognition (no central processor independent of the body-environment system), and by the hard problem of consciousness. The philosophy of mind from the 1970s onward is philosophy of cognitive science.

### Language and meaning

Kripke, Putnam, and semantic externalism revolutionize the theory of reference: words are connected to the world not through descriptions in the head but through real-world causal chains and the actual natures of things. Combined with Grice's implicature (what is communicated beyond what is literally said) and speech act theory (Austin, Searle), the result is a mature philosophy of language that distinguishes:
- **Semantics**: literal sentence meaning (truth-conditions)
- **Pragmatics**: speaker meaning (what is communicated given context, cooperative principles)
- **Reference**: how terms connect to the world (rigidly, through causal chains, with deference to experts)

These distinctions are foundational for linguistics, cognitive science, and cognitive linguistics.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

The philosophy of action (Davidson) grounds ethics in a theory of rational agency: action is explained by beliefs and desires (rationalization, not just causation); responsibility requires that the agent's mental states are causally efficacious in producing action. This grounds a broadly Kantian account of agency (the normative dimension of the intentional) in a naturalist metaphysics.

The narrative self (MacIntyre, Ricoeur, Taylor): identities are constituted not by Cartesian substances or functionalist systems but by narratives embedded in traditions and communities. Charles Taylor's *Sources of the Self* (1989) traces the moral sources that define modern identity through the tradition covered in this survey — from Plato and Aristotle through Augustine, Descartes, Locke, the romantics, Nietzsche, and the phenomenologists.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

Semantic externalism has implications for the philosophy of science: natural-kind terms are not defined by operational procedures but by the actual natures of the natural kinds — the reference of "electron" is fixed by its actual physics, not by any description physicists use. This grounds **scientific realism** — scientific theories are genuinely about the world (not just empirically adequate predictive tools) — and anti-reductionism (higher-level sciences like psychology and biology are not eliminable in favor of physics, because their natural kinds are multiply realized).

## The Arc Forward

Philosophy of mind:
- **Cognitive neuroscience**: the interface between philosophy of mind and neuroscience — Churchland's eliminactive materialism (folk psychology will be replaced by neuroscience), the neural correlates of consciousness, and the detailed neuroscience of attention, memory, and action.
- **Enactivism** (Varela, Thompson, Evan Thompson): moving beyond the computational model to a phenomenologically-informed biology — mind as life, cognition as embodied action.
- **4E cognition** (Embodied, Embedded, Enacted, Extended): the synthesis of phenomenological and pragmatist insights with cognitive science.

Philosophy of language:
- **Formal pragmatics** (Sperber and Wilson's relevance theory, dynamic semantics).
- **Radical interpretation** (Davidson) and **interpretivism** (Brandom).
- **Experimental philosophy** (x-phi): using empirical psychological methods to test philosophical claims about concepts and intuitions.
