# Aristotle: Systematizer, Naturalist, and the Foundation of Western Science (384–322 BCE)

If Plato is the philosopher of ascent — the drive from the visible to the invisible, from opinion to knowledge, from matter to Form — Aristotle is the philosopher of *descent*: the Forms are not elsewhere, they are *here*, in things; the proper study of philosophy is the world as it actually is, investigated through careful observation and rigorous argument. Aristotle is the great systematizer: he produced the first treatises in logic, physics, metaphysics, psychology, biology, ethics, politics, rhetoric, and poetics. The sheer range is stunning, and each of these treatises is genuinely foundational — the first serious investigation of its subject in the Western record.

Aristotle studied in Plato's Academy for twenty years, from the age of seventeen until Plato's death in 347 BCE. He then tutored the young Alexander of Macedon (later "the Great") from 343 to 340 BCE — one of the stranger juxtapositions in intellectual history. He returned to Athens to found his own school, the Lyceum, in 335 BCE. The works that survive are almost certainly lecture notes or working texts, not the finished literary dialogues he also wrote (now lost). They have the character of dense, technical argument rather than lively conversation — a reversal of the literary priority between the two great figures.

## 1. Against Plato: Hylomorphism and the Immanence of Form

Aristotle's first and most important philosophical move is his critique of the Theory of Forms. The problem is not that there are no universals — there obviously are: many things are beautiful, many are just, many are human. The problem is that the Platonic picture mislocates them. Separate, transcendent Forms are explanatorily useless:

- **The Third Man regress** (which Plato himself noticed): positing a Form to explain what many things have in common requires a further Form to explain what the first Form has in common with the things it explains, and so on.
- **The mystery of participation**: how does a particular beautiful thing "participate in" or "imitate" the Form of Beauty? No account of this relation is coherent — it is a metaphor, not an explanation.
- **The point of forms is explanation**: we want to know what makes *this* thing what it is, here, present. The answer cannot be something wholly elsewhere.

Aristotle's alternative is **hylomorphism** (*hyle* = matter, *morphe* = form). Every natural substance is a compound of matter and form — not as two *things* glued together, but as two aspects of a single reality, distinguishable in analysis but inseparable in existence. The matter is what something is made of; the form is its organizing structure, what makes it the *kind* of thing it is. The form of a dog is not a Platonic Dog-Form somewhere in a separate realm; it is the actual structural-functional principle immanent in this dog that makes it a dog rather than a wolf or a rock.

This move has significant advantages:
- The form of a thing is fully present in the thing and explains its behavior directly and locally.
- It accommodates individual variation: Socrates and Callias are both human (sharing their form) but are different individuals (differing in their matter).
- It explains natural change: a seed becomes a tree because the form of the tree is the goal (*telos*) toward which the seed's matter is organized. Development is not an accident but the actualization of potential.

## 2. Actuality, Potentiality, and the Four Causes

Aristotle's response to the Parmenidean problem — how can change be real if only being is and non-being is not? — is the distinction between **actuality** (*energeia* or *entelecheia*) and **potentiality** (*dynamis*). An acorn is potentially an oak tree but actually an acorn. Change is the actualization of what is potential — a passage from "potential X" to "actual X." This is real, not illusory, because the potential is a real (if unrealized) feature of the current thing: the acorn *really is* oak-tree-able in a way that a rock is not.

The **four causes** provide the complete explanatory framework for natural change. For anything that comes to be, there are four causal factors:

1. **Material cause** (*causa materialis*): what it is made of. The matter from which the statue is made.
2. **Formal cause** (*causa formalis*): its form or essence — the structural pattern that makes it what it is. The plan or design of the statue.
3. **Efficient cause** (*causa efficiens*): what initiates the change. The sculptor's action.
4. **Final cause** (*causa finalis*): the *telos*, the purpose or end toward which the change is directed. The intended statue, the completed artifact.

For natural things (as opposed to artifacts), form and final cause typically coincide: the *telos* of an oak seed is the form of the mature oak, which is also its formal cause. Natural explanation must include teleology — explaining what something is *for* — because natural processes are characteristically directed toward ends. This is Aristotle's sharpest disagreement with Democritean mechanism, which recognizes only material and efficient causes.

The teleology is *immanent*, not transcendent: purposes are built into natural things as their organizing principles, not imposed from outside by a divine craftsman (contra Plato's demiurge). This makes Aristotle's teleology naturalistically respectable in a way Plato's is not — it is closer to the modern biologist's talk of functions (the function of the heart is to pump blood) than to cosmic design.

The **Unmoved Mover** (*Metaphysics* XII): the ultimate causal explanation of the cosmos is something that moves without being moved — pure actuality without potentiality, pure form without matter. It moves the cosmos not by acting on it (that would involve potentiality) but by being the object of desire and thought — the cosmos strives toward it as the highest good. This is not quite the personal God of Christianity, but it is the conceptual ancestor, and Aquinas will argue that it *is* the Christian God.

## 3. Logic: The Organon

Aristotle invented formal logic — there is nothing like it anywhere in the prior tradition, East or West, though there were concurrent developments in Indian logic (see `non-western/indian-philosophy.md`). The logical treatises, gathered under the title *Organon* ("instrument"), include:

- **Categories**: an analysis of the different kinds of things that can be said, organized around the question: what kinds of things can be the ultimate subject of predication? The answer is *substance* — individual things like Socrates or this cat — which have various predicates attributed to them: quantity, quality, relation, place, time, etc.
- **On Interpretation**: the basic units of assertion (subject, predicate, affirmation, denial); the square of opposition (relationships between universal/particular, affirmative/negative propositions).
- **Prior Analytics**: syllogistic logic — the systematic analysis of deductive inference in terms of three figures of syllogism, their valid moods, and how they reduce to one another. The *Barbara* syllogism: all M are P; all S are M; therefore all S are P. This is the first *formal* system of logic — not just good arguments but an analysis of the *form* of valid inference abstracted from content.
- **Posterior Analytics**: the theory of scientific *demonstration* (*apodeixis*) — how we go from first principles (definitions, axioms) to scientific knowledge. Science is demonstrative — it shows why its conclusions follow necessarily from their principles. This shapes the model of science from Euclid through Descartes.
- **Topics** and **Sophistical Refutations**: the analysis of dialectical (probable, not demonstrative) reasoning, and of fallacies.

The logical tradition Aristotle founded remained largely unchallenged until Frege's *Begriffsschrift* (1879) created quantificational logic. For two millennia, to learn logic was to learn Aristotle's logic.

Porphyry's later *Introduction* to the *Categories* raised the "problem of universals" — one of the central problems of medieval philosophy directly generated by Aristolelian logic: are species and genera (*universalia*) real things existing in the world, or only in the mind, or merely names?

## 4. De Anima: The Soul as Form of the Body

*De Anima* ("On the Soul") is Aristotle's psychology and one of the most directly relevant texts for the mind-body discussions in this memory system's existing files (`emergence-consciousness-iit.md`, `llm-vs-human-mind-comparative-analysis.md`).

The soul is the *form* of the living body — not a thing separate from the body but the body's organizing principle, what makes a living body alive. "If the eye were an animal, sight would be its soul." The soul is not trapped in the body; the soul-body compound is one integrated thing. There is no more a soul-body problem for Aristotle than there is a wax-shape problem: once you understand that the shape is not a separate thing from the wax but is the form the wax has, the question "where does the shape go when the wax melts?" dissolves.

This sounds like a naturalization of the soul — and for the nutritive soul (governing growth and reproduction, shared with plants) and the sensitive soul (governing perception and desire, shared with animals), it is. But the **intellect** (*nous*) is anomalous. Aristotle distinguishes:

- **Passive intellect**: the receptive potentiality for thought — analogous to a blank wax tablet before inscription.
- **Active intellect** (*nous poietikos*): the principle that "makes all things" as light makes colors actually visible. It is "separable, impassible, unmixed" and "this alone is immortal and eternal."

The active intellect passage (*De Anima* III.5) is among the most controversial in the entire Aristotelian corpus. If the soul's cognitive capacities are forms of the body, how can any part of the soul be separable from the body? Aristotle may have been positing an impersonal intellect that is not particular to any individual — a cosmic principle of intellectual actualization. Or he may have been inconsistent. Medieval Islamic and Christian philosophers debated the passage intensively (Averroes's "monopsychism" — one intellect for all humanity — was particularly controversial).

The theory of **perception** is subtle: when you perceive a red apple, the eye receives the *form* of redness without the matter (without turning red). The sense organ becomes formally, but not materially, like its object. This distinguishes genuine cognition from mere mechanical causation: the eye is affected by the apple in a way that preserves the apple's cognitive significance (its form) without becoming an apple. This is a precursor to the functionalist idea that mental states are defined by their functional role rather than their physical realization.

## 5. Ethics: Eudaimonia, Virtue, and the Mean

The *Nicomachean Ethics* is the most influential ethical text in the Western tradition. Its governing concept is *eudaimonia* — usually translated "happiness" but better rendered as "flourishing" or "living well." *Eudaimonia* is not a feeling but an activity: it is the activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, over a complete life, with sufficient external goods.

The move from Plato: instead of deriving ethics from a separate Form of the Good (ungrounded in ordinary life), Aristotle begins from an analysis of *function* (*ergon*). Every craft and activity aims at some good; ethics asks: what is the good for humans *as humans*? Answer: the exercise of the distinctively human function — rational activity — well. This naturalistic approach means that ethics is continuous with biology and psychology: human flourishing is the actualization of human nature.

**Virtue** (*arete*) is a *hexis* — a stable disposition to feel and act characteristically well. This distinguishes Aristotle from the Socratic intellectualist view that virtue is knowledge: you can *know* that courage is good while still having an undeveloped disposition to feel excessive fear. Virtues must be cultivated through habituation — you become courageous by practicing courageous actions until the appropriate emotional responses become second nature. This is the "wisdom of practice" that MacIntyre will defend against the Enlightenment project in *After Virtue* (see `contemporary/ethics-political-philosophy.md`).

The **Doctrine of the Mean**: virtues are means between extremes of excess and deficiency. Courage is the mean between recklessness (excess of confidence) and cowardice (excess of fear). The mean is not arithmetic (50% between two extremes) but relative to the person and situation — a skilled athlete needs more food than a sedentary person; the mean for each is different. Practical wisdom (*phronesis*) is the meta-virtue: the capacity to perceive what kind of situation one is in and to respond with the appropriate virtue.

**Two kinds of virtue**: moral virtues (habits of right feeling and action, developed through practice) and intellectual virtues (developed through teaching). The highest intellectual virtue, and hence the highest life, is *theoria* — contemplation. The contemplative life, in which reason reflects on the highest objects, is the most fully human and most fully fulfilling. This seems to conflict with Aristotle's political philosophy; he tries to harmonize them by arguing that theoria requires a political community (which provides security and leisure) and that the virtuous politician is living a noble life even if not quite the highest one.

**Friendship** (*philia*) receives extended treatment: Aristotle distinguishes friendship based on utility, pleasure, and character. Only character-friendship (loving the other for who they are) is genuine philia and constitutes genuine flourishing. The good person needs friends as mirrors of virtue and as companions in the shared life of rational activity. This anticipates much in contemporary social philosophy and virtue ethics.

## 6. Politics, Poetics, and the Arc of the System

**Politics**: Humans are political animals (*zōon politikon*) — not humans who happen to form political associations, but beings whose nature is fully realized only within a *polis* (city-state). The city is prior to the individual in the sense that the individual is defined by their roles within the community. Aristotle classifies constitutions by who rules (one, few, many) and whether they rule for the common good (monarchy, aristocracy, polity) or for themselves (tyranny, oligarchy, democracy). The best practically achievable constitution is typically a mixed regime ("polity") balancing the claims of wealth and numbers — Aristotle is consistently anti-utopian compared to Plato.

**Poetics**: The first systematic theory of narrative and its power. Tragedy (the focus of what survives) is *mimesis* of *praxis* — an imitation of human action that is more philosophical than history because it shows universals (what kinds of people do what kinds of things) rather than particulars (what Alcibiades did). The primary element of tragedy is *muthos* — plot — not character, diction, thought, spectacle, or song. A well-formed plot has a beginning, middle, and end (not arbitrary); it involves a reversal (*peripeteia*) and a recognition (*anagnorisis*); it produces *catharsis* — a clarification or purgation of pity and fear. The protagonist's downfall issues from *hamartia* — a flaw, miscalculation, or missing of the mark.

The connection to cognitive science and narrative theory (`narrative-cognition.md`): Aristotle's claim that plot is the organizing principle of tragedy — not character — anticipates Ricoeur's argument that narrative identity is constituted by emplotment rather than by a fixed substantial self. The soul (form of the body) and narrative (form of action) are parallel Aristotelian moves: what matters is not the matter (body/events) but the organizing structure (soul/plot).

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Aristotle's hylomorphism radically naturalizes the mind. The soul is not a prisoner in the body (Plato) nor simply atoms (Democritus), but the body's form — and the intellect's reception of forms without matter models how mind can be informationally continuous with the world while remaining formally distinct from it. The active intellect's anomalous status raises, without resolving, the question of what in cognition exceeds naturalistic explanation.

The *Posterior Analytics*' model of scientific knowledge — demonstrative, grounded in first principles, universally valid — defines the ideal of knowledge for the next two millennia. It is both inspiring and limiting: science as Aristotle conceives it is about necessary truths, which makes it hard to accommodate probability, induction, and the fallibilism of modern science.

### Language and meaning

The *Categories* is simultaneously a logic and an ontology: it asks what kinds of things can be said and uses the answer to organize a map of reality. The primary substance (individual things) is the ground of all predication. The problem of universals — are species and genera real, mental, or merely nominal? — directly generates the central controversy of medieval philosophy.

The *Rhetoric* is a serious study of the cognitive and emotional mechanisms through which persuasion works. Aristotle legitimates rhetoric as a systematic discipline rather than dismissing it (as Plato does in the *Gorgias*) as mere manipulation.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Aristotle's ethics is the most fully naturalistic ancient ethics. *Eudaimonia* is something humans can achieve in this world, through habituation, community, and the exercise of reason in action. It does not require Platonic contemplation of transcendent Forms or Stoic indifference to external goods.

The self for Aristotle is not a soul imprisoned in a body (Plato) or a social construction (some later views), but an embodied, rational, social animal whose identity is constituted by character — by the stable dispositions that make one the kind of person one is. This is the core of virtue ethics and the tradition MacIntyre revives.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

Aristotle founds natural science — in the sense of a systematic, empirical, observational investigation of the natural world — while insisting that explanation must include final causes. The Unmoved Mover is a philosophical-theological position reached by way of cosmological argument, not by revelation. It is the model for all subsequent cosmological arguments for the existence of God.

Aristotle's science shaped Western understanding of nature from the 4th century BCE to the 16th century CE — longer than any other scientific framework. Its eventual overthrow (Galileo, Descartes, Newton) required specifically overturning its teleological physics and its commitment to qualitative distinctions between kinds of substance. The early modern scientific revolution is in large part a systematic replacement of Aristotelian physics with mathematical mechanism.

## The Arc Forward

Aristotle's legacy divides. His logic was preserved, adapted, and extended by the Scholastics into the most powerful inferential system available before modern logic. His ethics was revived by Aquinas and remains the foundation of Catholic moral theology and contemporary virtue ethics. His biology — empirically richest of all his sciences — was not superseded until Darwin.

His physics and cosmology were overthrown, but the questions his physics raised — about teleology, natural kinds, the relationship between form and function — recur in philosophy of biology and philosophy of mind. The debate between Aristotelian hylomorphic dualism and Cartesian substance dualism, reconstructed by Putnam, is effectively a debate about whether functionalism (mental states as forms of the body) or substance dualism (minds as separate things) better accounts for cognition. The hylomorphic tradition looks increasingly attractive to philosophers of mind who want to avoid both Cartesian dualism and crude reductivism.
