# Contemporary Ethics and Political Philosophy (1971–present)

Contemporary ethics and political philosophy in the Anglo-American tradition were reshaped by John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971) — the most influential work of political philosophy in the 20th century. What follows traces the main lines of debate: the Rawlsian program and its critics (libertarian, communitarian, feminist, postcolonial), the revival of virtue ethics, the utilitarian-inspired effective altruism movement, and the most pressing current debates about global justice, political legitimacy, and the ethics of emerging technologies.

The continental tradition's contributions to ethics and politics were covered in the files on phenomenology/existentialism (Sartre, de Beauvoir, Camus) and continental structuralism-poststructuralism (Foucault, Deleuze) — here the focus is on the analytic tradition and on the debates that cut across the divide.

## 1. Rawls and the Liberal Tradition

### A Theory of Justice (1971)

**John Rawls** (1921–2002) set out to answer a central question in political philosophy: what principles should govern the basic structure of a just society? His method is contractualist: the principles of justice are those that rational persons would choose from behind a **veil of ignorance** — not knowing their place in society, their social class, their natural abilities, their conception of the good, or their psychological dispositions.

The devices:
- **Original position**: a hypothetical decision procedure, not a historical state of nature. Rational contractors behind the veil of ignorance choose principles to govern a society they will enter not knowing their position within it.
- **Veil of ignorance**: strips contractors of particular information, ensuring they cannot bargain for principles that favor their actual position. Forces choices that are impartial.

The principles chosen (Rawls argues):
1. **Equal basic liberties principle**: each person has a right to the most extensive system of equal basic liberties compatible with a similar system for all.
2. **Difference principle**: social and economic inequalities are permitted only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society.
3. **Fair equality of opportunity**: positions and offices must be genuinely open to all under conditions of fair opportunity.

The principles are lexically ordered: liberty takes priority over equality; fair opportunity takes priority over the difference principle.

**Justice as fairness**: the name Rawls gives to his account. The intuition: people in the original position, not knowing whether they will be the worst-off member of society, will choose the difference principle as a form of "maximin" (maximizing the minimum) — rational insurance against landing at the bottom.

### Political Liberalism (1993)

Rawls, responding to critics, distinguishes:
- **Comprehensive moral doctrines**: full accounts of the good life (utilitarian, Kantian, religious, etc.) — reasonable people disagree about these.
- **Political justice**: a module-style account of political principles, designed to be acceptable to people with different comprehensive doctrines, grounded in values already implicit in the public political culture of a democratic society.

**Public reason**: the constraint that political argument be conducted using publicly accessible reasons — not appeals to religious revelation or contested comprehensive moral doctrines. Citizens owe each other reasons they can accept as fellow free and equal citizens.

## 2. Critics of Rawls: Libertarianism and Communitarianism

### Nozick: Libertarian Response

**Robert Nozick** (1938–2002), *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974): a libertarian critique of Rawlsian distributive justice.

**Entitlement theory**: justice in holdings is not a matter of *patterned* distribution (who ends up with what) but of *historical* process. Holdings are just if they were justly acquired and justly transferred. The state has no right to redistribute holdings from the better-off to the worse-off — this violates rights.

**Wilt Chamberlain argument**: even if we start from a perfectly equal distribution, free transactions will quickly produce inequality (as people voluntarily give money to Chamberlain for basketball tickets). Any patterned principle of justice will continuously require interference with voluntary transactions — and hence with individual liberty.

**Minimal state**: Nozick argues that only a minimal "nightwatchman" state — protecting rights against force, theft, and fraud — is justified. The state cannot legitimately engage in positive redistribution.

**The experience machine** thought experiment: if you could plug into a machine that would give you any subjective experience you desired, for the rest of your life, would you? Most people say no — suggesting that what matters to us is not just the quality of experience but genuinely doing things, being a certain kind of person, engaging with the actual world. This is an argument against hedonistic versions of utilitarianism.

### Communitarian Critiques

The communitarian critics of Rawls (Michael Sandel, Alasdair MacIntyre, Charles Taylor, Michael Walzer) argue that liberal political philosophy rests on a defective picture of the self.

**Michael Sandel** (*Liberalism and the Limits of Justice*, 1982): Rawls's "unencumbered self" — the self behind the veil of ignorance — is not a realistic picture of human beings. We are constituted by our community memberships, traditions, and shared understandings; there is no self separable from these attachments that could choose its values from a position of pure neutrality. The liberal ideal of individual autonomy presupposes a picture of the self that is false.

**Alasdair MacIntyre** (*After Virtue*, 1981): the dominant modern ethical discourse (emotivism — moral claims express only emotional attitudes; Kantian deontology; Rawlsian contractualism) has failed because it has abandoned the Aristotelian framework of virtue, practice, and narrative within which moral concepts were at home. Modern ethics is an incoherent collection of inherited fragments. The recovery of ethics requires Aristotelian virtue ethics, grounded in the social practices and traditions within which virtues make sense — and narrative as the unity of a human life.

MacIntyre's account of **narrative identity** connects directly to Ricoeur's narrative philosophy: who we are is not the abstract rational will of Kant's ethics, nor the preference-bundle of economic theory, but a character in a story — embedded in a tradition, with a history, oriented by roles and commitments.

## 3. The Revival of Virtue Ethics

**Anscombe, Foot, Williams, MacIntyre**: the 20th-century revival of Aristotelian virtue ethics. G.E.M. **Anscombe**'s "Modern Moral Philosophy" (1958) argues that deontological and consequentialist ethics both rely on hollow concepts ("moral ought," "moral obligation") that have no content without the ancient teleological framework — and that philosophy should return to the Aristotelian concepts of virtue, practical wisdom (*phronesis*), and flourishing (*eudaimonia*).

**Philippa Foot** (*Natural Goodness*, 2001): natural goodness as the basis of virtue ethics — what is good for a human is determined by what kind of thing a human is (its natural kind / life form). Virtues are the traits that enable a human being to live and flourish as the kind of thing it is. This is a naturalistic virtue ethics that grounds moral evaluation in biology-like facts about human nature.

**Bernard Williams** (*Ethics and the Limits of Philosophy*, 1985): a serious critique of both Kantian and utilitarian ethical theory — both fail to account for personal integrity, special obligations, and the importance of projects that give a life its meaning. Williams's concept of **moral residue** (the remainder of guilt or regret that persists even when an agent does the right thing in a tragic situation) is incompatible with the utilitarian picture (in which doing the right action leaves no residue).

## 4. Utilitarian Extensions: Singer and Effective Altruism

**Peter Singer** (*Animal Liberation*, 1975; *Practical Ethics*, 1979; *The Life You Can Save*, 2009): the philosopher most responsible for the contemporary prominence of utilitarian thinking in ethics.

**The drowning child argument**: if I walk past a drowning child and fail to save it because getting wet would ruin my shoes, I'm morally culpable. But why is the distant child dying in poverty morally different? Distance does not alter moral importance; we have strong obligations to help those we can help, even at significant cost.

**Animal liberation and equal consideration**: if suffering is what matters morally, then animal suffering matters as much as human suffering — "speciesism" (giving more weight to suffering simply because it is human suffering) is unjustifiable, like racism. This grounds the moral importance of animal welfare.

**Effective altruism** (EA): the movement (partly inspired by Singer; developed by Toby Ord, William MacAskill, and others at Oxford) that we should use evidence and reason to determine how to do the most good with whatever resources we have — typically interpreted as donating to the most cost-effective charities focused on global poverty reduction or (in the long-termist version) on existential risk reduction.

The philosophical criticism: EA's consequentialist framework is itself contested; the "calculation" of value across different lives, different species, and different times relies on contested assumptions about welfare comparison and aggregation; "longtermism" — the view that we should weight the welfare of future beings heavily — has been criticized as potentially justifying extreme current sacrifice for speculative future benefits.

## 5. Feminist Ethics and Political Philosophy

**Carol Gilligan** (*In a Different Voice*, 1982): identifies an "ethics of care" as a distinctive moral orientation — typically (though not exclusively) associated with women's moral development — emphasizing relationships, context, and responsiveness to particular others, in contrast to the abstract principles of justice emphasized in Kohlberg's moral development theory (on which Rawls draws). Gilligan rejects the hierarchy (justice > care) as an artifact of male-biased research and male-dominated institutional practice.

**Nel Noddings** (*Caring*, 1984): develops care ethics as a systematic alternative to principle-based ethics. The fundamental moral relationship is the one-caring to the one-cared-for — a relation of attentive reception and responsive care that is the ground of all ethical life.

**Feminist political philosophy**: demanding that liberal political philosophy address the gendered character of the public/private distinction — liberal politics takes the public sphere (markets, elections, formal equality) as its domain and treats the private sphere (family, care work, domestic labor) as pre-political. But the private sphere is a principal site of gender subordination; feminist politics requires critique and transformation of both spheres.

**Martha Nussbaum**: the **capabilities approach** (developed with Amartya Sen) — what matters for justice is not the distribution of primary goods (Rawls) or welfare (utilitarianism) but people's actual capabilities — what they are capable of doing and being. The list of central human capabilities (bodily integrity, affiliation, play, control over one's environment, etc.) grounds a substantive account of justice that is sensitive to context, gender, and cultural difference.

**Judith Butler** (*Gender Trouble*, 1990): the most influential feminist philosopher of the late 20th century. **Gender performativity**: gender is not an expression of a prior natural sex but is constituted by the performative reiteration of gendered acts — the appearance of "gender" as a natural fact is itself an effect of the regulatory performance. This is simultaneously a critique of heteronormativity (the assumption that heterosexuality is natural/normal) and an account of the conditions of possibility of gender transformation.

## 6. Global Justice and Political Philosophy After Rawls

Rawls's later work (*The Law of Peoples*, 1999) refuses to extend the difference principle to global distribution — the duties of justice are owed only within political societies, not globally. This has generated enormous debate.

**Cosmopolitan critics** (Charles Beitz, Thomas Pogge, Simon Caney): the same arguments that support the difference principle domestically apply globally — wealth disparities between nations are morally arbitrary from the global standpoint; principles of justice must apply across national boundaries.

**Thomas Pogge**: a negative duties argument — wealthy countries are not merely failing to help the global poor; they are actively harming them by maintaining institutional arrangements (international trade rules, recognition of authoritarian government property rights, etc.) that deprive the poor of what they are owed. This is not (merely) a duty of beneficence but a duty to stop causing harm.

**Postcolonial political philosophy**: critiques of the Western liberal tradition's complicity in imperialism and colonialism. The liberal tradition (Mill, Locke) justified colonial domination as bringing civilization to "backward" peoples who could not govern themselves. Contemporary postcolonial political philosophy (Frantz Fanon, Aime Cesaire, Achille Mbembe, Gayatri Spivak) insists on the philosophical reckoning that is required — not merely adding marginalized perspectives but confronting how the liberal tradition's central concepts were shaped by colonial relations.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Epistemology and political philosophy intersect in debates about **testimony** and **epistemic injustice**. **Miranda Fricker** (*Epistemic Injustice*, 2007) identifies two forms: **testimonial injustice** (giving someone less credibility as a knower because of their identity — as a woman, as a person of color) and **hermeneutical injustice** (having no adequate concepts available to understand an aspect of one's experience — as in the absence of "sexual harassment" as a recognized concept before feminism named it). Epistemic injustice is not merely practical but philosophical: it reveals that credibility judgments are socially structured and systematically biased.

**Standpoint epistemology** (feminist, postcolonial): social location affects epistemic position; marginalized groups may have epistemic advantages in seeing aspects of social reality that dominant groups are motivated to overlook. This is not pure relativism but the claim that good epistemology requires taking seriously the knowledge perspectives of the marginalized.

### Language and meaning

Political philosophy is deeply shaped by the linguistic turn — by questions about what political concepts mean and how they do their work. Rawls's "veil of ignorance" and "original position" are constructive idealizations, not metaphysical claims; "capabilities" (Nussbaum) must be specified in language that is sensitive to cultural contexts; "care" (feminist ethics) is not merely a psychological category but an ethical concept that had been systematically devalued.

**Deliberative democracy** (Habermas, Benhabib, Cohen): the model of political legitimacy grounded in free and equal public discourse — laws are legitimate when they could be justified in a free and equal discourse. This is explicitly modeled on speech-act theory (Habermas explicitly draws on Austin and Grice) and on communicative rationality as opposed to strategic rationality.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

The central debate: what is the self that ethics and politics address?
- **Liberal individualism**: the self is prior to its social roles; individual autonomy is the fundamental political value.
- **Communitarianism**: the self is constituted by community; the traditions and practices of specific communities are the condition of any meaningful choice.
- **Feminist/postcolonial critique**: the "universal" liberal self was a very specific historical — male, affluent, European — subject; the extension of liberal rights to those previously excluded required transformation, not just application, of the liberal framework.
- **Care ethics**: the self is fundamentally relational and dependent; vulnerability and interdependence are not deficits to be overcome but constitutive features of human life.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

**Rawls's public reason** and its limits: should religious arguments be excluded from public political discourse? Rawls's answer (yes, under conditions of reasonable pluralism) has generated debate. **Nicholas Wolterstorff** and other religious philosophers argue that excluding religious reasons from public discourse is itself a form of discrimination — citizens cannot be required to abandon their deepest convictions when entering the public sphere. **Habermas** responds with a more nuanced account: citizens may enter religious reasons into public discourse but must be willing to translate them into publicly accessible terms.

**Naturalistic ethics**: if there are genuine moral facts, what is their metaphysical status? The most live contemporary positions: (a) **Non-naturalism** (Parfit, Scanlon) — moral facts are real but not natural facts; (b) **Constructivism** (Rawls, Korsgaard) — moral facts are constructed by us under appropriate conditions of reflection; (c) **Naturalism** (Foot) — moral facts are natural facts about human flourishing.

## The Arc Forward

- **Political philosophy and AI**: questions about the rights of AI systems, the justice of algorithmic decision-making, the political economy of AI development — emerging at the frontier.
- **Climate ethics and intergenerational justice**: moral obligations to future generations generated by climate change; the debate between discounting the future (standard economic approach) and giving equal weight to future suffering (Parfit, Stern).
- **Democratic theory in crisis**: questions about the legitimacy of democratic institutions designed for an earlier era, the rise of authoritarian populism, and the philosophy of political resilience.
