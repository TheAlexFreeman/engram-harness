# Philosophy of Science: Popper, Kuhn, and the Contemporary Landscape

Philosophy of science asks: what distinguishes genuine scientific knowledge from pseudo-science, opinion, or common sense? What is the structure of scientific explanation? How do theories relate to evidence? How does science develop over time? Does science give us the truth about reality, or a useful instrument for prediction? These questions become pressing in the 20th century as physics (relativity, quantum mechanics) and biology (genetics, evolutionary theory) produce their most dramatic results while also generating foundational puzzles.

---

## 1. The Logical Empiricist Inheritance and Its Problems

The Vienna Circle's **verification principle** — a proposition is meaningful only if it is either analytically true or empirically verifiable — provided the first rigorous philosophical account of what distinguishes science from metaphysics. But this program faced devastating internal difficulties:

1. **The criterion itself is unverifiable**: the verification principle is neither analytic nor empirically verifiable by its own standard. It commits suicide.
2. **Laws of nature cannot be verified**: general laws ("all copper conducts electricity") cannot be verified by any finite number of observations — they apply to infinitely many cases.
3. **Observational sentences are theory-laden**: there is no theory-neutral observation language; what counts as an observation depends on the theoretical framework.

These problems motivated the alternatives of Popper and of Quine (Quine's contribution was covered in the analytic-foundations file).

## 2. Popper: Falsificationism

**Karl Popper** (1902–1994), *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1934, English 1959); *Conjectures and Refutations* (1963); *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945).

### The Demarcation Problem

Popper rejects verification as the criterion of scientific meaningfulness. His alternative: **falsifiability**. A theory is scientific if it is capable of being falsified by some possible observation — if it makes predictions that could in principle be shown to be false. Non-falsifiable propositions (metaphysics, Freudian psychoanalysis, astrology) are not meaningless — Popper doesn't endorse the positivist dismissal — but they are not *scientific*.

What struck Popper: Freudian theory and Adlerian theory could *explain* any human behavior — they were immune to disconfirmation. But Einstein's theory of general relativity made specific, concrete predictions (the bending of light by gravity, confirmed by Eddington's 1919 eclipse expedition) that could have easily proved the theory false. The asymmetry: genuine science is willing to put itself at risk.

### Hypothetico-Deductivism and the Method of Conjectures and Refutations

Science proceeds not by induction (which Popper, following Hume, holds to be logically unjustifiable) but by **conjecture and refutation**:
1. Identify a problem.
2. Propose a bold conjecture (hypothesis) — the bolder the better, since bold conjectures have more falsifiable content.
3. Attempt to falsify it. If falsified, revise or abandon.
4. If not falsified (corroborated), this is not confirmation — it is a failed attempt to falsify. The theory survives for now.

No amount of corroboration can confirm a theory — but a single falsification is logically decisive (by modus tollens: if T implies O, and not-O, then not-T). Science progresses through the *elimination of errors*, not through the accumulation of verifications.

### Critical Rationalism

Popper's broader epistemological view: **critical rationalism**. The rational attitude is to hold beliefs provisionally, to subject them to the most stringent available tests, to revise them when they fail. This is Popperian science extended to all rational inquiry — including politics. *The Open Society* applies this: the open society is one that permits criticism of institutions and ideas; totalitarian ideology (Marxism, fascism) protects itself from criticism — it is the political analogue of irrefutable pseudo-science.

### Problems with Falsificationism

1. **The Duhem-Quine problem**: a falsifying observation refutes the whole system of theoretical assumptions, not a single hypothesis. Scientists always have the option of modifying auxiliary hypotheses rather than the central theory — and often this is rational (as when the anomalous orbit of Uranus was explained by positing a new planet rather than rejecting Newton's laws). This means falsification is never logically decisive.
2. **Ad hoc modifications**: Popper's response is a methodological injunction against *ad hoc* modifications — modifications that do not generate additional testable predictions. But the line between legitimate and illegitimate modification is unclear.
3. **Falsification of novel domains**: string theory, for example, has been criticized for making no falsifiable predictions — yet many physicists regard it as genuine science.

## 3. Kuhn: Paradigms and Scientific Revolutions

**Thomas Kuhn** (1922–1996), *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) — probably the most widely read philosophy of science text of the 20th century, with enormous influence in history, sociology, and cultural theory.

### Normal Science and Paradigms

Science does not normally operate as Popper describes — with bold conjectures continuously subjected to potential falsification. Most scientific work is **normal science**: puzzle-solving within an accepted **paradigm** — the matrix of shared assumptions, exemplary problem-solutions (*exemplars*), standard instruments, and background knowledge within which a scientific community operates.

A paradigm includes: theoretical commitments (Newton's laws), methodological norms (experimental design), standard examples of how to apply the theory (the paradigm cases from which students learn), and even ontological commitments (what kinds of things exist). Scientists working within a paradigm do not question it; they use it to solve puzzles. Anomalies (data or observations that the paradigm cannot account for) are initially set aside — they are failures of the scientist, not of the paradigm.

### Scientific Revolutions

When anomalies accumulate sufficiently — when normal science fails to solve more and more puzzles — the field enters a period of **crisis**. During crisis, paradigm alternatives begin to proliferate; scientists begin to do foundational work rather than puzzle-solving; the authority of the paradigm is questioned.

A **scientific revolution** occurs when the scientific community shifts allegiance from one paradigm to a new one — not by the gradual accumulation of evidence (in the normal-science sense) but by a gestalt-like switch. **Incommensurability**: paradigms before and after a revolution are not strictly comparable — they define different problems, use the same words differently, and cannot be evaluated by a shared standard. Newton and Einstein share the word "mass" but mean different things by it.

### The Sociological Challenge

Kuhn's account raises the question: if paradigm shifts are not driven purely by evidence (since evidence is always theory-laden and the competing theories may be incommensurable), what drives them? Kuhn suggests: social factors — the persuasiveness of key figures, generational change, institutional dynamics. The old paradigm dies with its adherents; the young generation is converted.

This is not Kuhn's own reading — he is not a social constructivist — but his work inspired the **sociology of scientific knowledge** (SSK, Edinburgh School: Bloor, Barnes) which holds that the content of scientific knowledge is shaped by social interests and cannot be explained purely by "nature" or rational evidence-tracking.

## 4. Lakatos, Feyerabend, and the Responses to Kuhn

### Lakatos: Research Programs

**Imre Lakatos** (1922–1974) attempts to save Popperian falsificationism from the Duhem-Quine objection while accounting for the historical pattern Kuhn describes. The unit of analysis is not the single theory but the **research program** — a series of theories related by a common hard core (unfalsifiable by methodological decision) and a *protective belt* of auxiliary hypotheses (which may be modified to accommodate anomalies).

A research program is **progressive** if successive theories within it increase the empirical content — make new testable predictions that are confirmed. It is **degenerative** if successive theories only accommodate anomalies *post hoc* without new predictions. Scientists should shift from degenerating to progressive programs — but this is a rational choice that may take time to become clear. Lakatos preserves rationality while allowing the flexibility that Kuhn observed.

### Feyerabend: Against Method

**Paul Feyerabend** (1924–1994), *Against Method* (1975): the most radical critique of the normative ambitions of philosophy of science. Historical examination of famous scientific episodes (Galileo's argument for heliocentrism) shows that no proposed methodological rule — not Popper's falsificationism, not Lakatos's progressiveness criterion — was consistently followed. Galileo violated the rules: his telescopic evidence was not unambiguous; he appealed to circular motion principles from Copernicus; he used rhetoric and propaganda as well as argument.

**"Anything goes"**: Feyerabend's provocative slogan — there is no universal methodological rule that science always follows and should always follow. Methodological pluralism, including the willingness to preserve and develop theories that are refuted by current evidence (because they may become productive later), is the actual practice of successful science.

Feyerabend also argues for the cultural and epistemic value of non-Western knowledge traditions against the assumption that Western science is the uniquely legitimate knowledge system — an early argument for epistemic pluralism.

## 5. Scientific Realism vs. Antirealism

The deepest metaphysical question in philosophy of science: does science tell us how the world *really is*, or only how to predict observations successfully?

### Scientific Realism

**Scientific realism**: the theories of mature science are approximately true, and the unobservable entities they postulate (electrons, genes, fields) really exist. The best argument: the **No Miracles Argument** (Hilary Putnam) — if scientific theories were not approximately true, it would be a miracle that they so reliably produce accurate predictions of novel phenomena. The success of science is best explained by its truth.

### Antirealist Alternatives

**Instrumentalism**: theories are not true or false descriptions of reality but instruments for making predictions. "The electron" is not a real entity but a useful cognitive tool. The electron has whatever ontological status a hammer has — it is an artifact of practice, not a discovered feature of the world.

**Constructive Empiricism** (Bas van Fraassen, *The Scientific Image*, 1980): science aims to produce theories that are empirically adequate — correctly describing all observable phenomena — not theories that are true of unobservable reality. We have no epistemic right to believe in unobservable entities; we should believe only what is observable in principle. (Note: this is not instrumentalism — the observable/unobservable distinction is real for van Fraassen, unlike for the instrumentalist; he just holds that belief should not extend beyond the observable.)

**The argument for antirealism**: **Pessimistic Meta-Induction** (Laudan) — the history of science shows that successful, mature theories have repeatedly been replaced by theories that are conceptually incompatible with them (phlogiston was replaced by Lavoisier's oxygen theory; the luminiferous ether was replaced by electromagnetic field theory). If successful theories of the past were not true (as their successors show), why should we believe our current successful theories are true?

**Scientific realist responses**: **Structural Realism** (Worrall) — what stays stable through theory change is not the entities but the mathematical structure. Maxwell's equations survived the transition from ether theory to special relativity; what was preserved was the structure, not the ontology. We can be realists about structure without being realists about the specific natures of the underlying entities.

## 6. Philosophy of Special Sciences

The most productive contemporary philosophy of science is philosophy of *specific* sciences — asking the methodological and conceptual questions that arise within particular domains.

### Philosophy of Biology

**Evolutionary theory**: the philosophical interpretation of Darwinian natural selection has generated debate about the level of selection (gene, organism, group, species); the role of adaptation vs. drift; and what counts as a biological function. **Ruth Millikan**'s teleosemantic theory (biological functions are historically determined — the function of the heart is to pump blood because ancestors with organs that pumped blood reproduced more; this grounds a theory of intentional content in biological history) connects philosophy of biology directly to philosophy of mind.

The species problem: what are species? The Biological Species Concept (Ernst Mayr: a species is a group of interbreeding natural populations reproductively isolated from other groups) is the most influential but faces problems with fossils, bacteria, unisexual organisms. Its philosophical interest: it reveals that the concept of "natural kind" — central to realism about science — is more complex than it appears.

**Altruism and evolutionary theory**: Hamilton's kin selection and inclusive fitness; Trivers's reciprocal altruism; the problem of the evolution of cooperation. The connection to ethics: can evolutionary psychology explain moral intuitions? Does this explain (in a reductionist way) or merely show that we have reason to be suspicious of our moral intuitions?

### Philosophy of Physics

**Quantum mechanics** presents the most profound foundational puzzles in the history of science. The measurement problem: quantum systems evolve according to the Schrödinger equation (deterministic, linear, wave-like) until measured, at which point they appear to collapse to a definite state (probabilistic, non-linear). What is measurement? Why does collapse happen? The philosophical interpretations:
- **Copenhagen interpretation**: the wave function is not a description of physical reality but a tool for predicting observations. Questions about "what happens before measurement" are meaningless.
- **Everettian / Many-Worlds**: the Schrödinger equation is always true; there is no collapse; "measurement" causes the observer to become entangled with the measured system, producing a superposition of branches. All outcomes occur in different branches.
- **Pilot wave (de Broglie-Bohm)**: the wave function is real and guides a real particle; quantum mechanics is deterministic but non-local.
- **GRW / Objective Collapse**: the collapse is a real physical process (not caused by observation) that occurs spontaneously with low probability.

Each interpretation has different implications for the nature of probability, the role of the observer, and the character of physical reality.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Philosophy of science is epistemology applied to our most successful knowledge-producing institutions. The debate between realism and antirealism is ultimately about the relationship between our best theories and the world they purport to describe — about whether scientific knowledge puts us in contact with mind-independent reality or only with the structure of our experience.

Kuhn's account of normal science has clear cognitive science implications: scientists working within a paradigm see the world through its lens — there is a deep analogy to Gestalt psychology (Kuhn acknowledges this) and to the claim in cognitive linguistics that conceptual frameworks structure perception. The history of science illustrates how deeply our conceptual apparatus shapes what we observe.

### Language and meaning

The terms of scientific theories — "mass," "electron," "gene" — raise deep questions about meaning and reference. Kripke's **rigid designation** and the **causal-historical theory of reference** (covered in the philosophy-of-mind-language file) have implications for the historical continuity of scientific terms across theory change: perhaps "water" refers to H₂O even before chemists knew this, because there is a causal-historical chain linking the term to the substance. This allows that theories about water can be *about the same thing* even as they change.

**Incommensurability** (Kuhn, Feyerabend): if competing paradigms use the same words with different meanings, rational comparison between them is compromised. But this is contested: even if "mass" means something different in Newton and Einstein, we can still understand both theories well enough to compare their empirical performances.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Science has political dimensions: who funds it, who conducts it (demographics of scientific communities), whose questions get asked, whose populations get studied. **Values in science**: the view that science is "value-neutral" is contested — choices about which research programs to pursue, which data to collect, and how to interpret ambiguous results are influenced by values, not purely by evidence.

Feminist philosophy of science (Longino, Harding, Keller): scientific communities are not neutral communities of inquiry but socially structured groups whose background assumptions can systematically affect the science they produce. "Objectivity" is not achieved by eliminating all perspective but by critical engagement among perspectives — a social epistemology that emphasizes the community of inquiry over individual researchers.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

The demarcation problem (distinguishing science from non-science) has obvious relevance to the science/religion relationship. Creationism and intelligent design claim scientific status — the philosophical challenge to this claim is partly about evidence and partly about the structure that genuine scientific theories must have (falsifiability, progressive research programs).

The relationship between philosophy and science: the logical positivists wanted to replace metaphysics with science; Popper wanted to make philosophy continuous with science (while retaining its normative role); Quine wanted to make epistemology continuous with psychology. The current situation: philosophy of science is a genuinely interdisciplinary field in which philosophers and scientists co-contribute — the foundations of quantum mechanics, the nature of biological function, the metaphysics of time — are actively worked on by both philosophers and scientists.

## The Arc Forward

Philosophy of science continues to develop:
- **Machine learning and induction**: statistical learning theory and the success of deep learning renew questions about the nature of induction and generalization — a vindication of Humean patterns? or a new paradigm for understanding knowledge?
- **Replication crisis**: recent failures of replication in psychology, medicine, and nutrition have led to widespread reflection on methodology — small sample sizes, p-hacking, publication bias. Philosophy of science is directly relevant to understanding and addressing the replication crisis.
- **Philosophy of data science and AI**: the methodology of machine learning systems, the nature of the features they extract, and the question of what they "know" — philosophically uncharted territory.
- **Philosophy of climate science**: the nature of the evidence for anthropogenic climate change; the epistemology of model-based science; the relationship between scientific consensus and policy.
