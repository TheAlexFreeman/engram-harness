# The Self Across the History of Philosophy

No question is more philosophically vexed than the question of what we are — what constitutes the self, its unity, its persistence through time, its relationship to the body, its relationship to others, and what grounds the moral and political standing we ascribe to persons. The history of philosophy's engagement with the self is also the history of changing conceptions of interiority, subjectivity, agency, and identity that are among the most consequential ideas in Western (and non-Western) culture.

This file traces the major accounts of the self from Plato to the present, attending to how each account arises from and responds to its predecessors, and how the various approaches map onto each other across traditions.

---

## 1. Plato: The Self as Rational Soul

For Plato, "you" are your soul (*psychē*), and the soul's truest and deepest nature is its rational part. The *Republic*'s tripartite soul — reason (*logos/nous*), spirit (*thymos*), appetite (*epithymia*) — gives a functional psychology: reason aims at truth and wisdom; spirit aims at honor and excellence; appetite aims at physical pleasures and satisfactions. The just soul is one in which reason governs spirit and appetite — each performing its proper function.

The *Phaedo* emphasizes the soul's affinity with the eternal Forms: the truest part of the self is the part that participates in the unchanging, the eternal, the purely intelligible. The body and its desires drag the soul down toward the temporal and sensory. Death — the soul's separation from the body — is liberation.

What makes you *you*, for Plato, is not the body (which is constantly changing and ultimately mortal) but the rational soul — and ultimately, your participation in the eternal order of the Good. Individual identity is bound up with orientation toward the transcendent.

This picture has enormous downstream consequences: in Augustine, the inner self is defined by its orientation toward God; in Descartes, the essential self is the thinking thing; in Kant, the moral self is characterized by practical reason; in Western liberal political philosophy, the subject of rights is the rational autonomous agent.

---

## 2. Aristotle: The Self as Substance With a Soul

Aristotle's soul-as-form picture (covered in the mind-body synthesis) has implications for self-identity. The self is not a hidden inner entity but the organized, functioning living body — the substance that is this individual human being, constituted by its characteristic form of life. Personal identity over time is the identity of the *substance* — the continuity of the organizing form through the changes of matter.

The *Nicomachean Ethics* focuses on the self as a character (in the original sense: *ēthos*, a stable disposition toward action and feeling). You are not primarily what you think about privately but what you *do* — what character traits your actions, over time, express. Virtue is a stable disposition (*hexis*) — to have the virtue of courage is to be reliably disposed to act courageously in appropriate circumstances, not just to do courageous things on occasion. The self is constituted through practice, action, and the cultivation of character.

**The role of narrative**: Aristotle's *Poetics* account of plot — the unity of action constituted by beginning, middle, and end — is (as Paul Ricoeur will argue much later) the model for understanding the unity of a human life. A life has a narrative shape: not just events following each other but actions whose significance depends on their position in an unfolding story. The Aristotelian self is intrinsically temporal and narrative.

---

## 3. Augustine: The Inner Self and the Turn Inward

Augustine is the pivotal figure in the history of the self: he introduces the *inwardness* that will dominate Western self-understanding from his time to Descartes and beyond.

The *Confessions* perform the turn inward: "our heart is restless until it find rest in Thee" — the self is defined by its desire, its love (*amor*), its orientation toward what it loves. And the deepest truth about the self is accessible not through the senses (which track the outer world) but through **inner examination** — *recueillement*, attention to what goes on inside. "Not in my wanderings but in myself I found Thee."

Augustine's self-examination reveals: the will, the memory, the intellect — and crucially, the will's restlessness, its bondage to sin, its capacity for free choice that is also a capacity for self-destruction. The self is defined by what it loves — and the fallen self loves wrongly, preferring the temporal to the eternal, the creature to the Creator.

**Memory** (*Confessions* Book X): the self's identity over time is bound up with memory. Augustine explores memory's astonishing feature: in memory, we encounter not just images of past things but, somehow, the very content of past experiences — we "find" within ourselves things we have never perceived through the senses (mathematical truths, emotions) and things that are no longer present (past events). Memory is the "present of things past" — and the self is constituted by being the subject of this remembered history.

This is the direct ancestor of Locke's memory theory of personal identity, and of Ricoeur's analysis of narrative memory as constitutive of personal identity.

---

## 4. Descartes: The Cogito and the Essential Self

Descartes's *Meditations* strip away all that can be doubted — the body, the external world, mathematics — until only the thinking thing remains. *Cogito ergo sum*: I am thinking, therefore I exist. The self is essentially a *res cogitans*, a thinking thing — defined entirely by its capacity for thought, will, imagination, and sensation (as represented in the mind).

The **transparency of the mental**: for Descartes, the mind is entirely transparent to itself. Whatever occurs in the mind is immediately known to the mind — there is no hidden unconscious, no part of the self inaccessible to first-person introspection. This assumption will be attacked from many directions: Hume (the self cannot find itself through introspection), Freud (the unconscious), Heidegger (Dasein is primarily characterized by *Verfallenheit* — falling, not transparency to itself), and Sartre (bad faith — denying what one knows about oneself).

What makes you *you*, for Descartes, is the thinking thing — and this seems remarkably thin. Descartes does not give a satisfying account of personal identity over time — he is so focused on the present, indubitable cogito that he does not address what connects the thinking I of this moment to the thinking I of yesterday.

---

## 5. Locke: Memory and Personal Identity

**Locke's Essay** (Book II, chapter 27) is the first systematic philosophical account of **personal identity** — what makes you now the same person as you were ten years ago? Locke distinguishes:
- **Substance identity**: the same material substance (not sufficient — the atoms of my body have changed).
- **Life identity**: the same living organism (Locke's "man").
- **Personal identity**: something further — the person is a *conscious being* that can think of itself as the same self at different times through **memory**.

"As far as any intelligent being can repeat the idea of any past action with the same consciousness it had of it at first, and with the same consciousness it has of its present thoughts and actions, so far it is the same personal self."

Locke's memory theory: you are personally identical with a past self if you can remember (are conscious of) being that past self. This generates the famous problem: what about gaps in memory? (Butler's objection; Reid's brave officer paradox — the officer who can remember the flogged boy but not the beaten child). Locke's account is widely regarded as needing refinement.

But the philosophical move is profound: personal identity is not tied to substance (either material or immaterial) but to *psychological continuity* — specifically, memory-based continuity of consciousness. This opens space for accounts of personal identity across radical psychological and physical change.

---

## 6. Hume: The Bundle Theory

Hume's *Treatise* (Book I, Part IV) performs the most ruthless phenomenological examination of the self before Husserl. If I look "inward" for the self that has my experiences, what do I find?

"For my part, when I enter most intimately into what I call *myself*, I always stumble on some particular perception or other, of heat or cold, light or shade, love or hatred, pain or pleasure. I never can catch *myself* at any time without a perception, and can never observe any thing but the perception."

There is no impression of the self as a persistent, unified subject — only the stream of impressions and ideas. The self is a **bundle of perceptions** — not a substance or a unified thing, but a collection of perceptions, "which succeed each other with inconceivable rapidity, and are in a perpetual flux and motion."

Hume's bundle theory directly anticipates Buddhist *anātman* (no-self): there is no permanent, unified self underlying the stream of experiences; what we call "the self" is a conventional designation for the organized pattern of mental events. Hume himself, in the *Appendix* to the *Treatise*, confesses dissatisfaction with his account — he cannot see what unifies the bundle, and suspects this is a genuine philosophical problem he has not solved.

---

## 7. Kant: The Transcendental Unity of Apperception

Kant's response to Hume: there must be a self, because experience presupposes it — but not in the way Hume was looking for. The **transcendental unity of apperception**: the "I think" that must be capable of accompanying all my representations is not an object of experience but the formal condition of experience. It is not something I find within experience (as Hume looked for and failed to find) but the transcendental condition — the unity of the subject that makes experience *mine*.

But the transcendental self is not a *known* thing. The paralogisms show that all attempts to give it positive metaphysical characterization — as simple, as identical, as immaterial — are transcendental illusions. The self is the formal condition of experience, not a further object within experience.

The **empirical self** — the self as it appears through inner experience — is ordered in time and is knowable through psychological investigation. But the noumenal self — the self as it is in itself — is the subject of freedom, autonomous rational agency, beyond the causal order of nature.

Kant's moral philosophy grounds the dignity of persons in this noumenal selfhood: persons are ends in themselves, capable of autonomous legislation of moral law, because they participate in the rational order of the intelligible world. This provides the philosophical grounding for liberal rights: what makes persons worthy of moral and political respect is their rational autonomy.

---

## 8. Hegel: The Social, Historical Self

Hegel dissolves the Kantian subject-in-isolation. The self is not constituted prior to social relationships and then entering them; it is constituted *through* them. The **master-slave dialectic** (*Phenomenology of Spirit*) is the core argument: self-consciousness requires recognition. I become aware of myself as a self-conscious subject only through the recognition of another self-conscious subject. Mutual recognition (*Anerkennung*) is the condition of self-consciousness — not something added to an already constituted self, but constitutive of selfhood.

The implications: there is no such thing as the abstract, pre-social individual of liberal political theory who then "contracts" into social relations. The self is intrinsically social, historical, and embedded in institutional life. The *Philosophy of Right* traces how the fully actual self is realized through the family, civil society, and the state — each providing forms of recognition and freedom that the others cannot.

**Spirit** (*Geist*) is the collective subject — the social-historical "we-subject" — realized through individual selves who participate in shared institutions, practices, and narratives. Individual selves are moments in the self-development of Geist, not the ultimate unit of selfhood.

---

## 9. Kierkegaard: The Existing Individual

Kierkegaard attacks Hegel from the opposite direction: the Hegelian system dissolves the individual into the universal — into History, into Spirit — at the cost of the singular, existing, concrete person.

The self (*Sickness Unto Death*, written as Anti-Climacus): "The self is a relation that relates to itself" — it is not a thing but an ongoing activity of self-relating. More precisely: the self is a relation between the finite and the infinite, the temporal and the eternal, freedom and necessity — and this relation is constituted by the self's relationship to what constituted it (God, for Kierkegaard).

**Despair** is the condition of failing to be the self one truly is — either because one flees from selfhood (living in the aesthetic stage's distraction) or because one mistakes the ethical stage's finite self-mastery for the final word. The self is most itself in the absolute relationship to the Absolute — in faith, the "teleological suspension of the ethical," the individual before God.

The categories: **anxiety** (*Begrebet Angest*) as the dizziness of freedom — the self's confrontation with its own possibilities, which are simultaneously what it must choose among and what it cannot choose without foreclosing others. The self is constituted by its freedom, and freedom is vertiginous. This analysis of anxiety is the direct predecessor of Heidegger's *Angst* and Sartre's nausea.

---

## 10. Nietzsche: The Self as Multiplicity and Becoming

Nietzsche's attack on the self is the most thoroughgoing critique in the tradition before poststructuralism. The subject — the "I" that Descartes found as the indubitable ground — is itself a grammatical fiction: "a thought comes when 'it' will, not when 'I' will; so that it is a falsification to say: the subject 'I' is the condition of the predicate 'think'" (*Beyond Good and Evil* §17). Our grammar compels us to add a "doer" to every "deed" — but this is a linguistic necessity, not a metaphysical one. There is the thinking — "it thinks" — not the substantial self that thinks.

The self is not a unified subject but a **multiplicity** — a dynamic plurality of drives, affects, valuations in ongoing tension and power-struggle. Integration — the apparent unity of a person — is not a given but an achievement, and always partial. "Assuming that the soul is only a society constructed out of the drives and affects."

Yet Nietzsche also articulates the most demanding vision of selfhood: **amor fati** (love of fate) — affirming the whole of one's life, including suffering and failure, not despite but as part of what one is. The **eternal recurrence** as an existential test: if you were to live this life again, infinitely many times, would you affirm it? The self worth creating is the self that can say yes to this question. The Übermensch is not a biological category but an existential achiever — the one who creates values rather than inheriting them, who gives style to their character.

---

## 11. Phenomenological Accounts: The Experiencing Self

**Husserl**: the self as the **transcendental ego** — not an empirical self (psychological) but the formal condition of all conscious experience, the ultimate pole of all acts of consciousness. Unlike Kant's transcendental unity of apperception, Husserl's transcendental ego is a genuine object of phenomenological investigation — we can describe its formal structure. It is characterized by **time-consciousness** (the thick present of retention-primal impression-protention that constitutes the unity of experience over time) and by **habituality** (the ego as a system of settled convictions and dispositions — not purely formal but having a concrete character built up through its history).

**Heidegger**: *Dasein* — being-there — is the term Heidegger uses instead of "subject" or "self" to avoid the Cartesian connotations. Dasein is always already in a world, with others, embedded in practices and possibilities it did not choose. Its selfhood is not a given essence but a task: *authenticity* — owning one's ownmost possibility (death, *Sein-zum-Tode*) and pulling back from the *das Man* (the they-self, the anonymous collective self of everyday social conformism). Authentic selfhood is not a retreat into private inwardness but a resolute owning of one's thrown possibilities.

**Sartre**: "existence precedes essence" — there is no pre-given human nature that determines what a person is; we are radically free, and this freedom is what makes bad faith possible. **Bad faith** (*mauvaise foi*) is the attempt to deny one's own freedom — to convince oneself that one is an in-itself (fixed, determinable thing) rather than a for-itself (consciousness, nothingness, freedom). The person who says "I can't help it, I'm just like that" is in bad faith.

---

## 12. Ricoeur: Narrative Identity

Paul Ricoeur's *Oneself as Another* (1990) synthesizes many threads into the most complete contemporary account of personal identity as **narrative identity** (*identité narrative*).

Ricoeur distinguishes:
- **Idem identity** (sameness): the physical, biological, and characterological continuity of the person — the same body, the same personality traits, the same cultural formation.
- **Ipse identity** (selfhood): the kind of identity constituted by commitments, promises, and faithfulness — "I will be there" — which *maintains* itself through change precisely because it is not hostage to the continuity of properties but to the self's own pledged word.

**Narrative** integrates these: a life is not a collection of disconnected events but a story — with character (idem), plot (events constituted into meaningful sequence), and the narrative identity of the protagonist (ipse — the "who" of the story). The "self" is constituted neither by a metaphysical substance (Locke's critique was right) nor by a bundle of impressions (Hume) but by the narrative unity of a life — which is always ongoing, always open to reinterpretation, and always embedded in larger shared narratives (tradition, community, culture).

This account connects self to narrative cognition, to the Aristotelian unity of plot, to the phenomenological time-consciousness of Husserl, and to the Hegelian insight that the self is constituted in and through its history.

---

## 13. Contemporary: Psychological Continuity, Extended Selves, and Social Constitution

**Derek Parfit** (*Reasons and Persons*, 1984): the most radical contemporary attack on the "further fact" view of personal identity. What matters in survival is not personal identity *per se* but **psychological continuity** — overlapping chains of memory, belief, intention, and character. If my brain were split and each half was implanted in a different body, there would be no fact of the matter about which resulting person is "really me" — but both would be psychologically continuous with me. Parfit concludes: personal identity is not what matters; psychological continuity is. And the view that there is a deep metaphysical self — a "further fact" beyond all physical and psychological facts — is an illusion that causes much harm (in excessive self-concern, fear of death, etc.).

**Andy Clark** (*Being There*, *Natural-Born Cyborgs*): the self extends into the environment. Otto's notebook is part of Otto's cognitive system and hence part of Otto. The extended mind thesis has implications for the external constitution of the self — what I am partly depends on what my external resources are.

**Butler, Foucault**: the self is not a pre-given substance but a subject constituted through social and discursive practices. Gender, sexuality, and the "natural" body are not found but made — through the performative repetition of regulatory norms. The subject is not the author of discourse but an effect of it.

## The Shape of the Through-Line

Seen across the entire arc, several movements are visible:

1. **From substance to activity**: the self moves from being a substance (the Platonic soul, the Cartesian res cogitans) to being an activity or relation (Kant's transcendental unity, Kierkegaard's self-relating, Sartrean freedom, Ricoeur's narrative identity).

2. **From private to social**: the Cartesian picture of the self as a private inner theater, knowable only to itself, gives way progressively to a picture of the self as constituted in and through its relationships — with others (Hegel), with language (Wittgenstein), with the world (Heidegger), with its narrative embedding (Ricoeur), with its social-discursive context (Foucault, Butler).

3. **From given to achieved**: the self is not simply found (discovered through introspection) but made — through practice (Aristotle), through choices and commitments (Sartre, Kierkegaard), through the cultivation of character (Aristotle again, MacIntyre), through the ongoing interpretation and reinterpretation of one's life (Ricoeur).

4. **From unity to multiplicity**: the assumption that the self is necessarily unified (one soul, one substance, one transcendental apperception) is progressively questioned — by Hume's bundle, by Nietzsche's multiplicity of drives, by the poststructuralist critique of the unified subject. Yet the demand for some account of integration or coherence persist: a multiplicity of drives that is radically incoherent is pathological, not simply "authentic."

5. **Non-Western convergence**: Buddhist *anātman* (no-self), Zhuangzi's dissolution of the fixed self in the Tao's flowing change, Advaita Vedānta's identification of the apparent individual self with the cosmic Brahman — all arrive, from entirely different directions, at the insight that the ordinary sense of a bounded, fixed, self-possessed individual self is a construction, not a metaphysical given. The philosophical question is what to do with this insight — whether it leads to liberation (Buddhism, Vedānta), to lightness and playfulness (Zhuangzi), to existential anxiety (Heidegger), or to political critique (Foucault).
