# Language, Thought, and Meaning Across the History of Philosophy

Language sits at the intersection of all four through-lines: it is the medium of thought (epistemology), the means by which we designate and categorize the world (metaphysics), the vehicle of moral argument and political deliberation (ethics), and the site where scientific and religious claims are made and contested. The history of philosophy of language is simultaneously a history of philosophy's self-understanding — because philosophy is conducted *in* language, and every account of language's powers and limits reflects back on what philosophy itself can do.

This file traces the central questions: What is the relationship between words, thoughts, and things? What is meaning and how is it constituted? How does language affect thought? Can language reach reality directly, or only represent it at a remove?

---

## 1. Plato: The Cratylic Question

The *Cratylus* poses the founding question of Western philosophy of language: do names naturally fit their objects (*physis*, nature) or are they purely conventional (*nomos*, convention)?

**Hermogenes's conventionalism**: names are whatever we agree to call things. There is no natural connection between a word and its referent — "tree" could have been anything; it just happens to be the English convention. **Cratylus's naturalism**: names inherently reveal the nature of their objects — the Greeks called earth *gē* because it fits the nature of the earth better than other sounds would.

Plato is sympathetic to naturalism (and at his most playful produces elaborate pseudo-etymologies of Greek words to show how they express the natures of their objects) but ultimately dissatisfied with it — etymology cannot get all the way to knowledge. True knowledge requires grasping the Forms directly, not through the medium of language. Language can point toward the Forms but cannot replace or constitute knowledge of them. The *Seventh Letter* (if authentic) explicitly holds that direct knowledge of the Good cannot be put into words.

This ambivalence about language's cognitive reach — can it express or must it always fall short of the deepest truths? — recurs throughout the tradition: in negative theology, in the Tractatus's silence, in Derrida's différance.

## 2. Aristotle: Language, Logic, and Categories

Aristotle systematizes the analysis of language in the *Categories*, *De Interpretatione*, and *Organon*. Key contributions:

**The semantic triangle** (*De Interpretatione* 1): spoken words (*phone*) are symbols (*symbola*) of mental states (*pathēmata*) which are likenesses (*homoiōmata*) of actual things (*pragmata*). The connection between word and thing is mediated by mental likenesses. This triadic structure — word, thought, thing — recurs throughout the history of semiotics (Peirce's representamen/object/interpretant) and linguistics (Ogden and Richards's "semantic triangle").

**Predication and categories**: the *Categories* analyzes what can be said of things — substance (primary and secondary), quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, state, action, affection. These are the fundamental kinds of predicate — the kinds of things we can say. The analysis is simultaneously a logic of predication and an ontology of being. What kinds of things are there? The same as the kinds of things that can be truly predicated.

**The syllogism**: in the *Prior Analytics*, Aristotle systematizes the logic of the syllogism — the forms of valid inference in terms of predication. This is the first formal logic in any tradition and remained the dominant logical theory until Frege (though Buddhist and Nyāya logic developed independently sophisticated alternatives). The syllogism links logic (formal inference) to ontology (the structure of being) through predication.

**Metaphor** (*Poetics*, *Rhetoric*): Aristotle's account of metaphor — seeing similarities between different things — is the first systematic treatment of figurative language. He regards metaphor as a cognitive achievement, not merely a stylistic ornament: good metaphors illuminate real structural similarities. The resonance with cognitive linguistics (Lakoff and Johnson's conceptual metaphor theory — abstract thought is structured by metaphorical mappings from embodied source domains) is strong, though Aristotle takes metaphor to be a vehicle for insight that literal language could in principle express.

## 3. Medieval Linguistics: The Universals Controversy

The dominant philosophical dispute of medieval philosophy concerns **universals**: do general terms like "humanity," "redness," or "triangularity" refer to real universal entities, or only to groups of particular things, or only to mental concepts, or only to words?

- **Realism** (Plato, Anselm, early Aquinas position): universals are real entities — there is a real universal "humanity" that all individual humans share. The general term refers to something genuinely in the world beyond any collection of particulars.
- **Conceptualism** (Abelard's position): universals are mental concepts — general terms refer to mental representations, not to anything extra-mental. There is a real similarity structure in the world that grounds the application of general terms, but the universal itself is only in the mind.
- **Nominalism** (Ockham): universals are only names (*nomina*) — there is nothing common to all the things a general term applies to, beyond the fact that we apply the same term to them. Ockham's razor: do not multiply entities beyond necessity. Only particulars exist; general terms are linguistic conventions for grouping similar particulars.

This debate directly anticipates and sets up the 20th-century debates about the semantics of general terms: Kripke and Putnam's natural kind essentialism (a form of realism about chemical and biological kinds); Wittgenstein's family resemblance (a form of anti-essentialism that undermines both robust realism and the conceptualist assumption of necessary and sufficient conditions); prototype theory in cognitive linguistics (a development of anti-essentialist conceptualism).

**The modistae** (grammarians of modes of signifying, 13th–14th century): Speculative Grammar — the most sophisticated medieval philosophy of language. Grammar is philosophically grounded: the modes of signifying (*modi significandi*) mirror the modes of understanding (*modi intelligendi*) which in turn mirror the modes of being (*modi essendi*). To understand the grammar of a language is to understand the fundamental structure of being as it presents itself to intellect. This is the medieval equivalent of the 20th-century claim that language and world share a logical form.

## 4. Locke and the Empiricist Picture

Locke's *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (Book III) gives the most influential early modern theory of language: words are **signs of ideas**. A word signifies the speaker's idea — not the thing in the world directly. Communication proceeds by one person producing a word, the hearer interpreting it as signifying an idea, and (ideally) having the same idea triggered. But this immediately raises the problem: how do I know that your idea of "red" is the same as mine? There is a *veil of ideas* between speakers, parallel to Locke's *veil of perception* between minds and world.

**Abstract ideas**: general terms are made general by abstraction — the idea of "triangle" is formed by stripping away the specific properties of any particular triangle (isoceles, scalene, etc.) until only the general "triangularity" remains. Berkeley famously attacks this: there is no such thing as an abstract general idea; every idea must be a particular image. "Triangle" cannot designate a shape that is simultaneously neither isoceles, nor scalene, nor equilateral — there is no such shape. Locke's abstraction is a fiction.

Berkeley's critique anticipates Wittgenstein's family resemblance (there is no common essence corresponding to general terms) and Rosch's prototype theory (general terms are anchored to prototypical exemplars, not to abstract necessary-and-sufficient conditions).

## 5. Leibniz and the Universal Characteristic

**Leibniz** envisioned a *characteristica universalis* — a universal formal language that would represent all human thought in an unambiguous symbolic system. Disputes between thinkers could then be settled by calculation (*calculemus!*). Thought would be revealed to have a formal logical structure; the language would make that structure visible and prevent the confusions generated by natural language's ambiguity and metaphor.

This is the ancestor of multiple 20th-century projects: Frege's *Begriffsschrift* (the concept-script that realizes the idea of a logical formalism for thought); Russell and Whitehead's *Principia Mathematica* (the attempt to derive all mathematics from logical principles); the Vienna Circle's ideal of a unified science in a single formal logical language; formal semantics in linguistics. The idea that language, thought, and logical structure can be aligned — that the deep grammar of thought is logical — runs from Leibniz through Frege, Russell, and the early Wittgenstein.

---

## 6. Frege: The Logical Turn

**Frege** achieves what Leibniz dreamed of in one domain: a formal logical language (*Begriffsschrift*, 1879) adequate for expressing all mathematical inference. But the philosophical consequences go far beyond mathematics.

**Sense and reference** (*Sinn und Bedeutung*, 1892): Frege distinguishes the **reference** (*Bedeutung*) of an expression (the object it picks out in the world) from its **sense** (*Sinn*) (the "mode of presentation" of that object — the information carried by the expression that determines which object is picked out). "The morning star" and "the evening star" have the same reference (Venus) but different senses. The informativeness of "The morning star is the evening star" (an astronomical discovery) demonstrates that sense and reference are distinct — yet both expressions pick out the same planet.

This distinction is philosophically foundational: it allows that identity statements can be informative (not trivially true); it distinguishes the cognitive contribution of an expression (its sense) from the object it picks out (its reference); and it raises the question of where senses live — Frege holds they are objective abstract entities, neither in the mental nor in the physical, which are "grasped" by the mind.

**The context principle**: never ask for the meaning of a word in isolation; only in the context of a sentence does a word have meaning. This principle (from the *Grundlagen*) places the sentence, not the word, as the primary unit of meaning — and anticipates the later Wittgenstein's point that words have meaning only in the practice of their use.

## 7. Russell and the Early Wittgenstein: Logical Atomism

**Russell's theory of descriptions** exposes a crucial distinction: the surface grammatical form of a sentence and its logical form can diverge. "The present King of France is bald" appears to make a subject-predicate claim (the present King of France is the subject; bald is predicated of him). But there is no present King of France, so who is the subject? On Russell's analysis, the sentence disguises a quantificational logical form: "There exists exactly one x which is presently King of France, and x is bald." This existential claim is simply false — no mystery about the non-existent subject needed.

The lesson: philosophy's confusions are often traceable to the surface grammar of natural language, which misleadingly suggests one logical form when the real logical form is different. Philosophical analysis — revealing the real logical form — is the philosopher's proper task.

The early **Wittgenstein** (*Tractatus*, 1921) radicalizes this: language's propositional pictures must share "logical form" with the facts they represent. The *Tractatus* holds that elementary propositions are directly correlated with possible states of affairs — they "picture" atomic facts. The structure of language mirrors the structure of the world at the most fundamental level: the logical form they share is what makes representation possible.

What cannot be said — what the Tractatus itself tries to show but cannot claim — is the relationship between language and world, the logical form they share. Ethics, aesthetics, the philosophical propositions of the *Tractatus* itself, and the mystical — all fall outside the limits of what can be said. "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent."

## 8. Logical Positivism and the Verification Principle

The Vienna Circle took the Tractarian insight radicaly: meaningful propositions are either *analytically* true (true by definition, logical truth) or *empirically verifiable* (confirmed or disconfirmed by observation). Metaphysical statements — about God, the soul, the Absolute — are cognitively meaningless, neither true nor false but merely the expression of emotions or attitudes. Philosophy's task is the logical clarification of scientific language.

The failure of the verification principle (it is itself neither analytic nor empirically verifiable) inaugurated the post-positivist philosophy of language — and forced attention back to the question of what meaning actually is, if not reducibility to verification conditions.

---

## 9. The Later Wittgenstein: Meaning as Use

The *Philosophical Investigations* (published 1953) performs a systematic dismantling of the Tractarian picture. **Meaning as use**: "the meaning of a word is its use in the language" — not its psychological correlate (an image or feeling), not the object it names, not its place in a formal logical system, but the role it plays in the practice of language as embedded in a form of life.

**Language games**: language is not one thing (a single representational-logical system) but many different activities — giving orders, describing, constructing models, reporting events, speculating, making jokes, translating, asking, thanking, telling stories, singing. Each is governed by different rules; what counts as correct use in one context may be incorrect in another; there is no super-game of language governing all the others.

**Family resemblance**: general terms do not require a common essence shared by all instances. "Game" has no property that all games share; there is a network of overlapping similarities — some games share some features with some other games, those share other features with still other games, and so on. This is the most influential 20th-century attack on essentialism in semantics, and it directly inspired Rosch's prototype theory in cognitive psychology.

**Private language argument**: no purely private ostensive definition can anchor a norm of correct use. If I try to define "S" as "this sensation" (pointing inward), I have no criterion for whether I am using "S" correctly on future occasions — I cannot check against an external standard, and a purely internal "seeming to remember correctly" is not a check. Language is essentially norm-governed, and norms require a social context.

## 10. Grice, Speech Acts, and Pragmatics

**J.L. Austin** distinguishes **locutionary** acts (saying something), **illocutionary** acts (doing something in saying — promising, warning, declaring), and **perlocutionary** acts (effects produced by saying — persuading, moving, alarming). Language is not primarily representation; it is action. The dominant question becomes not "what does this sentence mean?" but "what is this speaker doing by saying this?"

**Grice** analyzes what a speaker *means* (non-natural meaning) as distinct from what a sentence means (natural/conventional meaning). Speaker meaning involves intentions — specifically, the intention to produce an effect in the hearer by means of the hearer's recognition of that intention. Communication requires mutual recognition of the communicative intent. The **cooperative principle** and **maxims** (quantity, quality, relation, manner) explain how we say less than we mean and are understood nonetheless — conversational *implicature*.

This is the philosophical foundation of pragmatics: the study of how meaning in context goes beyond what is literally said. Its connection to cognitive linguistics (Sweetser's work on the illocutionary use of conditionals; Sperber and Wilson's relevance theory) is direct and productive.

## 11. Kripke, Putnam, and Semantic Externalism

The Fregean picture treats the meaning of a name as the description associated with it (its sense). Kripke's *Naming and Necessity* (1980) demolishes this with a series of thought experiments.

**Rigid designators**: proper names (and natural kind terms) are *rigid* — they designate the same individual (or kind) in every possible world in which that individual exists, regardless of which description the speaker associates with the name. "Aristotle" does not mean "the pupil of Plato who wrote the *Nicomachean Ethics*" — it is a rigid designator directly bound to the individual Aristotle. In a world where Aristotle never wrote the *Nicomachean Ethics*, "Aristotle" still refers to Aristotle.

**A posteriori necessities**: "water is H₂O," "Hesperus is Phosphorus," "pain is C-fiber firing" (if true) are all *necessarily* true (identical in all possible worlds where water/Hesperus/pain exists) but known only *a posteriori* (through empirical investigation, not by analysis of the concepts). This dissolves the assumed equivalence of necessary/a priori and contingent/a posteriori — and opens space for a scientific essentialism about natural kinds.

**Putnam's Twin Earth**: "water" on Earth and on Twin Earth pick out different substances (H₂O vs. XYZ), even if speakers on both planets associate exactly the same description with "water." Meaning — what determines reference — is partly constituted by the world, not entirely by mental content. **Semantic externalism**: "meanings ain't in the head." The reference of our terms is partly determined by facts about the environment and the social division of linguistic labor.

## 12. Cognitive Linguistics: Language, Thought, and Embodiment

**Lakoff and Johnson** (*Metaphors We Live By*, 1980) make what may be the most empirically consequential contribution to the philosophy of language in the late 20th century. The dominant assumption (shared by Frege, the Vienna Circle, and formal semantics) was that the basic concepts of thought are **literal**, **context-independent**, and **amodal** — the same regardless of who thinks them or what body they think with. Lakoff and Johnson argue the opposite:

**Conceptual metaphor**: our understanding of abstract domains is systematically constituted by metaphorical mappings from embodied source domains. ARGUMENT IS WAR (we attack positions, defend claims, shoot down ideas). TIME IS MONEY (we spend, save, waste, budget time). UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING (I grasp the concept; the idea eluded her; she caught on). These are not merely linguistic expressions but cognitive structures — they govern inference in the abstract domain.

The embodied source of conceptual metaphor is not accidental: concepts originate in sensorimotor experience (the "container" schema from the experience of containment; the "path" schema from the experience of moving through space; the "force" schema from the experience of being pushed). Abstract thought is not independent of the body but built up through metaphorical extension from primary embodied concepts.

This is the philosophy of language's connection to embodied cognition: meaning is not a Fregean abstract entity, nor a function from contexts to extensions, but is constituted through the body's engagement with the world and the brain's cross-domain mappings. Saussure's **arbitrariness of the sign** (the connection between signifier and signified is conventional, not natural) is partially refined by cognitive semantics: the connection between the *form* and the referent may be arbitrary, but the conceptual structure that organizes the semantic field is not arbitrary — it is grounded in universal features of embodied experience.

## The Arc of the Through-Line

The history of the language-thought-meaning through-line reveals several deep shifts:

1. **From word to sentence to use**: the primary unit of meaning moves from the word (Plato, Aristotle) to the proposition/sentence (Frege, the early Wittgenstein) to the speech act in context (the later Wittgenstein, Austin, Grice).

2. **From meaning as object to meaning as activity**: the picture of meaning as a Platonic Form, a Fregean sense, or a Lockean idea (an entity of some kind that a word "has") gives way to the picture of meaning as something *constituted* in practice — in use, in context, in the interaction of speakers and hearers.

3. **From amodal to embodied**: the assumption that the semantic content of thought is amodal — not tied to any specific sensory or motor system, representable in any medium — gives way (in cognitive linguistics and embodied cognition) to the claim that meaning is grounded in the body's sensorimotor engagement with the world.

4. **From private to public**: the Lockean picture of meaning as constituted by the speaker's private ideas gives way progressively — through Frege (objective senses), Wittgenstein (social use), Putnam (environment-determined reference) — to the insight that meaning is constituted in the public, social, world-engaging practice of language.

5. **Language and reality**: the ambivalence Plato expressed — language can gesture toward reality but cannot capture it fully in any formulation — recurs in the Tractatus's silence, in Derrida's différance (meaning is always deferred, never fully present), and in the apophatic traditions of negative theology. Whether this is a genuine insight about the limits of propositional representation or a confusion produced by taking the limitative aspects of language too seriously remains deeply contested.
