# Schopenhauer and Pragmatism: Will, Action, and Consequence (1818–1910)

This file covers two currents that run alongside the main Hegel–Marx–Kierkegaard–Nietzsche axis: **Schopenhauer's voluntarist metaphysics** (which feeds directly into Nietzsche, Wagner, Freud, and the aesthetics of late Romanticism) and **American Pragmatism** (Peirce, James, Dewey — a genuinely original philosophical tradition that anticipates much of 20th-century philosophy of language, cognitive science, and democratic theory). Both emerge from Kant but develop in radically different directions.

---

## 1. Schopenhauer: The World as Will

**Arthur Schopenhauer** (1788–1860) was Hegel's bitterest contemporary rival — he deliberately scheduled his Berlin lectures to conflict with Hegel's and attracted approximately nobody — and Hegel's posthumous nemesis: when German Idealism collapsed in the 1840s, Schopenhauer's philosophy experienced a sudden enormous success. His central work, *The World as Will and Representation* (*Die Welt als Wille und Vorstellung*, 1818, expanded 1844), is one of the most readable philosophical masterpieces.

### Representation and the Veil of Maya

Schopenhauer accepts Kant's distinction between phenomena (the world as represented) and the thing-in-itself (the ultimate reality behind representations). But where Kant holds that we can know nothing about the thing-in-itself, Schopenhauer argues that we have one privileged point of access to it: our own **body**.

The body is given to us in two ways: as representation (externally, as an object in space and time, like any other physical object) and directly, from within, as *will* — as the immediate force that drives our actions, desires, and striving. When we act, we do not first form a representation of the act and then execute it; the act is already the expression of will. This inner access to the thing-in-itself through one's own body is the key to Schopenhauer's metaphysics.

**The veil of Maya** (borrowing from Hindu philosophy, which Schopenhauer was among the first Western philosophers to take seriously): the phenomenal world — the world as representation — is like an illusion; the real thing behind appearances is not Hegel's Reason or Spirit but **Will** — blind, purposeless, striving, insatiable force. This same will is operative in all of nature: in the force of gravity, in the growth of plants, in animal instinct, in human desire. The difference between humans, animals, plants, and stones is a difference in the *grade* of will's objectification, not a difference in kind.

### The Will and Suffering

The Will is not rational, benevolent, or teleological. It is **blind striving without purpose** — it wants, achieves satisfaction for a moment, and then wants again; the cycle has no goal, no termination, no real satisfaction. Human life is the will's clearest expression: we desire intensely, we achieve intermittently, we suffer from unsatisfied desire in the intervals, and then we die. The famous pessimist conclusion: **existence is suffering**.

This is not merely emotional pessimism but a metaphysical claim: given what the will is (endless striving without achievable final satisfaction), and given that we *are* will, suffering is the structural condition of conscious existence. Leibniz's "best of all possible worlds" is parodied: a world governed by a blind, purpose-free will could not be the best; it would be better if nothing existed at all.

The diagnosis anticipates — and may have influenced — Freud: the id (biological drive, pleasure-seeking and pain-avoiding without purpose or rationality) is recognizably Schopenhauerian will, operating below conscious rational control. The ego and superego are the will's later self-regulatory constructions.

### Aesthetics and the Escape from Will

Schopenhauer's account of art is one of the richest in the philosophical tradition. If existence is suffering (because the will is endless striving), the question becomes: is there any escape? Two answers:

**Aesthetic contemplation**: In the moment of genuine aesthetic experience — looking at a beautiful object, hearing music — the ordinary will-driven subject is temporarily silenced. We become **pure knowing subjects** — not a person with desires, history, and ego, but an eye of knowledge that takes in the Platonic Idea (the universal form) embodied in the particular object. This is a moment of will-less contemplation — genuine, though temporary, escape from the will. It is not accidental that aesthetic experience feels liberating: it is liberation, momentarily, from the will that constitutes us.

**Music as the direct voice of will**: But music is special. Other arts present Platonic Ideas — objectifications of the will at various grades. Music does not represent any Idea; it directly expresses the will itself — not a particular manifestation of will but will's inner nature, movement, striving, and resolution. This makes music the highest art — a direct communication of the metaphysical ground of existence in a form that bypasses representation.

This aesthetic metaphysics was decisive for the history of art: **Wagner** took it as his philosophical foundation; Nietzsche's early *Birth of Tragedy* (1872) develops it (the Apollonian/Dionysian distinction — order vs. will-expression); Tolstoy, Hardy, and Conrad share Schopenhauerian pessimism about human nature and the natural world; Mahler and the late romantics explore it in music.

**Ethical compassion**: The deeper escape from will is ethical. If all individual beings are manifestations of the one will, then the individuality that separates self from other is — like the veil of Maya — ultimately illusion. Genuine ethical perception (*Mitleid* — compassion, literally "suffering-with") is the recognition that the suffering of another is the same as one's own suffering, because ultimately there is only one will. Schopenhauer here draws explicitly on Buddhist and Hindu philosophy — the individual will's renunciation (the ascetic, the saint who denies the will-to-live) is the most complete escape, the nearest Western approximation to nirvana.

## 2. Pragmatism: Meaning, Truth, and the Life of Ideas

American Pragmatism is a distinctly American philosophical tradition — emerging from the *Metaphysical Club* discussions in Cambridge, Massachusetts in the 1870s among a group that included Charles Sanders Peirce, William James, Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., and Chauncey Wright. It is the most influential philosophical movement native to the United States and has become increasingly central to global philosophy of mind, language, and normativity.

The core pragmatist commitment: the meaning and truth of ideas is constituted by their practical consequences — what difference it makes to our experience, behavior, and inquiry whether an idea is true. Ideas are not mirrors of a mind-independent reality; they are instruments, tools, plans of action — to be evaluated by whether they work, whether they guide successful inquiry and action.

## 3. Peirce: Pragmaticism, Signs, and the Method of Science

**Charles Sanders Peirce** (1839–1914) is the founder of pragmatism (which he later renamed **pragmaticism** to distance it from James's formulations) and one of the most original philosophers in American history — also one of the most eccentric and difficult, whose collected papers fill eight volumes but who never held a regular academic position after his early 40s.

### The Pragmatic Maxim

The pragmatic maxim (*How to Make Our Ideas Clear*, 1878): "Consider what effects, that might conceivably have practical bearings, we conceive the object of our conception to have. Then, our conception of these effects is the whole of our conception of the object."

The maxim is a criterion of semantic content: a concept means nothing beyond its conceivable practical effects. The concept of "transsubstantiation" (the Catholic doctrine that bread and wine literally become the body and blood of Christ), if it has no different practical consequences from "symbolic meaning" — same appearance, same taste, same nutrition — then the two doctrines say the same thing. If trans-substantiation *does* have practical consequences (it does, for Catholics), those consequences are what the doctrine means.

The maxim does not reduce all questions to the practically consequential in a crude utilitarian way. Peirce is interested in the consequences for future *inquiry* and *experience* — including abstract mathematical and logical inquiry.

### Semiotics

Peirce's most technically original contribution is his **theory of signs** (semiotics). For Saussure (roughly contemporary), the sign is a dyadic relation: signifier (sound-image) and signified (concept). For Peirce, the sign is **triadic**:
- **Representamen** (the sign vehicle: word, image, gesture)  
- **Object** (what the sign stands for)
- **Interpretant** (the meaning produced in a mind — which is itself a further sign)

The triadic structure is crucial: there is no direct relation between sign and object; the interpretant mediates. And the interpretant is itself a sign (with its own interpretant), producing an open-ended chain of semiosis. Meaning is not a fixed relation between sign and thing but a dynamic, ongoing, social process of interpretation.

Peirce distinguishes three types of signs:
- **Icon**: represents by resemblance (a portrait, a map)
- **Index**: represents by causal or physical connection (smoke as sign of fire; pointing finger)
- **Symbol**: represents by convention (words, mathematical symbols)

This taxonomy — and the general triadic theory — is foundational for 20th-century semiotics (Sebeok, Eco), for linguistics (Jakobson), for cognitive linguistics (Langacker's symbolic assemblies are sign-like structures), and for contemporary philosophy of language and mind.

### Fallibilism and the Community of Inquiry

Peirce's epistemology: no belief is immune from revision. **Fallibilism** — we might be wrong about anything — combined with the method of science (the only method that corrects itself) gives the epistemological framework. Truth is what the **community of inquiry** would converge on in the long run — not correspondence to a mind-independent reality that exists all at once, but the limit of inquiry pursued indefinitely.

This social, dynamic conception of truth — truth as what would survive sustained collective inquiry — is both anti-foundationalist (no incorrigible starting point) and anti-relativist (not anything goes; inquiry has standards and self-corrects).

## 4. James: Radical Empiricism and the Will to Believe

**William James** (1842–1910) is the most widely read pragmatist — gifted prose stylist, psychologist, moralist, philosopher, and proto-philosopher of religion. His *Principles of Psychology* (1890) is a foundational text of psychology; his *Pragmatism* (1907) is the most accessible statement of the movement; *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) is the best philosophical approach to religious life.

### The Stream of Consciousness

The *Principles of Psychology* coins the phrase "stream of consciousness" — thought flows; it does not leap from state to state. Every thought is continuous with what precedes and follows it; the earlier and later states bleed into each other. This is directly relevant to the phenomenological literature: Husserl's analysis of time-consciousness (retention of the just-past, primal impression, protention of the about-to-come) articulates philosophically what James describes psychologically.

James also challenges the associationist picture (Hume, Bain, Mill) in which mental life is a series of discrete atomic ideas related by association. Real thought is holistic, flowing, fringe-laden — the "fringe" of consciousness (the feeling of tendency, of relations, of what is coming) is as real as the explicit cognitive content.

### The Will to Believe

*The Will to Believe* (1897) defends the right to hold beliefs on passional rather than evidential grounds under certain conditions. Against Clifford's rule (it is always wrong to believe anything on insufficient evidence), James argues: sometimes we face live options — genuine choices where evidence is insufficient and choosing not to decide is itself a choice (with consequences). The option is forced (must be decided), living (genuinely possible for this person), and momentous (significant stakes). In such cases, it is not irrational to choose on the basis of what you need to believe to act as a full human being.

This is most relevant to religious belief: if the existence of God is a live, forced, momentous option and the evidence is insufficient to decide, then choosing to live as if God exists (and getting the full benefits of religious engagement) is rational. James is not defending wishful thinking but arguing that the strict evidential rule is itself a choice with consequences that a pragmatist must assess.

### Pragmatic Truth

James's (more controversial) pragmatic theory of truth: "True ideas are those that we can assimilate, validate, corroborate and verify. False ideas are those that we cannot." Truth happens to an idea as it works, as it proves itself in action and inquiry. "The true is the name of whatever proves itself to be good in the way of belief, and good, too, for definite, assignable reasons."

This is the most radical pragmatist position on truth and got James into immediate trouble: Bertrand Russell and G.E. Moore attacked it savagely. Does "true" just mean "useful"? Then false beliefs that work are true, and useless true beliefs are false. James's response: in the long run, only accurate beliefs work consistently — but the formulation remained controversial. Peirce's conception (truth as the limit of inquiry) is more defensible and became the standard pragmatist view.

## 5. Dewey: Instrumentalism, Democracy, and Education

**John Dewey** (1859–1952) is the most politically engaged and the most educationally influential of the pragmatists. His career spans from the 1880s to 1952; his influence on American progressive education is enormous (and partially misapplied by his followers into an "anti-discipline" pedagogy he would not have recognized).

### Instrumentalism

Dewey's version of pragmatism: **instrumentalism** — ideas, concepts, theories are **instruments** for solving problems, not representations of a fixed reality. Inquiry begins from a situation of doubt, uncertainty, or blocked action; it ends when a satisfactory resolution is achieved; theories are the tools developed in inquiry. The *Logic: The Theory of Inquiry* (1938) develops this account rigorously.

Dewey extends Kant's Copernican revolution: not only does the mind structure experience, but inquiry itself transforms the situation. The world is not a pre-given fixed reality but is reconstructed through inquiry. This is neither idealism (we don't make reality up) nor naive realism (we don't passively mirror a fixed reality) but **transactionalism**: knowers and known co-constitute each other through the transactions of inquiry.

### Democracy and Education

Dewey's social philosophy: democracy is not merely a political form (majority vote) but a **way of life** — a community in which the capacities of all members are developed through participation in shared projects of inquiry and problem-solving. Education is the central democratic practice: not the transmission of fixed knowledge (authoritarian, hierarchical) but the cultivation of habits of intelligent inquiry (experimental, collaborative, responsive to experience).

*Democracy and Education* (1916) remains one of the great educational philosophy texts: it situates education within the whole context of social life, refuses the sharp distinction between learning and doing, and insists that democratic education requires experiencing democracy — not just hearing about it.

### Continuity with Nature

Dewey's philosophy is anti-dualist in a specifically pragmatist way. Against Descartes: mind is not a separate substance but a development out of nature — organic responses to environment, emergent from and continuous with non-cognitive biological life. *Experience and Nature* (1925) argues that experience is not a veil between us and nature (the empiricist picture) but is *in* nature — the transaction between organism and environment. This is the philosophical position closest to embodied cognition: mind is always already bodily, environmental, embedded.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

The pragmatists are the 19th century's clearest predecessors of contemporary cognitive science and philosophy of mind. James's stream of consciousness (anti-atomistic, temporally extended, fringe-laden), Peirce's dynamic semiosis (meaning as ongoing interpretive process, not static correspondence), Dewey's organism-environment transaction (mind as embedded in the world, not a spectator) — these are the philosophical frameworks within which the embodied, enactive, extended cognition tradition works.

Schopenhauer's contribution: the body as the site of privileged access to the thing-in-itself is the first philosophical argument for the primacy of the phenomenological body over the Cartesian spectating mind — the same insight that Merleau-Ponty will develop phenomenologically in *Phenomenology of Perception*.

### Language and meaning

Peirce's semiotic is the most philosophically developed account of meaning in the 19th century, and arguably the most developed in the entire tradition. The triadic structure of the sign (representamen–object–interpretant), the taxonomy of iconic, indexical, and symbolic relations, and the open-ended chain of semiosis are the conceptual framework for all subsequent semiotic research.

The pragmatic maxim — meaning is practical consequence — anticipates the verificationist criterion of logical positivism (the meaning of a proposition is its method of verification) but also its critique: the pragmatic maxim is broader (consequences for inquiry generally, not just perceptual verification), and it avoids the positivist's failure to account for the meaning of theoretical and mathematical claims.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Dewey's democratic philosophy is the most comprehensive account of social ethics in the pragmatist tradition. Democracy is not consensus management but the cultivation of intelligent public inquiry — which requires free expression, freedom of inquiry, and education that develops the capacities for participation. Dewey is the philosophical ancestor of deliberative democracy (Habermas, Benhabib) and of civic republicanism.

Schopenhauer's ethics: compassion as the recognition that the other's suffering is one's own (the veil of individual identity dissolved) is the most rigorous Western derivation of an ethics of care from metaphysical premises. It influenced Schweitzer's reverence for life, the animal rights movement (Schopenhauer is the first major Western philosopher to insist that animals are moral patients), and Simone Weil's ethics of attention.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

Schopenhauer and pragmatism orient themselves to Buddhism and Eastern philosophy in ways others don't. Schopenhauer's explicit engagement with Hindu and Buddhist thought — the concept of Maya, the will-to-live as the source of suffering, the ethical ideal of compassion, aesthetic and ascetic liberation — represents the first serious philosophical engagement with Asian traditions in Western philosophy, a precursor of the comparative philosophy that will flourish in the 20th century.

Peirce's fallibilism and community-of-inquiry conception of truth is the scientific epistemology that takes scientific practice most seriously — not formal inductivism (Bacon) or falsificationism (Popper) but a dynamic, social, self-correcting process of inquiry. James's pragmatic religion is distinctive: not proof of God's existence but the pragmatic value of religious belief for the one who can hold it — a position that takes religious experience as an experiential datum (not a delusion) without making dogmatic metaphysical claims.

## The Arc Forward

Schopenhauer's arc:
- **Nietzsche**: begins as a Schopenhauerian (*Birth of Tragedy*) but eventually inverts Schopenhauer — affirm the will rather than deny it; life is to be celebrated, not escaped.
- **Freud**: *Beyond the Pleasure Principle* introduces the death drive — the organism's tendency toward non-being, self-destructive repetition — that is recognizably Schopenhauerian in character. Freud acknowledged the connection.
- **Wagner**: *Tristan und Isolde* and *Parsifal* are direct Schopenhauerian art — the triumph of compassion over will, of will-less music over narrative.
- **Thomas Hardy, Joseph Conrad, Samuel Beckett**: literary pessimism with Schopenhauerian philosophical roots.

Pragmatism's arc:
- **20th-century analytic philosophy**: Quine explicitly acknowledges pragmatist roots; his naturalized epistemology is a pragmatist epistemology. Rawls's reflective equilibrium is a pragmatist method. Sellars's myth of the given (attacking foundationalism) continues Dewey's critique of the spectator theory of knowledge.
- **Rorty's neopragmatism**: *Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature* (1979) — the most famous 20th-century pragmatist return, dissolving the representationalism of both empiricist and rationalist traditions.
- **Brandom's inferentialism**: a Sellarsian-Peircean account of meaning as normative inferential relations.
- **Habermas's communicative action**: Dewey's democratic inquiry formalized through speech act theory and universal pragmatics.
- **Cognitive linguistics and cognitive science**: embodied cognition (Lakoff, Johnson, Varela, Thompson, Rosch) is the scientific version of Dewey's organism-environment transaction.
