# Marx and the Materialist Turn (1844–1867)

**Karl Marx** (1818–1883) is both a philosopher and far more than one: his work spans philosophy, economics, political theory, history, and sociology; his practical influence on the 20th century exceeds any other philosopher's; and his philosophical ideas remain among the most contested in the tradition. Understanding Marx as a philosopher — rather than as a political ideologist — requires attention to his roots in German Idealism, his relationship to Hegel (which he describes as inverting), and the genuine philosophical problems his work confronts: alienation, ideology, historical explanation, and the relationship between theory and practice.

---

## 1. From Hegel to Feuerbach to Marx

### The Young Hegelians and Feuerbach

After Hegel's death in 1831, his school split into Right (conservative, orthodox) and Left (radical, critical) Hegelians. The Left Hegelians accepted Hegel's dialectical method but rejected his political conclusions: if Spirit's self-knowledge culminates in the Prussian rational state, then philosophy endorses the status quo. The Left Hegelians saw in Hegel's own method a tool for critique — the "negative dialectic" that exposes the contradictions between what is actual and what is rational.

**Ludwig Feuerbach** (1804–1872) put Hegel right-side-up by *naturalizing* him: Hegel had made human consciousness the vehicle of an Absolute Spirit developing through history. Feuerbach reversed the priority — God (the absolute) is not the source of human consciousness but its projection. Humans, experiencing their own species-essence (consciousness, reason, love), project these qualities onto an imagined infinite being — God — and then experience their own humanity as inadequate against this standard. **Religion is alienation**: self-alienated human consciousness worshipping its own essence in external, objectified form.

Feuerbach's *Essence of Christianity* (1841) defines the program: not theology but *anthropology* — the study of human nature as the ground of all apparent theology. "Not God created man in his image but man created God in his image."

Marx absorbed Feuerbach's materialist inversion of Hegel but found Feuerbach's materialism limited — it sees religion as the problem and consciousness as the medium, without addressing the *material conditions* that produce religion and religious alienation. The eleventh Thesis on Feuerbach (1845) is the most famous: "Philosophers have only interpreted the world in various ways; the point is to *change* it." This is not anti-theoretical but rather the claim that genuine understanding of the world requires understanding it as *changeable through practice*.

## 2. Alienation and Species-Being

The *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844* (not published until 1932 — crucial for the reception history of "humanist Marxism") develop a philosophical anthropology around the concept of **alienation** (*Entfremdung*).

### Species-Being

For Marx, humans are uniquely distinctive among natural beings not because they have rational souls (Aristotle, Descartes) or because they are subjects of pure practical reason (Kant) but because they make their own life-activity into an *object* of their consciousness. An animal produces according to immediate instinct; a human consciously shapes production according to a mental model, transforming nature according to a plan. This capacity for **purposive, conscious self-objectification** in activity is what Marx calls **species-being** (*Gattungswesen*) — humans are beings of a species that reflects on and constitutes its own generic nature through collective production.

### Alienated Labor

Under capitalism, wage labor involves four forms of alienation:
1. **Alienation from the product**: the worker produces an object that belongs not to them but to the capitalist; the product confronts the worker as an alien power.
2. **Alienation from the activity of production**: labor — which should be the expression of human species-being, the conscious self-objectification — becomes mere means to wage, not an end in itself. The worker feels most themselves when not working; at work they feel most inhumanly constrained.
3. **Alienation from species-being**: the conscious, free, purposive production that defines humanity is inverted into mere means for physical survival — species-being is reduced to animal functioning.
4. **Alienation from other humans**: estrangement from the activity and product becomes estrangement from each other — the human community (the species as such) becomes an alien force confronting individuals.

This is Hegel's master-slave dialectic recast in economic terms: the worker objectifies themselves in labor; the product (their own species-being made concrete) confronts them as an alien power that belongs to and enhances another (the capitalist). De-alienation requires not just a change of consciousness (Feuerbach's suggestion) but a change in the material relations of production.

## 3. Historical Materialism

The *German Ideology* (1845–46, also unpublished until the 20th century) and the *Preface to the Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* (1859) present Marx's mature philosophical framework: **historical materialism**.

### Base and Superstructure

The guiding idea: at any historical period, the **mode of production** — the forces of production (technology, labor power, natural resources) combined with the relations of production (who owns what, what social relationships organize production) — determines the general character of social, political, and intellectual life.

The famous architectural metaphor: the mode of production is the real **foundation** (base, *Basis*) of society; the legal system, political forms, and ideological forms (religion, philosophy, art) are the **superstructure** (*Überbau*) — built on and shaped by the base. When the forces of production develop to a point where the existing relations of production become a fetter — when the productive forces are ready for a higher ordering of society but the existing property relations hold them back — a period of social revolution begins.

This gives history an internal logic: each mode of production (slavery; feudalism; capitalism; the anticipated communism) has its own productive rationality and its own contradictions that drive it toward a successor. History is not the development of Spirit's self-knowledge (Hegel) but the development of human productive powers through successive social formations.

The base/superstructure model is subtle and has been frequently oversimplified. Marx never claims that all ideas, culture, and politics *mechanically* reflect economic relations; the relationship is one of determination in the last instance, with relative autonomy for superstructural elements. The debate about exactly how much autonomy is among the most important in 20th-century Marxist theory (Gramsci, Althusser, E.P. Thompson).

### Ideology

If the ruling ideas of an era reflect the ruling material interests, then ideas are not ideologically neutral — they serve social functions of legitimation and naturalization. **Ideology** (in the pejorative Marxist sense) is not mere false belief but systematically distorted belief that serves the interests of the dominant class — making historically contingent and changeable arrangements appear natural, necessary, and universal.

Classic examples: the "naturalness" of private property; the claim that free contract between employer and employee is genuinely free (ignoring structural coercion); the claim that national citizenship transcends class interest. Ideology is not simply lying: ideological beliefs are often sincerely held, because the material conditions of life generate patterns of thought that are locally coherent but systematically distorted. The task of ideology critique is to reveal the social conditions (and the interests they serve) underlying apparently neutral claims.

**False consciousness** (a phrase more Engels's than Marx's) — the holding of beliefs contrary to one's own interests — is the extreme form. But the subtler claim is more important: even beliefs that accurately represent immediate experience can systematically misrepresent the deeper social relations from which that experience arises. The critique of commodity fetishism (*Capital* vol. 1, ch. 1) is the masterclass: market exchange makes commodities (social relations between people) appear as relations between things; the social character of labor is hidden behind the apparent objectivity of market prices.

## 4. Capital: Value, Labor, and Surplus Value

*Capital: A Critique of Political Economy* (vol. 1, 1867) is Marx's major intellectual achievement — an attempt to explain the internal logic, dynamics, and contradictions of the capitalist mode of production.

### The Labor Theory of Value

Marx builds on Ricardo's labor theory of value: the exchange value of a commodity is determined by the socially necessary labor time required to produce it. This is not an empirical generalization but a theoretical claim about what makes commodities commensurable — why we can exchange a coat for a quantity of linen.

### Surplus Value

Where does profit come from? The capitalist buys labor-power (not labor itself) as a commodity; its value is determined by the cost of reproducing the worker (subsistence wages). But the worker can produce more than their own subsistence cost in a working day. This surplus — **surplus value** — is appropriated by the capitalist, not because the capitalist cheats or violates market rules, but because the market's own principles govern the sale of labor-power at subsistence value. Exploitation is not a deviation from capitalism but its normal operation.

**Absolute surplus value**: extend the working day (more hours of work beyond the necessary labor time).
**Relative surplus value**: increase productivity through technology, so the necessary labor time (what it takes to reproduce the worker) decreases, and the surplus portion grows even at the same working day length.

### Accumulation, Competition, and Crises

Capitalists must accumulate — they compete against each other and must reinvest surpluses in improved production or be outcompeted. This drives technological development and increasing productivity on one side and the **tendency of the rate of profit to fall** on the other (as the ratio of living labor — the only surplus-value-creating factor — to dead labor — accumulated capital — tends to fall). Periodic crises of overproduction, falling profits, and financial crisis are built into the logic of accumulation, not contingent failures.

Marx predicts increasing concentration of capital (monopolization), increasing pauperization (immiseration) of the working class, and periodic crises of increasing severity — culminating in revolution when the capitalist relations of production become an absolute fetter on productive forces.

## Five Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Marx's epistemology (most explicit in the Theses on Feuerbach and the *German Ideology*) is **praxis-centered**: knowledge is not pure contemplation but is embedded in and shaped by practical activity. The world is not a spectacle given to a detached knower but a field of transformative action. Social being determines social consciousness, not vice versa.

This is the philosophical foundation of **standpoint epistemology**: different positions in the social structure (class positions, gendered positions, racial positions) generate different and partly incommensurable perspectives on social reality; those at the bottom of social hierarchies may have access to truth that those at the top cannot see (because dominant ideology distorts what those in power perceive). The feminist standpoint epistemology of Hartsock, Harding, and Collins directly extends this Marxian insight.

### Language and meaning

The Marxist tradition is thin on philosophy of language, but ideology critique is specifically a linguistic practice — the analysis of how meaning-systems naturalize and legitimize social arrangements. The later development of ideology critique through Gramsci (hegemony — ideological leadership as cultural consent-making), Althusser (ideological state apparatuses, interpellation — the process by which ideology "hails" subjects into their social positions), and the Frankfurt School (the theory of the culture industry — Adorno and Horkheimer) all develop the Marxian analysis of language and culture as sites of social reproduction.

Closer to the philosophy of language: Voloshinov's *Marxism and the Philosophy of Language* (1929) offers a materialist account of the sign — signs are sites of social struggle; the same sign-form carries different meanings for different social groups; communication is never merely the transmission of neutral information but the arena of social antagonism.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Marx's ethics is deliberately suppressed — he distrusted moralistic appeals to justice and rights as ideological distractions from the real (material) mechanisms of oppression. But the early manuscripts are full of moral-philosophical content: alienation is normatively loaded; species-being is a normative conception of human nature; the critique of capitalism is based on the implicit standard of a social order in which productive activity would be genuinely self-expressive. The "mature" Marx wanted science, not moral philosophy — but science in service of human emancipation.

The question of the relationship between descriptive historical materialism (this is how capitalism works) and normative socialist politics (this is what we ought to do about it) — the Humean is-ought gap applied to Marxism — is unresolved in Marx and the subject of endless debate.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

Historical materialism claims to be a *science* — not a philosophical system but an empirical theory of historical development. Whether it is a genuine scientific theory (falsifiable, revisable) or a philosophical framework masquerading as science is contested by friends and enemies alike (Popper's famous critique: Marxism is unfalsifiable — any evidence is reinterpreted to fit).

On religion: following Feuerbach, Marx holds that religion is the expression of real suffering and protest against real oppression in mystified, inverted form. "Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world, and the soul of soulless conditions. It is the opium of the people." The critique of religion is not an end in itself but the beginning of the critique of its real material conditions.

## The Arc Forward

Marx's work sets two distinct agendas:

**Political**: the communist movement and socialist theory — Marxism-Leninism, social democracy, democratic socialism, the debates of the 20th century labor movement.

**Philosophical**: a program of ideology critique, economic analysis, and political theory that generates:
- The **Frankfurt School** (Horkheimer, Adorno, Marcuse, Habermas): critical theory — Marx's ideology critique combined with Freudian theory, Weber's rationalization thesis, and Hegel's dialectic. The *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (Horkheimer and Adorno, 1944): does Enlightenment rationality itself become a form of domination?
- **Structural Marxism** (Althusser): scientific (not humanist) Marxism; reading Capital as a theoretical rupture from ideology; ideological state apparatuses.
- **Cultural Marxism** and cultural studies (Gramsci's hegemony, the Birmingham School, Jameson's postmodern Marxism).
- **Analytical Marxism** (Cohen, Roemer, Elster): reconstructing historical materialism using the technical tools of analytic philosophy and social science.
- **Post-Marxism** (Laclau and Mouffe): hegemony as discursive construction; the impossibility of a fully sutured social totality; radical democracy.

The debates generated by Marx remain among the most fundamental in contemporary political philosophy, sociology, and cultural theory.
