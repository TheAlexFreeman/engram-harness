# Kierkegaard and Nietzsche: The Individual Against the System (1843–1895)

Kierkegaard and Nietzsche are the 19th century's great individualists — philosophers who stand outside the dominant currents of their time (Hegelian systematics, scientism, bourgeois progressivism) and insist on the irreducibility of the singular, existing human being. They do not form a "school" and share no agreement on conclusions — Kierkegaard is a Christian (of sorts), Nietzsche is the anti-Christ — but they share a **style** and a **problem**: philosophical thought must engage the individual who actually exists, in their passion, their crisis, their confrontation with death and meaning, not just the universal categories in which such individuals are subsumed.

Both are primarily read in the 20th century: Kierkegaard is almost unknown outside Denmark in his own time; Nietzsche's influence explodes after his 1889 mental collapse. Together they define the agenda of existentialism, perspectivism, and much of continental philosophy.

## 1. Kierkegaard: Stages of Existence and the Leap of Faith

**Søren Kierkegaard** (1813–1855) is the father of existentialism — not because he uses the word (he doesn't), but because he makes *existing* — the singular, passionate, temporally situated being of a specific human being — the central philosophical concern, as opposed to the eternal, universal categories within which Hegelian thought comprehends existing things.

### The Critique of the System

Hegel's system claims to comprehend all reality — history, nature, religion, art, philosophy — within a single rational framework in which everything is a moment of Spirit's self-development. Kierkegaard finds this unacceptable at a fundamental level: no system can capture existence, because existence is what slips through the conceptual net. **To exist is not to be an instance of a universal but to be this singular individual, here, now, uncertain, anxious, in time.**

The system is oriented toward mediation — all opposites are synthesized at a higher level. But some things, Kierkegaard insists, resist mediation: either/or. The choice between the aesthetic and the ethical life is not a logical contradiction to be sublated but a radical either/or that the individual must decide. Philosophy that collapses this into a synthesis falsifies the actual structure of human existence.

### The Three Stages (Existential Spheres)

Kierkegaard (in *Either/Or*, *Stages on Life's Way*, and the pseudonymous works) presents three modes of human existence, each with its own logic, its own conception of the self, and its own failure:

**1. The Aesthetic Stage**: The aesthete lives for the immediate enjoyment of experience — beauty, sensory pleasure, intellectual stimulation, novelty. Don Juan (the seducer who catalogues his conquests) is the limiting case; the cultivated aesthete who crafts ever more refined modes of enjoyment is another. The aesthetic life is ultimately self-defeating: pleasure exhausts itself; novelty becomes routine; the aesthete who refuses commitment discovers that they have no self — only a collection of fleeting experiences without identity. The characteristic mood: **boredom and despair**.

**2. The Ethical Stage**: The transition from aesthetic to ethical is not a logical argument but a choice — a leap. The ethical person chooses commitment, duty, universal moral principles — acts as a representative of the universal, as Kant's good will. This is a deeper mode of existence than the aesthetic: the self is constituted through commitment and responsibility rather than dissolved in passing experience. But the ethical stage has its own failure: the ethical person discovers in the end that they *cannot* fulfill the moral law perfectly; guilt is inescapable. The ethical is not self-sufficient.

**3. The Religious Stage**: The movement from ethical to religious is the most difficult — the "teleological suspension of the ethical." Abraham is commanded to sacrifice his son Isaac: this is straightforwardly unethical (murder), yet Abraham obeys God. There is no universal ethical principle that justifies Abraham; indeed, the act *violates* the ethical universal. Abraham acts on the basis of a radically singular, absolutely private relation to God — a relation that cannot be communicated, understood, or justified to anyone else. This is the **leap of faith**: the suspension of rational justification in the movement to an absolute, unconditional relation to the absolute.

The movement is illustrated in *Fear and Trembling* (1843) — Kierkegaard's greatest single text — through the "knight of infinite resignation" (who gives up the finite, accepting loss absolutely) and the "knight of faith" (who gives up the finite and then, impossibly, receives it back by virtue of the absurd). Faith is not a feeling but a movement — returning to the finite (Abraham gets Isaac back) after passing through infinite resignation.

### Indirect Communication and the Pseudonyms

Kierkegaard publishes his major works under pseudonyms — *Either/Or* is published by "Victor Eremita," *Fear and Trembling* by "Johannes de Silentio," *Philosophical Fragments* by "Johannes Climacus." This is deliberate: each pseudonym represents a particular stage or position; Kierkegaard as author does not identify with any of them but puts them in relationship in what he calls **indirect communication**.

Indirect communication reflects the general point: existence cannot be directly transmitted. If I tell you directly "have faith," I have said nothing — it is empty information. Faith must be arrived at; the most a philosopher can do is to remove obstacles, present existing possibilities, and create the conditions under which the individual might make their own leap. This is why Kierkegaard writes *narratively*, ironically, in character — not to prove propositions but to embody existential possibilities.

This is the philosophical foundation of a theory of communication that goes beyond propositional content — meaning as something that must be enacted, not merely transmitted. Its influence runs to Heidegger's account of authentic communication (vs. the idle talk of *das Man*), to speech act theory, and to narrative philosophy of mind.

### Anxiety and the Self

*The Concept of Anxiety* (1844) develops a philosophical psychology around the concept of **anxiety** (*Angest*) — not fear (which is fear of a specific thing) but anxiety as the free consciousness of freedom itself: the dizzying awareness that I am not determined in advance, that my future is genuinely open, that I must choose.

Anxiety is not a pathology to be cured but the structural condition of human freedom: the "dizziness of freedom" before the vertigo of possibility. It is the precondition of both sin (the leap into avoidance) and genuine selfhood (the authentic choice of oneself).

*The Sickness Unto Death* (1849) defines the self as a **relation that relates itself to itself** — not a substance but an act of self-constitution. Despair is the failure to be oneself — either the despair of not willing to be a self (fleeing into the aesthetic, the crowd, the universal) or the despair of willing to be a self in one's own strength (without grounding in God). Only when the self rests transparently in the power that constituted it (God) is despair overcome.

## 2. Nietzsche: The Death of God and the Transvaluation of Values

**Friedrich Nietzsche** (1844–1900) is one of the most misunderstood, most abused, and most philosophically fertile thinkers in the Western tradition. He has been (falsely) appropriated by Nazis, by libertarians, by political reactionaries, and by almost every counter-cultural movement of the 20th century. His actual positions are specific, complex, and internally consistent. His influence on 20th-century continental philosophy (Heidegger, Foucault, Derrida, Deleuze), on psychoanalysis (Freud acknowledges Nietzsche's anticipation of key ideas), on literature, and on culture generally is enormous.

### The Death of God

Nietzsche's most famous proclamation — the madman in *The Gay Science* (1882, §125): "God is dead. God remains dead. And we have killed him." This is not an atheist triumphalism but a diagnosis. The Enlightenment has undermined the cognitive foundations of Christian theology; educated Europeans no longer genuinely believe in the Christian God (even if they go to church). But the moral framework — Christian values, the moral imperative, the pursuit of truth — has not yet recognized its own foundation is gone. We are living on borrowed time: "The event itself is far too great, too distant, too remote from the multitude's capacity for comprehension even for the tidings of it to be thought of as having arrived as yet."

The death of God generates the problem of **nihilism** — the collapse of the highest values, the loss of meaning, the sense that nothing is worth anything. This is the great danger of the modern age: after the collapse of Christianity, what will prevent the slide into passive nihilism — the "last men," comfortable, pleasure-seeking, incapable of great aspiration?

### Critique of Morality: Slave Morality and Ressentiment

Nietzsche's *On the Genealogy of Morality* (1887) offers a **genealogical** critique of moral concepts — not asking "is this moral claim true?" but "what are the historical conditions (of power, psychological mechanisms, social struggles) from which this moral claim arose? What does it do? Who does it serve?"

The central distinction:

**Master morality**: the morality of the strong, the noble, the active — who define "good" as the expression of their own power, excellence, and affirmation. The primary value-pair is good/bad (excellent vs. mediocre); the bad is not evil but merely weak, low, inadequate. Master morality is self-affirming and creative.

**Slave morality**: the morality of the weak, the suffering, the oppressed — who, unable to overcome their oppressors directly, develop a **reactive** value-system. They take the values of the masters and invert them: the powerful (formerly "good") become "evil"; the weak (formerly "bad") become "good." The primary value-pair is good/evil; evil is primary (defined by the masters whom the slaves resent), good is secondary (whatever is opposite to evil).

The psychological mechanism is **ressentiment** (Nietzsche uses the French term) — repressed anger, envy, and the desire for revenge that, unable to express itself in action, turns inward and becomes the engine of moral idealism. The moral law ("thou shalt not kill," "the meek shall inherit the earth") is the slave's revenge against the master, dressed up in the language of universal morality. Nietzsche traces this process from the Hebrew prophets through Christianity to modern progressivism and egalitarianism.

This is deliberately provocative and deliberately partial — Nietzsche knows he is inverting values, not describing natural facts. The genealogical method is not a debunking (showing morality is "really" just psychology) but a de-naturalization — showing that what presents itself as eternal, universal moral truth is in fact the contingent product of historical struggles and psychological mechanisms.

### Perspectivism

Nietzsche holds that there are no facts, only interpretations — not as a nihilistic conclusion but as a **epistemological** position: all knowledge is from a perspective; there is no view from nowhere. Every claim to know the truth is a claim made from a particular standpoint, with particular interests, using particular methods that foreground some features of the world and suppress others.

This does not reduce to the trivial claim that everyone has a subjective viewpoint. Nietzsche's perspectivism is a more radical claim: the concept of "fact" — of how things are independently of any perspective — is itself a philosophical illusion. Not: "I can only see from my perspective, but the truth is what it is independent of perspective." Rather: "perspective is constitutive of what-there-is for us, and the claim to a perspective-free truth is itself a perspective that denies its own perspectival character."

The influence on 20th-century philosophy is immense: Heidegger's analysis of the historical situatedness of understanding, Gadamer's hermeneutics, Foucault's power-knowledge nexus, Derrida's critique of presence, and cognitive linguistics's attention to construal and conceptualization all bear Nietzschean marks.

### Will to Power

The metaphysical thesis underlying Nietzsche's psychology and ethics: the fundamental drive of all organic life — and arguably of all reality — is not self-preservation (Spencer's Darwinian reading) but **will to power** (*Wille zur Macht*) — the drive to expand, to overcome resistance, to discharge energy, to impose form and meaning. This is not a desire for domination over others (that's the slave's will to power in the reactive mode) but the drive toward self-overcoming: becoming what one is by continually surpassing oneself.

The will to power is also Nietzsche's account of value-creation: values don't exist in the world to be discovered; they are created by beings of sufficient strength and self-mastery. The philosopher-creator legislates new values from the fullness of life.

### Eternal Recurrence

The most abyssal thought: *The Gay Science* §341 asks — what if this moment, and every moment of your life, repeated infinitely? Would you affirm it or deny it? The eternal recurrence is not a cosmological thesis but an **existential test**: the measure of whether you have lived in such a way that you can will the eternal repetition of your life. The response that can say "yes" — that can will the eternal recurrence — is the mark of the highest affirmation.

The thought is closely related to Nietzsche's concept of **amor fati** (love of fate) — not merely acceptance of what was and is but genuine love of it, including the suffering and failure.

### The Übermensch

The *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* (1883–85) presents the **Übermensch** (overman, superman) as the figure who creates new values after the death of God. The Übermensch is not a biological or racial category (a Nazi distortion) but an existential aspiration: the human being who *over-comes* themselves, who channels the will to power creatively rather than reactively, who legislates new values from the fullness of life rather than seeking comfort in inherited values or reactive revenge.

The Übermensch is contrasted with the "last man" — the comfortable, mediocre, herd-following figure who asks only for warmth and a little poison (entertainment) and defines happiness as modest contentment. The last man is Nietzsche's nightmare vision of democratic modernity — and of the utilitarian-progressive calculus of happiness as the political aim.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Kierkegaard's contribution to the philosophy of mind is indirect but profound: the analysis of anxiety (the vertigo of freedom, the consciousness of possibility) in *The Concept of Anxiety* is among the first philosophical accounts of existential affect — moods as modes of cognitive openness to the world rather than mere subjective states. This prefigures Heidegger's account of *Stimmung* (mood, attunement) as a pri mordial mode of being-in-the-world (not a distortion of perception but a disclosure of the world in its character).

Nietzsche's perspectivism directly challenges the dominant epistemological framework: knowledge as correspondence between a neutral representation and an objective fact. The alternative: knowledge as perspectival engagement — shaped by interests, values, embodied positions. Contemporary cognitive science, particularly in the embodied/enactive tradition, converges on this claim: cognition is embedded in a biological body with evolutionary history and interests; there is no view from nowhere in cognition.

### Language and meaning

Nietzsche's philosophy of language is marked by his analysis of metaphor in "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense" (1873): "What is truth? A mobile army of metaphors, metonyms, and anthropomorphisms — in short, a sum of human relations, which have been enhanced, transposed, and embellished poetically and rhetorically, and which after long use seem firm, canonical, and obligatory to a people." Truths are worn-out metaphors — the original figural origin forgotten, the transfer from domain to domain naturalized.

This is the most direct philosophical anticipation of cognitive linguistics's conceptual metaphor theory: all abstract thought is constituted by systematic metaphorical transfers from concrete domains (ARGUMENT IS WAR; TIME IS MONEY; LIFE IS A JOURNEY). Nietzsche sees this as an exposure of the metaphorical character of "literal" thought; Lakoff and Johnson see it as a description of the scaffolding of abstract cognition.

Kierkegaard's indirect communication raises a problem that speech act theory will later formalize: what does a text do beyond stating propositions? *Fear and Trembling* is not primarily an argument (the "argument" is deliberately inconclusive) but a performance of existential possibility that the reader must inhabit.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Kierkegaard and Nietzsche are both critics of dominant ethical frameworks: Kierkegaard's attack on the ethical stage (it cannot bear the weight of existence alone; the teleological suspension shows its limits); Nietzsche's genealogical critique of slave morality and the devaluation of life through moral idealism.

What remains? For Kierkegaard: the absolute relation to the absolute (which cannot be formalized). For Nietzsche: the affirmation of life, amor fati, the will to power as self-overcoming. Neither gives a system; both insist that moral life requires existing consciousness (Kierkegaard) or creative emergence from strength (Nietzsche) rather than compliance with universal principles.

Their 20th-century heirs: existentialism (Heidegger, Sartre — authenticity as the form Kierkegaard's leap assumes in secular form), poststructuralism (Foucault's genealogy as an extension of Nietzsche's, Derrida's critique of presence as perspectivism radicalized).

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

Kierkegaard and Nietzsche share a rejection of the scientific worldview as the ultimate measure of reality — but for opposite reasons.

Kierkegaard: science gives objective truth, but the existing individual's deepest concern is **subjective truth** — how to live, how to relate to the eternal, what to stake one's life on. The scientist's objectivity is purchased at the cost of personal stake; the existential question requires exactly the personal stake that science excludes.

Nietzsche: the will to truth (the scientific ethos) is itself an expression of will to power — one of the most powerful and admirable, but subject to genealogical critique. The unconditional demand for truth (including about the conditions of truth itself) will eventually undermine the foundations of science and metaphysics, leaving only the creative affirmation of the life-affirming interpreter. The "eternal truths" of science are themselves perspectives that serve the interests of certain ways of organizing life.

## The Arc Forward

Kierkegaard's legacy:
- **Existentialism**: Heidegger's *Being and Time* (1927) is Kierkegaard's *Concept of Anxiety* transformed through the Husserlian phenomenology of intentionality — anxiety, authenticity, death, the call of conscience are all present. Sartre's radical freedom and bad faith develop Kierkegaard's aesthetic/ethical failure directly.
- **Theological existentialism**: Barth, Tillich, Buber — the rediscovery of Kierkegaard transforms 20th-century Protestant theology.
- **Philosophy of therapy**: the analysis of despair in *The Sickness Unto Death* is an ancestor of existential psychotherapy (Frankl, May, Yalom).

Nietzsche's legacy:
- **Heidegger**: *Nietzsche* (1936–46) — the four-volume lecture course reading Nietzsche as the culmination of Western metaphysics and the beginning of its overcoming.
- **Foucault**: genealogy as method (Discipline and Punish, History of Sexuality) is explicitly Nietzschean — the descent (not the progress) of institutions and norms.
- **Derrida**: the critique of presence and the metaphysics of logocentrism is perspectivism radicalized.
- **Deleuze**: *Nietzsche and Philosophy* (1962) rehabilitates Nietzsche as a philosopher of difference — will to power as difference, not domination; becoming, not being.
- **Cognitive linguistics**: conceptual metaphor theory as the scientific verification of Nietzsche's "Truth and Lies."
