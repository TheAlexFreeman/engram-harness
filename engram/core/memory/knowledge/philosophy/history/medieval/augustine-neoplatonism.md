# Augustine and Christian Neo-Platonism: The Synthesis of Athens and Jerusalem (354–430 CE and its Legacy)

Augustine of Hippo stands at the hinge between the ancient and medieval worlds. He was schooled in the Latin rhetorical tradition (Cicero turned him toward philosophy), passed through Manichaeism (a dualist religion that accounted for evil by positing two equal cosmic principles) and Neo-Platonism (which solved the Manichaean problem of evil and gave him the conceptual vocabulary to think about God), and finally arrived at Christian faith — a conversion he narrates in the *Confessions* with unparalleled psychological depth. The result of this trajectory is a synthesis that defined Western Christianity's philosophical self-understanding for a millennium.

What Augustine did, essentially, was take the Neo-Platonic architecture — the hierarchy of being from the One down through Intellect and Soul to matter — and identify the One with the Christian God, the Intellect with the eternal Word (*Logos*), and the Soul with the created spiritual order. The emanationist metaphysics became a doctrine of creation; the Neo-Platonic return (*epistrophê*) of the soul to the One became Christian conversion and contemplative prayer. The materials were Greek; the urgency was Christian; the result was something neither tradition could have produced alone.

## 1. The Confessions: Time, Memory, and the Restless Heart

The *Confessions* (397–401 CE) is the most intimate work of ancient philosophy and among the most formally innovative. It is a prayer — addressed to God throughout — that is also a philosophical autobiography. The famous opening line names its governing theme: "Thou awakest us to delight in thy praise; for thou madest us for thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in thee." The restlessness of the heart — its inability to find rest in finite goods — is both a spiritual fact and a philosophical argument: if the soul were made for finite things, it should be satisfied; it is not; therefore it was made for something infinite. The argument from the structure of desire to the nature of its object is one of Augustine's most persistent moves.

Book XI — on time — is the most philosophically extraordinary section and the starting point for Ricoeur's entire project in *Time and Narrative* (see `narrative-cognition.md`). Augustine begins with what seems like a simple question: "What was God doing before he created the world?" The question is confused — before creation, there was no time; God created time with the world. But this launches a genuinely profound investigation:

What is time? Augustine observes that the past no longer exists, the future does not yet exist, and the present is instantaneous — as soon as you attend to it, it is already past. Yet we seem to experience duration. Paradox: neither past nor future exists, yet we speak of them. Resolution: time exists in the soul, not in things. The past, present, and future are not three times but three "attentions of the soul":
- **Present of past** = memory (*memoria*)
- **Present of present** = attention (*contuitus*)
- **Present of future** = expectation (*expectatio*)

The soul stretches itself (*distentio animi*) between memory and expectation. This stretching — the simultaneous holding of past and anticipated future in present attention — is what it is to experience duration. The present is not a durationless instant but an extended synthesis.

This is the first phenomenology of temporal experience in Western philosophy, and it is directly foundational for Husserl's analysis of time-consciousness (retention / primal impression / protention) and for Ricoeur's analysis of narrative as the cognitive technology that extends *distentio animi* from the moment to the whole of a life. The narrative binding of past, present, and anticipated future that Ricoeur analyzes as constitutive of personal identity is Augustine's *distentio animi* scaled up from the moment to the life.

## 2. Against the Manichaeans: The Problem of Evil and Neo-Platonic Privation

Manichaeism explained evil by positing two equal and opposite powers — a Good God and an Evil God — engaged in cosmic warfare. This is theodically clean (God is not responsible for evil) but theologically catastrophic: omnipotent goodness is impossible. Augustine was a Manichaean for nine years before finding the Neo-Platonic way out.

The Neo-Platonic solution: **evil is not a substance but a privation** (*privatio boni*) — an absence of being and goodness, as darkness is an absence of light, not a positive force. Everything that exists is, insofar as it exists, good (since existence and goodness are convertible with being at the level of the One). Evil is a deficiency — a falling away from being. This allows God to create everything good while accounting for evil: beings that are genuinely less than God will inevitably fall short of perfect goodness; they are genuinely creatures, genuinely limited, genuinely capable of failure.

The application to sin is significant: sin is not the power of an evil substance (the Manichaean view) but *turning away from God* — from being, truth, and goodness — toward lesser goods. The libido dominandi (lust for domination) and the libido sentiendi (lust for sensation) are not positive forces of evil but misdirected loves, loves drawn toward goods too small to satisfy the soul's infinite longing. Augustine's theory of sin is a theory of disordered desire — *concupiscence*, the tendency to attach to finite goods as if they were ultimate. The cure is not the discipline of desire (Stoic) but its *reordering* through love toward God as ultimate good.

## 3. Free Will, Grace, and the Pelagian Controversy

The thorniest and most consequential of Augustine's doctrinal developments concerns the relationship between human freedom and divine grace.

**Pelagius** (a British monk, late 4th-early 5th century) argued that human beings are capable of freely choosing virtue — God created us with the capacity for moral excellence, and original sin is not a transmissible corruption of nature but a bad example. Grace helps, but the will is genuinely free and capable.

Augustine's response, increasingly sharp over the course of his career, is that Pelagius underestimates the damage done by the Fall (*peccatum originale*). After Adam's sin, human nature is wounded — the will is turned away from the good by concupiscence; without divine grace, we cannot freely choose God. Grace is not an assistance to an otherwise capable will but the restoration and transformation of a genuinely damaged will. The elect — those God has chosen, from all eternity, for salvation — receive prevenient grace that redirects their will; the reprobate do not, through no fault of God's (the elect receive an undeserved gift; the reprobate receive what their deserving requires).

This doctrine of **predestination** makes Augustine the ancestor of Calvinist theology and creates the most concentrated tension in the tradition between divine sovereignty and human freedom. If God has eternally chosen who will be saved without regard to foreseen merit, in what sense can we be said to freely choose? Augustine's response: the will that is converted by grace acts freely (it is the will's own act) — divine causation and human freedom operate at different levels and are not in competition. This is a proto-Leibnizian "compatibilism" — liberty is compatible with determined causes — though Augustine does not develop it logically in this way.

The philosophical legacy is enormous: the free-will problem as a theological puzzle becomes the free-will problem as a philosophical puzzle about mechanism and agency, which becomes the contemporary debate between compatibilism and libertarianism about free will. The structure of Augustine's answer prefigures compatibilist moves from Hume and Mill to Frankfurt and Dennett.

## 4. The City of God: History, Politics, and the Two Cities

The *City of God* (413–426 CE) was occasioned by the sack of Rome by the Visigoths in 410 CE — an event that shocked the Roman world. Pagans blamed the new Christian religion for having undermined the martial virtues that had made Rome great. Augustine's response is a massive work of philosophy of history and political theology.

The central thesis: two cities co-exist in history, distinguished not by membership in Rome or Carthage but by what they love. The **earthly city** is defined by love of self to the point of contempt of God — *libido dominandi*, the lust for domination. The **heavenly city** (the City of God) is defined by love of God to the point of contempt of self. These two cities are not identical to the institutional Church and the Roman Empire — they are mixed together in history, distinguishable only by love, visibility only to God.

The political consequence is significant: the earthly city is not simply evil. It pursues a genuine good — peace, order, security — and Christians can use and value this peace even while not ultimately belonging to the earthly city. The Church is not the ideal political community; it is a pilgrim community passing through the earthly city, appropriating its peace for its own purposes. This is a more realistic and less utopian political theology than Eusebius's Christian imperialism — it allows Christians to be good citizens without idolizing the state.

Augustine's philosophy of history is the first sustained Christian response to the classical *cyclical* view of history — the idea that history is a series of rises and falls with no direction. Against this, Augustine proposes a *linear* eschatological history: creation, Fall, redemption, final judgment. History has a beginning, middle, and end; it is a narrative with a plot, not an endless cycle. This is the template for all subsequent Western philosophies of history — including, at many removes, Hegel's dialectical history and Marx's historical materialism.

## 5. Inner Illumination: The Philosophical Core

Augustine's epistemology is a Christianized version of Platonic *anamnesis*. How does the mind attain truth? Not by constructing it from sensory experience (empiricism); not by intuiting Platonic Forms in a separate realm (Plato's literal teaching). The mind is illuminated from within by an interior light — Augustine calls this the **illumination theory**. When the mind perceives necessary, eternal truths (mathematical and logical truths, moral truths), it perceives them by a divine illumination: God is the uncreated light that makes truth visible to the mind, as the sun makes physical things visible to the eye.

The exact relationship between divine illumination and human intellect is debated: does illumination give content (God implants specific ideas in us, à la Descartes's innate ideas)? Or does illumination give the *power* to perceive truths that are objectively available? Augustine seems to hold the latter — we perceive the eternal truths, not God directly — but the vocabulary is fluid.

The epistemological argument for God's existence from within: the mind encounters truths that are above its own contingent, changing powers. Mathematical truths don't depend on what I think; they were true before I was born and will be true after my death. This eternal, mind-independent truth cannot be a product of my finite, historically situated mind. But it is accessible *through* the mind. Therefore there is something above the finite mind and common to all minds — the eternal Truth, which Augustine identifies with God (or the Logos/Word). This is the Augustinian version of the ontological argument's logic applied to truth rather than being.

Descartes will make a closely related move: the idea of an infinite perfect being cannot have been produced by my finite mind; therefore it must have been placed in me by God, who is the cause adequate to producing such an idea. The structure — arguing from what the finite mind finds within itself to a transcendent necessary cause — is Augustinian before it is Cartesian.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Augustine's *distentio animi* is the first phenomenology of temporal experience and the philosophical ancestor of both Husserl's time-consciousness and Ricoeur's narrative identity. His illumination theory is the first Christian theory of a priori knowledge — necessary truths are known through divine illumination rather than empirical abstraction. His *cogito*-like argument in *De Trinitate* (to doubt is to think; to think is to be — before Descartes formalized it) establishes the priority of the inner life as the site where truth is encountered.

### Language and meaning

Augustine's *De Magistro* ("On the Teacher") — a dialogue with his son Adeodatus — argues that words cannot teach by themselves; what is taught is learned through inner illumination, not through the verbal sign. The word is a prompt; the teaching comes from the "inner teacher" (Christ as the Word within). This is a radical non-conventionalism: language directs attention but does not carry truth. Wittgenstein will quote Augustine in the first paragraph of the *Philosophical Investigations* to set up what he regards as a fundamentally mistaken picture of language — the Augustinian picture that every word names something, and sentences are combinations of names. Wittgenstein's critique of this picture is the starting point of his later philosophy.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Augustine founds the Western tradition of **confessional selfhood** — the idea that the self is constituted by the narrative of its relation to God, told in first person, with full exposure of interior motivation. The *Confessions* invents a form of self-examination and self-narration that has no precedent and extraordinary consequents: Rousseau's *Confessions*, the Protestant tradition of spiritual autobiography, psychoanalysis, the contemporary memoir. The examined life is here not Socratic dialogue but inner prayer — a form of self-presence to oneself before God.

The political theology of the two cities resists both theocracy (the Church is not the ideal political state) and secularism (the earthly city pursues only a penultimate good). This is a philosophically serious intermediate position that anticipates the "post-secular" political theology of Rawls's critics and Habermas's late work.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

The privation theory of evil is Augustine's most philosophically precise move — it solves the problem of evil by denying that evil is a substance, while accounting for its reality as a deficiency. The doctrine of creation ex nihilo — God creates not from pre-existing matter (contra Plato's demiurge) but from nothing — has direct metaphysical consequences: creatures are entirely dependent on God for their being at every moment, not just their origin. This is the doctrine of **ontological dependence** or **participation**: created things share in being only insofar as they are sustained by the divine being. This is the engine of medieval metaphysics.

Augustine's linear philosophy of history — history as a narrative with beginning, middle, and end, directed toward a definite telos — is the template for every subsequent Western philosophy of history. It distinguishes Western historical consciousness from both the cyclical cosmologies of classical antiquity and the atemporal cosmologies of many Eastern traditions.

## The Arc Forward

Augustine's synthesis shapes the whole of medieval Latin philosophy. No medieval Christian philosopher is doing philosophy without Augustine in the background, and usually in the foreground. His doctrine of illumination becomes the central epistemological question of the Scholastic period: does the intellect need special divine illumination (the Augustinian tradition, represented by Bonaventure) or can it arrive at truth by natural abstraction from sense experience (the Aristotelian tradition, represented by Aquinas)? This is a debate about whether the intellect has natural self-sufficiency or depends on supernatural assistance — with obvious implications for the relation between philosophy and theology, reason and faith.

The will-grace-predestination complex is the dominant issue in Western theology from Augustine through Luther, Calvin, and the Council of Trent. Kant's ethics is partly an attempt to recover human moral autonomy after the Augustinian tradition has made the will so thoroughly dependent on grace that ordinary moral effort seems insufficient.

The *Confessions* creates the genre of self-reflective autobiography that shapes personal narrative through Rousseau, Wordsworth, Mill, Newman, and Simone de Beauvoir. The specific Augustinian structure — restless desire seeking an adequate object; the failure of finite goods to satisfy; the turn inward that is simultaneously a turn toward the transcendent — echoes in Romantic literature, existentialist philosophy, and contemporary discussions of meaning and purpose.
