# Scholasticism: Faith, Reason, and the Ambitions of Medieval Philosophy (c. 1100–1350 CE)

The Scholastic period in medieval Latin philosophy is usually undervalued in popular histories, which tend to treat it as an obscurantist interlude between the Greeks and the Renaissance. This is almost exactly wrong. The Scholastics were doing philosophy — real, technically demanding, original philosophy — in conditions that required extraordinary intellectual creativity. They had to integrate Aristotle's newly recovered philosophy with Christian theology; they had to develop formal tools for systematic argument and debate; and they did so while producing some of the most lasting contributions to logic, metaphysics, and philosophy of language in Western history.

The recovery of Aristotle's complete works through Arabic translations and Latin translations from the Greek in the 12th–13th centuries was the intellectual event that triggered Scholasticism's golden age. It was not a gentle discovery but a shock: here was a comprehensive, systematic philosophy of nature, science, and ethics that had been developed without reference to Christian revelation, that contradicted Christian doctrine on several points (eternity of the world, nature of the soul, intellectual immortality), and that was clearly the most powerful philosophical system available. What was to be done?

Three responses divided the period:
1. **Augustine-first conservatism**: Aristotle is dangerous; use Neo-Platonism and Augustine to limit philosophical speculation (Bonaventure).
2. **Radical Aristotelianism**: Aristotle's philosophy should be followed wherever it leads, even if it conflicts with theology (the Latin Averroists — Siger of Brabant, Boethius of Dacia).
3. **Integration**: carefully distinguish what Aristotle gets right (in physics, logic, metaphysics) from what Christianity must correct, and build the synthesis (Aquinas).

## 1. Anselm: The Ontological Argument and the Intelligibility of God

**Anselm of Canterbury** (1033–1109 CE) is the founding figure of the Scholastic style — *fides quaerens intellectum*, faith seeking understanding. The point is not to establish faith by reason (faith is a gift) but to understand rationally what faith already affirms. God is not a hypothesis to be tested; God is the presupposition from within which understanding is sought.

The **ontological argument** (*Proslogion*, c. 1078) is the most famous and most disputed argument in the history of philosophy. Its structure:

1. God is "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" (*id quo maius cogitari non potest*).
2. Even the fool who denies God's existence understands what the phrase means — the concept exists at least in the intellect (*in intellectu*).
3. But a being that exists in reality (*in re*) is greater than one that exists only in the intellect.
4. Therefore, if God exists only in the intellect, we can conceive of something greater than God — namely, God existing also in reality.
5. But "greater than God" contradicts the definition: God is that than which nothing greater can be conceived.
6. Therefore God must exist in reality.

Guanilo of Marmoutiers offered the classic objection immediately: by the same logic, I can conceive of a "perfect island" and argue it must exist — the argument proves too much. Anselm's response: the argument only works for a being that has necessary existence as part of what it is; contingent beings, even perfect ones, can be coherently conceived as non-existent. The argument is specifically about a being for which existing is not merely contingent.

The argument provokes immediate skepticism — it seems to derive existence from a concept. Kant's famous objection: existence is not a predicate; adding "exists" to a concept doesn't add a property to the thing, it just says the concept has an instance. But the argument keeps coming back. Modal versions (Hartshorne, Plantinga) reformulate it using possible-worlds semantics: if it is *possible* for a necessary being to exist, then it exists in some possible world, and if it exists necessarily in any possible world, it exists in all possible worlds, including the actual world. Whether this works depends on whether it is in fact possible for a necessary being to exist — which the argument cannot establish without begging the question.

The philosophical significance is independent of whether the argument works: it is the first sustained argument that the *concept* of God is internally structured in a way that connects to existence, and it establishes the framework for all subsequent arguments about whether the existence of God can be established by reason alone.

## 2. Aquinas: The Synthesis of Aristotle and Christianity

**Thomas Aquinas** (1225–1274 CE) is the most ambitious philosophical theologian in the Western tradition. His two great *Summae* — the *Summa contra Gentiles* (aimed at educating missionaries who needed to argue with non-Christians on philosophical rather than scriptural grounds) and the *Summa Theologiae* (the definitive synthesis, left unfinished at his death) — represent a sustained attempt to integrate everything: Aristotelian physics, metaphysics, psychology, and ethics with Neo-Platonic metaphysics and Christian theology. The ambition is extraordinary, and the execution is often brilliant.

### The Five Ways

The *Quinque Viae* (Five Ways, *Summa Theologiae* I.2.3) are Aquinas's proofs for God's existence, each an *a posteriori* argument (from observed fact to cause) rather than an *a priori* argument (from concept alone, as in Anselm):

1. **Argument from motion (change)**: everything that changes is changed by something else; an infinite regress of changers is impossible; therefore there is a first unmoved mover.
2. **Argument from efficient causation**: nothing causes itself; a regress of efficient causes is impossible; therefore there is a first cause.
3. **Argument from contingency (necessary/possible being)**: contingent beings can not-be; if everything were contingent, there would have been a time when nothing existed, and from nothing, nothing can come; therefore there must be a necessary being. (This is a development of Avicenna's argument.)
4. **Argument from degrees of perfection**: we observe beings that are more or less good, true, noble; the maximum of any quality is the cause of that quality in others; therefore there is a maximally perfect being that is the cause of whatever perfection exists (most influenced by Neo-Platonism).
5. **Argument from teleology (governance)**: unintelligent things act for ends — consistently and reliably, not randomly; this directedness toward ends requires a directive intelligence. (This is Aristotle's unmoved mover adapted for Christian theology.)

Each of the Five Ways is a form of the cosmological argument — from the observed fact of the world's existence or character to the necessary existence of God as the ultimate explanatory ground. Kant will argue that all cosmological arguments fail because they either lean on the ontological argument (in the third way and fifth way) or commit to the illegitimate application of the categories beyond experience (the first and second ways). The debate continues.

### Essence and Existence

Aquinas's most original metaphysical contribution is his development of the essence-existence distinction (from Avicenna) in a distinctively Christian direction. In creatures, **essence** (*essentia*, what the thing is) is really distinct from **existence** (*esse*, that the thing is). A horse's essence doesn't include its existence — you can fully understand horseness without thereby knowing that any horse exists. Existence is an act received into an essence as a limiting principle — the essence "receives" existence from God, who is the cause of being as such.

In God alone, essence and existence are identical. God does not have existence; God *is* existence (*ipsum esse subsistens* — subsisting existence itself). This means that God's essence cannot be known by finite minds — we can know that God exists (by the Five Ways) and what God is not (by negative theology), but not what God positively and intrinsically is, because that would require comprehending unlimited being. Aquinas thus integrates Maimonides's negative theology into a broader metaphysical framework.

This essence-existence distinction transforms natural theology: the proper philosophical question about God is not "What is God like?" (Plato's Forms) or "What is God's function in the cosmos?" (Aristotle's unmoved mover) but "Why does anything exist rather than nothing?" Leibniz will later ask this question explicitly; Aquinas has already provided the framework for the answer.

### Hylomorphic Psychology and Human Nature

Aquinas follows Aristotle on the soul: it is the form of the body, not a Platonic substance separate from it. The soul is the organizing principle of the living body, not a different thing trapped inside the body. This allows Aquinas to hold (against Plato and against Descartes before Descartes) that the human person is a unified being, not a soul using a body as a tool.

The tension: Christianity expects personal resurrection and intellectual immortality. How can the soul be the body's form and still survive the body's death? Aquinas's answer: the intellect (*intellectus*) differs from other soul-functions (nutrition, sensation) in that its operation is not wholly dependent on a bodily organ. The intellect receives the forms of things without matter — it is the cognitive faculty precisely because it is not the sort of thing that corresponds one-to-one with a physical organ. This makes the intellect, uniquely among soul-powers, capable of some form of subsistence even without the body — though Aquinas insists that the separated soul is incomplete and requires the resurrection of the body to be fully human.

This position is philosophically curious: if the soul is Aristotle's form of the body, how can the intellect be separable? Aquinas is trying to preserve Aristotelian hylomorphism while accommodating the Christian doctrine of immortality and resurrection. Critics have argued the position is incoherent; defenders have argued it is a genuine philosophical achievement.

### Natural Law and Ethics

Aquinas's ethics is virtue ethics in the Aristotelian mode, supplemented by natural law theory. Humans have a natural inclination toward their proper end (*telos*) — the perfection of their rational nature. The **natural law** is the rational creature's participation in the eternal law — the rational ordering of all things by divine wisdom. Practical reason discovers the natural law by reflection on what our natural inclinations (to life, to reproduction, to knowledge, to society) require.

The Stoic heritage is clear: the natural law is what reason discovers about how to act in accordance with human nature. But Aquinas gives it a theological grounding: the natural law is not just a philosophical achievement but an actual participation in divine reason. This allows natural law ethics to be simultaneously a philosophical ethics (accessible by reason alone) and a theological ethics (grounded in God's eternal law).

## 3. Duns Scotus: Haecceity, Univocity, and the Will

**John Duns Scotus** (c. 1266–1308 CE) is the most technically sophisticated Scholastic philosopher after Aquinas, and philosophically his most interesting counterpoint. An Oxford Franciscan (Aquinas was a Dominican in Paris), Scotus developed positions that challenged Thomistic orthodoxy on several crucial points.

**Haecceity** (*haecceitas*, "thisness"): one of the deepest problems in medieval metaphysics is the principle of individuation — what makes Socrates *this particular Socrates* rather than another human? Aquinas answered: matter (individual substances are individuated by their matter). Scotus objected: matter in itself is just potentiality, devoid of actuality — how can a mere potency do the work of individuation? His answer: haecceity — a positive, formally distinct formal feature of the individual that is not its form (shared with the species), not its matter, but a third thing: the individual's own specific "thisness." Haecceity is what makes John's humanity into this particular human named John, rather than any other.

This notoriously difficult concept has attracted philosophical attention for its connection to questions about individuation, identity, and what it means to be a concrete particular. It anticipates modern debates about individual essences and transworld identity.

**Univocity of being**: here Scotus directly contradicts Aquinas in a philosophically consequential way. Aquinas held that "being" (existence) is said *analogically* of God and creatures — they exist in ways that are different, though with some structural similarity (analogy). Scotus argued that there must be a *univocal* concept of being — a single concept that applies to God and creatures in exactly the same sense — otherwise when we argue from properties of creatures to properties of God, the argument equivocates (uses the same word in different senses). You need a shared conceptual framework for theological argument to be valid.

The philosophical stakes are enormous. Scotus's univocity makes God and creatures metaphysically comparable — they share a common concept of being — though God has it infinitely and creatures finitely. This makes metaphysics possible as a single science. Heidegger will argue that Scotus's univocity inaugurates *onto-theology* — a tradition that thinks of God as a being among beings (the highest being) rather than as the "ground of being" beyond all beings. Whether Heidegger's reading is fair remains debated.

**Will over intellect**: Aquinas held that the intellect, not the will, is primary — we will what we judge to be good; the will's act follows on the intellect's judgment. Scotus inverted this: the will is the primary faculty, not determined by the intellect's judgment. We can will something even when the intellect judges it is not best — and more importantly, God is primarily a will rather than primarily an intellect. God could have made a different moral law — the moral law is what it is because God willed it, not because it reflects eternal necessities of reason. This is **voluntarism**, and it has consequences: if God's will determines what is right, then morality is contingent on divine choice — a version of the Euthyphro dilemma Socrates raises, now with Scotus apparently on the "problematic" side.

## 4. William of Ockham: Nominalism and the Dissolution of Scholasticism

**William of Ockham** (c. 1287–1347 CE) is the most radical philosophical disruptor of the Scholastic period. He is the father of **nominalism** — the position that universals have no existence outside the mind; only individuals exist.

Scholastic metaphysics had generally accepted that universals (species, genera) have some form of reality — the debate was about *what kind* of reality. For Ockham, this entire framework violates his famous razor: *Entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem* — "Entities are not to be multiplied beyond necessity." Reality consists of individual things; the universal "human" is not a thing (not a Platonic Form, not even a *forma communis* in Scotus's sense) but a *name* (*nomen*), a mental concept that groups similar individuals together. It has no existence beyond the mind's act of grouping.

The consequences cascade:
- The entire apparatus of Thomistic metaphysics — essences, real universals, natural kinds with intrinsic natures — is undercut. If universals are just names, there are no essential natures; things are just bundles of particulars related by similarity.
- Natural law becomes harder to ground: if there are no real human natures, what do we mean by "contrary to human nature"? Ockham's voluntarist theology (following Scotus) compensates: moral law is what God commands, not what nature requires.
- Science becomes harder to ground: Aristotelian science is about necessary, universal truths about kinds of things. If kinds are merely nominally defined, scientific generalizations are descriptive, not revealing essences.

**Fideism**: Ockham drew a sharp line between what reason can establish and what faith must accept. More sharply than Aquinas, and less confidently, he thought reason could establish very little about theology — the Five Ways fail; even the necessary existence of God cannot be proven. Faith is required. This fideism is the *dissolution* of the Scholastic project — not from theological indifference but from philosophical rigor about what demonstrative argument can actually establish.

Ockham's political philosophy was also radical: he argued against papal infallibility and for the independence of civil authority from ecclesiastical authority — positions that contributed to the growing tension between Church and secular power that would eventually produce the Reformation.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

The Scholastic period is the most technically productive in medieval epistemology. The theory of **species** (*species intelligibilis*) — the cognitive form through which the mind knows the object, distinct from both the physical object and the psychological act — is the central mechanism of Scholastic cognitive theory. Aquinas's hylomorphic psychology (the intellect receives the form without the matter) is a sophisticated indirect realism. Scotus and Ockham developed it in different directions; Ockham eventually questioned whether the species is even necessary (direct realism).

The **problem of universals** — arguably the central problem of medieval philosophy — has contemporary descendants. The debate between Platonic realism (universals exist mind-independently), Aristotelian moderate realism (universals exist in things), and nominalism (universals are mental or linguistic) directly corresponds to contemporary debates about abstract objects, natural kinds, and mathematical entities.

### Language and meaning

Ockham's nominalism is the most important medieval contribution to philosophy of language. If universals are mental terms, then logic and language are *about* terms and their relations, not about mind-independent structures. This "terminist logic" or "logic of terms" (Ockham, Buridan, Albert of Saxony) developed sophisticated theory of supposition (what a term stands for in a given propositional context), which anticipates some aspects of modern reference theory.

Aquinas's doctrine of **analogy** — that theological language is neither univocal (same sense as literal use) nor purely equivocal (entirely different sense) but analogical (related sense, preserving structure while transforming content) — is a serious answer to the question of how finite language can speak meaningfully about an infinite God. It anticipates Wittgenstein's work on family resemblance and Lakoff's analysis of radial categories.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

The natural law tradition from Aquinas through Scotus to Ockham is the most important ethical development of the period. Aquinas grounds it in human nature and eternal law; Scotus emphasizes the first precepts of the natural law (do good, avoid evil) as necessary but the secondary precepts as more contingent; Ockham moves toward divine command theory. The natural law tradition remains the dominant framework in Catholic moral theology and contributes to the development of international law theory (Vitoria, Suarez, Grotius) in the early modern period.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

The Scholastic period represents the most self-conscious attempt in Western intellectual history to integrate natural knowledge and theological knowledge into a single system. Aquinas's distinction between natural theology (accessible to reason) and revealed theology (requiring faith) — with each domain having its proper methods — is the template for all subsequent attempts to delineate the proper domains of science and religion.

Ockham's nominalist physics (if universals are not real, natural explanation refers to individual particulars and their regularities, not to essential natures) anticipates the empiricist turn of Galileo and Newton. The Scholastic crisis produced by Ockham's challenge to Aristotelian natural philosophy created the intellectual space that the Scientific Revolution would fill.

## The Arc Forward

The Scholastic synthesis collapsed for two reasons, roughly simultaneous. The first was internal: Ockham's nominalism dissolved the metaphysical foundations on which the synthesis rested. If universals are names and essences are useful fictions, the entire Thomistic edifice lacks grounding. The second was external: Renaissance humanism (beginning in the 14th–15th centuries) turned away from Latin Scholastic discourse toward the literary and rhetorical heritage of ancient Greece and Rome. Scholasticism came to be associated with arid logic-chopping and meaningless disputations — a caricature, but an effective one.

What persisted: Thomistic ethics (still the dominant framework of Catholic moral theology); Scotus's haecceity and modal distinctions (influential in modern metaphysics); Ockham's razor (one of the most used principles in philosophy of science); the vocabulary of essence, existence, substance, accident, form, matter (the technical vocabulary of Western metaphysics to this day). The Scholastics did not solve their problems, but they articulated them with a precision that made subsequent progress possible.

Descartes' project — to rebuild philosophy on a new foundation — was partly a response to the ruins of Scholasticism. He kept the vocabulary (substance, attribute, mode) while attempting to replace the Aristotelian physics with a mechanistic alternative. Leibniz tried to revive Scholastic metaphysics in updated form. Kant attempted to adjudicate between the metaphysical claims of rationalism and the empiricist challenges — setting the terms for philosophy from 1781 onward. The Scholastic debates are not antiquarian curiosities; they are the conditioning context of everything that follows.
