# The Islamic and Jewish Middle Ages: Transmission, Transformation, and Independent Discovery (c. 800–1200 CE)

The standard narrative of Western intellectual history often jumps from the fall of the Roman Empire to the Scholastic synthesis of Aquinas in the 13th century as if the intervening seven centuries in the Islamic world were merely a holding pattern — a storage facility for Greek texts. This is profoundly wrong. Islamic philosophy developed Aristotle and Neo-Platonism in substantially new directions, produced original positions in metaphysics, epistemology, philosophy of mind, and political philosophy that have no clear Greek precedents, and transmitted to the Latin West not just the texts of Aristotle but commentaries, syntheses, and critiques that shaped how Aristotle was read for centuries. Without this transmission, there is no Aquinas; without Aquinas's synthesis, the entire subsequent history of Western philosophy takes a different shape.

The story also involves the Jewish philosophical tradition — Saadia Gaon, Maimonides, Crescas — which developed in sustained dialogue with Islamic philosophy and produced work of comparable depth and originality. And it involves the reception of Neo-Platonism, Ptolemaic astronomy, and Galenic medicine alongside Aristotelian philosophy — understanding Islamic philosophy requires understanding the whole package.

---

## 1. The Context: Why Greek Philosophy Came to the Islamic World

After the fall of Rome, Greek philosophical and scientific texts were largely inaccessible to Latin-speaking Western Europe. The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) preserved them in Greek but produced little original philosophical work. The center of philosophical activity shifted to the Near East, where Syriac-speaking Christians (particularly the School of Alexandria and later the School of Nisibis and Gundishapur in Persia) had been translating and commenting on Aristotle since the late antique period.

When the Islamic Abbasid Caliphate (founded 750 CE) established Baghdad as its capital, it initiated the most ambitious translation project in intellectual history — the **Bayt al-Hikma** (House of Wisdom). Under the patronage of caliphs Al-Mansur, Harun al-Rashid, and especially Al-Ma'mun, virtually the entire corpus of available Greek scientific and philosophical writing was translated into Arabic: Aristotle's logic, physics, metaphysics, and ethics; the Neo-Platonic commentaries on Aristotle; Plato's *Republic* and *Timaeus*; Ptolemy's *Almagest*; Euclid's *Elements*; Galen's medical writings. The translators — primarily Syriac Christians, most famously Hunayn ibn Ishaq — were doing intellectually demanding work, not mechanical copying: they had to develop Arabic philosophical vocabulary from scratch, often debating how to render key Greek terms.

The critical point: this was not passive preservation. The Islamic philosophical tradition (collectively known as *falsafa*, from the Greek *philosophia*) was creatively engaged with the material from the beginning, asking which ideas could be integrated with Islamic theology, which posed challenges to it, and which had to be rejected.

## 2. Al-Kindi: The First Arab Philosopher

**Al-Kindi** (c. 801–873 CE) is called the "Philosopher of the Arabs" — the first thinker in the Islamic world to produce original philosophical work in Arabic, the language of the Quran. His most important contribution is the attempt to harmonize Greek philosophy (particularly Aristotle) with Islamic monotheism.

Al-Kindi argued that philosophy and Islamic revelation are not in conflict: they both seek truth; revelation gives truth by a different (more direct) route than philosophy, but the conclusions about God, creation, and ethics can be consistent. This sets the terms for the fundamental debate in Islamic intellectual history: can philosophy be reconciled with revelation, or are they in conflict?

He made important contributions to the philosophy of the intellect — distinguishing types of intellect (the potential intellect, the actual intellect, the acquired intellect, the divine active intellect) in a scheme that will be developed by later philosophers. He also worked in cosmology, mathematics, optics, and music — a true polymath in the Islamic tradition.

---

## 3. Al-Farabi: The Second Teacher

**Al-Farabi** (c. 872–950 CE) was known in the Islamic world as the "Second Teacher" (*al-mu'allim al-thani*) — second only to Aristotle as a master of philosophy. His range is comparable to Aristotle's: he wrote on logic (refining the Aristotelian system and commenting extensively on the Organon), music theory, political philosophy, and philosophy of the intellect.

Two contributions stand out:

**Political philosophy**: Al-Farabi's *On the Perfect State* adapts Plato's *Republic* to an Islamic setting — the ideal state is ruled by a philosopher-king who is also a prophet, someone who receives divine inspiration and can translate philosophical truths into images and symbols accessible to the people. This sophisticated political epistemology asks how the philosophical truth accessible to a few can govern a community of many; the prophet-philosopher's role is to translate the intellectual content of philosophical truth into the imaginative form of revelation. This is a remarkable synthesis of Platonic politics and Islamic prophetology.

**The intellect**: Al-Farabi systematized the theory of the active intellect — a cosmic being (identified with the Aristotelian unmoved mover and the Neo-Platonic Intellect) that illuminates human minds and enables actual thinking. The human intellect receives from the active intellect both the first principles of reasoning and the capacity to actualize potentialities into actual knowledge. This framework will be the background for Avicenna's much more developed theory.

## 4. Avicenna (Ibn Sina): The Floating Man and Modal Metaphysics

**Avicenna** (Ibn Sina, 980–1037 CE) is the greatest philosopher of Islamic civilization — a universal genius whose philosophical system shaped both Islamic and Latin medieval thought in decisive ways. His two major philosophical works are the *Shifa'* (Book of Healing, a philosophical encyclopedia) and the *Isharaat wa-l-Tanbihat* (Pointers and Reminders).

### The Floating Man

Avicenna's most original contribution to philosophy of mind is the "**floating man**" thought experiment, which anticipates Descartes' cogito by six centuries. Imagine a person brought into existence in a fully formed adult state — but floating in air, unable to perceive their senses (blindfolded, no touch, no proprioception), unable to feel their own limbs. Would they be aware of anything?

Avicenna's answer: yes. They would be aware of *themselves* — of their own existence as a thinking, self-aware thing — even in the total absence of sensory input and bodily awareness. This self-awareness is immediate and non-inferential; it does not require the senses, the body, or even the intellectual faculties in their normal operation. There is a mode of *ittihad* (self-unification) or *self-presence* that is prior to all discursive knowledge.

The implications:
- The soul is neither identical to the body nor dependent on the body for its self-awareness. This is a philosophical basis for the soul's distinctness from the body and its potential post-mortem existence.
- Self-knowledge is direct and non-propositional: the soul does not infer its own existence from evidence; it is simply present to itself.
- This is a direct precursor to Descartes' *cogito ergo sum* (1641) and to Husserl's phenomenological self-awareness. Avicenna gets there without the methodological framing of systematic doubt, but the underlying insight — that self-awareness is foundational and cannot be dissolved into bodily or sensory facts — is the same.

### Modal Metaphysics

Avicenna made another major metaphysical contribution: the distinction between **necessary being** (*wajib al-wujud*) and **possible being** (*mumkin al-wujud*). 

A necessary being is one whose existence cannot be denied without contradiction — its essence involves existence. A possible being is one whose existence or non-existence depends on something else; in itself, it is neither necessarily existent nor necessarily non-existent. Possible beings need a cause for their existence, and the regress of possible beings as causes of one another cannot go on infinitely. Therefore there must be a necessary being — one whose existence is self-explanatory — as the ultimate ground of all possible existence.

This argument becomes **Aquinas's Third Way** and is a central argument in natural theology from Avicenna through Leibniz (his principle of sufficient reason) to contemporary modal cosmological arguments. The distinction between necessary and contingent existence transforms medieval metaphysics: questioning becomes asking not just "what is X?" but "does X's essence include its existence?"

**Essence-existence distinction**: in creatures, essence (what something is) is distinct from existence (that it is). A horse's essence doesn't include existence — there is no contradiction in conceiving horses that don't exist. Only in God does essence include existence. This distinction is taken up and developed by Aquinas in a different direction: he argues that in God, essence *is* existence — God is pure *esse* (being).

## 5. Averroes (Ibn Rushd): The Commentator

**Averroes** (Ibn Rushd, 1126–1198 CE) was born in Córdoba and spent his life in Al-Andalus (Islamic Spain). He is called "The Commentator" in the Latin West because his commentaries on Aristotle (Short, Middle, and Long commentaries on almost every Aristotelian text) were the standard guides to Aristotle in both the Islamic and Christian worlds. Roger Bacon and Dante both revered him; Thomas Aquinas cites him on virtually every page of his Aristotelian commentaries, usually as an adversary but always as a serious interlocutor.

Averroes's philosophical program was to recover the *real* Aristotle from the Neo-Platonic distortions that the *falsafa* tradition had introduced. He thought Avicenna had mixed too much Neo-Platonic emanationism into a properly Aristotelian framework. For Averroes, Aristotle's philosophy is as close to the truth as unaided human reason can get — more than any other thinker, he produced a philosophically consistent materialism with respect to the natural world.

**Monopsychism**: Averroes's most controversial position, and the one that most exercised the Latin Scholastics. Aristotle's active intellect (*nous poietikos*) is the one-single intellect shared by all humans — it is not individuated, not particular to any person. This means that individual post-mortem intellectual survival is impossible — there is no Socrates-specific intellect that persists after Socrates's death; what persists is the one universal intellect. This position, developed by Latin "Averroists" (Siger of Brabant, Boethius of Dacia), was condemned by the Bishop of Paris in 1277.

**The "double truth" controversy**: Averroes is sometimes said to have held that something can be true in philosophy but false in theology, and vice versa. This is mostly a misrepresentation created by his Latin interpreters. Averroes's actual position is more subtle: philosophy and theology are different discursive registers for the same truth. Theology uses imaginative demonstration aimed at the whole community; philosophy uses strict demonstrative argumentation aimed at those capable of it. They don't contradict each other — they present the same truth at different levels of abstraction and with different rhetorical means. But the misrepresentation had a life of its own and shaped how Latin thinkers understood the philosophy-theology relationship.

## 6. Al-Ghazali: The Incoherence of the Philosophers

**Al-Ghazali** (1058–1111 CE) was the most powerful critic of the *falsafa* tradition from within Islamic thought. His *Tahafut al-Falasifa* ("The Incoherence of the Philosophers") took aim specifically at what he saw as the three most dangerous errors of the philosophers (particularly Al-Farabi and Avicenna):

1. The eternity of the world: the philosophers hold the world has always existed; the Quran says God created it in time.
2. God's knowledge of particulars: the philosophers hold God knows only universals (like Aristotle's unmoved mover, which thinks only itself); the Quran's God knows and responds to individual human actions and prayers.
3. The impossibility of bodily resurrection: the philosophers hold that only the rational soul survives death; the Quran promises bodily resurrection.

On each point, Al-Ghazali argues that the philosophical position is incoherent — it fails on its own terms, not just from a theological standpoint. The book is not simply religious polemic; it is technically sophisticated philosophical criticism. Its most famous argument is against the causal necessity posited by the philosophers: when fire touches cotton and the cotton burns, is the burning causally necessary? Al-Ghazali says no — God creates each event separately through divine power; there is no necessary causal connection between events, only habitual regularity established by divine habit. This is **occasionalism** — a position that anticipates Malebranche and that Hume's analysis of causation will effectively (if unwittingly) converge on: there is no observed necessary connection between events, only constant conjunction.

Averroes wrote a response: *Tahafut al-Tahafut* ("The Incoherence of the Incoherence"). This back-and-forth — philosophy criticizing theology, theology criticizing philosophy, philosophy responding — is the intellectual vitality of the 10th–12th-century Islamic world.

## 7. Maimonides: Negative Theology and the Jewish-Aristotelian Synthesis

**Maimonides** (Moses ben Maimon / Musa ibn Maymun, 1138–1204 CE) is the dominant figure of medieval Jewish philosophy. Born in Córdoba, he eventually settled in Cairo and became the leading physician and halakhic authority of his generation alongside his philosophical work.

The *Guide for the Perplexed* (*Dalālat al-Ḥāʾirīn*, written in Judeo-Arabic) addresses a specific problem: how should an educated Jew, trained in science and philosophy, understand the anthropomorphic language of the Torah? When the Torah says God has a hand, or that God is angry — should this be read literally? Maimonides's answer is a systematic negative theology: **all positive attributes of God, taken literally, are false**. God is not just very powerful — God is the cause of all power; not just very wise — God is the cause of all wisdom. Positive attributes imply limitation (to be X is to not be not-X); God has no limitations.

What can we say about God? Either (a) negative attributes ("God is not limited," "God is not temporal") — which tell us what God is not without presupposing positive content; or (b) attributes of action ("God created," "God protected") — which describe effects in the world without describing God's inner nature. This negative theology (also present in Pseudo-Dionysius, Eriugena, and the Christian mystical tradition) is philosophically rigorous: it takes divine transcendence so seriously that it refuses to domesticate God by assigning him properties comprehensible to finite minds.

Maimonides's cosmological proof for God's existence draws on Avicenna's necessary/possible being distinction. His account of prophecy (on a scale of intellectual and imaginative perfection, with Moses at the apex) is deeply philosophical — prophecy is less a miraculous interruption of nature than the highest achievement of philosophical-imaginative perfection.

## Four Through-Lines

### Mind, knowledge, and world

Avicenna's floating man is the most important epistemological contribution of this period — a phenomenological argument for the self-luminosity of the soul that anticipates Descartes and Husserl. The theory of the active intellect (developed by Al-Farabi, Avicenna, and Averroes in different directions) is the central philosophical-psychological question of the period: what is the relationship between individual human intellect and the cosmic principle of intellection? This question drives debates about intellectual immortality, the species of self-knowledge, and the nature of abstraction that continue into Scholasticism.

### Language and meaning

Maimonides's negative theology is the most important linguistic contribution: it argues that no positive predication can capture divine nature, which means that religious language must be understood as systematic negation or as describing effects rather than essence. This radical qualification of theological language opens onto broader questions about the limits of descriptive language: if God cannot be described, what is religious language *doing*? This is a precursor to the logical positivists' challenge to theological language and to Wittgenstein's language-games analysis.

Al-Farabi's political philosophy of the prophet-philosopher addresses the question of how philosophical truths can be communicated to a general public through imaginative and rhetorical rather than demonstrative means — an ancient version of the question about how to convey complex ideas in popular form without distorting them.

### Ethics, politics, and the self

Islamic political philosophy — Al-Farabi, Avicenna, Ibn Tufayl (*Hayy ibn Yaqzan*) — recurrently returns to the question of the philosopher's relationship to the community. The philosopher has access to a truth that most people cannot grasp by demonstrative reasoning; the city, however, is governed by law and religion (imaginative presentations of truth). How should the philosopher relate to this community? Active governance (the philosopher-king model of Al-Farabi)? Strategic esoteric teaching (the hidden meaning within public religious instruction)? Philosophy pursued in solitude (Ibn Tufayl's autodidact on a desert island)? These political-epistemological questions connect to contemporary debates about public reason and democratic deliberation.

### Science, metaphysics, and religion

The central tension of the period: can philosophy and revelation be reconciled? The *falsafa* tradition (Al-Kindi, Al-Farabi, Avicenna, Averroes) says yes — careful philosophical interpretation can show that revelation, properly understood, is consistent with philosophical truth. Al-Ghazali says some philosophical positions are simply inconsistent with revelation and must be rejected. Maimonides says the reconciliation requires understanding how allegorical reading of scripture must inform literal reading.

Avicenna's distinction between necessary and possible being transforms medieval metaphysics — every being other than God exists contingently, dependent on the necessary being; creation is not just an original act but a continuous ontological dependence of creatures on the divine being. Al-Ghazali's occasionalism anticipates Hume's analysis of causation and stands as a philosophically serious option even today.

## The Arc Forward

The Islamic philosophical tradition did not simply pass Greek texts to Europe; it modeled how to take philosophical problems seriously while operating within a religious framework, showed how to read Aristotle critically rather than reverentially, and contributed positions (Avicennan modal metaphysics, Averroist psychology, Al-Ghazalian occasionalism, Maimonidean negative theology) that became central debates in Latin Scholasticism.

The transmission happened primarily through Islamic Spain (Al-Andalus) and Sicily — regions where Arabic-to-Latin translation movements were active in the 12th century. Gerard of Cremona (Toledo), Hermann the German, Michael Scot — these translators made Aristotle, Avicenna, and Averroes available in Latin and triggered the intellectual explosion of the 13th century that produced Aquinas.

Maimonides's influence on Aquinas was direct and acknowledged — Aquinas cites him as "Rabbi Moses" alongside Averroes and Aristotle as a trusted philosophical authority. The Guide's framework for reconciling reason and faith — the distinction between beliefs that reason can establish (God's existence, divine unity), beliefs that revelation supplements (creation in time, particular providence), and beliefs that must be accepted by faith — is structurally parallel to Aquinas's own distinction between natural theology and revealed theology.
