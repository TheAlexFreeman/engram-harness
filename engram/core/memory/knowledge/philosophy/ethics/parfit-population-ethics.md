# Parfit on Population Ethics: The Repugnant Conclusion and Beyond

## The core problem

Population ethics asks: **when our choices affect both the number and the quality of future lives, what should we do?** This sounds abstract but has enormous practical significance: climate policy, AI development, reproductive technology, and resource allocation all affect which and how many future people exist.

Parfit (*Reasons and Persons*, Part Four, 1984) demonstrated that every plausible population ethical theory leads to at least one deeply counterintuitive conclusion. This is "the impossibility theorem of population ethics" — there is no theory that satisfies all our moral intuitions simultaneously.

## Total utilitarianism and the Repugnant Conclusion

### Total utilitarianism
"The best outcome is the one with the greatest total welfare." Total welfare = number of people × average welfare per person.

### The Repugnant Conclusion
**For any world A of 10 billion people all living excellent lives, there is a world Z of vastly more people all living lives barely worth living that has greater total welfare.** If each person in Z has welfare ε > 0, then for sufficiently large population N, N × ε > 10 billion × high welfare.

Parfit found this "repugnant" — intuitively, world A is better than world Z. But total utilitarianism says Z is better. Hence the name.

### Why it matters
The Repugnant Conclusion is not merely academic. If we use total utilitarianism to guide policy:
- We should prefer policies that produce more people living mediocre lives over fewer people living excellent lives
- We should accept any degradation of individual quality of life if it enables sufficient population increase
- Long-term AI-driven population expansion could justify enormous present sacrifices

## The main alternative theories

### Average utilitarianism
"The best outcome maximizes average welfare per person."

**Advantages**: avoids the Repugnant Conclusion (adding people with below-average welfare reduces average welfare even if their lives are worth living).

**Devastating objection — the Mere Addition Paradox**: consider adding one person with a good life to an existing population. If this person's welfare is below the existing average, average utility drops — so average utilitarianism says adding a happy person to the world is bad. Worse: it says a world of one ecstatically happy person surrounded by misery is better than a larger world where everyone has very high welfare (if the ecstatic person's welfare exceeds the latter average).

**The sadistic conclusion**: average utilitarianism can prefer adding people with negative welfare (suffering lives) to adding people with below-average positive welfare, if the negative-welfare people bring the average closer to what it would be without any addition. This is worse than the Repugnant Conclusion.

### Critical-level theories
"An addition of a person with welfare above critical level c improves the outcome; below c, it worsens it." (Blackorby and Donaldson, 1984; Broome, 2004)

**Advantages**: avoids the Repugnant Conclusion (set c high enough that world Z falls below the threshold). Avoids the sadistic conclusion.

**Problem**: the **critical level** is arbitrary. What is the level of welfare below which adding a life to the world is not a moral improvement? Any specific answer seems insufficiently motivated. And if c is set too high, the theory implies that adding many people with very good lives is morally neutral or bad — the "Very Repugnant Conclusion."

### Person-affecting theories
"An outcome is better only if it is better *for someone*."

**Advantages**: respects the intuition that moral evaluation should be about persons, not abstract welfare functions.

**Fatal problem**: the non-identity problem. If a policy choice determines which people exist, then the people who exist under policy A are different from those under policy B. Neither group is "harmed" by the policy that brings them into existence (assuming their lives are worth living). Person-affecting theories cannot compare outcomes involving different people — they are silent on the most important policy choices.

### Variable value theories
"The moral value of additional welfare diminishes as population grows." (Hurka, 1983; Ng, 1989)

**Advantages**: captures the intuition that at some point, adding more mediocre lives doesn't improve the world even if total welfare increases.

**Problem**: determining the variable value function is arbitrary, and some versions still generate quasi-repugnant conclusions.

### Prioritarianism
"Benefits to worse-off people count for more than equal-sized benefits to better-off people." (Temkin, 1993; Parfit, *Lindley Lecture*, 1991)

**Advantages**: respects both total welfare and distributional fairness. Avoids some standard counterexamples.

**Relationship to population ethics**: prioritarianism primarily addresses distribution within a fixed population but can be extended to population ethics by prioritizing the welfare of the worst-off possible persons. However, this extension is complex and contested.

## The Mere Addition Paradox in detail

Parfit's most famous argument sequence:

1. **A vs. A+**: World A has a population at high welfare. World A+ has the same population at the same welfare, plus an extra group at lower (but positive) welfare. A+ seems at least as good as A — the extra people have good lives, and no one is worse off.

2. **A+ vs. B**: World B has a slightly larger population than A+ with welfare slightly above A+'s worst-off group but slightly below A's group. B has greater equality and (potentially) greater total welfare. B seems at least as good as A+.

3. **B vs. A**: By transitivity, B should be at least as good as A. But B has more people at lower average welfare than A — this starts the slide toward the Repugnant Conclusion. Repeating the argument leads from A through B, C, D, ... to Z.

**The paradox**: each step seems acceptable (A → A+ and A+ → B), but the cumulative effect is the Repugnant Conclusion. Parfit's conclusion: our moral reasoning about population size contains deep inconsistencies.

## Impossibility results

### Arrhenius's impossibility theorem
Gustaf Arrhenius (2000, 2011) formalized Parfit's insight: no population ethical theory can satisfy all of these conditions simultaneously:
1. **Dominance**: if everyone in A is better off than everyone in B, A is better than B
2. **Egalitarian dominance**: if A is more equal and has higher total welfare, A is better
3. **Quality addition**: adding good lives to A does not make A worse
4. **Non-anti-egalitarianism**: perfect equality above adequate welfare is not the worst outcome
5. **Avoidance of the Repugnant Conclusion**

Any theory that satisfies four of these five violates the fifth. This is a genuine impossibility — not a matter of insufficient ingenuity but a structural constraint on moral reasoning about population.

### The significance
This means population ethics requires **principled trade-offs** between deeply held moral intuitions. There is no theory that matches all our moral convictions. We must decide which convictions to revise.

## Attempts at resolution

### Parfit's "Theory X"
Parfit hoped for a "Theory X" that would avoid all the paradoxes. In *Reasons and Persons*, he left this as an open problem. In later work, he was pessimistic about finding one.

### Broome's expected critical-level theory
John Broome (*Weighing Lives*, 2004) developed the most sophisticated critical-level theory, treating the critical level as uncertain but not arbitrary. The expected value of adding a life is computed under uncertainty about the critical level.

### Temkin: deny transitivity
Larry Temkin (*Rethinking the Good*, 2012) argued that the Mere Addition Paradox shows that "at least as good as" is **not transitive**. This is radical — it means we cannot rank outcomes in a single coherent ordering — but Temkin argues this correctly reflects the plural and incommensurable values at stake.

### Accepting the Repugnant Conclusion
Torbjörn Tännsjö and other total utilitarians bite the bullet: world Z really *is* better than world A, and our repugnance is a cognitive bias. The intuition against Z trades on failure to appreciate the enormous number of worthwhile lives it contains.

## Implications for AI alignment and long-termism

### Long-termist AI safety
Population ethics is **foundational** for long-termist AI safety reasoning:
- If AI development determines the long-term future of civilization (and the number and quality of future lives), then population ethics determines what "good outcomes" means for alignment
- Total utilitarianism says: maximize the number of good lives (potentially astronomical — the "cosmic endowment")
- Person-affecting views say: focus on people who will exist regardless of our choices
- The choice between these views dramatically affects AI safety priorities

### The astronomical stakes argument
Nick Bostrom (*Superintelligence*, 2014) and others argue: since the potential future population is astronomically large, even tiny reductions in existential risk have astronomical expected value. This argument **depends on total utilitarianism** (or something close to it). Under person-affecting views, it has much less force.

### AI-driven population expansion
Advanced AI might enable:
- Space colonization (vastly more people)
- Digital minds (vast numbers of simulated persons)
- Life extension (existing people persist much longer)

Population ethics determines whether these scenarios are morally desirable:
- Total utilitarianism: expand — more good lives is better
- Average utilitarianism: expand only if new lives have above-average quality
- Critical-level theories: expand only if new lives exceed the critical level
- Person-affecting theories: creation of new digital minds has no moral value per se

### The quality/quantity trade-off in AI outputs
At a smaller scale, population ethics structure appears in AI design trade-offs:
- More users served at lower quality vs. fewer users served at higher quality
- More AI-generated content of mediocre quality vs. less content of excellent quality
- These are micro-scale population-ethics problems

### Alignment under moral uncertainty
Given the impossibility results, alignment researchers cannot resolve population ethics. The appropriate response: **design AI systems that are robust across plausible population ethical theories** rather than committed to any one. This means:
- Avoiding actions that are catastrophic under any theory (existential risk reduction)
- Preserving optionality (don't foreclose future moral choices)
- Deferring to human judgment on contested value questions
