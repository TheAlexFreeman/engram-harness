# Moral Realism: Are Moral Facts Real?

## The core question

Metaethics asks: **what is the nature, scope, and grounding of moral claims?** Moral realism answers: **there are objective moral facts, independent of what anyone thinks or feels about them.** Just as "water is H₂O" is true regardless of anyone's beliefs, "gratuitous cruelty is wrong" is objectively true — not merely an expression of disapproval or a cultural convention.

This file covers the major forms of moral realism and the arguments for and against them.

## The realism/anti-realism landscape

The central distinctions:

| Position | Moral facts exist? | Mind-independent? | Truth-apt? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Robust realism** | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Constructivism** | Yes (constructed) | No (mind-dependent) | Yes |
| **Expressivism** | No | N/A | No (or quasi-) |
| **Error theory** | No | N/A | Yes, but all false |

**Truth-apt**: moral statements can be true or false (as opposed to being mere exclamations or commands).

## Robust moral realism

### Non-naturalist realism (Moore, Parfit, Scanlon)

**The fundamental claim**: moral properties are **real but non-natural** — they are not identical to or reducible to any natural (empirically observable) property. "Good" is a simple, unanalyzable, non-natural property that we grasp through moral intuition.

**Moore's open question argument** (1903): for any natural property F, it is always an "open question" whether F is good. "Pleasure is good" — but is it *really* good? The question is open, not trivially settled by definition. Therefore "good" ≠ any natural property. This is the founding argument of non-naturalism.

**Parfit's non-naturalism** (*On What Matters*, 2011): Parfit defended non-naturalist realism as the correct metaethical position. Moral truths are:
- Not natural facts (not reducible to physics, biology, or psychology)
- Not supernatural facts (not dependent on God or a transcendent realm)
- Truths of reason — in the same category as logical and mathematical truths
- Knowable through rational reflection

Parfit's key argument: we have **decisive reason** to believe that suffering is bad, that we should keep our promises, that gratuitous cruelty is wrong. These are self-evident to competent moral reasoners, just as "2 + 2 = 4" is self-evident to competent mathematical reasoners.

**Scanlon's metaethics**: Scanlon (*Being Realistic about Reasons*, 2014) defended a similar position: normative facts (including moral facts) are "just true" — they are not grounded in natural facts, psychological facts, or anything else. Normative truths are fundamental and irreducible.

### Naturalist realism (Boyd, Brink, Railton, Sturgeon)

**The fundamental claim**: moral properties are **natural properties** — they are identical to or constituted by natural properties, even though this identity is not knowable a priori. Just as "water = H₂O" was a surprising empirical discovery, "good = maximizing human welfare" (or some functional property) would be an a posteriori identity.

**Cornell realism** (Boyd, Brink, Sturgeon): moral properties are **natural functional properties** that supervene on physical properties. "Goodness" is a natural-kind term, like "water" — it refers to whatever natural property actually plays the causal-functional role that "goodness" tracks in moral discourse. This is a **synthetic identity**: discoverable through empirical investigation, not conceptual analysis.

**Railton's naturalism** (*Moral Realism*, 1986): moral facts are facts about what would satisfy an **idealized version** of the agent — a version with full information, coherent preferences, and adequate imagination. This grounds morality in naturalistic facts (about psychology and circumstances) while preserving objectivity (idealized preferences, not actual ones).

**Advantages of naturalism**:
- Integrates moral properties into the natural world (no mysterious non-natural properties)
- Allows empirical investigation of moral questions
- Explains moral progress (we discover moral facts as we learn more about human nature and social dynamics)

**Disadvantages**:
- Vulnerable to Moore's open question argument (though naturalists argue this begs the question against a posteriori identities)
- Hume's is/ought gap: how can facts about what *is* (natural properties) entail claims about what *ought* to be?
- Moral disagreement is harder to explain if moral facts are natural facts (we don't disagree nearly as much about chemistry)

### Sensibility theory (McDowell, Wiggins)

**The fundamental claim**: moral properties are **response-dependent** — they exist because beings with appropriate sensibilities would recognize them, but they are still objective (not merely subjective).

Analogy: colors are response-dependent (they exist only in relation to visual perceivers) but not purely subjective (tomatoes really *are* red, not merely perceived as red). Similarly, cruelty is really wrong — it's not merely that we disapprove of it — but its wrongness is constitutively related to the reactions of beings with moral sensibility.

**McDowell**: moral perception is a form of perception — trained moral sensibility allows one to *see* the moral features of situations, just as trained aesthetic sensibility allows one to *see* the beauty in a painting. Moral education is the training of this perceptual capacity.

## Arguments for moral realism

### The argument from moral progress
We believe that moral progress has occurred: slavery is wrong, and recognizing this was moral progress, not merely moral change. But progress implies a direction — and direction implies a standard external to particular moral beliefs. Moral realism provides this standard; anti-realism struggles to distinguish progress from mere change.

### The argument from moral phenomenology
Moral experience presents itself as **discovery**, not invention. When we judge that torturing innocents is wrong, it feels like recognizing a fact, not expressing a preference. Moral realism takes this phenomenology at face value.

### The companions in guilt argument
If you're skeptical about moral facts, you should be equally skeptical about mathematical facts, logical truths, and other normative truths (epistemic reasons, reasons for action). But we're not skeptical about mathematics — so we shouldn't be about morality either. Both involve non-empirical truths knowable through reason.

### The argument from explanatory indispensability
Moral facts figure in the best explanation of certain events: "The revolution occurred because the regime was unjust" — justice/injustice appears in causal explanations. If moral properties are genuinely explanatory, they must exist (same argument structure as scientific realism about unobservable entities).

### Parfit's "decisive reasons" argument
Some moral truths are so compelling that denying them would be irrational: gratuitous cruelty is wrong; we have reason to avoid future suffering; promises create obligations. The denial of these truths is not a tenable intellectual position. Therefore moral truths exist.

## Arguments against moral realism

### The queerness objection (Mackie)
J.L. Mackie (*Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong*, 1977): moral properties would be metaphysically "queer" — unlike anything else in the natural world. They would be intrinsically motivating (knowing the moral fact automatically gives you a reason to act on it), categorically prescriptive (applying to all agents regardless of their desires), and ontologically strange (not reducible to physical properties). Nothing else has these features simultaneously. **Response**: this objection begs the question — it assumes that only natural properties exist, which is what's being debated.

### The disagreement objection
Deep, persistent moral disagreement (abortion, euthanasia, distributive justice) is hard to explain if moral facts are objective. We'd expect convergence on objective facts. **Response**: moral disagreement is often explained by non-moral factors (different factual beliefs, limited perspective, bias, different weighing of considerations). And some moral disagreement *has* resolved over time (slavery).

### Evolutionary debunking arguments (Street, Joyce)
Sharon Street (*A Darwinian Dilemma for Realist Theories of Value*, 2006): natural selection shaped our moral intuitions for reproductive fitness, not for tracking moral truth. If realism is correct, it's a remarkable coincidence that our evolved moral beliefs track mind-independent moral facts. If anti-realism is correct, no coincidence is needed — moral beliefs are just adaptive responses. Therefore moral realism requires an implausible explanation of why evolution would produce truth-tracking moral faculties.

**Responses**:
- Third-factor explanation (Enoch, Wielenberg): there is a third factor that explains both why evolution would produce certain moral beliefs AND why those beliefs are true — e.g., survival-promotion and moral value both track well-being
- The debunking argument, if successful, would also undermine trust in our cognitive faculties generally (including those used in science and mathematics)

### Hume's is/ought gap
No set of purely descriptive (factual) premises can entail a normative (moral) conclusion. This challenges naturalist realism: if moral facts are natural facts, how does the normative character of moral claims emerge from descriptive natural properties? **Response**: the is/ought gap may only block *deductive* derivation; moral properties might be natural properties knowable through ampliative (non-deductive) inference.

## Moral realism and AI alignment

### The alignment target problem
If moral realism is correct, alignment has a well-defined target: the AI should track moral truths. An aligned AI is one whose behavior conforms to objective moral facts. This makes alignment a knowledge problem — the challenge is getting the AI to know what's right.

If anti-realism is correct, there is no uniquely correct moral target. Alignment becomes a preferences-aggregation problem or a power-negotiation problem — fundamentally different in character.

### Moral uncertainty and realism
Even if moral facts exist, we may not know them reliably. This motivates **moral uncertainty** approaches: design AI systems that reason well under uncertainty about which moral theory is correct, rather than committing to a specific theory.

### The convergence hope
Parfit's convergence thesis (realist version): if there are objective moral facts, different ethical traditions are converging on them. For AI alignment, this means that training on diverse ethical traditions should produce better moral reasoning than training on any single tradition — the convergence extracts the signal from the noise.

### Companions in guilt for AI
If AI systems can reason mathematically and logically, the "companions in guilt" argument suggests they should in principle be able to reason morally — moral facts are in the same epistemic category as mathematical and logical facts. This is an argument for the feasibility of moral reasoning in AI, at least on a realist metaethics.

### Evolutionary debunking and AI moral advantage
If human moral intuitions are distorted by evolutionary pressures that don't track moral truth, an AI system without evolutionary heritage might *reason more clearly about morality* than humans. This is a speculative but important possibility: AI moral reasoning might be less biased than human moral reasoning, precisely because AI has no evolved moral intuitions to be debunked.
