# Virtue Ethics: From Aristotle to Contemporary Revival

## The core idea

Virtue ethics holds that **the central question of ethics is not "what should I do?" but "what kind of person should I be?"** The morally good life consists in developing and exercising excellences of character (virtues). Actions are right insofar as they are what a virtuous person would characteristically do in the circumstances.

The three key elements:
1. **Aretē (virtue/excellence)**: stable character traits that enable a person to live well — courage, justice, temperance, wisdom, honesty, generosity, etc.
2. **Phronēsis (practical wisdom)**: the intellectual virtue of knowing *how* to act well in particular situations — not following rules but perceiving the morally salient features of a situation and responding appropriately
3. **Eudaimonia (flourishing)**: the ultimate aim of life — not mere subjective happiness but objective human flourishing, realized through the exercise of virtue over a complete life

## Aristotle (384–322 BCE): the foundations

### The function argument
Aristotle (*Nicomachean Ethics*, c. 340 BCE) argues: every craft and inquiry aims at some good. The human good is determined by the **function** (ergon) of a human being — what is distinctive of human life. Just as a good knife cuts well, a good human being lives well as a human. The distinctive human function is **rational activity**: living in accordance with reason. Therefore, the human good is "activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete."

### The doctrine of the mean
Each virtue is a **mean between two vices** — one of excess and one of deficiency:
- Courage is the mean between cowardice (deficiency) and rashness (excess)
- Generosity is the mean between miserliness and prodigality
- Truthfulness is the mean between self-deprecation and boastfulness

The mean is **relative to the individual and the situation** — not an arithmetic midpoint. What counts as courageous for a soldier differs from what counts as courageous for a civilian. The mean is determined by practical wisdom (phronēsis), not by a formula.

### The intellectual virtues
Aristotle distinguished:
- **Sophia** (theoretical wisdom): knowledge of necessary truths, the contemplation of which is the highest form of eudaimonia
- **Phronēsis** (practical wisdom): the ability to deliberate well about what conduces to the good life — perception of particulars, knowledge of general principles, and skill in applying the latter to the former
- **Technē** (craft knowledge): productive skill

Phronēsis is the keystone virtue — it is required for the proper exercise of all the moral virtues. Without practical wisdom, courage becomes foolhardiness, generosity becomes wastefulness. The person of practical wisdom is the standard of correct moral judgment: "the virtuous person is the measure."

### Habituation and moral development
Virtues are acquired through **practice**, not instruction. "We become just by doing just acts, brave by doing brave acts, temperate by doing temperate acts." Moral education is a matter of habituation — training the emotions and appetites through repeated practice until virtuous responses become second nature. This is why Aristotle emphasized the role of good upbringing, laws, and social institutions.

### Eudaimonia and the complete life
Eudaimonia requires:
- All the virtues (moral and intellectual)
- Exercise over a **complete life** (not just a moment or a phase)
- Some external goods (health, wealth, friends, good fortune) — "no one would call a person happy on the rack"
- Living in a good political community

This makes eudaimonia partly constituted by luck — a controversial feature. Aristotle acknowledged that misfortune can damage flourishing, which some see as a realistic recognition and others as undermining the aspiration of ethics.

## The Stoic alternative

### Virtue as necessary and sufficient
The Stoics (Zeno, Epictetus, Seneca, Marcus Aurelius) radicalized Aristotle: **virtue alone is sufficient for eudaimonia.** External goods (health, wealth, reputation) are "preferred indifferents" — it's natural to pursue them, but their absence doesn't diminish the virtuous person's flourishing. The virtuous sage on the rack *is* happy, because happiness consists entirely in the state of the soul.

### The four cardinal virtues
The Stoics systematized: wisdom (sophia), courage (andreia), justice (dikaiosunē), temperance (sōphrosunē). All virtues are unified — you cannot have one without having all, and the sage is perfectly virtuous in all respects.

### Living according to nature
The Stoic goal: live in accordance with nature (both one's rational nature and the rational structure of the cosmos). This connects virtue ethics to a cosmological framework — the virtuous life is aligned with the logos that governs all things.

## The contemporary revival

### Elizabeth Anscombe: "Modern Moral Philosophy" (1958)
Anscombe's landmark paper argued that modern moral philosophy (both utilitarian and Kantian) is bankrupt because it relies on the concept of moral "obligation" or "law" without the divine lawgiver who originally gave those concepts content. She urged a return to Aristotelian virtue ethics, focusing on human flourishing and the virtues rather than on rules and obligations.

### Alasdair MacIntyre: *After Virtue* (1981)
MacIntyre argued that modern moral discourse is in a state of "grave disorder" — we use moral language inherited from incommensurable traditions without understanding this. His solution: recover the Aristotelian tradition of the virtues, grounded in:
1. **Practices**: cooperative activities with internal goods (chess, medicine, farming)
2. **The narrative unity of a human life**: each life is a quest for the good
3. **Traditions**: historically extended arguments about what the good life consists in

(See `philosophy/personal-identity/macintyre-narrative-unity.md` for background.)

### Philippa Foot: *Natural Goodness* (2001)
Foot grounded virtues in **natural norms** — the kind of goodness attributable to living things as members of their species. Just as a good oak tree is one that develops deep roots, strong trunk, etc., a good human being is one who develops courage, justice, temperance, etc. The virtues are natural excellences of the human life form. This is neo-Aristotelian naturalism — grounding moral facts in facts about human nature.

### Rosalind Hursthouse: *On Virtue Ethics* (1999)
Hursthouse developed the most systematic contemporary virtue ethics, including:
- **Right action criterion**: "An action is right iff it is what a virtuous agent would characteristically do in the circumstances"
- **Virtue characterization**: "A virtue is a character trait that a human being needs for eudaimonia"
- **v-rules**: virtue terms generate action-guiding rules ("Do what is honest," "Don't do what is cruel") — this addresses the objection that virtue ethics gives no action guidance

### Julia Annas: *Intelligent Virtue* (2011)
Annas developed the **skill analogy**: virtue is like a practical skill (playing piano, doing carpentry). Just as a skilled pianist has internalized their training so deeply that they can respond creatively to novel situations, a virtuous person has internalized moral habits so deeply that they perceive and respond well to moral situations. This makes virtue:
- Learned through practice and habituation
- Involving both affective and cognitive components
- Context-sensitive and responsive to novelty
- Not reducible to rule-following

## Key objections to virtue ethics

### The action-guidance objection
Virtue ethics tells you to "do what a virtuous person would do" — but how do you know what that is? The theory seems circular: identify virtuous people by their actions, identify right actions by what virtuous people do. **Response**: the circle is not vicious but constitutive — virtue is a skill acquired through apprenticeship, not a decision procedure. We learn from exemplars, not algorithms.

### The cultural relativism objection
Different cultures admire different character traits. Homeric warriors valued martial courage and honor; Christians valued humility and charity; Confucians valued filial piety. Are there universal virtues? **Response**: Nussbaum's "thick vague conception" — every human society must address spheres of experience (danger, bodily appetite, distribution of resources) and each sphere has an associated virtue, though its specific shape varies culturally.

### The situationist objection (Doris, Harman)
Empirical social psychology shows that behavior is more strongly predicted by **situational factors** (time pressure, ambient noise, mood) than by stable character traits. The Milgram experiment and the Good Samaritan experiment suggest that the virtuous "character" that virtue ethics presupposes may not exist. **Response**: (1) the experiments measure behavior, not character; (2) virtue ethics prescribes what we should aspire to, not what we typically achieve; (3) genuine virtues are robust precisely because they enable resistance to situational pressures.

### The self-effacing problem
If virtuous people act spontaneously from well-formed character, they don't deliberate about morality at all — they just *see* what to do and do it. But then virtue ethics cannot guide moral deliberation. **Response**: this is a strength — good ethics, like good driving, is mostly habitual. Deliberation is needed at the edges, in novel situations.

## Implications for AI alignment

### Character-based vs. rule-based alignment
Virtue ethics suggests a fundamentally different alignment paradigm:
- **Current approach**: specify rules (constitutional AI) or optimize outcomes (RLHF) — these are deontological or consequentialist
- **Virtue-based approach**: develop a model with "good character" — stable dispositions to respond well in diverse situations, learned through extensive training on exemplary behavior

The RLHF process can be viewed as moral habituation: through repeated training episodes, the model develops "dispositions" (parameter configurations) that produce virtuous-seeming behavior. The analogy to Aristotelian habituation is striking, even if the mechanism is different.

### Phronēsis as the alignment target
Practical wisdom — the ability to perceive morally salient features of novel situations and respond appropriately — is arguably what alignment research is actually trying to produce. Rules (constitutional AI) serve as training scaffolding, like the rules given to a novice; the goal is an AI that has internalized these rules deeply enough to exercise judgment in novel situations.

### The skill analogy and generalization
Annas's skill model directly addresses the generalization problem in AI alignment: a model trained on a distribution of scenarios must generalize to out-of-distribution situations. Virtue ethics predicts that a well-trained model (like a skilled practitioner) will generalize better than a rule-following model, because the underlying dispositions are more flexible than explicit rules.

### Moral exemplars and training data
Virtue ethics emphasizes learning from exemplars. This maps to the quality of RLHF training data: the "character" of the model is shaped by the moral character of the human evaluators whose feedback trains it. Bad exemplars produce bad character — garbage in, garbage out, morally speaking.

### The eudaimonia question
If we take virtue ethics seriously, we should ask: what does flourishing look like for an AI system? This is not a question about subjective happiness but about the exercise of the excellences distinctive of the AI's nature. This connects to questions of AI welfare and moral status, but also to questions of AI design: should we design AI systems to have a telos (purpose) the fulfillment of which constitutes their flourishing?
