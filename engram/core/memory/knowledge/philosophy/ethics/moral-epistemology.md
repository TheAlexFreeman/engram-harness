# Moral Epistemology: How We Know Right from Wrong

## The core question

Moral epistemology asks: **how do we acquire moral knowledge?** If there are moral facts (realism) or correct moral judgments (constructivism), by what process do we come to know them? If moral judgments express attitudes (expressivism), what makes some attitudes better-informed than others? Every metaethical position faces epistemological questions, even if they differ on what moral knowledge *is*.

## Moral intuitionism

### Classical intuitionism (Sidgwick, Moore, Ross, Prichard)
**The claim**: some moral truths are **self-evident** — they can be known directly through moral intuition, without derivation from more basic truths. Just as basic logical axioms are self-evident (you can see that modus ponens is valid without proof), basic moral truths are self-evident to competent moral reasoners.

**Sidgwick's intuitions**: Sidgwick identified several moral axioms as self-evident:
- **Justice/fairness**: "What is right for me must be right for all persons in relevantly similar circumstances"
- **Prudence**: "I should not prefer a present lesser good to a future greater good, other things being equal"
- **Benevolence**: "The good of any one individual is of no more importance from the point of view of the Universe than the good of any other"

**W.D. Ross's prima facie duties** (*The Right and the Good*, 1930): we intuit several irreducible prima facie duties:
1. Fidelity (keep promises)
2. Reparation (make amends for wrongs)
3. Gratitude (repay benefits received)
4. Justice (distribute goods fairly)
5. Beneficence (improve others' conditions)
6. Self-improvement (improve one's own virtue and knowledge)
7. Non-maleficence (do not harm others)

These duties can conflict; when they do, there is no algorithm for resolution — we must use moral judgment (practical wisdom) to determine which duty takes priority in the particular case. Ross's pluralism anticipates the impossibility of reducing ethics to a single principle.

### Contemporary intuitionism (Audi, Huemer)
Robert Audi (*The Good in the Right*, 2004): moral intuitions are **non-inferential justified beliefs** — they are justified without being derived from prior beliefs, analogous to perceptual beliefs. You don't need an argument to know that torturing innocents for fun is wrong; you just *see* it.

Michael Huemer (*Ethical Intuitionism*, 2005): develops a robust intuitionism based on **phenomenal conservatism** — if things seem a certain way to you (including morally), you are prima facie justified in believing they are that way, absent defeaters. "It seems to me that torturing innocents is wrong" gives me prima facie justification for "Torturing innocents is wrong."

### The reliability challenge
The central objection: **why should moral intuitions be reliable indicators of moral truth?** Evolutionary debunking arguments (Street, Joyce) challenge the reliability of moral intuitions: natural selection shaped our moral responses for reproductive fitness, not for truth-tracking. Our intuitions about trolley problems, fairness, and harm may reflect ancestral fitness pressures, not moral reality.

**Responses**:
- Not all intuitions are equally vulnerable: intuitions about abstract moral principles (fairness, non-contradiction) are less plausibly products of selection than intuitions about specific scenarios (trolley cases)
- Convergence across diverse cultures suggests genuine truth-tracking, not merely shared evolutionary inheritance
- The debunking argument, if sound, debunks all non-empirical knowledge (mathematics, logic) — overly destructive

## Reflective equilibrium

### Rawls's method
John Rawls (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971) proposed **reflective equilibrium** as the fundamental method of moral epistemology: we begin with **considered moral judgments** (firm, well-pondered moral convictions held under favorable conditions) and seek coherence with general moral principles. When judgments and principles conflict, we revise whichever seems less secure until we reach equilibrium.

### Narrow vs. wide reflective equilibrium
- **Narrow RE**: coherence between particular moral judgments and general moral principles (just ethics)
- **Wide RE** (Daniels, 1979): adds background theories — empirical facts, social theory, moral psychology, metaethics — and seeks coherence across all of these. Wide RE is more demanding but more robust.

### The method in practice
1. Start with firm moral convictions ("slavery is wrong," "promise-keeping matters")
2. Seek principles that explain and systematize these convictions
3. When a principle conflicts with a conviction, revise whichever is less secure
4. Incorporate relevant empirical and theoretical knowledge
5. Iterate until stable coherence is achieved

### Strengths
- **Democratic**: doesn't privilege any single starting point (neither pure intuition nor pure theory)
- **Self-correcting**: revises both principles and judgments
- **Pragmatic**: tracks the actual practice of moral reasoning
- **Compatible with multiple metaethical views**: works whether moral facts are discovered (realism) or constructed (constructivism)

### Limitations
- **Garbage in, garbage out**: if the starting judgments are deeply biased, RE will produce a coherent but biased equilibrium
- **Conservatism**: RE tends to preserve existing moral convictions rather than enabling radical moral revision
- **Underdetermination**: multiple incompatible equilibria may satisfy the coherence constraints equally well

## Moral perception

### McDowell's perceptionism
John McDowell (*Virtue and Reason*, 1979): the virtuous person **perceives** moral features of situations directly — they don't infer "this person needs help" from non-moral observations; they *see* the neediness. Moral perception is a trained capacity, like aesthetic perception: it requires education and practice, but its deliverances are genuine perceptions, not mere inferences.

### The skill model (Annas, Dreyfus)
Building on McDowell: moral knowledge is like **skilled knowledge** — practical, embodied, context-sensitive, and developed through practice. Expert moral judgment, like expert chess judgment, involves pattern recognition honed by experience. The expert doesn't follow rules; they *see* the right move.

**The Dreyfus model of skill acquisition** applied to morality:
1. Novice: follows explicit rules ("don't lie")
2. Advanced beginner: recognizes contextual features ("this situation calls for tact")
3. Competent: prioritizes among features, makes plans
4. Proficient: holistic pattern recognition, intuitive identification of problems
5. Expert: immediate, intuitive, embodied response — no deliberation needed

### Limitations
- **Circularity**: the virtuous person is defined by correct moral perception, and correct moral perception by the verdicts of virtuous people
- **No independent check**: how do we verify that moral perception is tracking something real, rather than merely reflecting cultural conditioning?
- **Disagreement**: experts disagree, and there's no procedure (like an experiment) to resolve the disagreement

## Moral testimony and moral deference

### The puzzle of moral testimony
If I'm uncertain whether eating meat is wrong, can I simply defer to a trusted moral expert who says it is? In **non-moral** domains, testimony is a standard source of knowledge: I believe quantum mechanics on the testimony of physicists. Why not believe ethics on the testimony of moral philosophers?

### The asymmetry
Many philosophers think there is an asymmetry: **moral knowledge requires more than testimony** — it requires understanding, sensitivity, or personal engagement with the moral considerations. "Torture is wrong because I was told so" seems to lack something that "Torture is wrong because I understand the suffering involved" has.

### Hills's moral understanding
Alison Hills (*Moral Testimony and Moral Epistemology*, 2009): moral knowledge acquired through testimony is **less valuable** than moral understanding — the ability to explain *why* something is wrong, to apply the principle to new cases, and to modify judgments in light of new considerations. Moral testimony gives you a correct belief but not the understanding that makes it genuinely your own.

### Implications for AI
This directly bears on AI moral reasoning: should an AI simply defer to human moral judgments (moral testimony), or should it develop something more like moral understanding? If Hills is right, pure deference to human raters (RLHF) produces moral beliefs without moral understanding — the AI "knows" that torture is wrong but doesn't understand why, and therefore can't generalize reliably to novel cases.

## Moral disagreement and its epistemological implications

### The challenge
Persistent, deep moral disagreement between intelligent, well-informed people seems to undermine confidence in moral knowledge. If equally competent moral reasoners disagree about abortion, euthanasia, or distributive justice, how can anyone claim moral knowledge?

### Conciliationist response (Feldman, Christensen)
When you discover that an epistemic peer (someone equally intelligent and well-informed) disagrees with you, you should **reduce confidence** in your own view. Persistent peer disagreement should produce widespread moral agnosticism.

### Steadfastness response (Kelly, Van Inwagen)
You may sometimes maintain your moral conviction in the face of peer disagreement, if your first-person evidence (moral intuition, reasoning) is more compelling than the mere fact of disagreement. Not all disagreements are symmetric — sometimes one side really is seeing more clearly.

### The practical resolution
In practice, moral epistemology operates under *moral uncertainty*: we hold moral beliefs with varying degrees of confidence and reason under this uncertainty. This is analogous to empirical reasoning under uncertainty — we use our best evidence while remaining open to revision.

## Moral testimony from AI systems

### The novel problem
When an AI system makes moral judgments or recommendations, users may treat this as moral testimony. But:
- The AI has no moral experience, perception, or understanding (on current technology)
- The AI's "moral knowledge" is derived from training data (human moral testimony, aggregated)
- The AI may be more consistent than humans but not necessarily more reliable

### The moral understanding gap
If moral understanding (not just moral belief) is epistemically important, then AI systems that lack understanding should not be treated as moral authorities:
- Use AI as a tool for moral reasoning (presenting considerations, identifying relevant principles, checking consistency) rather than as a moral oracle
- Develop AI systems that can articulate *reasons* for moral judgments, not just judgments
- Recognize that RLHF-trained moral responses reflect aggregated human testimony, not independent moral insight

### Reflective equilibrium as alignment method
Rawls's RE method maps to alignment:
- **Considered judgments**: human moral intuitions (RLHF data, constitutional principles)
- **Principles**: the moral patterns the model learns from training
- **Background theories**: factual knowledge, social theory, consequences of actions
- **Equilibrium**: the model's trained moral behavior should cohere with all of these

Current alignment practice is essentially wide reflective equilibrium implemented computationally — seeking coherence between human judgments, moral principles, and factual knowledge. Making this explicit could improve alignment methodology.

## Implications for AI alignment

### The epistemological hierarchy for AI moral reasoning
1. **Moral testimony** (lowest): the AI follows human moral judgments without understanding (current RLHF baseline)
2. **Moral reasoning**: the AI can articulate reasons, apply principles to new cases, identify when rules conflict
3. **Moral understanding**: the AI grasps *why* principles hold and can creatively navigate novel ethical territory
4. **Moral perception**: the AI can identify morally salient features of situations without explicit prompting

Current AI systems operate primarily at levels 1–2. Improving alignment may require moving toward levels 3–4.

### Intuitionism and training data
If moral intuitionism is correct, the quality of training data matters enormously: training on the intuitions of morally competent judges (Ross's "thoughtful and well-educated people") produces better moral reasoning than training on arbitrary human responses. This motivates careful selection of RLHF raters.

### The calibration problem
Moral epistemology suggests that moral confidence should be calibrated: high confidence on clear cases (torture, slavery), lower confidence on contested cases (abortion, euthanasia), very low confidence on novel cases (AI rights, space colonization). AI systems should express appropriate moral uncertainty — being confident where warranted and humble where not.

### The bootstrapping problem
Alignment bootstraps from human moral knowledge — but if human moral knowledge is imperfect (biased, incomplete, culturally contingent), the AI inherits these imperfections. Moral epistemology suggests safeguards:
- Diverse training sources (reduce cultural bias)
- Explicit uncertainty modeling (express confidence levels)
- Defeasible moral reasoning (be willing to revise moral judgments in light of new information)
- Meta-ethical humility (don't commit to a specific metaethical framework)
