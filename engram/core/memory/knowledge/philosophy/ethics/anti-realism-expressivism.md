# Anti-Realism and Expressivism: Morality Without Moral Facts

## The core idea

Moral anti-realism denies the central claim of moral realism: **there are no objective, mind-independent moral facts.** Moral judgments are not descriptions of an independent moral reality but are instead expressions of attitudes, projections of sentiments, constructions of social agreements, or simply false claims about a non-existent domain.

Anti-realism comes in several importantly different varieties, from the moderate (constructivism: moral facts exist but are mind-dependent) to the radical (error theory: all moral claims are literally false).

## Emotivism: the classical beginning

### A.J. Ayer (*Language, Truth and Logic*, 1936)
The logical positivist approach to ethics: meaningful statements are either **analytic** (true by definition) or **empirically verifiable**. Moral statements are neither. Therefore moral statements are **literally meaningless** as claims about the world. They are merely expressions of emotion — "Stealing is wrong" means something like "Stealing! Boo!" (the "Boo/Hurrah" theory).

### C.L. Stevenson (*Ethics and Language*, 1944)
Refined emotivism: moral statements have both **descriptive meaning** (conveying factual information) and **emotive meaning** (expressing and evoking attitudes). Moral disagreement is fundamentally disagreement in **attitude**, not disagreement in **belief**. When we argue about morality, we are trying to influence each other's attitudes, not to discover facts.

### Limitations of emotivism
- Cannot explain the **logical structure** of moral reasoning: "If stealing is wrong, and this is stealing, then this is wrong" is a valid inference, but emotivism treats moral claims as non-propositional (not truth-apt), so they can't participate in logical inference
- Cannot distinguish moral judgments from mere personal preferences: "Torture is wrong" seems fundamentally different from "I dislike broccoli," but emotivism assimilates them
- The Frege-Geach problem: moral terms appear in embedded contexts ("If stealing is wrong, then assisting stealing is wrong") where they are not being asserted — emotivism cannot explain what moral terms mean in unasserted contexts

## Prescriptivism: the Hare alternative

### R.M. Hare (*The Language of Morals*, 1952; *Freedom and Reason*, 1963)
Moral judgments are **prescriptions** (commands) that are:
- **Universalizable**: if I prescribe that stealing is wrong, I prescribe it for all relevantly similar cases (including cases where I would be the thief)
- **Overriding**: moral prescriptions take precedence over other prescriptions

Hare argued that universalizability + the agent's own preferences, universalized, converges on **utilitarian conclusions**. If I must prescribe for all persons in all relevantly similar situations, and I give equal weight to everyone's preferences (because I might be any of them), I end up maximizing overall preference-satisfaction.

### Advantages over emotivism
- Explains logical structure (prescriptions can participate in logic: "If you should do X, and doing X requires doing Y, then you should do Y")
- Explains moral action-guidance (prescriptions guide action; descriptions don't)
- Connects metaethics to normative theory (universalizability → utilitarianism)

### Limitations
- The universalizability requirement seems to smuggle in a substantial moral commitment (why must moral judgments be universalizable?)
- The derivation of utilitarianism from universalizability is contested
- The "fanatic" problem: a consistent Nazi who is willing to universalize "eliminate Jews" (including in scenarios where they themselves are Jewish) cannot be condemned by the formal universalizability test alone

## Expressivism: the sophisticated successor

### Simon Blackburn: quasi-realism (*Spreading the Word*, 1984; *Ruling Passions*, 1998)
Blackburn's project: start from an anti-realist base (moral judgments express attitudes, not beliefs about moral facts) and **earn back** all the features of moral discourse that seem to require realism:
- Moral truth ("It is true that torture is wrong" — earned as: we can distinguish better and worse attitudes, and the best attitudes are "true")
- Moral knowledge ("I know that torture is wrong" — earned as: having the right attitudes well-grounded in moral sensitivity)
- Moral logic ("If P is wrong, then assisting P is wrong" — earned through a logic of attitudes)
- Moral objectivity (earned as: some attitudes are better than others by standards internal to moral practice)

**The quasi-realist program**: build up from expressivism to something that looks and functions exactly like moral realism, without the metaphysical commitments. If successful, the debate between realism and anti-realism becomes "merely" metaphysical — making no practical difference.

### Allan Gibbard: norm-expressivism (*Wise Choices, Apt Feelings*, 1990; *Thinking How to Live*, 2003)
Gibbard's expressivism: moral judgments express **norms** — rules for what to feel, think, and do. "Stealing is wrong" expresses the norm: it is rational to feel guilt about stealing and to disapprove of stealing.

In his later work, Gibbard generalized: **all normative judgments** (moral, prudential, epistemic) express plans — "Stealing is wrong" expresses the plan to not steal, to feel guilt if one steals, to disapprove of others' stealing, etc. This "planning" expressivism unifies normative discourse under a single expressive analysis.

### The Frege-Geach problem and its solutions
The central technical challenge for expressivism: moral terms appear in **unasserted** contexts:

"If stealing is wrong, then assisting theft is wrong."

In this conditional, "stealing is wrong" is not asserted (the speaker isn't claiming stealing is wrong — they're stating a conditional). But if moral judgments are expressions of attitude, what attitude is being expressed in the antecedent of a conditional where the speaker may not hold the attitude?

**Solutions**:
- Blackburn: develop a logic of attitudes that handles embedding (the conditional expresses a higher-order attitude: "from the attitude of disapproving of stealing, I form the attitude of disapproving of assisting theft")
- Gibbard: norms and plans have logical structure that enables embedding
- Schroeder (*Being For*, 2008): a formal treatment using a "being for" relation that handles the compositional semantics

The consensus view: expressivists have made significant progress on Frege-Geach but the solutions remain more complex than the realist alternative (where moral terms simply refer to moral properties).

## Error theory: all moral claims are false

### J.L. Mackie (*Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong*, 1977)
Mackie's position is unique: moral claims are **truth-apt** (they purport to describe objective moral facts) but **systematically false** (there are no objective moral facts to describe). Moral discourse involves a massive, systematic error — we talk as if there are moral facts, but there aren't.

**Arguments for error theory**:
1. **The argument from queerness**: moral properties would be metaphysically strange (see moral realism file)
2. **The argument from disagreement**: persistent moral disagreement is best explained by the absence of moral facts to converge on
3. **The argument from relativity**: the variation of moral codes across cultures suggests that moral beliefs are culturally constructed, not truth-tracking

### The aftermath of error theory
If all moral claims are false, what follows practically?
- **Moral fictionalism** (Joyce, 2001): continue to use moral language as a useful fiction — it coordinates behavior, promotes cooperation, etc., even though it's strictly false
- **Moral abolitionism** (Garner, 2007): abandon moral discourse entirely and replace it with explicit negotiation of interests
- **Moral conservationism** (Olson, 2014): preserve the practice of moral judgment for its social benefits while acknowledging its theoretical falsity

## Constructivism: the middle ground

### Korsgaard's constructivism
Christine Korsgaard (*The Sources of Normativity*, 1996): moral truths are **constructed** by rational agents through the process of reflective endorsement. There are no pre-existing moral facts to discover — but the output of correct moral reasoning is genuinely normative (action-guiding in a way that mere preferences are not).

The construction: when a rational agent reflects on their impulses and asks "should I act on this?", they must endorse or reject the impulse. The principles they endorse through this reflective process are the moral law. This is Kantian constructivism — morality is self-legislated, not discovered.

### Street's constructivism
Sharon Street (*What is Constructivism in Ethics and Metaethics?*, 2010): moral truths are constituted by the attitudes of agents after full rational reflection. There is no standard external to the reflective process — the reflective process *is* the standard. This is anti-realist (there are no stance-independent moral facts) but not nihilistic (correct moral reasoning produces genuine normative conclusions).

### Advantages of constructivism
- Avoids the metaphysical commitments of realism (no mysterious moral properties)
- Avoids the nihilism of error theory (moral reasoning produces genuine norms)
- Explains how morality can be both "objective" (not arbitrary) and "mind-dependent" (constructed through reasoning)

### Limitations
- The construction process itself must be justified — but how? If the justification is moral, the theory is circular. If non-moral, the theory reduces to some other grounding.
- Why should I accept the outputs of *your* construction? Constructivism seems to threaten relativism — each agent constructs their own morality.

## Moral relativism

### Descriptive relativism
The empirical observation that moral beliefs vary across cultures. This is uncontroversial — cultures disagree about many moral issues.

### Metaethical relativism
The philosophical claim that moral truths are **relative to** a culture, framework, or individual. "X is wrong" means "X is wrong relative to framework F." There is no framework-independent truth.

**Problems**:
- **The reformer's dilemma**: a moral reformer (e.g., an abolitionist in a slave-owning society) claims the society's moral framework is wrong. Relativism makes this incoherent — the reformer can only say the framework is wrong relative to some other framework.
- **The tolerance paradox**: if relativism implies we should be tolerant of other cultures' moral practices, then tolerance is itself a non-relative moral demand — but relativism denies non-relative moral truths.
- **Practical incoherence**: we actually treat moral questions as having objective answers (we argue about them, cite evidence, try to convince each other) — this practice is incoherent on a relativist metaethics.

## Implications for AI alignment

### The metaethical stakes for alignment
The choice between realism and anti-realism has profound implications for alignment:

**If realism**: there are right answers, and we can (in principle) train AI to find them. Alignment is a technical problem with a well-defined target.

**If expressivism**: moral claims express attitudes, and alignment means aligning the AI's "attitudes" with those of its users/society. But whose attitudes? This is a political problem, not an epistemic one.

**If error theory**: moral claims are false, and alignment cannot be grounded in morality at all. We need some other framework (social contracts, game theory, institutional design).

**If constructivism**: moral truths are constructed through correct reasoning. An AI that reasons correctly will construct the right moral principles. Alignment is a reasoning-quality problem.

### Quasi-realism and practical alignment
Blackburn's quasi-realism suggests a pragmatic resolution: **it doesn't matter** whether moral facts "really" exist. What matters is that we can distinguish better from worse moral reasoning, and we can train AI to do better moral reasoning. The abstract metaphysical question can be set aside if the practical question (does the AI behave well?) is answered.

This is arguably the implicit metaethics of current alignment research: treat moral judgments as if they're objective for practical training purposes, without committing to a specific metaethical position.

### Expressivism and RLHF
If expressivism is correct, RLHF is literally expressing human attitudes to the AI and training it to reproduce them. This is not discovering moral facts but transmitting moral attitudes. The quality of alignment then depends on the quality of the attitudes transmitted — which depends on the quality of the human raters.

### Error theory and alignment by contract
If error theory is correct, we cannot align AI to moral facts (there are none). But we can align AI to social contracts — practical agreements about how AI should behave, negotiated among stakeholders. This aligns with contractualist approaches to alignment and with institutional governance of AI.

### Moral relativism and cross-cultural AI deployment
AI systems deployed across cultures face the relativism problem directly: whose moral standards should the system follow? Options:
- **Universalist**: follow one set of moral standards globally (whose?)
- **Relativist**: adapt to local moral standards (which may include standards we find repugnant)
- **Pluralist**: identify a core of cross-culturally shared values and allow variation on contested issues
- **Proceduralist**: follow the output of legitimate democratic processes in each jurisdiction
