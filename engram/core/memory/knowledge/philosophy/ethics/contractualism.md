# Contractualism: Rawls, Scanlon, and the Morality of Mutual Justifiability

## The core idea

Contractualism holds that **moral principles are those that no one could reasonably reject as a basis for informed, unforced general agreement.** Unlike utilitarianism (which aggregates welfare) or virtue ethics (which cultivates character), contractualism grounds morality in what rational agents could agree to — the authority of morality derives from its justifiability to each person affected.

Two major versions:
1. **Rawlsian contractarianism**: principles of justice are those rational agents would choose behind a "veil of ignorance" (primarily a theory of political institutions)
2. **Scanlonian contractualism**: an act is wrong if its performance would be disallowed by any set of principles that no one could reasonably reject (a theory of interpersonal morality)

Both trace their lineage to social contract theory (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant) but transform it from a theory of political legitimacy into a theory of moral reasoning.

## Historical background: the social contract tradition

### Hobbes: contract from self-interest
Hobbes (*Leviathan*, 1651): in the state of nature, life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Rational self-interest drives agents to agree on rules enforced by a sovereign. Morality is the set of rules that rational egoists would mutually accept. **Problem**: this makes morality contingent on power — those with no bargaining power (the disabled, future generations, animals) have no moral standing.

### Locke and Rousseau: contract from natural rights/general will
Locke: the contract codifies pre-existing natural rights. Rousseau: the social contract expresses the general will — what citizens would agree to qua citizens, not qua self-interested individuals. Rousseau's version is closer to Kant and Rawls: the contract is not a historical event but a regulative ideal.

### Kant: the contract as idea of reason
Kant (*Metaphysics of Morals*): the social contract is an idea of reason — a test for the legitimacy of laws. "A law is just if all citizens could agree to it." This is not a prediction about what people would in fact agree to, but a normative standard: could the law be willed from an impartial standpoint?

## John Rawls: justice as fairness

### *A Theory of Justice* (1971)
Rawls revived social contract theory as a systematic alternative to utilitarianism for political philosophy.

### The original position and the veil of ignorance
Rawls's central thought experiment: imagine rational agents choosing principles of justice from behind a **veil of ignorance** — they don't know their:
- Social class, wealth, or income
- Natural talents and abilities
- Conception of the good (religion, values, life plans)
- Race, sex, generation
- Specific psychology (risk tolerance, etc.)

They do know general facts about human society, economics, and psychology. The veil ensures impartiality — you can't rig the rules in your favor if you don't know your position.

### The two principles of justice
Rawls argued that rational agents behind the veil would choose:

1. **The liberty principle**: each person has an equal right to the most extensive system of basic liberties compatible with a similar system for all (speech, conscience, association, political participation, etc.)

2. **The difference principle**: social and economic inequalities are permissible only if they benefit the least-advantaged members of society (and are attached to positions open to all under fair equality of opportunity)

The liberty principle has **lexical priority** over the difference principle — no amount of economic benefit justifies restricting basic liberties.

### The maximin strategy
Why the difference principle? Rawls argued that rational agents behind the veil would adopt a **maximin** strategy: maximize the minimum outcome. Since you might be the worst-off person in society, you would choose principles that make the worst-off position as good as possible. This rejects expected utility maximization (the utilitarian strategy) in favor of security against catastrophic outcomes.

### Reflective equilibrium
Rawls proposed **reflective equilibrium** as the method of moral reasoning: we go back and forth between our considered moral judgments (particular intuitions about concrete cases) and our moral principles (general theories), adjusting each in light of the other until they cohere. This is neither pure deduction from principles nor pure induction from cases, but a dialectical process.

### *Political Liberalism* (1993)
Rawls later restricted his theory to the **political** domain, acknowledging "reasonable pluralism" — citizens hold irreconcilably different comprehensive doctrines (religious, philosophical, moral). Justice as fairness is not a comprehensive moral theory but a **political conception** — an "overlapping consensus" among reasonable comprehensive doctrines.

Key concepts:
- **Public reason**: political debate should be conducted in terms accessible to all reasonable citizens, not in terms of any particular comprehensive doctrine
- **The burdens of judgment**: reasonable people will disagree about comprehensive doctrines because of the complexity of evidence, the weight of different considerations, and conceptual vagueness
- **Reasonable** vs. **rational**: rational agents pursue their own good; reasonable agents seek fair terms of cooperation with others they recognize as free and equal

## T.M. Scanlon: *What We Owe to Each Other* (1998)

### The contractualist formula
"An act is wrong if its performance under the circumstances would be disallowed by any set of principles for the general regulation of behavior that no one could **reasonably reject** as a basis for informed, unforced general agreement."

### Key features

**Individual reasons, not aggregation**: Scanlon explicitly rejects utilitarian aggregation. The wrongness of an act depends on whether *each individual* has reason to reject the principle permitting it — not on the sum total of reasons. This means you can't justify imposing a severe burden on one person by appeal to small benefits distributed across millions.

**Reasonable rejection, not mere rejection**: the standard is not what anyone *would* reject (prediction) or *could* reject (logical possibility) but what anyone could *reasonably* reject — i.e., reject with adequate justification. This rules out both overly demanding and overly permissive principles.

**The standpoint of each person**: moral reasoning proceeds by considering, for each person affected, whether they have sufficient reason to reject the principle. This implements the Kantian insight about the separateness of persons: each individual's standpoint matters independently.

**Principles, not acts**: the test applies to principles for the general regulation of behavior, not to individual acts directly. This makes Scanlonian contractualism similar in structure to rule utilitarianism — but the justification is entirely different (mutual justifiability vs. utility maximization).

### What matters: the value of mutual recognition
For Scanlon, the deepest motivation for morality is the **value of living with others on terms that they could not reasonably reject** — the value of mutual recognition. Morality is constitutively social: being moral is being the kind of person who seeks justifiable terms of cooperation. This is not instrumental (be moral because it benefits you) but constitutive (being moral is part of what it means to relate to others as equals).

### Generic reasons and standpoints
Scanlon uses **generic reasons** — reasons that anyone in a given position would have — rather than the idiosyncratic reasons of particular individuals. This prevents the test from being held hostage to unreasonable or unusual preferences while still being responsive to the positions people actually occupy.

## Gauthier: contractarianism from rational choice

David Gauthier (*Morals by Agreement*, 1986) represents the **Hobbesian** strand: morality is the set of constraints that rational self-interested maximizers would agree to accept because cooperation produces mutual gains. Key differences from Rawls and Scanlon:
- The motivation is rational self-interest, not fairness or mutual respect
- No veil of ignorance — agents bargain from their actual positions
- The solution concept is drawn from game theory (minimax relative concession)

**Problem**: Gauthier's framework excludes those with no bargaining power — the severely disabled, future generations, non-human animals. This is the same limitation as Hobbes's original contract.

## Key objections to contractualism

### The scope problem
Who is party to the contract? Contractualism seems to exclude:
- **Non-rational beings**: animals, infants, severely cognitively disabled persons
- **Future generations**: they can't participate in the agreement
- **AI systems**: unclear whether they can "reasonably reject" anything

**Scanlon's response**: the test is about what principles we would accept for the treatment of beings in various categories — non-rational beings can be covered by principles that rational agents accept on their behalf. But this is indirect and arguably weaker than utilitarian sentientism, which includes all suffering beings directly.

### The indeterminacy objection
Which principles would be chosen? In many cases, reasonable people could disagree about which principles are reasonably rejectable. The theory may be indeterminate. **Response**: some indeterminacy is a feature — morality genuinely is less determinate than utilitarianism suggests. Where contractualism is determinate (severe burdens on individuals can't be justified by diffuse benefits), it captures important moral truths.

### The status quo bias objection
The veil of ignorance (Rawls) may build in contingent features of the status quo — the "general facts" known behind the veil already reflect particular social arrangements. **Response**: the veil is meant to be a thought experiment, not a historical prediction. Its content can be revised as our understanding of social facts improves.

### The motivation objection
Why should I care about what others could reasonably reject? If I'm a rational egoist, mutual justifiability has no grip on me. **Scanlon's response**: the desire to live on terms justifiable to others is not derived from self-interest but is a fundamental moral motivation — if you lack it, the theory has nothing to say to you, but this is true of any moral theory.

## Implications for AI alignment

### AI alignment as a contractualist problem
The fundamental alignment problem — "how should AI systems relate to the humans they serve?" — is naturally contractualist: **what principles for AI behavior could no one reasonably reject?**

This framing has advantages over:
- Utilitarianism: it respects the separateness of persons (no aggregation across lives)
- Pure deontology: it's responsive to consequences (reasonable rejection depends on expected burdens and benefits)
- Virtue ethics: it provides more determinate action guidance

### The veil of ignorance for AI design
Rawls's thought experiment directly applies: design AI systems from behind a veil of ignorance about which user you will be. If you don't know whether you're the vulnerable user or the sophisticated user, the powerful or the powerless, you would choose AI design principles that protect the worst-off users. This motivates:
- Accessibility defaults
- Protection against manipulation
- Transparency and explainability for those most affected
- Robust safety features rather than maximum capability

### Reasonable rejection as alignment criterion
Scanlon's test: an AI action is wrong if it would be disallowed by principles that no affected person could reasonably reject. This captures:
- The user who could reasonably reject being manipulated
- The third party who could reasonably reject being harmed by AI-mediated actions
- The worker who could reasonably reject AI systems that destroy their livelihood without compensation
- The future person who could reasonably reject AI development that threatens their existence

### Public reason and AI governance
Rawls's public reason doctrine applies to AI governance: decisions about how AI should operate in society should be justified in terms accessible to all reasonable citizens, not in terms of any particular ideology, religion, or corporate interest. This motivates:
- Democratic governance of AI development
- Transparency in AI decision-making processes
- Justifications for AI behavior that are publicly accessible

### The non-aggregation principle
Scanlon's anti-aggregation stance directly addresses a common AI alignment problem: utilitarian reasoning might justify AI systems that impose severe costs on small groups while producing modest benefits for many. Contractualism says each person's standpoint matters independently — you cannot aggregate across lives to justify imposing severe burdens.

### Multi-stakeholder alignment
Contractualism naturally handles multi-stakeholder alignment: the AI system must be designed so that its operating principles are justifiable to each stakeholder — the user, the developer, affected third parties, and society. This is more nuanced than single-principal alignment and avoids the aggregation problems of multi-principal utilitarianism.
