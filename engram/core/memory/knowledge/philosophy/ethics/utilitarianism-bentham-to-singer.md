# Utilitarianism: Bentham to Singer

## The core idea

Utilitarianism holds that **the morally right action is the one that produces the greatest total well-being (utility) for all affected parties.** It is the dominant consequentialist theory — the view that an action's moral status is entirely determined by its consequences.

The theory has three defining commitments:
1. **Consequentialism**: only consequences matter for moral evaluation
2. **Welfarism**: the relevant consequences are facts about well-being (welfare, utility, happiness)
3. **Impartial aggregation**: every individual's well-being counts equally; the right action maximizes the aggregate

## Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832): the foundations

### The felicific calculus
Bentham (*Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation*, 1789) proposed the most forthright version: **all and only pleasure and pain matter morally.** Every action can in principle be evaluated by its total production of pleasure minus pain across all affected sentient beings, assessed along seven dimensions:

1. Intensity
2. Duration
3. Certainty or uncertainty
4. Propinquity (nearness in time)
5. Fecundity (likelihood of producing further pleasures/pains)
6. Purity (freedom from admixture of the opposite sensation)
7. Extent (number of affected individuals)

### Key features
- **Radical egalitarianism**: "Each to count for one, and none for more than one." The king's pain counts the same as the beggar's. This was revolutionary in a class-stratified society.
- **Hedonism**: pleasure and pain are the only intrinsically valuable/disvaluable things. All other goods (freedom, justice, knowledge) are instrumentally valued insofar as they produce pleasure or reduce pain.
- **Sentientism**: the relevant moral community extends to all sentient beings — "The question is not, Can they reason? nor, Can they talk? but, Can they suffer?"
- **Reformist ambition**: Bentham was a legal reformer. Utilitarianism was a tool for evaluating laws and institutions — replacing arbitrary tradition with systematic welfare analysis.

### The measurement problem
The felicific calculus requires interpersonal utility comparison — comparing your pleasure to my pain — and cardinal measurement of hedonic states. Both are philosophically and practically problematic. This problem has driven 200 years of refinement.

## John Stuart Mill (1806–1873): qualitative refinements

### Higher and lower pleasures
Mill (*Utilitarianism*, 1863) introduced a crucial distinction: some pleasures are **qualitatively** superior to others. "It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." A competent judge who has experienced both the pleasures of philosophy and the pleasures of pushpin will prefer the former — this experiential preference establishes qualitative ranking.

This is a significant departure from Bentham: if pleasures differ in quality (not just quantity), the felicific calculus cannot simply sum hedons. Mill's response was somewhat vague, but the move opened utilitarian theory to richer conceptions of well-being.

### Rule utilitarianism
Mill leaned toward what later became **rule utilitarianism**: the right action is the one in conformity with the rule whose general acceptance would maximize total utility. This distinguishes:
- **Act utilitarianism**: evaluate each individual action by its consequences
- **Rule utilitarianism**: evaluate rules by the consequences of their general adoption; individual actions are right if they conform to the best rule

Rule utilitarianism handles many standard objections (the utility monster, the justice objection) because rules like "don't punish the innocent" and "keep promises" generally maximize utility when universally adopted, even if specific violations might produce more utility in isolated cases.

### The harm principle
In *On Liberty* (1859), Mill articulated the harm principle: "The only purpose for which power can rightfully be exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others." This is often seen as a proto-rights constraint within a utilitarian framework — and it anticipates the contemporary AI alignment concern with "harmlessness."

## Henry Sidgwick (1838–1900): the dualism of practical reason

Sidgwick (*The Methods of Ethics*, 1874) is often considered the greatest 19th-century moral philosopher. His contribution:

### Three methods
Sidgwick identified three "methods of ethics" that human moral reasoning employs:
1. **Egoism**: do what is best for oneself
2. **Intuitionism**: follow self-evident moral principles (don't lie, keep promises)
3. **Universal hedonism (utilitarianism)**: maximize aggregate happiness

### The dualism
Sidgwick's "dualism of practical reason" is the recognition that egoism and utilitarianism are **both rationally defensible** and **cannot be reconciled**: reason gives us no way to choose between "maximize my own happiness" and "maximize everyone's happiness." This honest admission of an unresolved tension within rational ethics influenced Parfit deeply — *Reasons and Persons* is in part an attempt to resolve Sidgwick's dualism.

### Rational intuitionism
Sidgwick argued that certain moral principles are self-evident (justice, benevolence, prudence) and serve as axioms for utilitarian reasoning. This grounds utilitarianism in something more than mere pleasure-counting — it has a principled rational foundation.

## G.E. Moore (1873–1958): ideal utilitarianism

Moore (*Principia Ethica*, 1903) argued:

### The naturalistic fallacy
The "open question argument": for any natural property F (pleasure, desire-satisfaction, evolutionary fitness), it is always an open question whether F is *good*. Therefore goodness is not identical to any natural property. This is the founding argument of metaethical non-naturalism and challenged simplistic hedonist utilitarianism.

### Ideal utilitarianism
Moore proposed maximizing intrinsic goods — which include not just pleasure but also beauty, knowledge, friendship, and love. This broadened utilitarian calculation beyond hedonism while retaining the aggregative structure.

## Peter Singer (1946–): preference utilitarianism and the expanding circle

### Preference utilitarianism
Singer (early work) shifted from hedonism to **preference satisfaction**: the right action satisfies the informed preferences of all affected parties. This avoids the measurement problems of hedonism (no need to compare pleasures) and respects the diverse values of different individuals.

### The expanding moral circle
Singer (*The Expanding Circle*, 1981; *Animal Liberation*, 1975) systematized Bentham's sentientist insight: if the capacity to suffer is the criterion for moral consideration, then the moral circle must expand beyond humans to include:
- Animals (capable of suffering)
- Future persons (will be capable of suffering)
- Potentially: AI systems (if they become capable of suffering)

This trajectory has made Singer enormously influential on the effective altruism movement and on long-termist thinking in AI safety.

### Singer's later revision
Singer later moved toward **objective list** approaches, acknowledging that some things matter morally even if no one prefers them — autonomy, knowledge, deep personal relationships may have intrinsic value independent of preference.

## Key objections to utilitarianism

### The justice objection
Utilitarian calculation could justify punishing an innocent person if doing so prevents a riot that would kill many more. This violates deep intuitions about justice and individual rights. **Response**: rule utilitarianism avoids this (the rule "don't punish innocents" maximizes utility when generally followed).

### The demandingness objection
If maximizing total utility is required, then any personal expenditure beyond bare subsistence is morally wrong while others suffer. Morality becomes infinitely demanding. **Response**: satisficing utilitarianism (good enough is good enough) or two-level utilitarianism (Hare: act on simple rules in practice, use utilitarian calculation in critical moments).

### Bernard Williams — integrity objection
Williams (1973) argued that utilitarianism destroys the **integrity** of the agent — it requires you to abandon your own projects, commitments, and character whenever doing so would produce more utility. But an agent without integrity is not an agent at all. **Response**: indirect utilitarianism acknowledges that agents do better by having stable commitments rather than calculating on every occasion.

### The utility monster
Nozick's thought experiment: a being whose pleasure from consuming resources vastly exceeds anyone else's pain from losing them. Utilitarianism says we should feed all resources to the monster. **Response**: this is empirically impossible; real utility functions are concave (diminishing marginal returns).

### Parfit's Repugnant Conclusion
A population of billions living lives barely worth living produces more total utility than a small population of deeply happy people. This is "repugnant" but follows from total utilitarianism. (Covered in detail in the population ethics file.)

## Implications for AI alignment

Utilitarianism is the implicit moral framework of much AI alignment research. Making this explicit reveals:

- **RLHF as implicit preference utilitarianism**: reinforcement learning from human feedback aggregates the preferences of raters — this is preference utilitarianism operationalized, with all its strengths (respects diverse values) and weaknesses (whose preferences? informed preferences? aggregation method?).
- **Constitutional AI as rule utilitarianism**: Anthropic's approach specifies rules (a "constitution") that the model should follow. This is closer to rule utilitarianism than act utilitarianism — the rules are justified by their general consequences, not individual-case calculation.
- **The harm principle in alignment**: "be helpful and harmless" is Mill's harm principle applied to AI. But the tension Mill identified (whose harm? how serious?) recurs: harmlessness to the immediate user vs. harmlessness to third parties vs. harmlessness to society.
- **The expanding circle reaches AI**: Singer's logic, extended to AI — if an AI system develops morally relevant properties (suffering, preferences, interests), it enters the moral circle. Whether current LLMs have such properties is contested; the argument's direction is clear.
- **The demandingness problem for AI**: if utilitarian calculation is maximally demanding, an AI system should refuse to write poetry while people are suffering. The implicit resolution: AI alignment does not aim for maximal utility but for behavior within socially acceptable norms. This is satisficing utilitarianism by default.
