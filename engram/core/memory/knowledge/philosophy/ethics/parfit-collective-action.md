# Parfit on Collective Action: What We Together Do

## The core problem

*Reasons and Persons* Part One includes a sustained analysis of **collective action problems** — situations where individually rational actions produce collectively irrational outcomes. Parfit called this the problem of "what we together do" and considered it among the most practically important problems in ethics.

The question: **when many agents each make individually insignificant choices, they can collectively produce enormous harm. Who is responsible, and what should each person do?**

## The structure of collective harm

### The five types
Parfit identified a taxonomy of cases where many individual actions combine to produce harm:

1. **Each person's act makes a perceptible difference**: smoking in a crowded room. Each additional smoker makes the air measurably worse for everyone. Standard consequentialism handles this — each person should consider their marginal contribution.

2. **Threshold effects**: no individual act makes a perceptible difference, but the accumulation crosses a threshold. Each additional gram of pollution in a river is imperceptible, but the millionth gram kills all the fish. No individual's contribution is "the one that caused it."

3. **Sorites-like cases**: continuous collective harm with no sharp threshold. Each person's marginal contribution is below the threshold of perception, and there is no point at which the collective harm "begins."

4. **Overdetermination**: even if I don't add my pollution, enough others will that the fish will die anyway. My individual contribution is causally unnecessary.

5. **Coordination failures**: the harm comes not from individual contributions but from failure to coordinate. If half the drivers switched to public transport, traffic would vastly improve — but no individual switch makes a noticeable difference.

### The harmless-torturer argument
Parfit's most vivid example: a thousand torturers each turn a dial that delivers electric current to a victim. Each torturer's turn of the dial is imperceptible to the victim — no single increment of current is felt. But together, the thousand turns produce agonizing pain.

**The puzzle**: if no individual's act makes a perceptible difference to the victim, is each individual act wrong? Common sense says it must be wrong — torture is happening. But on what grounds? No single dial-turn is sufficient or necessary for the victim's suffering.

## The inadequacy of standard moral frameworks

### Act consequentialism's problem
AC says: "do what produces the best consequences." But in imperceptible-contribution cases, each person's individual contribution produces **no perceptible effect.** The marginal consequence of my action is zero (or below any threshold of moral significance). Therefore AC cannot condemn my individual contribution — even though the *collective* consequence is terrible.

This is a genuine failure of AC, not a thought-experiment trick. Climate change is exactly this structure: no individual's carbon emissions make a perceptible difference to global temperature, but the aggregate is catastrophic.

### Kantian universalizability
The categorical imperative fares better: "What if everyone did this?" If everyone polluted, the result would be catastrophic, so I cannot universalize my maxim of polluting. But this test is too crude — if I'm one of billions, my *specific* contribution is needed for the collective harm only in a statistical sense. And the universalizability test doesn't handle asymmetric cases where some can pollute harmlessly if others don't.

### Common-sense morality's failure
CSM relies on individual causation and perceptible harm. If my specific act doesn't perceptibly harm anyone, CSM cannot condemn it. But this makes CSM helpless before the most important moral problems of the modern world — climate change, environmental degradation, systemic inequality — which are all imperceptible-contribution problems.

## Parfit's analysis

### Expected harm
Parfit argues we should evaluate acts by their **expected effect**, not their perceptible effect. Even if my individual contribution to pollution is imperceptible, it has a tiny but real probability of being the one that pushes the system across a threshold. In expectation, the harm caused by my act = (probability of causing threshold-crossing) × (magnitude of threshold harm).

For large numbers of contributors, the expected harm per person converges on (total harm / number of contributors) — which can be substantial even when individual perceptible harm is zero.

### The share-of-total-harm principle
Parfit proposed: each person's act is wrong to the extent that it is their **share** of the collective harm, even if they cannot perceptibly identify their specific contribution. If 1000 torturers produce agony, each is 1/1000th responsible for the total agony — and that's a significant moral weight.

### Rejecting the perceptibility threshold
The key philosophical move: **reject the claim that moral significance requires perceptible individual causation.** An act can be wrong even if its individual causal contribution is imperceptible, because:
1. Its expected harm is non-zero
2. It is part of a pattern that collectively produces significant harm
3. The agent cannot will the universal adoption of their maxim

### Moral mathematics
Parfit coined the phrase "moral mathematics" for this domain: the systematic analysis of how individual contributions aggregate into collective outcomes. This is not utilitarian calculation in the traditional sense — it's a framework for navigating a class of problems that traditional moral theories handle poorly.

## The free-rider problem

### Classical formulation
Public goods (clean air, national defense, herd immunity) are provision problems: everyone benefits whether or not they contribute. Rational self-interest says: don't contribute (free-ride on others' contributions). If everyone reasons this way, the public good is not provided.

### Parfit's moral analysis
Free-riding is wrong even when:
- My individual non-contribution makes no perceptible difference
- The public good would be provided even without my contribution
- Everyone else is contributing

Why? Because **I cannot universalize the maxim of free-riding** (Kantian), the **share of collective harm is mine** (consequentialist), and **no one could reasonably accept a principle permitting free-riding** (contractualist). All three frameworks converge on condemning free-riding, even though the individual causal impact is negligible.

### The convergence
This is one of Parfit's clearest examples of the convergence thesis: Kantian, consequentialist, and contractualist reasoning all condemn free-riding, for somewhat different but compatible reasons.

## Tragedy of the commons

### Hardin's classic formulation
Garrett Hardin (1968): shared resources (fisheries, atmosphere, grazing land) are degraded when each user takes more than their sustainable share. Each herdsman adds one more cow to the commons; the benefit accrues to the herdsman while the cost is distributed across all users. The rational strategy for each is to add cows until the commons is destroyed.

### Beyond Hardin: Ostrom's institutional analysis
Elinor Ostrom (*Governing the Commons*, 1990) demonstrated that commons problems can be solved through institutional design:
- Clear group boundaries
- Rules matched to local conditions
- Collective-choice arrangements
- Monitoring and graduated sanctions
- Conflict-resolution mechanisms
- Nested enterprises for larger-scale problems

Ostrom's empirical work showed that real communities often solve commons problems without either privatization or government regulation — through evolved institutional arrangements. This is directly relevant to AI governance: AI commons problems (shared training data, shared computing resources, shared information environments) may benefit from Ostrom-style institutional design.

## Implications for AI alignment

### AI systems as collective-action participants
AI systems are now participants in collective-action problems:
- **Content generation**: each AI-generated piece of content is individually insignificant, but collectively AI content transforms the information ecosystem
- **Resource allocation**: each AI model's energy consumption is individually manageable, but collective AI computation is becoming a significant fraction of global energy use
- **Labor displacement**: each AI automation decision is individually rational, but collective AI-driven automation transforms labor markets

### The moral mathematics of AI at scale
Parfit's moral mathematics is essential for evaluating AI at scale:
- Individual AI decisions may be individually harmless but collectively harmful
- The "no individual harm" defense (each user interaction is fine) fails when the aggregate is problematic
- AI developers bear share-of-total-harm responsibility for collective effects

### Institutional design for AI commons
Ostrom's framework directly applies to AI governance:
- **Shared training data**: who has access, on what terms?
- **Shared information environment**: how do we prevent collective degradation of information quality?
- **Shared compute resources**: how are energy and computing resources allocated?
- **Collective safety**: individual AI safety decisions aggregate into collective safety or risk

### The free-rider problem in AI safety
AI safety is a public good: all benefit from safe AI, regardless of whether they invest in safety. The rational strategy for each developer: let others bear the safety costs while racing to deploy. This is the free-rider problem applied to AI development. Parfit's analysis says: each developer bears a share of responsibility for collective safety outcomes, even if their individual choices don't perceptibly affect overall safety.

### Imperceptible contributions and gradual harm
AI systems can cause gradual, imperceptible harm at scale:
- Each interaction slightly degrades epistemic norms (but imperceptibly)
- Each recommendation slightly narrows information diversity (but imperceptibly)
- Each automated decision slightly reduces human agency (but imperceptibly)
- Collectively, these imperceptible contributions may transform society

Parfit's framework says: reject the perceptibility threshold. Evaluate each AI action by its expected contribution to the collective outcome, not by its individual perceptibility.
