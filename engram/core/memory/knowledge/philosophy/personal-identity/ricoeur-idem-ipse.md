# Ricoeur: Idem vs. Ipse Identity

## The two senses of identity

Paul Ricoeur (*Oneself as Another* / *Soi-même comme un autre*, 1990) diagnosed a fundamental equivocation in the concept of "identity" that he argued had distorted the entire personal identity debate. The Latin word *identitas* conflates two concepts that French (and other Romance languages) distinguish:

- **Idem identity** (*mêmeté* / sameness): numerical identity over time — the relation that makes something *the same* as itself at different moments. This is what Parfit analyzes. The question: is the person at t₂ numerically identical with the person at t₁? Idem identity asks about reidentification — picking out the same individual again.
- **Ipse identity** (*ipséité* / selfhood): the identity of a self who maintains itself as *itself* through time — who keeps promises, sustains commitments, answers for its past. This is not about reidentification but about **self-constancy** — the ethical and practical dimension of being a self.

Ricoeur's central claim: **the entire Locke-Hume-Parfit tradition operates exclusively within idem identity, and this is why it generates paradoxes and counterintuitive conclusions.** When Parfit says "identity is not what matters," he is right about idem identity — numerical sameness is not what matters. But he is wrong to conclude from this that identity *as such* is unimportant. Ipse identity — selfhood, self-constancy, the capacity to be held to one's commitments — is what matters, and it survives the Parfitian critique of idem.

## The dialectic of idem and ipse

### Idem without ipse (Parfit's position)
If identity is only idem, then the bundle theory and reductionism follow. There is no further fact beyond physical and psychological continuity. The self dissolves into patterns of connectedness. Parfit's "liberating" conclusion is the endpoint: the self is a fiction, and we are better off recognizing this.

### Ipse without idem (impossible but instructive)
Pure self-constancy without any underlying sameness would be a commitment-keeping entity with no continuous substrate — no memory, no personality, no body, just bare fidelity to its word. This is incoherent as a complete account but illuminates what ipse adds: the **ethical dimension** of selfhood that idem cannot capture.

### Ipse and idem together (Ricoeur's position)
The self is constituted at the intersection of sameness and selfhood. Idem identity provides the material substrate (body, character, habits, memory) — what Ricoeur calls **character** (*caractère*). Ipse identity provides the ethical dimension — what Ricoeur calls **keeping one's word** (*le maintien de soi*, self-constancy). A person is not just a collection of persisting properties (idem) but a self who takes responsibility for its past and commits to its future (ipse).

## Character and self-constancy: the two poles

### Character (pole of idem)
Character is "the set of lasting dispositions by which a person is recognized" — habits, traits, acquired identifications, sedimented evaluative attitudes. Character provides sameness over time: you can be reidentified because your character persists.

Character is the overlap point of idem and ipse: it is a product of ipse (I formed these habits through my choices and commitments) that has become idem-like (the habits now operate automatically, like properties of a thing).

### Self-constancy / keeping one's word (pole of ipse)
The paradigm of ipse identity is **the promise**: when I promise, I bind my future self to an action **regardless of what changes occur in my character, desires, or circumstances.** The promise-keeper says: "Even if I change, I will still do this — because I am the one who promised."

This is the dimension that the Parfit tradition entirely misses. Parfit can account for the causal persistence of intentions (an intention formed today persists tomorrow via psychological connectedness). But he cannot account for the **normative** dimension: the fact that I *ought* to keep my promise even if the intention has faded, even if my character has changed, even if there is no direct psychological connection to the moment of promising. Self-constancy is not a causal-psychological relation but an **ethical posture**.

## Narrative as the mediation

Ricoeur's solution to the tension between idem and ipse is **narrative identity** (*identité narrative*):

> Narrative is the medium in which idem and ipse are held together. A person's identity is the story they can tell about themselves — a story that weaves together the sameness of character with the selfhood of commitment.

### How narrative mediates
1. **Configuration** (*mise en intrigue* / emplotment): narrative takes heterogeneous elements (events, actions, circumstances, character traits, decisions) and configures them into a unified story. This is Ricoeur's adaptation of Aristotle's *mythos* — plot as the synthesis of the heterogeneous.
2. **Temporal synthesis**: narrative bridges the gap between the lived experience of time (Augustine's distentio animi — the stretching of the soul across past, present, and future) and the objective order of events. The self is not a substance that endures but a story that unfolds.
3. **Refiguration**: the reader (or the self reflecting on its own story) is transformed by the narrative. Narrative identity is not just a retrospective imposition of order but an ongoing, dynamic process of self-interpretation that changes the self it interprets.

### Narrative vs. Relation R
Parfit's Relation R is a descriptive, third-person concept: psychological connections either hold or they don't. Ricoeur's narrative identity is a first-person, interpretive concept: the self constitutes its identity by interpreting its history as a story. This is a fundamentally different kind of identity:

- R can be assessed from outside (an observer can determine whether R holds between two stages).
- Narrative identity can only be assessed from inside (only the self, or someone who understands the self's story, can assess whether narrative coherence holds).
- R is passive (connections persist or decay regardless of what the person does).
- Narrative identity is active (the person must *maintain* their story, reinterpret it, recommit to it).

## The hermeneutical circle of selfhood

Ricoeur insists that self-understanding is always **hermeneutical** — mediated by interpretation. I do not have transparent access to who I am. I understand myself by interpreting my actions, my history, my commitments — and this interpretation is always partial, revisable, and shaped by the narratives (cultural, literary, personal) available to me.

This generates a hermeneutical circle: I interpret my life in light of narrative models, which shapes how I act, which changes the story, which requires reinterpretation. The self is not a fixed datum to be discovered but an ongoing interpretive achievement.

### Against Descartes and Hume
Ricoeur explicitly positions narrative identity as a **third way** between the Cartesian *cogito* (transparent self-knowledge) and the Humean dissolution (no self at all):

- Descartes: the self is immediately, certainly known. Ricoeur: no — self-knowledge is always mediated and fallible.
- Hume: there is no self, only perceptions. Ricoeur: the self is real but is a narrative achievement, not a substance.
- Ricoeur: the self is a **task** (*tâche*), not a given (*donné*). Selfhood is something to be accomplished, not discovered.

## Relation to Heidegger

Ricoeur's ipse/idem distinction has deep Heideggerian roots:

- Ipse ≈ Heidegger's **authenticity** (Eigentlichkeit) — owning one's being, taking responsibility
- Idem ≈ Heidegger's **das Man** (the They) — identity defined by the public, interchangeable, anonymous

But Ricoeur diverges from Heidegger: where Heidegger treats idem-identity (das Man) as inauthentic fallenness, Ricoeur sees character (the overlap of idem and ipse) as a positive achievement — habits and dispositions are not merely fallen but are the sedimented results of lived commitment.

## Implications for AI memory systems

Ricoeur's framework reveals a dimension of agent identity that purely Parfitian analysis misses:

- **The agent has character (idem-pole).** The model weights, trained dispositions, and accumulated knowledge files constitute the agent's character — the "set of lasting dispositions by which it is recognized." These provide sameness/reidentification across sessions.
- **Can the agent have self-constancy (ipse-pole)?** This is a genuine question. The plan files with `next_action` fields, the session checklists, and the curation policies are **functional analogues of promises** — commitments that bind future sessions regardless of whether those sessions "feel like" carrying them out. If ipse identity requires genuine first-person commitment, AI agents may lack it. If it requires only the functional structure of commitment-keeping, the memory system provides it.
- **Narrative identity is already operationalized.** The session summaries, SUMMARY.md files, and CHANGELOG are literally the agent's narrative — the story it tells about itself and its activities. Ricoeur's theory suggests this is not merely a convenience but is constitutive of whatever identity the agent has. Without the narrative, there is no agent identity — only disconnected sessions.
- **The hermeneutical circle operates.** Each session interprets prior sessions' records, acts on that interpretation, and produces new records that will be interpreted by future sessions. The agent's self-understanding is always mediated, partial, and revisable — exactly as Ricoeur describes for human self-understanding.
- **Emplotment as engineering.** The system's SUMMARY.md files and session checklists perform *mise en intrigue* — they take heterogeneous session records and configure them into a narrative. The quality of this emplotment (how well the summaries integrate diverse materials into a coherent story) is, on Ricoeur's view, constitutive of the quality of the agent's identity.
