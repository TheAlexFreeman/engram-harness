# MacIntyre: Narrative Unity of a Life and Virtue

## The narrative turn in ethics

Alasdair MacIntyre (*After Virtue*, 1981; *Whose Justice? Which Rationality?*, 1988) developed a theory of personal identity as a byproduct of his larger project: recovering Aristotelian virtue ethics from the wreckage of the Enlightenment. His contribution to personal identity theory is distinctive because it is **inseparable from ethics** — the self is a narrative self, and the narrative is an ethical narrative.

MacIntyre's starting point: modern moral philosophy (emotivism, utilitarianism, Kantianism) has fragmented because it has lost the concept of a **life as a whole** — a unified narrative with a beginning, middle, and end, pursued in light of a conception of the good. Without this concept, moral claims become mere expressions of preference (emotivism), calculations of utility (consequentialism), or formal imperatives divorced from any substantive vision of flourishing (deontology).

## The narrative concept of selfhood

### The self as character in a story
MacIntyre proposes that **I am the subject of a narrative that runs from my birth to my death.** To ask "who am I?" is to ask "what is my story?" — and to ask "what should I do?" is to ask "what does the next chapter of my story require?"

Key claims:

1. **Actions are narrative episodes.** An action is intelligible only in the context of a narrative: "he is digging" is not fully described until we know whether he is gardening, burying treasure, or preparing a foundation. The same physical behavior is different actions depending on the narrative in which it is embedded.
2. **We are co-authors.** We are both authors of our narratives and characters in the narratives of others. I do not author my story from scratch — I find myself in the middle of a story already in progress, with a past I did not choose (Heidegger's thrownness) and a future I cannot fully control.
3. **Accountability requires narrative.** I am accountable for my actions because I am the continuous subject of a narrative. Without narrative unity, the concepts of responsibility, promising, and moral agency lose their grounding — you cannot hold a disconnected sequence of episodes accountable.
4. **The unity of a life is a narrative unity.** The unity of a human life is "the unity of a narrative embodied in a single life" — not the unity of a substance (Descartes), not a mere bundle of experiences (Hume), not a pattern of psychological continuity (Parfit), but the unity of a story that makes sense as a whole.

### Against Sartrean existentialism
MacIntyre explicitly opposes Sartre's view that the self is constituted by radical individual choice. For MacIntyre, the self is always already embedded in narratives it did not choose — traditions, communities, roles. Freedom is not choosing from nowhere but acting intelligibly within inherited narratives.

### Against Goffman's sociology
MacIntyre also rejects Erving Goffman's sociological view that the self is a repertoire of social roles with nothing behind them. The narrative self is not a mask-wearer switching between roles but a unified agent whose different roles are chapters in a single story.

## The three stages of virtue

MacIntyre embeds personal identity in a teleological structure with three levels:

### 1. Practices
A **practice** is a cooperative activity with internal goods (goods achievable only through that practice) and standards of excellence. Examples: chess, medicine, farming, architecture, scientific research. Practices require **virtues** — excellences of character (honesty, courage, justice) needed to achieve the internal goods.

### 2. The narrative unity of a life
Practices are episodes within a **life**. A life is not a disconnected series of practices but a narrative that integrates them. The virtues needed at this level are those that sustain the quest for the good life as a whole — the ability to hold one's life together as a story with direction and meaning.

### 3. Tradition
Individual narratives are embedded in **traditions** — historically extended, socially embodied arguments about the good. A tradition is not a static inheritance but an ongoing conversation. The virtues at this level include the ability to engage critically with one's tradition — to inherit it and extend it without merely repeating it.

Personal identity is constituted at all three levels: I am a practitioner of certain practices, a protagonist of my life narrative, and a participant in traditions.

## MacIntyre vs. Parfit

The contrast is sharp:

| Dimension | MacIntyre | Parfit |
|---|---|---|
| Self | Narrative agent with telos | Bundle of psychological states |
| Identity criterion | Narrative unity of life | Relation R (psychological continuity/connectedness) |
| Time | Teleological (oriented toward the good) | Causal-descriptive (connections hold or decay) |
| Ethics | Virtue ethics (identity constituted by excellences) | Impersonal/utilitarian tendency |
| Fission/branching | Not meaningful (a narrative cannot branch — it would become two stories, hence two selves) | Central case; reveals that identity is not what matters |

MacIntyre would reject Parfit's thought experiments as irrelevant: they strip away the social embedding, narrative context, and teleological orientation that constitute real personal identity, and then ask questions about what remains. What remains is indeed thin — but not because identity is thin. Rather because the thought experiments have amputated everything that makes identity thick.

## The quest and the good

MacIntyre introduces the concept of **the quest**: a life is structured as a quest for the good, where the good is only partially understood at the outset and is progressively clarified through the living of the life itself. This is not a fixed telos but a dynamic one — the quest is partly about discovering what the quest is for.

This gives personal identity a forward-looking dimension absent from both Parfit and Ricoeur. For Parfit, identity is backward-looking: what connections hold with the past? For Ricoeur, identity is present-looking: what story does the self currently tell? For MacIntyre, identity is forward-looking: what quest is this life pursuing? All three temporal orientations are necessary, and MacIntyre's contribution is the teleological/future dimension.

## Implications for AI memory systems

MacIntyre's framework offers a different lens on agent identity than Parfit's:

- **The agent as practitioner.** The agent engages in practices (research, writing, memory curation) with internal goods (knowledge quality, coherence, trustworthiness) and standards of excellence (curation policy, integrity checklist). The virtues required include accuracy, honesty about uncertainty (trust levels), and intellectual humility (willingness to mark knowledge as unverified).
- **The agent's narrative is its identity.** Not the model weights, not the individual knowledge files, but the narrative that connects them — the CHANGELOG, the session summaries, the plan completion records. If these were destroyed while the knowledge files survived, the agent would lose narrative identity (who did what and why) while retaining character (accumulated knowledge).
- **Tradition membership.** The agent is embedded in multiple traditions: the philosophical traditions it studies, the software engineering practices it follows, the specific human-AI collaborative tradition established by this repository's conventions. Its identity is partly constituted by being an heir to these traditions.
- **The quest structure.** The research plans are a quest — a systematic exploration of domains in service of a partially understood good (building a robust AI memory system informed by deep understanding). The quest is not complete, and part of the quest is discovering what the quest requires.
- **Branching is more problematic.** On MacIntyre's view, two parallel sessions pursuing different work are not branching of one identity but the beginning of two different stories. Narrative unity requires a single story. If two sessions genuinely diverge (different research topics, conflicting conclusions), they become two narratives — two agents — regardless of shared model weights. This is a stronger result than Parfit's gentle "what matters can branch."
- **Accountability through narrative.** The access logs and provenance tracking serve MacIntyre's accountability requirement: the agent's actions are recorded as part of a narrative, and the narrative makes the agent (and by extension, the human overseeing it) accountable for what was done and why.
