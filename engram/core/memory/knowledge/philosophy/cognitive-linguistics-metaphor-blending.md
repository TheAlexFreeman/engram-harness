# Cognitive Linguistics: Metaphor, Blending, and Figurative Language

Deep dive into three foundational research programs in cognitive linguistics: George Lakoff & Mark Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT), Gilles Fauconnier & Mark Turner's Conceptual Blending Theory (CBT), and Eve Sweetser's work on polysemy, modality, viewpoint, and frame semantics. All three reject formal semantics' disembodied, truth-conditional approach and converge on the claim that meaning is constitutively grounded in embodied experience and structured by cognitive operations that are largely unconscious. Related files: `narrative-cognition.md`, `free-energy-autopoiesis-cybernetics.md`, `llm-vs-human-mind-comparative-analysis.md`.

## Part I: Lakoff & Johnson — Conceptual Metaphor Theory

### 1.1 The Core Thesis (*Metaphors We Live By*, 1980)

The central claim: **metaphor is primarily a cognitive phenomenon, not a literary or linguistic one.** Linguistic metaphors ("time is running out," "your argument has no foundation," "we're at a crossroads") are surface manifestations of underlying **conceptual metaphors** — systematic mappings between source and target domains that structure how we think, reason, and act.

Lakoff and Johnson's opening example: **ARGUMENT IS WAR**. We don't just talk about arguments using war language — we *experience* arguments as combat: we defend positions, attack weak points, destroy counterarguments, win and lose. The metaphor is constitutive of our experience, not just our vocabulary. If we cultivated a different metaphor — ARGUMENT IS A DANCE — we would experience and conduct arguments differently.

The implication is radical: **most abstract thought is metaphorical.** We cannot think about time, causation, mind, ideas, knowledge, relationships, or morality without drawing on systematic mappings from concrete, embodied source domains. This is not a limitation to be overcome but the basic architecture of conceptual cognition.

### 1.2 Primary Metaphors and Embodied Grounding (*Philosophy in the Flesh*, 1999)

By 1999, Lakoff and Johnson had substantially developed their account of *why* conceptual metaphors have the structure they do. The key move, incorporating Grady's (1997) work on **primary metaphors**:

**Primary metaphors** are the simplest, irreducible metaphorical mappings, and they are grounded in recurring correlations in bodily experience during development. They are not chosen or learned explicitly — they are acquired automatically as the nervous system builds associations between co-activated neural circuits.

Selected primary metaphors and their experiential grounding:


| Primary Metaphor          | Source Domain          | Target Domain     | Experiential Basis                          |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------- | ----------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| MORE IS UP                | Vertical space         | Quantity          | Piles get taller as more is added           |
| AFFECTION IS WARMTH       | Thermal sensation      | Social attachment | Being held by caregivers while warm         |
| KNOWING IS SEEING         | Vision                 | Epistemic states  | Perceiving the world to understand it       |
| IMPORTANT IS BIG          | Physical size          | Significance      | Large things command attention, have impact |
| PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS | Physical location      | Goals             | Reaching a place = achieving a goal         |
| DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS  | Physical weight        | Effort            | Carrying heavy loads while laboring         |
| INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS     | Physical proximity     | Social connection | Close physical presence with intimates      |
| TIME IS MOTION            | Physical movement      | Temporal flow     | Body moves through space as time passes     |
| THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS    | Constructed structures | Abstract systems  | "Foundation," "framework," "collapse"       |


**Complex metaphors** are built by composing primary metaphors. Example: A PURPOSEFUL LIFE IS A JOURNEY combines PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS + ACTIONS ARE SELF-PROPELLED MOTIONS + CAUSES ARE FORCES + DIFFICULTIES ARE IMPEDIMENTS TO MOTION — all primary.

The philosophical implication: the content of abstract reasoning is not a separate, purely formal domain but is *constituted* by these embodied mappings. There is no view from nowhere; all meaning is perspectival and grounded in the history of an embodied subject.

### 1.3 *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things* — Categories and Cognitive Semantics (1987)

Lakoff's 1987 book targets the classical theory of categorization (members share necessary and sufficient conditions, categories have crisp boundaries) and replaces it with a prototype-based, radially structured model.

**Prototype effects** (following Eleanor Rosch): category membership is graded. Robins are more "bird-like" than penguins; chairs are more "furniture-like" than lamps. Some members are *better examples* of a category than others, and people's judgments cluster around prototypical cases.

**Basic-level categories** are the cognitively privileged level of categorization — the level at which:

- Gestalt perception of shape is most distinct (you see a chair, not a piece of furniture or a Windsor armchair)
- Motor programs are most specific and unified (how you interact with a chair vs. a piece of furniture is more determinate)
- Mental imagery is richest and most detailed
- Category labels are acquired earliest in development
- Linguistic terms are shortest and most frequent

The basic level is intermediate — not too general (furniture), not too specific (Windsor armchair). It is the level where cognitive economy and informational richness are best balanced.

**Radial categories**: many categories are organized around a central prototype with extensions that form a structured network. The extensions are motivated (not arbitrary) but may not share all features of the prototype. Example: the English word "mother" has a central case (birth mother who raises the child, genetically related, married to the father) with radially organized extensions: adoptive mother, surrogate mother, birth mother (who gave child up), stepmother, foster mother. Each extends the central case by modifying one or two features. The category as a whole cannot be captured by a simple definition but is internally structured.

This framework has deep implications for how semantics, grammar, and lexical knowledge are organized — and for what it means for LLMs to "know" a concept.

### 1.4 Objections and Limitations

Lakoff and Johnson's framework has faced substantial criticism:

**The Pyrrhon critique (Rakova, Murphy, others):** The empirical evidence for conceptual metaphors as distinct cognitive structures (rather than lexical associations) is contested. Many studies testing "metaphorical activation" confound linguistic frequency with conceptual structure.

**The universality claim is overstated:** While primary metaphors based on basic embodied correlations likely are universal or near-universal, more complex conceptual metaphors show significant cross-linguistic variation (Kövecses has documented this extensively).

**The direction-of-fit problem:** Does metaphor *constitute* thought, or does it merely *express* it? The constitutive claim is harder to establish empirically than the linguistic claim.

**Asymmetry:** CMT explains one-directional mappings (source → target) well but struggles with cases where the mapping runs in both directions or where the "source" domain is itself abstract.

Despite these, the core architecture — that conceptual thought is organized through systematic mappings from embodied experience, and that these mappings are largely unconscious and culturally distributed — remains influential and has generated substantial empirical work.

## Part II: Fauconnier & Turner — Conceptual Blending Theory

### 2.1 Mental Spaces (Fauconnier, 1985)

Before blending, Fauconnier developed the theory of **mental spaces** — a framework for tracking referential and conceptual structures in discourse. Mental spaces are:

- Partial, temporary cognitive constructs built on-the-fly during thinking and speaking
- Structured by **frames** (background knowledge schemas) and populated with **elements** (the entities within the space)
- Built by **space builders**: linguistic expressions that open new viewpoint spaces (conditional "if," temporal "when," epistemic "suppose," quotative "Mary believes that...")
- Connected by **cross-space mappings** that link counterpart elements across spaces

Example: "In 1990, the president was George H.W. Bush" builds a time space with its own membership structure. "The queen of England is the richest woman in the world, but in the film she's a pauper" involves a "reality" space and a "film" space, with the same person (Elizabeth) appearing in both under different descriptions. Understanding requires cross-space mapping to track identity across spaces.

Mental spaces capture the dynamic, context-sensitive nature of meaning construction that static semantic theories miss. Pronouns, definite descriptions, presuppositions, and tense all require mental space machinery to analyze correctly.

### 2.2 Conceptual Integration Networks (*The Way We Think*, Fauconnier & Turner, 2002)

Blending theory extends mental spaces into a theory of **conceptual integration**: how humans combine conceptual structures in creative, flexible ways that generate emergent meaning neither input contains.

**The basic architecture** of a blending network:

- **Input Space 1** and **Input Space 2**: the contributing conceptual structures
- **Generic Space**: abstract structure shared by both inputs (the cross-space mapping is grounded here)
- **Blended Space**: inherits *selective* projections from both inputs; develops **emergent structure** that is new — not in either input

The emergent structure in the blend arises from three processes:

1. **Composition**: bringing elements from the inputs into new relations
2. **Completion**: pattern-completing against background knowledge and frames
3. **Elaboration** ("running the blend"): imaginatively simulating the blend, treating it as its own world with its own logic

### 2.3 Four Types of Integration Networks

Fauconnier and Turner classify networks by how the inputs relate:

**Simplex networks:** One input provides a frame with roles (e.g., AGENT, PATIENT, INSTRUMENT in a "buying" frame); the other provides the specific fillers. No real emergent structure — just role-value binding. Example: "Mary bought coffee" fills the BUYER, GOODS, SELLER roles.

**Mirror networks:** All spaces share the same organizing frame. Identity between roles across spaces is clear. Example: "The 1855 yacht race against the challenger" — both the historical race and an imagined contemporary race are organized by the same RACE frame; elements (boats, captains, course) are counterparts.

**Single-scope networks:** The two inputs have *different* organizing frames, and the blend inherits the frame from only *one* input — the other contributes elements but not its structure. Example: "The surgeon is a butcher" — the BUTCHER frame structures the blend; the SURGEON provides the human element (an actual person cutting flesh). The blend is understood through BUTCHER's frame (crude, indiscriminate cutting) applied to surgical contexts.

**Double-scope networks:** Both inputs contribute their organizing frames to the blend, and the blend has its own emergent frame combining structure from both. This is the most complex and cognitively demanding type, and Fauconnier and Turner argue it is *uniquely human* and responsible for the most creative cognitive products.

Example of double-scope blending: **"The desktop" metaphor in computing.** Input 1 is the physical desktop (objects, spatial arrangement, physical interaction). Input 2 is the computing system (files, processes, programs, commands). The blend has: desktop objects that *are* files (they look physical but behave computationally), operations that are physical (dragging, dropping, trashing) but have computational semantics. Neither input alone could generate this — the emergent structure requires both frames operating simultaneously.

Mathematical notation, grammatical constructions in natural language, the "Grim Reaper" (DEATH + HARVESTER → autonomous agent collecting souls), religious concepts combining divine and human frames — all are argued to involve double-scope blending.

### 2.4 Vital Relations and Compression

A central mechanism in blending is **compression** of **vital relations** — the basic conceptual relations that structure human experience:

- **Identity** (same entity across contexts)
- **Analogy** (structural similarity between situations)
- **Disanalogy** (structural contrast)
- **Cause-Effect** (causal dependency)
- **Time** (temporal sequence and duration)
- **Space** (spatial relation)
- **Change** (transformation of state)
- **Part-Whole** (mereological structure)
- **Representation** (standing-for relations: maps, models, icons)
- **Intentionality** (mental directedness: belief, desire, hope, fear)

Blending compresses diffuse, complex vital relations into **human-scale** representations. A long causal chain (evolution over millions of years) can be blended into a short narrative (the ancestral journey). Multiple analogy links between different cases can be compressed into a single prototype. This compression is what makes abstract reasoning manageable — it brings large-scale or abstract structures to the scale where human cognitive operations (pattern recognition, causal reasoning, goal-directed simulation) can grip them.

### 2.5 Optimality Principles

Blends are not unconstrained. Fauconnier and Turner specify several **optimality principles** that govern which blends are cognitively preferred:

- **Integration principle:** The blend should constitute a tightly integrated scene — "runnable" as a unified cognitive event.
- **Topology principle:** The blend should preserve the topological structure of its inputs (relations between elements should mirror those in the inputs as far as possible).
- **Web principle:** The blend should maintain recoverable connections to its inputs (you should be able to "unpack" the blend back to the inputs).
- **Unpacking principle:** The blend should be unpackable — it should be possible to reconstruct the inputs and the generic space from the blend alone.
- **Good reason principle:** All structure in the blend should have motivation — nothing arbitrary.
- **Metonymic tightening:** Where possible, compress metonymic chains (representation relations) into direct identity.

These principles often conflict, generating the creative tension that produces interesting blends.

### 2.6 Blending and Language

Blending is argued to be the cognitive basis of many grammatical constructions:

- **Counterfactuals** ("If Napoleon had won at Waterloo..."): Blend of reality space and a counterfactual input space, creating a world with its own internal logic
- **Caused-motion constructions** ("She sneezed the napkin off the table"): Blends SNEEZING and CAUSED-MOTION to create an event type for which no single word exists
- **Grammatical constructions as frames**: The ditransitive construction ("she gave him the book") carries its own meaning (X causes Y to receive Z) that is irreducible to the sum of its lexical parts
- **Metaphorical compounds** ("boat trip to heaven"): Double-scope blend of death-is-a-journey and a specific boat-trip frame

## Part III: Eve Sweetser — Polysemy, Modality, Viewpoint, and Frame Semantics

### 3.1 Background and Position

Eve Sweetser (UC Berkeley, PhD 1984) is one of the central figures of cognitive linguistics, working at the intersection of semantics, pragmatics, historical linguistics, and cognitive theory. Her signature contribution is showing that the **systematic ambiguity of natural language** (polysemy) is not arbitrary but reflects **principled metaphorical mappings** from physical-social domains into epistemic and speech-act domains.

### 3.2 *From Etymology to Pragmatics* (1990): Polysemy and Metaphorical Structure

The book's central claim: **lexical and grammatical polysemy is systematically structured by metaphorical mappings**, and the same mappings that produce synchronic polysemy are responsible for diachronic semantic change. Etymology is not just historical trivia — it reveals the cognitive architecture that makes systematic meaning extensions possible.

**The three-domain model:** Sweetser proposes that English (and likely other languages) structures meaning across three conceptual domains:

1. **The Content (Root/Deontic) Domain:** The physical and social world — objects, events, causal forces, social obligations and permissions
2. **The Epistemic Domain:** The domain of reasoning and inference — what we know, believe, infer, must conclude
3. **The Speech-Act Domain:** The domain of conversational interaction — what we are doing in saying something, what counts as an appropriate contribution to discourse

The key finding: **the same lexical items and grammatical constructions systematically appear in all three domains**, with meanings that are related but distinct. This polysemy is not accidental — it reflects a **metaphorical mapping** of the physical-causal structure of the content domain onto the epistemic and speech-act domains.

### 3.3 Modal Verbs: The Core Case

English modals (*can, may, must, will, shall, might, could, would, should*) have three reading types, systematically related:

**Root/Deontic readings** (content domain):

- "You *must* leave by 9" = social/physical compulsion, obligation
- "You *can* take the car" = permission, physical ability
- "You *may* enter" = granted permission

**Epistemic readings** (epistemic domain):

- "She *must* be home — the lights are on" = logical necessity given evidence
- "He *can't* be serious" = not epistemically possible
- "That *may* be true" = epistemic possibility

**Speech-act readings**:

- "I *must* say, that's impressive" = the speaker performs a speech act (asserting forcefully)
- "You *will* do this" = speaker's performative commitment/command
- "Can I help you?" (in service contexts) = performing an offer

**Sweetser's analysis:** The epistemic readings are metaphorical extensions from the root readings via the **MIND-AS-BODY** metaphor. Just as physical forces compel bodily movement, epistemic forces (evidence, logical entailment) compel mental movement (conclusion). "Must" in the epistemic sense = "the available evidence forces the conclusion." The same force-dynamic structure (from Talmy) that structures physical compulsion is mapped onto the domain of inference.

Historical corroboration: the epistemic senses are universally *later* developments in the history of languages. Old English had only deontic modals; epistemic readings emerged later through metaphorical extension. Etymology confirms the cognitive architecture.

**The same three-way split** holds for:

- **Conjunctions:** "because" (content causation: "She came because I called"), "because" (epistemic: "She must be home, because the lights are on"), "because" (speech-act: "I'm asking you to leave, because that's not appropriate here")
- **Conditionals:** "if" in content, epistemic, and speech-act contexts — "If it rains, we'll stay inside" / "If she's home, the lights will be on" / "If you need anything, there's a phone on the desk"
- **Perception verbs:** "see" (physical vision), "see" (comprehension: "I see what you mean"), "see" (epistemic inference: "I can see that you're upset")

The KNOWING IS SEEING primary metaphor underlies the polysemy of perception verbs — it's not lexical ambiguity but a single structured mapping extended productively across the domain.

### 3.4 Frame Semantics (Fillmore and Sweetser)

Frame semantics, primarily developed by Charles Fillmore (also at Berkeley), is a close companion to Sweetser's work. **Frames** are structured background knowledge schemas that organize how we understand events, entities, and situations.

Key frame-semantic ideas:

**Frames as prerequisites for meaning:** Words don't have meanings in isolation — they activate frames. "Buy" and "sell" describe the same event but from different perspectives, activating the COMMERCIAL TRANSACTION frame from different vantage points. Understanding "Wednesday" requires the WEEK frame; "orphan" requires the PARENTS frame.

**Frame inheritance and extension:** Frames are organized hierarchically (the MOTION frame has subframes: SELF-PROPELLED-MOTION, CAUSED-MOTION, etc.). Blending operates across frames.

**The FrameNet project** (Berkeley, Fillmore, and Sweetser's group): a large lexical database organized around semantic frames — each word's meanings are described by which frame(s) it evokes and which frame elements it profiles. This is a major empirical resource for testing frame-semantic predictions.

**Sweetser's extension:** Sweetser develops frame semantics in directions that connect to mental spaces and viewpoint — particularly in her analysis of conditionals, where different types of "if" clauses invoke different frames and establish different space-building operations.

### 3.5 Viewpoint, Subjectivity, and Construal

A major strand of Sweetser's later work (following Langacker's Cognitive Grammar) addresses **viewpoint and subjectivity** in language.

**Objective vs. subjective construal** (Langacker): The same situation can be construed from outside (objectively, as if observing from a distance) or from inside a perspective (subjectively, adopting a vantage point within the scene). Language encodes both:

- "The road goes through the mountains" (objective — the road as path)
- "We drove through the mountains" (objective — explicit mover)
- "As you drive along, the mountains rise on both sides" (subjective — experiencer's viewpoint implicit)

**Fictive motion:** Sentences like "The road runs along the coast" or "The fence goes from the barn to the tree" describe static configurations using motion predicates. Sweetser (following Langacker and Matsumoto) analyzes these as involving a subjective "mental scanning" — the conceptualizer's attention moves along the path, generating a fictive motion reading. This is only possible because language encodes viewpoint: the scanner is implicit (subjective), not an explicit agent in the scene.

**Subjectification as diachronic process:** Sweetser argues that semantic change frequently proceeds from more objective to more subjective readings — words that originally described external events come to describe internal states, speaker perspectives, or discourse operations. The modal shift from deontic to epistemic is one instance; many grammaticalization pathways follow the same vector.

**Mental spaces and viewpoint:** Sweetser collaborated with Fauconnier on *Spaces, Worlds, and Grammar* (1996) and with Barbara Dancygier on *Mental Spaces in Grammar* (2005). Her analysis of conditionals uses mental spaces to distinguish:

- **Predictive conditionals:** "If it rains, we'll stay in" — the if-clause establishes a hypothetical space, the consequent elaborates it
- **Counterfactuals:** "If it had rained, we'd have stayed in" — space is marked as contrary to reality
- **Generic conditionals:** "If you heat water to 100°C, it boils" — space is iterative/universal
- **Speech-act conditionals:** "If I may say so, that dress is lovely" — the if-clause does not hypothesize about content but about the appropriateness of the speech act

These distinctions are only capturable with mental spaces machinery, not with classical truth-conditional semantics.

### 3.6 Gesture and Multimodal Communication

Sweetser has increasingly focused on **co-speech gesture** as a window into meaning construction. Key findings:

- Gesture is not mere accompaniment — it carries semantic content that is co-expressive with speech but often distinct (speakers gesture meanings they don't verbalize)
- Metaphorical gestures enact image schemas (a hand moving forward for time, a hand pushing down for negation/suppression)
- Viewpoint in gesture: speakers gesture from their own body-as-deictic-center (egocentric) or from an observer position; these choices interact with linguistic viewpoint choices
- Blending is enacted in gesture: speakers can "be" both narrator and character simultaneously through gesture, embodying the blend

This work grounds cognitive linguistics in multimodal behavior — meaning is not just in words or internal representations but distributed across the full semiotic performance of communication.

## Part IV: Relations Among the Three Frameworks and Implications

### 4.1 How CMT, Blending, and Sweetser's Work Relate

These are complementary but distinct theories, each handling phenomena the others don't fully capture:

**CMT** (Lakoff/Johnson) is primarily about **stable, conventional metaphorical mappings** between domains — the background conceptual architecture that structures thought. It explains systematic patterns in how we talk about time, argument, causation, mind, etc., and grounds these in embodied experience. It does not handle dynamic, creative, on-the-fly meaning construction well.

**Blending** (Fauconnier/Turner) handles **dynamic, creative conceptual integration** — how we combine multiple inputs in real-time to generate novel meaning that neither input contains. It is more general than CMT (metaphors are a special case of single-scope blending) and handles creativity, novel constructions, counterfactuals, and humor. But it is more complex and harder to operationalize empirically.

**Sweetser's work** bridges the two: her **polysemy analysis** shows how CMT-style mappings (MIND-AS-BODY, CAUSATION IS FORCE) create systematic meaning extensions across lexical and grammatical items; her **mental spaces and viewpoint work** shows how speakers track multiple conceptual structures simultaneously in discourse. She provides the bridge between lexical semantics (stable meanings) and discourse dynamics (real-time meaning construction).

All three are united by:

- Rejection of formal semantics' truth-conditional, disembodied approach
- Commitment to embodied grounding (directly for CMT; indirectly for blending via the origins of frames in bodily experience)
- Recognition that most meaning is non-compositional in the classical sense — frames, metaphors, and blends introduce constraints that aren't derivable from parts plus logical combination rules

### 4.2 Frame Semantics as Foundation

Underlying all three programs is **frame-semantic** organization of knowledge. Frames are the cultural-cognitive packages that words activate. Metaphorical mappings are mappings across frames. Mental spaces are frame instantiations. Blending combines frames.

This means that the question "does a system understand language?" is, in part, the question "does it have frames?" — structured, interconnected packages of background knowledge that constrain inference and determine what counts as a coherent extension or violation of expectations.

### 4.3 Implications for LLMs and AI

These frameworks raise pointed questions about LLM linguistic competence:

**Primary metaphors and grounding:** LLMs have learned the distributional patterns of primary-metaphor language (the verbal surface). They know that "up" co-occurs with positive evaluation, "warm" with social closeness, "see" with comprehension. But the theory predicts these patterns are grounded in sensorimotor co-activations in development — LLMs have the correlations without the causal architecture that generated them. Whether the correlations alone are sufficient for something like conceptual metaphor is an open empirical question.

**Radial categories and prototype effects:** LLMs appear to exhibit prototype effects in their behavior (they treat typical members of categories differently from atypical ones, consistent with human patterns). But it's unclear whether this reflects genuine category structure or distributional frequency of typical members in training data. The two predictions can be pulled apart experimentally, but this work is ongoing.

**Blending and creativity:** Double-scope blending (the source of human creative language) requires holding two frames with *conflicting topology* simultaneously and finding an emergent integration. This is cognitively demanding for humans and may be systematically challenging for LLMs. Evidence suggests LLMs can retrieve and recombine — which handles simplex and mirror blending — but may struggle with genuinely novel double-scope integrations. Novel metaphors, creative analogies in new domains, and productive grammatical constructions in unusual contexts may reveal the boundary.

**Sweetser's three domains:** LLMs exhibit the distributional signatures of modal polysemy (they use "must" in deontic and epistemic contexts correctly by corpus statistics) but may fail to track the principled structure Sweetser identifies. Probe studies suggest LLMs sometimes conflate deontic and epistemic readings in ambiguous contexts, where humans reliably disambiguate using contextual frame constraints.

**Viewpoint and subjectivity:** Tracking whose perspective is active, shifting between subjective and objective construals, and maintaining viewpoint coherence across discourse is a genuine challenge for LLMs. They perform well on simple perspective-taking but degrade with nested or conflicting viewpoint structures — consistent with lacking the embodied point-of-view that grounds subjective construal.

**Frame evocation without frame understanding:** Perhaps the deepest issue. LLMs can evoke appropriate frames (the word "buy" activates COMMERCIAL TRANSACTION-appropriate inferences) at a statistical level. But frames in Fillmore/Sweetser's sense are not just lists of associated words — they are structured schemas with role relations, enabling conditions, and logical consequences. Whether LLMs have genuinely structured frames or merely frame-correlated distributional patterns is the central empirical question for assessing their semantic competence.

### 4.4 Connections to the Dynamical Systems Framework

These cognitive linguistic frameworks connect to the broader research project in several ways:

**Compression is universal.** Blending theory's central mechanism — compression of diffuse vital relations into human-scale representations — is a cognitive instance of the general information-theoretic compression that appears throughout the dynamical systems literature (AIT, Kolmogorov, Friston's free energy). Meaning construction is a form of lossy compression that preserves actionable structure.

**Frames as attractors.** From a dynamical perspective, frames function like attractor basins: once a frame is activated by a lexical item, the cognitive system settles into a structured configuration that constrains inference, expectation, and interpretation. Frame "clashes" (what Fauconnier/Turner call topology conflicts in double-scope blends) are the cognitive equivalent of a system being pulled toward two incompatible attractors simultaneously — the creative tension that generates emergent structure.

**Metaphor as structural coupling.** Lakoff/Johnson's embodied grounding maps naturally onto Maturana/Varela's structural coupling: the sensorimotor system and the conceptual system are structurally coupled over development, such that the same neural patterns that encode "up" for spatial movement also encode "up" for positive valence. The coupling is literally neural (co-activation builds associative links), consistent with Hebb's rule.

**Viewpoint and the event loop.** Sweetser's subjective construal — the implicit presence of an embodied perspective as the organizing center of meaning — is the linguistic encoding of what the narrative cognition file calls the "event loop": the organism's continuous somatic self-positioning as the reference point for all perception, action, and inference. Objective construal (stepping outside the perspective) is a cognitive achievement built on top of this default-subjective ground.
