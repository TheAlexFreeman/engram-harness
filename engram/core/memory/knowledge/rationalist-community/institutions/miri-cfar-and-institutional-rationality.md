# MIRI, CFAR, and Institutional Rationality

## 1. Why Institutions Matter for the Rationalist Story

The LessWrong community is often discussed primarily as a set of ideas — Bayesian epistemology, AI risk, heuristics-and-biases debiasing — and of texts — the Sequences, Slate Star Codex, early LessWrong posts. But ideas without institutions tend to dissipate. The rationalist community persisted, grew, and reproduced itself in part because it developed organizational structures: a suite of nonprofits that employed community members, attracted funding, and gave the community a professional infrastructure for pursuing its core projects.

The two most important institutions are the Machine Intelligence Research Institute (MIRI) and the Center for Applied Rationality (CFAR). They represent distinct but related projects: MIRI is primarily a technical AI alignment research organization; CFAR is a workshop and training organization focused on applied epistemic practice. Understanding both — what they actually do, how they relate to the broader community, where they have succeeded and where they have been criticized — is essential for understanding the community as a social formation rather than just an intellectual tendency.

## 2. From Singularity Institute to MIRI: Organizational History

MIRI's predecessor, the Singularity Institute for Artificial Intelligence (SIAI), was founded in 2000 by Eliezer Yudkowsky, Brian Atkins, and Sabine Atkins. The founding context was Yudkowsky's central intellectual project: the alignment problem — the challenge of ensuring that a recursively self-improving artificial general intelligence would reliably pursue human values rather than misaligned goals. SIAI was established to pursue this project organizationally, providing Yudkowsky with a nonprofit structure, fundraising infrastructure, and over time a small staff of researchers and assistants.

The early SIAI operated almost entirely on the intellectual authority of Yudkowsky and, to a lesser extent, the small group of researchers he collaborated with. The organization was not embedded in academic computer science, had no tenured faculty, produced essentially no peer-reviewed publications in its early years, and relied on a fundraising model based primarily on small donations from aligned individuals. This gave SIAI a distinctive character: high intellectual ambition, explicit rejection of the academic incentive structure (which was judged as poorly calibrated toward the most important long-term problems), and a community-facing communication style that was bloggy, argumentative, and aimed at general intelligent audiences rather than specialist peer reviewers.

By the late 2000s, the intellectual case for AI risk was becoming more widely known. The publication of Nick Bostrom's *Global Catastrophic Risks* (2008), the growing LessWrong community, and the increasing sophistication of AI capabilities (though still far from AGI) were drawing more serious attention from philosophical and policy audiences. Peter Thiel's Founders Fund made a significant contribution to SIAI in 2010, providing the organization with enough capital to professionalize and expand.

In 2013, SIAI renamed itself the Machine Intelligence Research Institute. The name change was more than rebranding: it signaled a shift in organizational identity. The "Singularity Institute" name was associated with transhumanist futurism and the Singularity concept that Yudkowsky himself had moved away from as too hand-wavy. "Machine Intelligence Research Institute" framed the organization's project in the language of technical research rather than speculative futurism. MIRI began, gradually, to produce more technical research — formal papers on decision theory, agent foundations, logical uncertainty, and related topics — and to present this work in contexts legible to professional AI researchers and philosophers.

The shift was contested within the community. Critics argued that MIRI's research agenda remained too disconnected from the actual practice of machine learning — that work on formal agent foundations and Bayesian decision theory was only loosely connected to the kinds of neural network systems actually generating rapid capability gains. Supporters argued that the foundational theoretical work was necessary precisely because the field's empirical progress had outrun its conceptual foundations, and that building the right conceptual framework now would be essential for safety work at higher capability levels. This debate has continued through the 2010s and into the present; it reflects a genuine uncertainty about the relationship between theoretical and empirical approaches to AI safety.

MIRI's research focus under Yudkowsky and later under Nate Soares (who became executive director in 2014) has emphasized what the organization calls "agent foundations" — formal theories of rational agency, logical uncertainty, and decision theory. Representative outputs include technical papers on Löbian obstacles to self-modification, on the properties of logical inductors, on decision theories for agents playing game-theoretic scenarios, and on what properties an ideal reasoner would have. These are genuine technical contributions, though their relevance to practical AI safety is disputed among researchers in the field.

## 3. The Center for Applied Rationality: Teaching Rationality as a Craft

CFAR was founded in 2012 by Anna Salamon, Julia Galef, Andrew Critch, and other community members. Its founding premise was distinct from MIRI's: where MIRI was focused on the technical research problem of AI alignment, CFAR was focused on the human cognitive problem of how to actually be more rational. The founding question was not "how do we build safe AI?" but "how do we help people become the kind of thinkers who can work on the most important problems?"

CFAR's model was the intensive residential workshop: a multi-day program combining instruction, practice, and peer discussion aimed at giving participants tools for improved decision-making, emotional processing, and epistemic practice. Early workshops drew on multiple sources:

**Rationalist community concepts.** The Sequences, heuristics-and-biases research, and LessWrong's canon of epistemic tools provided the intellectual framework. CFAR workshops taught concepts like reference class forecasting, Fermi estimation, and the distinction between object-level and meta-level thinking.

**Applied behavioral science.** Evidence-based techniques from cognitive behavioral therapy, motivational interviewing, and behavioral economics informed specific exercises. CFAR was not a therapy program, but it drew on the same literature that informed therapeutic practice — the idea that systematic cognitive patterns can be identified, practiced, and modified.

**CIRCLING, Internal Family Systems, and introspective techniques.** CFAR workshops came to incorporate techniques from the Human Potential Movement and adjacent traditions — practices for identifying and working with emotional and motivational patterns that could interfere with clear thinking. This represented a significant expansion beyond the rationalist canon's epistemology-first framework, toward something more like an integrative cognitive-emotional training.

The residential workshop format was chosen for reasons that the community has discussed extensively: intensive practice with immediate feedback, in a social environment where norms are temporarily suspended, is more likely to produce durable behavioral change than occasional reading or discussion. This is plausible as a pedagogical claim — it is the logic behind intensive language immersion or military training — but it also creates particular social dynamics. Residential workshops are high-context environments where participants form strong temporary bonds, share personal information in exercises, and experience social pressure to "get" the content. These dynamics can be beneficial for learning, but they can also produce groupthink, uncritical acceptance, or social pressure to report experiences as more transformative than they were.

## 4. What CFAR Actually Taught and Whether It Worked

The content of CFAR workshops evolved significantly between 2012 and the late 2010s, and the organization has been cautious about publicizing detailed curricula, partly because the content was considered proprietary and partly because ongoing experimentation meant it was always in flux. From public descriptions, participant accounts, and academic reviews, the core CFAR curriculum included:

- **Goal factoring**: Breaking down high-level goals into specific actions and examining whether current behavior actually serves stated goals
- **Murphyjitsu / Pre-mortems**: Anticipating ways plans can fail before executing them; stress-testing predictions
- **TAPs (Trigger-Action Plans)**: Behavioral specifications linking specific environmental cues to specific actions, aimed at making desired behaviors more automatic
- **Againstness**: Examining the emotional and motivational sources of resistance to specific changes or decisions
- **Propagation of evidence**: Formal exercises in Bayesian updating, designed to build intuitive calibration
- **Noticing**: Mindfulness-adjacent practices for registering emotional and cognitive states as they occur

The empirical question — did this work? — is genuinely difficult to answer. CFAR conducted some internal outcome tracking and commissioned external evaluations, but the results have not been widely published, and the methodological challenges are significant: self-reported changes in decision quality are hard to validate; selection effects are strong (people who attend CFAR workshops are self-selected for interest in rationality training, motivation to improve, and ability to pay); and the relevant outcomes (whether participants go on to do effective work on important problems) are hard to observe on any reasonable timescale.

Community accounts are mixed. Many CFAR alumni report the workshops as genuinely valuable — both for specific cognitive tools and for the social connections formed during intensive residential programs. Some report less durable effects than they expected, with initial enthusiasm fading after re-entry into ordinary social and professional environments. Critical accounts note that the workshop culture can create strong in-group identity that is difficult to distinguish from genuine cognitive improvement, and that some practices (particularly the more introspective or emotional exercises) were borrowed from traditions with weak empirical track records.

## 5. The Division of Labor: MIRI, CFAR, LessWrong, and Later Organizations

By the mid-2010s, the rationalist community had developed a semi-informal division of labor among its institutions:

**LessWrong** remained the primary public intellectual forum for the broad community — the place where ideas were developed, debated, and disseminated, where newcomers encountered the canon, and where community norms were elaborated through meta-discussion. Its readership was far larger than any individual institution's, but it was loosely organized and had no formal membership or mission.

**MIRI** was the primary technical AI alignment research organization, focused on mathematical foundations of agency, decision theory, and logical uncertainty. Its budget was supported primarily by major donors from the EA and tech philanthropist networks. By the late 2010s, it employed roughly 20–30 people, some with strong academic credentials in mathematics, computer science, and philosophy, others from the LessWrong community without formal research credentials.

**CFAR** was the primary applied-rationality training organization, focused on the human cognitive substrate — the belief that improving the reasoning of the people working on important problems was an important indirect contribution to those problems' eventual solution. Its revenue came primarily from workshop tuition and donations.

**The Alignment Forum** (launched 2018) was a more technical spinoff of LessWrong focused exclusively on AI alignment research: a platform for the community of researchers working on AI safety who wanted a discussion space with higher signal-to-noise than general LessWrong but more accessible than academic CS venues. The Alignment Forum quickly became the primary online venue for AI safety research discussion, with participation from MIRI researchers, academic AI safety researchers at universities, and researchers at Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and other organizations.

**Open Philanthropy / GiveWell** (adjacent, not part of core rationalist structure) — Dustin Moskovitz and Cari Tuna's philanthropic vehicle began as closely associated with effective altruism, itself closely related to the rationalist community, and became one of the primary funding sources for both MIRI and CFAR. The relationship between rationalist institutions and EA funding structures is complex: EA is a broader movement with significant overlap with rationalism but different emphasis (EA focuses on effectiveness of charitable giving and cause prioritization; rationalism focuses more on epistemic practice), and the funding relationships create accountability structures external to the rationalist community itself.

## 6. Criticisms of Both Institutions

MIRI and CFAR have attracted substantive criticisms that go beyond outside dismissal and include voices from within the broader community.

**Criticisms of MIRI:**

- The agent foundations research agenda is too disconnected from actual ML practice to be relevant to the safety problems that real AI systems pose. Critics including several prominent academic AI safety researchers have argued that the most important AI safety problems are engineering and empirical problems about current and near-future systems, and that theoretical work on ideal Bayesian agents and logical induction is too abstract to bear on them.
- MIRI's rejection of the academic publication and peer-review system, justified on grounds that peer review is poorly calibrated for important heterodox work, makes it harder to assess the quality of its research and produces a form of epistemic closure. Research that isn't subject to peer review may have higher variance in quality.
- The organization has absorbed talent and funding that might otherwise have gone to academic AI safety work that is more legibly connected to real AI systems.

**Criticisms of CFAR:**

- The workshop model has not produced documented improvements in the epistemic performance of participants measured in any rigorous way. Without such evidence, CFAR's resource consumption is hard to justify against alternative uses of the same funding and talent.
- The intensive residential format, combined with the social dynamics of a tight in-group community, can produce cult-like effects — not in the pejorative sense of manipulation, but in the descriptive sense of strong in-group pressure to report positive outcomes and align with community consensus.
- Some CFAR exercises, particularly the more explicitly emotional or introspective ones, are borrowed from traditions (est, Landmark Forum, IFS) with mixed or weak empirical support, which sits uncomfortably with the community's stated commitment to evidence-based practice.
- The community has noted with some concern that CFAR's workshop culture emphasized AI risk specifically as the primary important cause, which complicated the question of whether CFAR was teaching general rationality skills or recruiting for a specific ideological position on AI.

## 7. Mutual Reinforcement and the Problem of Self-Contained Communities

One structural feature of the MIRI/CFAR ecosystem deserves particular attention: the way the two organizations, combined with the broader LessWrong community and the EA funding network, created a relatively self-contained social world.

Participants moved among these contexts — reading LessWrong, attending CFAR workshops, working at MIRI, donating to EA causes, living in rationalist community houses — and found their professional, social, and intellectual lives all embedded in the same community. This created genuine positive feedback loops: people found high-quality intellectual community, were able to work on problems they found genuinely important, and built durable personal relationships within the community. It also created risks: a tight social world where intellectual and social rewards were distributed by community consensus, professional opportunities were concentrated in community organizations, and departure meant not just disagreement but social disruption.

These dynamics are not unique to the rationalist community — they characterize any tight intellectual community from academia to religious orders. The question is whether the community's epistemic norms are strong enough to resist the pressures that come with tight social integration. The rationalist community's stated norms — commitment to calibrated beliefs, willingness to update on evidence, explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty — are precisely the norms that would, if followed strictly, resist social pressure. Whether they actually do so, in the real social conditions of tight community life, is a question the community has discussed extensively and without entirely satisfying resolution.

## Summary

MIRI (formerly the Singularity Institute) is the community's primary technical AI safety research organization, focused on mathematical foundations of agency through an agent foundations agenda that remains controversial for its distance from practical ML. CFAR is the community's applied rationality training organization, using intensive residential workshops to build cognitive skills in people working on important problems; its empirical track record is uncertain. Together with LessWrong (the intellectual forum), the Alignment Forum (technical AI safety discussion), and the EA funding ecosystem, these organizations gave the rationalist community a professional infrastructure that allowed it to persist and attract resources. The division of labor among them is semi-formal and reflects different theories of what matters most: technical foundations (MIRI), human cognitive substrate (CFAR), and public intellectual culture (LessWrong/Alignment Forum). Each organization has attracted substantive criticisms regarding research agenda, pedagogy, and the social dynamics of tight community life.
