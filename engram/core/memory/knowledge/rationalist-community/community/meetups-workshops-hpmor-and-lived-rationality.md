# Meetups, Workshops, HPMOR, and Lived Rationality

*Coverage: The offline and social infrastructure of the rationalist community — meetups and group houses, CFAR-style workshops, forecasting communities, self-experimentation networks, and HPMOR as recruitment and identity text. ~3000 words. ## 1. From Online Community to Offline World

One of the most sociologically striking features of the rationalist community is the degree to which it reproduced itself in physical space. Most online communities remain primarily online; their members meet occasionally at conferences, form loose professional networks, but do not reorganize their housing, social lives, and professional choices around the community. The rationalist community did, to an unusual extent, make this transition — creating a set of offline social infrastructures that turned a discussion forum into a recognizable social world with geography, institutions, and lived practices.

This transition happened through several mechanisms: meetups that evolved from occasional gatherings into regular community anchors; group houses where community members lived together; CFAR workshops that created intense temporary community experiences with durable social bonds; and the concentration of the tech industry in the Bay Area, which made it possible for many community members to relocate to a shared geography without sacrificing professional opportunity. The result was something relatively unusual in the history of internet-originated communities: a community that maintained both a rich online presence and a significant, geographically concentrated offline life.

Understanding how rationality became a "lived" practice — not just a set of ideas to be applied internally but a set of social practices to be worked out in community — is central to understanding what distinguishes the rationalist community from an ordinary intellectual interest group.

## 2. The Meetup Culture

LessWrong meetups began in 2007–2008, roughly coinciding with the peak of the Overcoming Bias community and the early LessWrong launch. They were initially small and informal: a few readers who discovered they lived in the same city would arrange to meet for coffee or in someone's living room, typically organized through a LessWrong thread or a shared location-based notification system that the site developed for this purpose.

The geography of early meetups reflected the community's demographic concentration: the largest meetup scenes were in the San Francisco Bay Area, Boston, New York, and London, with smaller scenes in other tech-heavy cities and university towns. The Bay Area dominance was driven by the concentration of tech industry workers in the community, the proximity of MIRI and CFAR (both eventually based in the East Bay), and the general culture of the Bay Area tech scene, which was already unusually hospitable to heterodox ideas about technology, futurism, and the long-term future.

What did meetups actually do? The answer varied significantly: some meetups were primarily social, providing a setting for community members to meet face-to-face without any specific intellectual agenda; others were structured around reading discussions, paper presentations, or applied rationality exercises; others were organized around specific community projects or adjacent concerns (effective giving, AI safety, forecasting). The variation reflected the diversity within the rationalist community itself — different participants came for different reasons, valued different aspects of the community, and wanted different things from in-person contact.

The social dynamics of rationalist meetups were notable in several respects. The community was highly unusually frank about personal beliefs and practices by ordinary social standards — people discussing cryonics subscriptions, polyphasic sleep experiments, relationship structures, and medication self-experimentation in ways that would be unusual in ordinary social settings. This frankness was partly programmatic (the community valued epistemic honesty highly, and this extended to personal disclosure) and partly a result of self-selection (people comfortable with unusual beliefs self-selected into a community that tolerated them).

The tendency toward social homogeneity was also notable. Rationalist meetups were, and largely remain, disproportionately male, white, and technically educated. This demographic skew was the subject of considerable internal discussion — the community's stated commitment to epistemic virtue should, in principle, make it unusually hospitable to diverse perspectives, but in practice the social culture replicated familiar patterns of technical intellectual communities. The reasons are complex: self-selection (the pipeline runs through tech communities that are already skewed), social comfort effects (dense in-group jargon makes entry harder for people without shared background), and specific cultural patterns (the preference for extremely direct argument can be experienced differently across social groups).

## 3. Group Houses

The rationalist group house — a shared living arrangement among community members — became a significant social institution in the Bay Area and, to a lesser extent, other major hubs. The model varied: some houses were explicitly organized around community projects (residents working at MIRI or CFAR, coordinating on AI safety research); others were more loosely organized as social spaces where rationalist-affiliated individuals happened to live in proximity.

The rationale for group houses was argued from multiple directions. Practically, they provided affordable housing in expensive cities to people doing underpaid nonprofit work. Socially, they provided the dense community of like-minded people that isolated individuals in unconventional careers often lack. Epistemically, they offered something more unusual: an environment in which community norms were active around the clock, where the people you saw at breakfast were the same people who would challenge your reasoning in a community thread, and where being honest about uncertainty was not just a writing practice but a feature of ordinary conversation.

This last rationale was controversial within the community. The idea that living closely with people who shared your epistemic norms would reinforce good epistemic practice seems plausible, but critics noted the opposite risk: that intense co-residence with ideologically aligned people reinforces ideological alignment rather than epistemic independence, creates social pressure to reach community consensus, and makes disagreement personally costly in ways that are hard to recover from. When your housemates, co-workers, and social circle all overlap, the social cost of maintaining unpopular views within that community becomes very high.

The range of experiences reported by people who lived in rationalist group houses suggests that both effects were real: many report intellectually stimulating, personally supportive environments that helped them develop and refine their thinking; others report the social pressure effects more strongly and describe them as epistemically and personally damaging. As with most complex social environments, the quality of specific houses varied enormously with the personalities and norms of specific residents.

## 4. Forecasting and Prediction Markets: Applied Epistemic Practice

One of the most distinctive rationalist applied practices is systematic forecasting — making explicit probabilistic predictions about empirical outcomes, tracking accuracy over time, and using the track record to calibrate future judgments. This practice connects directly to the community's Bayesian epistemology: if you claim to believe X with 70% confidence, and you track outcomes over many such predictions, you should find that outcomes matching your 70% predictions actually occur about 70% of the time. Divergences reveal systematic biases.

The community's engagement with forecasting has taken multiple forms:

**Prediction Book** was an early community platform (launching around 2009) that allowed users to make explicit probabilistic predictions and track their accuracy. Community members used it to make predictions about AI timelines, geopolitical events, economic conditions, and personal decisions. The platform made calibration visible and, over time, identifiable: users with track records of being well-calibrated gained community credibility, while users with systematically overconfident or underconfident track records were identifiable as poorly calibrated.

**Metaculus** and **Manifold Markets** (the latter a prediction market platform with LessWrong community crossover) provided additional platforms for probabilistic forecasting. The community's AI-timeline forecasting on Metaculus has been particularly extensive and reveals interesting patterns: aggregate community forecasts about AI capability benchmarks have consistently revised toward shorter timelines as actual AI development accelerated, raising questions about whether the forecasting community was tracking good evidence or anchoring on existing community consensus.

**Superforecasting concepts** from Philip Tetlock's research (which showed that a small group of unusually accurate forecasters could be reliably identified and trained) were widely read in the community. Tetlock's *Superforecasting* (2015) was considered an important substantive contribution, and the associated Good Judgment Project became a community reference point for evidence-based thinking about prediction accuracy.

The forecasting culture reflects something important about how the rationalist community tried to turn its epistemic commitments into lived practice. Simply claiming to have calibrated beliefs is not enough; the claim can be tested, and the community developed the infrastructure to test it. This is one of the more genuinely distinctive features of rationalist practice — a willingness to operationalize epistemic claims in ways that could produce disconfirming evidence.

## 5. Self-Experimentation and the Quantified Self

Self-experimentation — the practice of systematically tracking personal behavior and physiology, running personal experiments on interventions, and drawing conclusions from individual data — was a related practice that developed within and adjacent to the rationalist community.

The intellectual motivation connects to the same themes: if you value empirical evidence, and if aggregate evidence from population studies is often not applicable to your individual circumstances, then generating systematic evidence about your own responses to interventions has genuine epistemic value. Gwern's exhaustive self-experimentation (documented on gwern.net) was the extreme case of this logic taken seriously; less rigorously, the broader community engaged extensively with questions of nootropics (cognitive supplements with varying evidence bases), sleep optimization, diet and metabolic interventions, and productivity tracking.

The community overlap with the broader "quantified self" movement (tracking wearable data, sleep metrics, etc.) was significant but imperfect — the rationalist community engaged with the empirical claims more rigorously than most quantified-self culture, attending more to effect sizes, confounds, and whether individual N=1 data could actually support specific conclusions. The community also brought a distinctive skepticism about pharmaceutical and nutritional interventions — testing claims rather than accepting them at face value, but also treating plausible evidence more seriously than mainstream medical conservatism suggested.

The self-experimentation culture had genuine value when conducted rigorously (generating real individual-level data, acknowledging limitations, not mistaking personal results for population-level evidence) and characteristic failure modes when conducted sloppily (confusing anecdote with data, underestimating placebo effects, overgeneralizing from one person's experience). The distribution of practice within the community covered the full range from serious to credulous.

## 6. HPMOR: Recruitment, Identity Text, and Bridge

*Harry Potter and the Methods of Rationality* (HPMOR) is a fan fiction novel by Eliezer Yudkowsky, published serially from 2010 to 2015, in which Harry Potter is reimagined as a rationalist prodigy who applies scientific methodology and Bayesian reasoning to the magical world of Hogwarts. The work is explicitly pedagogical — it was designed to teach rationalist concepts through narrative — but it became something that exceeded its pedagogical function.

HPMOR's significance for the community is threefold:

**As a recruitment text.** HPMOR reached audiences that would never have encountered the Sequences directly. Harry Potter fandom was and remains one of the largest fiction fan communities in the world; many HPMOR readers came to the work through fan fiction channels, with no prior exposure to LessWrong or rationalist ideas. A significant fraction of them went on to encounter the broader community through HPMOR — discovering Yudkowsky's non-fiction writing, joining LessWrong, attending meetups. By this path, HPMOR functioned as one of the community's major onramps.

**As an identity text.** For people already in the community, HPMOR served a different function: it was a narrative rendering of the community's core values that could be shared, discussed, and used to identify fellow community members. Quoting HPMOR, discussing its solutions to specific problems, appreciating its particular jokes and references — these were community markers in the way that quoting the Sequences was a community marker for more theoretically inclined participants. The work created a shared cultural experience that bridged people who came to the community through different routes.

**As a bridge to fiction fandom.** There is a significant overlap between rationalist community membership and engagement with particular fiction communities — science fiction fandom, mathematically inclined fan fiction communities, and specific online reading cultures. HPMOR both drew on and reinforced this overlap, creating pathways between the rationalist community and adjacent communities who found its central concerns — AI, mind, rationality, existential risk — compelling when rendered in narrative form.

The critical reception of HPMOR is worth noting. Literary critics who encountered it tended to find its prose functional rather than distinguished, its philosophical asides pedagogically heavy-handed, and its protagonist unrealistically perfect in ways that reflected authorial projection. Community members generally acknowledged these criticisms while maintaining that the work's pedagogical value outweighed its literary limitations. The gap between internal community appreciation and external critical assessment of HPMOR was itself revealing: the community had developed aesthetic preferences calibrated to its own values that were not universally shared.

## 7. Which Practices Are Central, Which Peripheral, Which Stereotypes

From the outside, the rationalist community is often characterized by a set of practices that range from genuinely central to peripheral to largely mythological:

**Genuinely central practices:**
- Explicit probabilistic reasoning and calibration tracking
- Citing primary sources and engaging with evidence directly
- Using community vocabulary (map/territory, noticing confusion, epistemic status) as shared shorthand
- Attending meetups and maintaining community social relationships
- Reading LessWrong, SSC/ACX, and related blogs

**Peripheral but real practices:**
- Living in group houses (common in Bay Area, unusual elsewhere)
- Self-experimentation with nootropics or biohacking
- Cryonics subscriptions (a significant minority of core community members, not a majority)
- Polyamorous relationship structures (common in some community niches, not universal)

**Outsider stereotypes with minimal grounding:**
- Unusual diet practices (paleo, fasting) as rationality-driven — real but not community-defining
- Lack of social skills or "weird nerd" affect — common stereotype, not representative
- Universal strong belief in specific AI timelines — more diversity than the stereotype suggests

The discrepancy between internal practice and external perception is partly deliberate and partly a function of what makes good stories. Outsiders encounter the most extreme or colorful community practices through journalistic profiles, which seek the unusual; insiders experience the community through its ordinary daily texture, which is mostly arguing on the internet and occasionally attending meetups.

## Summary

The rationalist community developed a significant offline and social infrastructure: meetups (especially in Bay Area, Boston, New York, and London), group houses, CFAR workshops, forecasting communities, and self-experimentation networks. These practices turned rationality training from a set of ideas into a lived social practice, embedded in the ordinary texture of participants' lives. HPMOR functioned as a recruitment and identity text, reaching audiences outside the core LessWrong readership and providing a shared cultural experience accessible to both technically inclined participants and fiction readers. The practices that are genuinely central (forecasting, citing evidence, community social engagement) differ significantly from the outsider stereotypes (unusual diets, cryonics, social dysfunction). The offline social world created genuine community value — intellectual density, personal support, shared projects — while also creating the risks of any tight in-group: conformity pressure, high exit costs, and epistemic closure.
