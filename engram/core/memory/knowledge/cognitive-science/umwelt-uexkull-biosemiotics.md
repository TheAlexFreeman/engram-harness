# Umwelt: Jakob von Uexküll and the Species-Specific World

## Overview

**Umwelt** (German: *surrounding world*) is the central concept of Baltic-German biologist Jakob von Uexküll (1864–1944). It designates the species-specific subjective world that each organism inhabits — the particular slice of reality that an organism's sensory and motor apparatus makes available to it. The umwelt is not a subjective illusion overlaid on an objective world; it *is* the organism's world, constituted through the functional relationship between organism and environment. No two species share exactly the same umwelt, and the umwelt of any species is radically impoverished relative to the total physical environment.

Uexküll's work established **biosemiotics** as a research tradition — the study of sign processes in all living systems — and influenced Gestalt psychology, existential phenomenology (Heidegger), ecological psychology (Gibson), and contemporary philosophy of biology (Agamben, Thompson). His most accessible work, *A Foray into the Worlds of Animals and Humans* (1934), remains one of the most beautiful works in the literature at the intersection of biology and philosophy.

## Historical Context

### Uexküll's Position

Uexküll was trained as a physiologist (under Wilhelm Kühne and Julius von Sachs) but became increasingly dissatisfied with the mechanistic, behaviorist approach to animal life that dominated his era. The mechanists (following Descartes and later Jacques Loeb's tropism theory) treated animals as stimulus-response machines: input a stimulus, out comes a reflex. For Uexküll, this missed everything that was interesting about biology.

His alternative: animals are *subjects*, not machines. Each organism has a perspective — a point of view organized by its own sensory and motor structure — from which it encounters a world meaningful to it. The meaning is not projected onto a neutral world but is constitutive of the organism's world as such.

### Intellectual Position

Uexküll was simultaneously:
- **Anti-mechanist**: Animals are subjects with their own worlds, not stimulus-response machines
- **Anti-Darwinist** (in a specific sense): Evolution is not a blind process of random variation and selection — there is a *form* (plan, *Bauplan*) to animal organization that constrains what kinds of umwelten are possible
- **Anti-realist** (in the Kantian sense): The umwelt is not a copy of the physical world; it is constituted through the organism's cognitive-motor apparatus, much as Kant thought human experience is constituted through the categories of the understanding

## The Functional Circle (Funktionskreis)

The core mechanism of the umwelt is the **functional circle** (Funktionskreis) — Uexküll's model of how organisms interact with their environments through sign-mediated loops.

### Structure of the Functional Circle

```
                    SUBJECT (organism)
                    /                \
          Perception organ      Effect organ
               (sense)              (motor)
                    \                /
               Perception sign   Effect sign
                    \                /
              OBJECT (feature of environment)
                    ←   Meaning carrier   →
```

Each object in the environment functions as a **meaning carrier** (Bedeutungsträger) that has two functional faces:
- A **perception tone** (Merkmal/Merkton): the feature of the object that the organism's sense organs detect
- An **effect tone** (Wirkmal/Wirkton): the feature of the object that the organism's motor organs act upon

The object mediates between sensing and acting. When the organism detects the perception tone, this triggers action on the effect tone, which modifies the object, which changes the perception tone, closing the circle.

### Example: The Tick

Uexküll's most celebrated example is the tick (*Ixodes ricinus*), which he uses to demonstrate the radical impoverishment of a simple umwelt. The tick is blind, deaf, and nearly insensitive to most environmental stimuli. Its entire umwelt consists of exactly **three meaning carriers**:

1. **Butyric acid** — emitted from the sebaceous follicles of all mammals. The tick, lurking on a branch, detects this odor and drops.
2. **Warmth (37°C)** — the temperature of mammalian blood. On landing on a warm-blooded animal, the tick orients toward the warmth.
3. **Hairiness** — the tactile texture of mammalian skin. The tick finds a hairless patch and bores in.

That is all. The tick can wait up to 18 years for a mammal host without eating, sustained only by its species-specific umwelt organized around these three sign-carriers. The rich world of a summer forest — birdsong, dappled light, insect hum — is *not* part of the tick's world at all. It is simply not there.

This example makes vivid what Uexküll is claiming: the organism's world is not a subset of the physicist's world, with some features attended to and others ignored. The organism's world is *constituted* differently. What is not a sign-carrier for the organism simply does not exist *as* anything for it.

## Merkwelt and Wirkwelt

The umwelt has two complementary sides:

- **Merkwelt** (perception world): The totality of perception signs available to the organism — the features of the environment that the organism can detect. The richness and structure of the Merkwelt determines what the organism can *notice*.

- **Wirkwelt** (effect world): The totality of effect signs — the features of the environment on which the organism's motor capacities act. The Wirkwelt determines what the organism can *do*.

The Merkwelt and Wirkwelt are linked through the functional circle: what the organism perceives it acts on, and what it acts on modifies what it perceives.

### Meaning (Bedeutung)

For Uexküll, objects in the environment have **meaning** (Bedeutung) for an organism to the extent that they function as sign-carriers in its functional circles. Meaning is not a psychological projection — it is a functional-relational property. A stone has the meaning "seat" for an exhausted hiker; the same stone has the meaning "prey-sensor" for a spider that has spun a web against it; for a crab, it has the meaning "tool" (carried for protection). *The stone does not change; its meaning changes because the functional circles of different organisms are different.*

This functional theory of meaning anticipates both Gibson's affordances and Peirce's semiotics, though Uexküll developed it through biological observation rather than philosophical analysis.

## Umwelt and Heidegger

Heidegger engaged directly with Uexküll in his lecture course *The Fundamental Concepts of Metaphysics* (1929–30). He adopted the umwelt concept but transformed it:

- For Heidegger, the animal is "poor in world" (weltarm) compared to Dasein (human being). The animal is *captivated* (benommen) by its ring of disinhibitors — those environmental features that elicit its instinctive responses. It cannot step back from its umwelt and thematize it.
- Dasein, by contrast, is "world-forming" (weltbildend): it does not merely inhabit an umwelt but *constitutes* a world through understanding, discourse, and care.
- Objects in the human world are not merely meaning-carriers in a functional circle; they are *entities that are* — they have being that Dasein can understand and question.

Heidegger's use of Uexküll is controversial: critics argue that he anthropocentrically devalues the animal's world rather than taking it seriously on its own terms. The biosemiotic tradition (Sebeok, Kull, Favareau) generally reads Uexküll non-hierarchically — each umwelt is equally complete as a world for its inhabitants. The question of whether there are real *grades* of world-richness between organisms (amoeba < tick < dog < human) is unresolved.

## Umwelt and Merleau-Ponty

Merleau-Ponty drew on Uexküll extensively in *The Structure of Behavior* (1942) and in his lectures at the Collège de France. He found in Uexküll's functional circle a biological correlate of his phenomenological account of the organism as an intentional, meaning-constituting subject:

- The functional circle is a *motor intentionality* at the level of the organism: the organism reaches out to the environment through its sensorimotor apparatus, and the environment answers back through the functional tones.
- The umwelt is the *lived body's* world — the world organized around what the body can perceive and do, not the world as described by physics.

Merleau-Ponty's synthesis: the organism's world (Uexküll) and the human body's world (his phenomenology) are not fundamentally different kinds of thing — they are points on a continuum of embodied world-constitution. This breaks down Husserl's hard distinction between nature (subject to mechanical causality) and mind (subject to intentional constitution): *all* living organisms are subject-bodies that constitute their worlds through functional intentionality.

## Biosemiotics: The Legacy

Uexküll's work directly founded **biosemiotics** — the study of sign processes in all living systems. The key figures in this tradition:

### Thomas Sebeok (1920–2001)

Sebeok, an American semiotician, was the most important transmitter of Uexküll's ideas into contemporary scholarship. He coined the term **zoosemiotics** (the study of animal sign use) and worked to integrate Uexküll's umwelt theory with Peircean semiotics. For Sebeok, every organism is a sign-user — even unicellular organisms "interpret" their environments by differentiating between nutritive and toxic gradients. The semiotic threshold (the minimum conditions for sign use) coincides with the threshold of life itself.

### Kalevi Kull (b. 1952)

Estonian biosemiotician Kull has done the most to give Uexküll's concept formal precision. He distinguishes:
- **Umwelt** (subjective world, species-specific)
- **Umgebung** (physical surroundings — Uexküll's term for the objective environment)
- **Welt** (world in the full sense — only applicable to humans, following Heidegger)

Kull's work establishes the connection between umwelt theory and contemporary philosophy of biology, ecology, and cognitive science.

### Jesper Hoffmeyer (1942–2019)

Hoffmeyer extended biosemiotics into molecular biology in *Signs of Meaning in the Universe* (1993). He argued that semiosis — sign interpretation — is present at the cellular level: cells "interpret" molecular signals rather than merely reacting to them chemically. The genetic code is itself a semiotic system (icon-index-symbol structure, in Peircean terms). This radical extension of umwelt theory dissolves the boundary between biochemistry and meaning.

### Donald Favareau

Favareau has written the most comprehensive history of biosemiotics (*Essential Readings in Biosemiotics*, 2010), tracing the tradition from Uexküll through Sebeok and arguing that biosemiotics provides the foundational framework for a naturalistic theory of meaning — one that neither reduces meaning to physics nor mysteriously elevates it above nature.

## Umwelt vs. Gibsonian Affordances: Comparison

| Dimension | Umwelt (Uexküll) | Affordances (Gibson) |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Primary vocabulary** | Sign-carriers, meaning, functional circles, semiosis | Affordances, optic array, direct perception, effectivities |
| **Theoretical tradition** | Biosemiotics, phenomenology, biology | Ecological psychology, perception science |
| **Approach** | First-person (subjective world of organism) | Third-person (ecological physics, information in optic array) |
| **What is perceived** | Signs (meaningful features that trigger functional responses) | Affordances (action possibilities directly specified) |
| **Role of meaning** | Constitutive — meaning is what distinguishes a world from mere surroundings | Implicit — affordances are "meaningful" in that they specify action, but Gibson avoids mentalistic language |
| **Novelty** | Objects become new meaning-carriers through learning and development | Novel affordances are perceived through exploration and skill development |
| **Human distinctiveness** | Heidegger uses umwelt to distinguish animal captivation from human world-formation | Humans and animals differ in the richness of their affordance landscapes, not in kind |

The umwelt and the affordance landscape are complementary: the umwelt is the *semiotic* description of the organism's world (what carries meaning), while the affordance landscape is the *ecological* description (what actions are possible). Both reject the idea that the organism inhabits the physicist's world; both insist that the organism's world is constituted by the organism-environment relation.

## Contemporary Relevance

### Philosophy of Mind

The umwelt concept poses a direct challenge to functionalism: if the mental is defined purely by functional organization, then two systems with different umwelten but similar functional organization (e.g., a human and a bat navigating by echolocation) should have similar experiences. But Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" argument suggests otherwise: the bat's sonar experience is *constitutively tied* to the bat's umwelt — its species-specific sensorimotor coupling with the world. Functions can be multiply realized; umwelten cannot. (See `cognitive-science/species-specific-reality-consciousness.md`.)

### Cognitive Science

Umwelt theory aligns with enactivism (Varela, Thompson, Rosch) in insisting that cognition is embedded in organism-specific structures of world-constitution. The "enacted world" of enactivism is the umwelt understood through the framework of autopoiesis and structural coupling. The convergence is not accidental: Uexküll was a major source for Merleau-Ponty, who was a major source for Varela et al.

### AI and Machine Cognition

Does an AI system have an umwelt? In a formal sense, yes: a language model has a "perception world" (the token-distribution space it processes) and an "effect world" (the token sequences it generates). Its "functional circles" are the training-feedback loops that tuned its weights to resonate with certain patterns. But the biological sense of umwelt involves autonomous, metabolically grounded, species-specific world-constitution — a perspective from which things *matter* to the organism's own viability. Without metabolic stakes, autonomous agency, and embodied sensorimotor coupling, an AI system's "umwelt" is a functional analogy, not the full biosemiotic concept.
