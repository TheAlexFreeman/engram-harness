# Biosemiotics: Peirce, Sebeok, and the Sign Basis of Life

## Overview

**Biosemiotics** is the study of sign processes in living systems — the view that semiosis (sign interpretation, meaning-making) is not a uniquely human or cultural phenomenon but a property of *all* life. Where classical semiotics (Saussure, structural linguistics) studied sign systems in human language and culture, biosemiotics extends the inquiry downward through the animal kingdom, through plant signaling, and into cellular and molecular biology. The founding claim: *wherever there is life, there is interpretation of signs*.

Biosemiotics synthesizes two foundational traditions:
1. **Peircean semiotics**: A general theory of signs that is not confined to language and that applies to any interpretive system
2. **Uexküll's umwelt theory**: A biological account of species-specific worlds constituted through organism-environment sign relations

The discipline was consolidated by Thomas Sebeok in the late twentieth century and has been developed by Kalevi Kull, Jesper Hoffmeyer, Claus Emmeche, and others into a systematic challenge to both purely mechanistic biology and purely cultural theories of meaning.

## Peircean Semiotics: The Theoretical Foundation

### Charles Sanders Peirce (1839–1914)

Peirce developed the most comprehensive theory of signs in the Western tradition. Unlike Saussure's dyadic sign (signifier + signified), Peirce's **triadic sign** involves three elements:

1. **Sign** (Representamen): The vehicle — anything that stands for something else. A word, a gesture, a symptom, a footprint.
2. **Object**: What the sign stands for. Can be actual (a particular apple) or general (the species *Malus domestica*).
3. **Interpretant**: The effect the sign produces — not simply "the interpreter" but the *meaning produced* in any system capable of responding to the sign appropriately. An interpretant can be another sign, a habit of action, or a feeling.

### The Triadic Structure and Its Importance

The triadic structure is crucial for biosemiotics because it explains how signs can function *without a conscious human interpreter*. The interpretant is not necessarily a thought; it can be a reflex, a metabolic change, a growth response, a behavioral disposition. If a cell's membrane protein "interprets" a molecule by changing its conformation and initiating a cascade, that is semiosis in Peirce's sense.

This breaks the anthropocentric grip on the concept of meaning: *meaning arises wherever there is an interpretive system* — a system that differentiates signs, connects them to objects, and responds with interpretants.

### Peirce's Sign Taxonomy

Peirce distinguished signs by the relation between sign and object:

| Sign type | Relation to Object | Examples |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|
| **Icon** | Resemblance — the sign shares properties with its object | Photograph, diagram, onomatopoeia, protein shape as template |
| **Index** | Contiguity — the sign is causally or existentially connected to its object | Smoke → fire; symptom → disease; footprint → animal; pointing gesture |
| **Symbol** | Convention — the sign stands for its object by rule or habit, not resemblance or causal connection | Words, mathematical notation, flags |

Icons and indices are natural (their sign relation is grounded in natural properties); symbols are conventional (their sign relation depends on shared interpretation habits). Crucially, *icons and indices are available to all organisms*, not just language users. A bacterium detecting a sugar gradient and orienting toward it is reading an *index*: the gradient is causally connected to the sugar source, and the bacterium's chemotaxis is the interpretant.

## The Semiotic Threshold

### Eco's Problem

Umberto Eco formulated the **semiotic threshold** problem: where in nature does genuine sign use begin? The concern: if we apply semiotic concepts too broadly (any causal relation = semiosis), we lose the concept's distinctive content. A thermostat "responds" to temperature; does it interpret a sign?

### Biosemiotic Answers

Different biosemioticians draw the threshold differently:

**Hoffmeyer and Emmeche**: The threshold coincides with *life itself*. What distinguishes living from non-living is precisely the capacity for sign interpretation: a living cell relates to its environment through *signs* (molecules that are interpreted, not merely caused to react), while a crystal's response to its environment is purely mechanical. The cell "cares" about its environment in a minimal sense — it interprets signals relative to its own self-maintenance.

**Kull**: The threshold requires *code duality* — a system that has both a digital code (discrete, combinatorial, like the genetic code) and an analog process (continuous, physical, like the metabolic dynamics that express the code). Only systems with code duality can have genuine semiosis; thermostats and crystals lack this.

**Barbieri**: The threshold is at the origin of the **organic codes** — the mapping systems (like the genetic code) that mediate between sequences of nucleotides and sequences of amino acids. These codes are arbitrary in the sense that the mapping is not determined by chemistry alone; it requires a specific molecular "codemaker." Where there are organic codes, there is semiosis.

The biosemiotic consensus: genuine semiosis begins at the cellular level. This means that *all life* — including bacteria, archaea, plants, fungi, and animals — is a sign-using system. The differences between these are differences of semiotic complexity, not the presence or absence of semiosis.

## Thomas Sebeok: Zoosemiotics and the Globalization of Semiotics

### Sebeok's Project

Thomas Sebeok (1920–2001) was the pre-eminent architect of biosemiotics as an academic discipline. He worked across linguistics, anthropology, and biology to make the case that:

1. **Communication is not a uniquely human phenomenon**: Animals communicate through complex sign systems — not just behaviorally but structurally. Animal communication is not a degenerate form of human language but a diverse set of semiotic systems each adapted to the organisms' umwelten.

2. **The study of animal sign use (zoosemiotics)** is a legitimate scientific field, continuous with both Uexküll's biology and Peircean semiotics.

3. **The life sciences need semiotics**: Biology without a theory of signs is incomplete. The organism does not merely undergo chemical reactions; it *interprets* its environment through sign-mediated processes. Semiotics without biology is culturally parochial.

### Sebeok on Uexküll

Sebeok was the primary figure who integrated Uexküll's umwelt theory into Peircean semiotics. He saw Uexküll's functional circle as a semiotic circuit: the meaning-carrier is the sign, the organism's detection is the interpretant formation, and the object is what the sign stands for. The umwelt is the totality of signs available to an organism — its *semiosphere* (borrowing and adapting Lotman's term for the culturally constituted sign-sphere of human life).

For Sebeok, the Uexküllian umwelt establishes that every organism lives in a *semiotic niche* — a species-specific envelope of sign relations that constitutes its world.

## Hoffmeyer: Signs of Meaning in the Universe

Jesper Hoffmeyer's *Signs of Meaning in the Universe* (1993) is the most ambitious attempt to show that semiosis is not just a feature of animal life but of the entire history of biological evolution.

### The Semiotic Scaffold

Hoffmeyer argues that evolution can be understood as the progressive elaboration of **semiotic scaffolding** — the development of sign systems that allow organisms to coordinate their activities at greater temporal and spatial distances:

- **Chemical signs** (molecules, gradients): The earliest and most universal. Bacteria use chemical gradients for navigation, communication, and quorum sensing.
- **Cellular sign systems** (second messenger cascades, receptor-ligand binding): Allow cells to integrate signals from multiple sources and produce coordinated responses.
- **Nervous systems**: Allow sign processing to be decoupled from immediate chemical proximity — signs can travel at the speed of nerve impulses rather than diffusion.
- **Brains**: Allow signs to be stored, combined, and projected into the future through memory and anticipation.
- **Language**: Allow signs to refer to absent objects, hypothetical states, past and future, and to self-refer (meta-semiosis).

Each level of scaffolding *increases the semiotic freedom* of the organism: its ability to respond to signs with a greater variety of interpretants, to generate its own signs, and to transcend the immediate here-and-now.

### The Genetic Code as Semiotic System

Hoffmeyer (and independently Barbieri) argues that the genetic code is a *genuine* code — not a metaphor. The relationship between codons (DNA triplets) and amino acids is:
- **Arbitrary**: There is no chemical necessity linking a particular codon to a particular amino acid. Different organisms could in principle use different codes (and some do — there are minor variations in the genetic code across organisms).
- **Conventional**: The mapping is maintained by the molecular machinery (tRNA, ribosomes) that functions as the "codemaker."
- **Semiotic**: The codon *stands for* the amino acid; the tRNA and ribosome system forms the interpretant.

This makes DNA sequence a *symbol* in Peirce's sense — a sign whose relation to its object is conventional, not iconic or indexical. Life, from its very beginning, is linguistic in structure.

## Kalevi Kull: Semiosis and Biological Diversity

Kull has contributed the most systematic theoretical framework for biosemiotics, integrating:

- **Lotman's semiosphere**: Kull argues that the biosphere is simultaneously a semiosphere — the totality of sign processes on Earth coincides with the totality of life.
- **Code duality** as the criterion for life: The distinction between genotype and phenotype, between the digital code (DNA) and the analog process (metabolism and development), is what makes life semiotic. Code duality is the biological form of the sign relation.
- **Umwelt diversity as semiotic diversity**: The richness of the biosphere is not just biological diversity (morphological forms, genetic variation) but *semiotic diversity* — the variety of umwelten, the variety of ways in which organisms interpret and constitute their worlds.

Kull's concept of **semiotic freedom** — the range of possible interpretants a system can generate for a given sign — provides a formal measure of cognitive complexity. A bacterium has low semiotic freedom (stereotyped chemotactic responses); a human has high semiotic freedom (open-ended symbolic interpretation and self-reference).

## Plant Semiotics

One of the most radical extensions of biosemiotics is its application to plants. Classical biology treats plants as purely mechanical systems — phototropism, gravitropism, and chemical signaling are explained without recourse to semiotic concepts. Biosemiotics challenges this:

- Plants communicate with each other through volatile chemical signals (terpenes, ethylene). A damaged plant releases signals that neighboring plants "interpret" by upregulating their defenses before being damaged themselves.
- Plants communicate with animals through flowers (color, shape, and scent as signs addressed to pollinators) and fruits (color, smell, taste as signs addressed to seed dispersers).
- The Arabidopsis plant integrates multiple environmental signals (light quality, day length, temperature, touch, gravity) and generates coordinated responses. This integration looks like a rudimentary form of multi-modal sign interpretation.

The biosemiotic claim: these processes are genuinely semiotic — they involve signs (the chemical/optical stimuli), objects (the environmental states the signs indicate), and interpretants (the plant's responses). They are not fully mechanistic if the criterion for semiosis is the triadic sign relation rather than consciousness.

## Biosemiotics and Cognitive Science

### The Semiotic Mind

Biosemiotics provides an alternative framework to both computationalism and enactivism for understanding mind:

- **Computationalism**: Mind is symbol manipulation — the brain computes over internal representations. *Biosemiotic critique*: Symbols are already signs; computation presupposes semiosis; the computational theory of mind takes for granted what needs explaining — the sign relation itself.
- **Enactivism**: Cognition is enacted through organism-environment coupling, not representation. *Biosemiotic supplement*: Enactivism explains the sensorimotor dynamics of cognition; biosemiotics explains the *semiotic structure* of those dynamics — why some environmental features become signs for the organism and what it means to interpret a sign rather than merely react to a stimulus.

The biosemiotic account: cognition is the elaboration of semiotic capacities that begin at the cellular level and become increasingly complex through evolutionary and ontogenetic development. Mind is not a special substance or a computational process but a *semiotic process* — the interpretation of signs by systems with increasingly rich semiotic freedom.

### Connection to Relevance Realization

Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization (see `cognitive-science/relevance-realization/`) can be read as a cognitive-scientific account of how semiotic processes are organized at the psychological level: relevance realization determines which environmental features become signs for an agent — which aspects of the Umgebung (physical surroundings) become part of the umwelt (the agent's meaningful world). The relevance problem is the problem of sign selection: what, out of the infinite environmental flux, becomes a sign?
