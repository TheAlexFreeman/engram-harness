# McGilchrist: Academic Reception and Critiques

## Overview

McGilchrist's work has received a deeply split reception: enormous popular enthusiasm, genuine appreciation from a range of scholars and intellectuals, and pointed skepticism from neuroscientists and philosophers who find the inferential leaps from the neuroscience to the cultural-historical and metaphysical claims difficult to justify. This file documents both the appreciation and the critiques, and provides McGilchrist's responses where available.

## Academic and Neuroscientific Critiques

The most concentrated academic critique appeared in a dedicated special issue of *Religion, Brain & Behavior* (Vol. 9, No. 4, 2019), edited by specialists, with multiple papers and McGilchrist's responses.

---

### Critique 1: Selective Citation and Inferential Overreach

**Source:** Multiple contributors to the 2019 *Religion, Brain & Behavior* special issue

**Argument:** McGilchrist bases his characterization of hemispheric attentional differences primarily on the visuospatial attention literature — a body of research addressing one sensory modality (vision) in experimental settings. He then generalizes, without systematic additional evidence, to "supramodal" or "amodal" hemispheric modes of being — entire *ways of engaging with the world*.

This is a very large inferential leap:
- From: "The right hemisphere has preferential roles in sustained visual vigilance and broad spatial monitoring"
- To: "The right hemisphere grasps the whole of reality as living, relational, embodied, and value-laden"

The move involves several unsupported steps: (a) generalizing from visuospatial to all modalities; (b) generalizing from experimental task performance to existential *mode of being*; (c) generalizing from statistical group differences in laboratory tasks to a description of what each hemisphere "wants" or "values."

**Assessment:** This is probably the strongest methodological criticism. The empirical base for the claim about attentional modes is genuine but limited; the extension to a comprehensive hemispheric "worldview" is speculative.

---

### Critique 2: The Homunculus Fallacy

**Source:** Dr. Haan (2019 special issue); also raised by various philosophers

**Argument:** McGilchrist's framework commits the **homunculus fallacy** — it explains human psychological characteristics (preferences, worldviews, motivations, the desire for power) by attributing those same characteristics to a neural structure (a brain hemisphere).

To say "the left hemisphere wants power," "the left hemisphere is self-interested," or "the left hemisphere prefers its own narrative" is to attribute *intentional properties* — the very properties one is trying to explain — to the explanatory mechanism. This does not explain psychological characteristics; it merely relocates them to a smaller entity inside the person.

**McGilchrist's implicit response:** McGilchrist is aware of this objection and qualifies his language in various ways. His view is that the hemispheres do have something like functional orientations or biases — not literal desires, but systematic dispositions. The homunculus framing is partly metaphorical; the underlying claim is about differential neural weighting of processing modes.

**Assessment:** A genuine logical problem, especially in the cultural-historical chapters of *MHE* where the hemispheres are described in quasi-agentive terms. The criticism is less damaging if McGilchrist is read charitably as making claims about *functional tendencies* rather than literal motivations.

---

### Critique 3: Generalization from Pathological to Normal (Chris McManus)

**Source:** Chris McManus, neuroscientist and psychologist, author of *Right Hand, Left Hand* (2002, Aventis Prize winner)

**Argument:** A substantial portion of McGilchrist's clinical evidence comes from:
- **Split-brain patients** (callosotomy for intractable epilepsy) — an extreme surgical condition that does not characterize normal brain function
- **Stroke patients** with unilateral hemisphere damage — an acute pathological condition
- **Psychiatric patients** with schizophrenia or related conditions

Generalizing from pathological conditions to normal cognition is methodologically problematic. The fact that certain capacities are lateralized when the normal corpus callosum is severed, or when a hemisphere is damaged, does not entail that those capacities are strictly hemispheric in the normal integrated brain. The normal, intact brain operates as a highly integrated system; the severe dissociations visible in split-brain or stroke patients may not reflect the ordinary balance of processing.

**Assessment:** A legitimate methodological concern. McGilchrist acknowledges that pathological cases are *windows* into hemispheric difference, not direct descriptions of normal cognition. The question is whether they reveal latent structures that are genuinely hemispheric, or artifacts of pathological disconnection.

---

### Critique 4: The Self-Contradictory Method Problem

**Source:** Kenan Malik; Raymond Tallis (philosopher and neuroscientist); several blog critiques

**Argument:** McGilchrist's cultural-historical thesis in Part Two of *MHE* — the sweeping analysis of Ancient Greece through postmodernism — is itself highly systematizing, requires massive quantities of carefully accumulated historical data, is presented as explicit argument, and draws on rationalist inference to reach its conclusions. In other words: the method of the thesis contradicts its content. If the LH is the systematizing, categorizing, explicit-reasoning hemisphere, then constructing a sweeping systematic argument for LH dominance looks suspiciously LH.

**More sharply:** Raymond Tallis observed that the *form* of McGilchrist's argument is "rather left hemisphere" — precisely the mode he is criticizing. A genuinely RH argument would not take the form of a lengthy, heavily footnoted, systematic scholarly treatise.

**McGilchrist's response:** He has acknowledged this tension and offers two responses:
1. The LH is necessary for *communicating* insights that originate in the RH — you need the Emissary to transmit the Master's wisdom
2. The cultural-historical argument uses LH tools in the service of a RH understanding — the argument is about LH pathology, not a product of it

**Assessment:** The tension is real and philosophically interesting. It doesn't refute the thesis but does raise legitimate questions about the limits of self-referential critique.

---

### Critique 5: The Cultural-Historical Methodology Is Unfalsifiable

**Source:** Multiple contributors to the 2019 special issue; various academic reviews

**Argument:** The claim that historical periods (Renaissance, Enlightenment, Romanticism, postmodernism) can be characterized as dominated by one hemisphere or the other is:
- **Unfalsifiable:** Cultural history does not come with hemisphere labels; any historical period can be characterized as exhibiting features of either mode depending on what one selects
- **Motivated:** Critics note that McGilchrist tends to evaluate historical periods in ways that align with his pre-existing aesthetic and philosophical preferences (e.g., treating medieval Catholic mysticism as RH, Calvinist Protestantism as LH — convenient given his sympathies)
- **Cannot in principle explain specific historical events:** The hemisphere hypothesis "cannot, in principle, explain anything like the rise of Calvinism" (Arnold in the special issue)

**Important Note:** In his response in the 2019 special issue, McGilchrist explicitly **denied** making any "causal" claim — he is not claiming that brain events *cause* historical developments. This is a significant clarification: the cultural-historical argument is, by his own account, a *correlation* or *resonance*, not a causal explanation.

**Assessment:** This denial substantially weakens the most ambitious readings of his cultural-historical thesis, but also makes the thesis harder to evaluate. If he is not claiming causal relations, what is the nature of the claim? An analogy? A structural parallel? Critics argue this makes the cultural argument unfalsifiable in a way that makes it difficult to take seriously as a scientific or historical claim.

---

### Critique 6: The Two-Personalities Problem

**Source:** Multiple critics; related to the homunculus objection

**Argument:** Even granting the neuroscientific evidence for hemispheric functional differences, the idea that the hemispheres have distinct *personalities*, *preferences*, or *goals* — that the LH "wants power" or the RH "seeks truth" — attributes intentional properties to neural structures without justification. The hemispheres are neural systems, not agents; they do not "want" or "prefer" anything in the sense that persons do.

McGilchrist is aware of this and often qualifies his language, but his rhetorical strategy — which relies heavily on the anthropomorphizing of hemispheres for communicative power — makes the objection stick.

---

### Critique 7: The Religion Problem

**Source:** Contributors to the 2019 special issue on religion

**Argument:** McGilchrist's treatment of the history of religion is normatively motivated. He tends to characterize religious traditions as "more right-hemisphere" or "more left-hemisphere" in ways that map suspiciously onto his pre-existing aesthetic and philosophical preferences:
- Medieval mysticism (Eckhart, *via negativa*, apophatic theology) = RH
- Calvinist Reformation (explicit doctrine, iconoclasm, legal framework) = LH
- Eastern Orthodox Christianity = more RH than Western Catholicism
- Buddhism = ambivalent (he distances himself from non-dual approaches)

Critics note that these characterizations reflect a particular aesthetic and intellectual sensibility, not a neutral reading of the religious traditions themselves.

## The 2019 *Religion, Brain & Behavior* Special Issue

This is the most concentrated academic engagement with McGilchrist's work. The issue included:
- Multiple critical papers from neuroscientists, psychologists, philosophers, and religious scholars
- McGilchrist's responses to each critique
- A lengthy "Concluding Eirenic (and Mostly 'Unscientific') Postscript" in which McGilchrist:
  - Denied that hemispheric brain activations "cause" or "explain" historical events
  - Insisted he has not "solved" the mind/body problem
  - Remained agnostic on the precise relationship between brain events and experience
  - Argued that critics had misread him as making stronger causal claims than intended

## Sympathetic but Critical Assessments

### Middle Way Society
Praised the practical insights of *TMWT* but criticized the metaphysics: the *coincidentia oppositorum* as a universal metaphysical principle overstretches what should be a pragmatic navigational heuristic.

### Los Angeles Review of Books
Two extended reviews of *TMWT*: described it as "simultaneously incredibly detailed and researched and, at the same time, kind of simplistic in its binarisms." The LH/RH binarism may be too blunt an instrument for the metaphysical claims McGilchrist builds on it.

### Online and Academic Communities
- Reddit's r/Neuropsychology and r/consciousness: Many find the neuroscientific core compelling and the cultural-historical thesis plausible as a structural argument; the metaphysics of TMWT often described as "profound but unfalsifiable"
- Academic philosophers of mind: Reception varies. Those sympathetic to process philosophy and dual-aspect monism (a serious minority position) tend to be appreciative; mainstream analytic philosophers tend to be skeptical

## How to Read McGilchrist Charitably and Critically

McGilchrist occupies a genuinely unusual niche: he is simultaneously a working neuroscientist, a phenomenological philosopher, a cultural historian, and a humanist. Critics from any one discipline can (and do) accuse him of overreaching in the others.

A defensible reading:
1. **The neuroscience of attentional lateralization:** Broadly supported, though the extension to global "modes of being" is speculative
2. **The phenomenological description of hemispheric worlds:** Rich and illuminating as a framework, even if not strictly derivable from the neuroscience alone
3. **The cultural-historical thesis:** Compelling as a structural resonance or analogy; unfalsifiable as a causal claim; best read as a heuristic framework for understanding modernity, not a historical-scientific hypothesis
4. **The metaphysics of TMWT:** A serious contribution to process philosophy and dual-aspect monism; goes well beyond what neuroscience can directly support; best evaluated on its philosophical merits independently of the neuroscientific frame

The strongest version of McGilchrist's project: even if the hemispheric neuroscience cannot carry all the weight he places on it, the phenomenological descriptions of two fundamental modes of engaging with reality — one grasping, categorizing, and re-presenting; the other attending, holding, and allowing — are useful and important. The brain science provides a *naturalistic grounding* for what phenomenologists and poets had already described; whether it *entails* or merely *illustrates* those descriptions is a secondary question.
