# The Matter with Things: McGilchrist's Metaphysical Extension

## Bibliographic Data

**Full title:** *The Matter with Things: Our Brains, Our Delusions, and the Unmaking of the World*
**Author:** Iain McGilchrist
**Publisher:** Perspectiva Press (small independent UK publisher)
**Published:** October 2021
**Format:** Two volumes; ~1,579 pages (~$87 / £75)
**Status:** Widely considered McGilchrist's definitive work; sometimes described as one of the most ambitious books of the 21st century

---

## What *The Matter with Things* Does Differently

*The Master and His Emissary* (2009) was primarily **descriptive and diagnostic**: it characterized what the hemispheres do differently and argued that Western civilization's cultural arc reflects progressive LH dominance. Its mode was largely empirical (neuroscience + cultural history) and its conclusion was a diagnosis of pathology.

*The Matter with Things* (TMWT) shifts to explicitly **normative and metaphysical** territory: **if the right hemisphere's mode of engagement with the world is the more truthful one — if it discloses reality more accurately than the left — then what does reality actually look like?** What must be true of the cosmos for the RH to be the Master, not merely a richer-seeming but ultimately subjective alternative to the LH's hard facts?

TMWT is McGilchrist's answer: reality is not what the left hemisphere's model says it is. The world disclosed by the right hemisphere — alive, processual, relational, value-laden, conscious at its root — is the real world. The LH model is a useful fiction.

## Structure

### Volume One

#### Part One: "The Hemispheres and the Means to Truth" (Chapters 1–9 + Coda)

A comprehensive neuropsychological examination of how each hemisphere handles each of the major faculties by which human beings come to know anything. McGilchrist argues systematically that on every measure of genuine cognition, the RH matches or surpasses the LH, and that the LH's claimed superiority depends on an artificially narrow definition of "intelligence."

**Chapter 2: Attention**
The foundational chapter. McGilchrist argues that the *quality of attention* determines what world appears — that how we attend is not a neutral, instrumental act but constitutes the reality we encounter. RH: broad, open, vigilant — receptive to the full texture of what is. LH: narrow, instrumental, focused — sees what it is already looking for.

**Chapter 3: Perception**
Each hemisphere perceives differently in kind, not just degree. The RH perceives the living, animated, unique — the singular face, the particular voice, the irreducible context. The LH perceives the general, classified, static — an instance of a category. Perception is not a passive reception of data but an active, hemisphere-structured construction of a world.

**Chapter 4: Judgment**
Judgment is only as good as the attention and perception that precede it. Because the LH begins with impoverished, decontextualized input, its judgments — however internally consistent — are systematically distorted in ways the LH itself cannot detect.

**Chapter 5: Apprehension**
The chapter on *tacit, holistic knowing* — what Michael Polanyi called "tacit knowledge." Apprehension is the capacity to grasp a situation as a whole before one can say why or how. This is paradigmatically a RH capacity; the LH's explicit, sequential reasoning arrives after the fact.

**Chapter 6: Emotional and Social Intelligence**
The RH is substantially superior in emotional attunement, empathy, prosody, and social cognition. McGilchrist draws on clinical evidence (RH-lesion syndromes) and psychophysiological research to argue that emotional intelligence is not a soft supplement to cognitive intelligence but a more fundamental mode of knowing — one that the LH systematically undervalues.

**Chapter 7: Cognitive Intelligence**
Perhaps the most provocative chapter in Part One. McGilchrist argues that conventional measures of intelligence (IQ, *g*-factor) are systematically LH-biased — they measure exactly the kind of explicit, analytical, context-free processing the LH specializes in, while being unable to measure the holistic, contextual, embodied knowing the RH specializes in. "Intelligence" in the dominant cultural framework = "LH processing." This is circular, not revelatory.

**Chapter 8: Creativity**
Genuine creativity requires: (a) broad activation of remote associates (RH coarse coding), (b) the capacity to hold ambiguity without premature closure (RH tolerance), (c) synthesis of disparate elements into a new whole (RH integrative function). The LH can execute a known creative template; it cannot generate genuine novelty. LH premature closure kills creativity by selecting the first locally good answer.

**Chapter 9: What Schizophrenia and Autism Can Tell Us**
The major clinical chapter. Schizophrenia — broadly construed as a syndrome of RH-disconnection and LH-hyperactivity — provides a natural experiment in what happens when the emissary loses its master. The phenomenological description of schizophrenic experience (hyper-salience, decontextualization, paranoia, formal thought disorder, loss of embodied ground) reads as a description of the pure LH world: everything is explicitly significant but nothing is contextually meaningful; relations between things are severed; the self is isolated and under threat. Autism provides a contrasting natural experiment: not LH hyperactivity but a global reduction in social-contextual processing, with preserved or enhanced local processing in specific domains.

**Coda to Part One**
McGilchrist's interim conclusion: in every domain of cognition examined, the RH is the hemisphere that makes contact with the fuller, richer, more contextually adequate version of reality. This does not mean the LH is useless — it is essential for execution, manipulation, and explicit communication. But it means the LH is always derivative, and its claim to be the arbiter of truth is unfounded.

#### Part Two: "The Hemispheres and the Paths to Truth" (Chapters 10–19)

Having established the hemispheric asymmetry in faculties, McGilchrist turns to the major *routes* by which human beings have sought truth: science, reason, intuition, and imagination. His argument: none of these, properly understood, is reducible to the others; all involve the RH in their fullest form; and the dominant tendency to identify truth-seeking with LH-style explicit reasoning is a form of self-deception.

**On Science:**
McGilchrist strongly affirms science as a genuine and important path to truth — he is not anti-science. What he attacks is **scientism**: the philosophical claim that science is the *only* valid route to knowledge, that truth is whatever science can confirm, and that what science cannot measure does not exist. Scientism is a philosophical position masquerading as a scientific one. Moreover, science *at its best* — in the creative phase of theory generation and paradigm shifts — is driven by RH modes (Kuhn's "gestalt switches," the intuitive leap that precedes formal proof). Science under LH dominance becomes paradigm-bound, dismissive of anomalies, and resistant to model revision.

**On Reason and Logic:**
Formal logic has LH characteristics — sequential, explicit, rule-governed. It is essential but insufficient: Gödel's incompleteness theorems demonstrate that even within well-specified formal systems, truth exceeds proof. A system cannot be both complete and consistent. This means no formal system can be self-grounding; reason requires extra-rational foundations (assumptions, intuitions, frameworks) that it cannot itself justify. The LH's fantasy of pure reason is internally incoherent.

**On Intuition:**
Intuition is systematically devalued by Enlightenment rationalism but is a genuine — and often more reliable — mode of knowing. Expert intuition (studied by Klein, Kahneman, and others) reflects implicit knowledge accumulated through embodied experience, pattern recognition, and holistic sensitivity that cannot be fully articulated. It is not mere feeling but is more complex and truth-tracking than many explicit inferences. McGilchrist treats this as paradigmatically RH.

**On Imagination:**
> "From the left hemisphere's point of view, imagination is a species of lying; from the right hemisphere's point of view, it is necessary for access to the truth." — TMWT, p. 767

Imagination is not the arbitrary production of fictions. It is the capacity to hold possible worlds, to simulate counterfactuals, to grasp what is not immediately present but is real. McGilchrist argues that imagination — especially as developed in great literature, music, and art — gives access to dimensions of reality inaccessible to empirical measurement. Science, reason, intuition, and imagination are complementary paths to truth, not competitors.

### Volume Two

#### Part Three: "What Then is True? The Unforeseen Nature of Reality" (Chapters 20–29)

The metaphysical core of the book. McGilchrist moves from epistemology to ontology: *what must the world be like* for the RH's engagement with it to be the more truthful one?

**Chapter 20: The Coincidence of Opposites**
Drawing on Nicholas of Cusa's *coincidentia oppositorum* (the coincidence of opposites) and the apophatic mystical tradition, McGilchrist argues that at deeper levels of reality, apparent opposites (self/world, mind/matter, being/becoming, finite/infinite) are not simply contradictory but mutually implied. This is not Hegelian synthesis (a resolved contradiction) but a recognition that reality transcends the LH's binary categorical distinctions.

**Chapter 22: Time**
One of McGilchrist's most original chapters. Clock time — the LH's spatialized, measured sequence of equal instants — is not the primary mode of time. Lived time (Bergson's *durée*) is indivisible, qualitative, and continuous — "a river that flows, not a series of pebbles." McGilchrist argues that the fundamental nature of time is processual duration, not measurable extension. The LH's reification of time into a series of instants is a useful fiction that mistakes the map for the territory.

**Chapter 23: Flow and Movement**
Reality is constituted by process, not substance. Things are not primary; events are. A wave is a better model of reality than a particle; a vortex (a stable pattern in flowing water) is a better model of an organism than a machine. McGilchrist draws on Bergson, Whitehead, and Bohm (implicate order). We are "more advanced whirlpools" — temporary, relatively stable patterns in a universal flow, not discrete objects accidentally moving through time.

**Chapter 24: Space and Matter**
Newtonian space (empty, homogeneous container) and Newtonian matter (inert particles) are not what reality is. Quantum mechanics reveals: probability waves and superposition (things do not have definite properties independent of measurement), non-locality (Bell's theorem: distant particles are correlated in ways that cannot be explained by local hidden variables), observer effects (measurement involves interaction; there is no view from nowhere). McGilchrist does not make naive claims about quantum consciousness — he cites these features as establishing that the Newtonian materialist picture is *definitely wrong* and that the universe is stranger than the LH's mechanical model.

**Chapter 25: Matter and Consciousness**
The pivotal metaphysical chapter. McGilchrist's core ontological claim:

> Consciousness is not produced by the brain. It is ontologically fundamental.

The brain amplifies, shapes, and constrains consciousness — but does not generate it from non-conscious matter. Evidence invoked:
- **The hard problem** (Chalmers): No physical account explains why there is subjective experience at all — why there is "something it is like" to be a conscious system
- **Quantum mechanics** again: The measurement problem (what collapses the wave function?), the observer effect, entanglement — suggesting that observation/consciousness is not epiphenomenal
- **The explanatory gap**: Even a complete neuroscientific account of brain processes leaves the question of experience unanswered
- **The tradition of philosophical idealism** (Berkeley, Schelling, later Bradley, Eddington, Jeans): "The universe is beginning to look more like a great thought than a great machine" (Jeans)

McGilchrist's position is not standard idealism (consciousness creates matter) or standard panpsychism (every particle is conscious). It is closer to **dual-aspect monism** or **neutral monism**: mind and matter are two aspects of a single underlying reality more fundamental than either. This reality is better characterized by concepts like "experience" or "process" than by "matter."

**Chapter 26: Value**
Value is not a projection of subjective preference onto a value-neutral world. It is "a constitutive element of reality … as foundational as consciousness" (TMWT, p. 1132). The world as the RH encounters it — already mattering, already meaningful, already calling forth response — is the real world. The value-neutral LH world is the abstraction. McGilchrist connects this to A.D. Craig's work on the insular cortex as the neural substrate of *how much things matter* — interoception and valuation are integrated in the same neural system.

**Chapter 27: Purpose, Life, and the Cosmos**
Teleology — purpose, goal-directedness — is not mere anthropomorphism. Living organisms exhibit genuine purposiveness that cannot be reduced to efficient causation alone. McGilchrist is careful: he does not endorse Lamarckism or vitalism. He invokes contemporary biology's recognition that organisms are not just mechanism-executors but active, self-organizing agents. At the cosmic level, he invokes David Mermin's view of quantum probability as "propensities" — dispositional, directional tendencies — suggesting something like cosmic teleological orientation without requiring a personal God who intervenes.

**Chapter 28: The Sense of the Sacred**
McGilchrist defends the sense of the sacred as genuine cognitive access to a real dimension of reality — not projection, not wish-fulfillment, not primitive superstition. The sacred is what the LH cannot reach: the inexhaustible surplus in things that exceeds all categories, all explanation, all representation.

Key influence: **Meister Eckhart** — "the greatest of the medieval mystics," associated with "a metaphysics of flow." Eckhart's *Abgeschiedenheit* (detachment/releasement) and *Gelassenheit* (letting-be) describe the stance in which the grasping LH is released and the RH's open receptivity is allowed to operate at depth.

The *via negativa* (apophatic theology): God cannot be positively characterized — only approached by the systematic negation of all categories. This is the RH's native stance toward the inexhaustible: not grasping, but receiving.

McGilchrist is not a conventional theist (which would be a LH move — pinning the divine to a category). He is also not an atheist (which accepts only the LH's world). He occupies what he calls a position of "reverent agnosticism" or something close to **panentheism** — the cosmos is "in" something larger, while that something is not simply identical to the cosmos.

## Intellectual Influences in TMWT

- **Alfred North Whitehead** (*Process and Reality*, 1929): process philosophy; "actual occasions of experience" as ontological primitives; rejection of the bifurcation of nature
- **Henri Bergson**: *durée*; life as creative evolution; the inadequacy of spatial models of time; intuition as direct knowledge of the flow of reality
- **Nicholas of Cusa**: *coincidentia oppositorum*; the coincidence of opposites as a feature of transcendent reality
- **Meister Eckhart**: metaphysics of flow; *Abgeschiedenheit*; *via negativa*
- **David Bohm**: implicate vs. explicate order; the deeper reality from which observable forms emerge
- **William James**: radical empiricism; the stream of consciousness; critique of vicious abstractionism
- **Owen Barfield**: evolution of consciousness; original participation as the primal mode of knowing

## Reception

TMWT was praised by many public intellectuals and humanist scholars but received a mixed reception from academic neuroscientists and philosophers. Critics noted:
- The metaphysical claims go far beyond what neuroscience can support
- Process philosophy and idealism are minority positions in academic philosophy, though substantive ones
- The book's length and density make it difficult to evaluate comprehensively

The Center for Process Studies (Claremont Graduate University) organized dedicated conferences on McGilchrist and Whitehead, treating TMWT as a significant contribution to process thought.
