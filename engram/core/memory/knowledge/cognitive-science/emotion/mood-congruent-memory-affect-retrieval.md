# Mood-Congruent Memory and Affect-Retrieval Effects

**Mood-congruent memory (MCM)** is the empirically demonstrated tendency for emotional state to selectively facilitate encoding and retrieval of emotionally congruent material. **Mood-state-dependent memory (MDM)** is the related but distinct phenomenon in which recall is better when the emotional state at retrieval matches the state at encoding. Both effects establish that **affect functions as an organisational and retrieval cue in episodic memory** — integrating the study of emotion into memory cognition and generating substantial clinical implications for understanding depressive rumination, trauma memory, and therapeutic change.

---

## Conceptual Distinctions

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Mood-state-dependent memory (MDM)** | Information learned in state A is better recalled in state A than in state B (state-as-context) | Words learned when happy are better recalled when happy again than when sad |
| **Mood-congruent memory (MCM)** | Stimuli whose emotional valence matches current mood are better encoded and/or retrieved | Sad mood → preferential recall of sad words/events regardless of learning state |
| **Mood-congruent attention bias** | Selective attention toward emotionally congruent stimuli | Depressed / anxious individuals show attentional capture by negative stimuli |

These are experimentally dissociable: MDM requires matched encoding and retrieval states; MCM holds even when encoding state does not match retrieval state (congruence of *material* valence with current state matters, not state-state matching).

## Gordon Bower's Network Theory

### The Emotion Node Model (1981)

**Gordon Bower** proposed the foundational theoretical account in his associative network theory of emotion. Core postulates:

1. Emotions are represented as **nodes** in a semantic/associative network
2. Each emotion node is connected by associative links to:
   - Autonomic patterns (heart rate patterns, etc.)
   - Expressive patterns (facial configurations)
   - Associated events, situations, and themes from personal history
   - Verbal/semantic representations of emotional content
3. **Spreading activation:** When an emotion node is activated (experiencing the emotion), activation spreads through the network to connected representations, priming their accessibility

$$P(\text{recall}(item)) \propto \text{associative strength}(item, \text{current emotion node})$$

**Predictions:**
- MCM should be strongest for **autobiographical memories** (richly connected to emotion nodes)
- MCM should extend to **imagery, fantasy, and free association** (material generated when in a mood will be tonally congruent)
- **Interpersonal perception** should be mood-congruent (happy people should make more generous interpersonal attributions)

### Bower's (1981) Classic Study

Bower induced happy or sad hypnotic moods in subjects during learning of word lists (half happy-semantic, half sad-semantic). At recall, subjects were re-induced to either the same or different mood state.

**Key results:**
- MDM: Words learned happy recalled better when happy (57% vs 46%) — and vice versa for sad
- MCM: Happy subjects recalled more happy words; sad subjects recalled more sad words — regardless of encoding state

**Replication difficulties:** Subsequent attempts to replicate MDM produced inconsistent results. Eich (1995) identified key moderating conditions:
- Internally generated states (genuine mood induction) > externally induced (emotional music)
- Autobiographical/episodic material > semantic material
- Strong mood induction > mild mood

## Eich and Macaulay's Integration

**Eich and Macaulay (2000)** conducted a meta-analytic review of MDM and MCM and clarified boundary conditions:

### Reliable MDM Conditions
1. **Internal/generated moods:** Mood induced through recall, imagination, music + autobiographical recall — not just music alone
2. **Recall of self-generated events:** MDM strongest for free recall of autobiographical events; weakest for cued recall of word lists
3. **Moderate-to-strong mood intensity:** Mild mood fluctuations produce negligible effects

### Reliable MCM Conditions
1. **Autobiographical memory:** MCM robust in autobiographical retrieval; inconsistent in word-list paradigms
2. **Evaluation and interpretation:** Mood colours interpretation of ambiguous stimuli (sad mood → interpret ambiguous faces as sad)
3. **Future thinking:** Mood-congruent bias extends to prospective memory and future-event simulation (anxious mood → more negative anticipated events)

## Affect as Retrieval Cue: Information Processing Account

Beyond network spreading activation, a second theoretical account treats affect as **context** — an organisational feature of encoding that is reinstated during recall:

**Contextual-cuing argument:** Memory is cue-dependent. Mood-at-encoding becomes part of the encoding context. At retrieval, matching that context (same mood) increases recall by reinstating more of the original encoding context.

$$\text{Recall success} = f(\text{cue overlap between encoding and retrieval contexts})$$

This account is continuous with classical encoding-specificity principles (Tulving & Thomson): the more retrieval context overlaps with encoding context, the better the recall.

**Affect-as-information** (Schwarz & Clore, 1983): In judgment and decision-making, people misattribute their incidental mood to the object being evaluated — "How do I feel about this?" → rely on current affective state as information about the object. This is mood-congruent *judgment*, related to but distinct from MCM proper.

## The Depressive Interlock: MDM/MCM in Clinical Context

Mood-congruent memory effects are theoretically central to understanding **depressive episodes**:

1. **Negative mood at onset** → selectively cues negative autobiographical memories
2. Flood of negative memories **reinforces and deepens the negative mood**
3. Deepened negative mood → further preferential retrieval of negative material
4. **Self-perpetuating cycle** (interlock): depressive episodes become self-maintaining via MDM/MCM

$$\text{Depressive mood} \xrightarrow{\text{MCM}} \uparrow\text{negative memory access} \xrightarrow{\text{mood congruence}} \downarrow\text{mood} \xrightarrow{...}$$

**Overgeneralised autobiographical memory** in depression (Williams et al., 1996): Depressed individuals show a specific memory pattern — they preferentially recall *categorical* (overgeneral) rather than specific episodic memories. Proposed mechanism: categorical retrieval is a defensive strategy (abort search before reaching painful specific memories). This is a form of MDM-interacting attentional avoidance.

**Mood Memory and Antidepressants:**
- Antidepressants (SSRIs) produce early shifts in emotional memory biases *before* clinical mood improvement — suggesting the emotional memory system is a therapeutic target
- Harmer et al. (2009): SSRIs selectively reduce recall of negative emotional words in healthy volunteers after a single dose — cognitive change may precede subjective mood change

## Attentional Bias: Pre-Memory Effects

MCM in attention allocation (attentional bias) typically *precedes* encoding and retrieval:

- **Dot-probe paradigm:** probe detection is faster when probe appears at location of mood-congruent vs mood-incongruent stimulus → attentional capture by emotionally congruent cues
- **Threat bias in anxiety:** anxious individuals show faster detection of threat stimuli (words, faces) than neutral stimuli — pervasive finding across state and trait anxiety
- **Negative interpretation bias in depression:** ambiguous scenarios are interpreted more negatively in depressed individuals

These biases partially explain MCM: mood-congruent stimuli receive more attentional processing → better encoded → better recalled — even without a direct retrieval stage MDM effect.

---

## Future Thinking and Mood

MCM extends beyond retrieval of past events to **prospective simulation**:

- **Mood-congruent future simulation** (Holmes et al., 2008): Depressed individuals generate more negative anticipated future events; anxious individuals generate more threatening future scenarios
- **Affect and hippocampal simulation:** both episodic memory and future simulation depend on hippocampal recombination of past experience; mood-biased retrieval → mood-biased raw material for simulation → mood-congruent future scenarios

This extension has implications for **planning under emotional arousal**: agents (human or AI) with biased simulations of future outcomes will make mood-congruent decisions — a structural amplification of any emotional state on forward-planning.

## Agent and AI Design Implications

1. **Context-state consistency in learning:** If an AI learning system has something analogous to "affective state" during training (e.g., through reward or penalty exposure), previously learned content may interact with state-at-retrieval in ways analogous to MDM — patterns learned in specific reinforcement conditions may be better retrieved in similar conditions
2. **Error propagation via state-dependent representation:** Systems that store and retrieve context representations may suffer mood-analogous retrieval biases if training and inference distributional properties shift
3. **Therapeutic intervention models:** CBT and MBCT (mindfulness-based cognitive therapy) interrupt MDM/MCM interlock by training meta-awareness of thought-affect cycles — a model for metacognitive regulation in AI systems that seek to avoid self-reinforcing error loops
