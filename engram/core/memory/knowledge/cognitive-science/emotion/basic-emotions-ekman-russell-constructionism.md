# Basic Emotions, the Circumplex, and Constructed Emotion

Three competing frameworks dominate theoretical emotion science: **Ekman's discrete basic-emotions**, **Russell's dimensional circumplex**, and **Barrett's constructed emotion theory**. They agree that emotions are real and important; they disagree fundamentally about what emotions *are*.

---

## Ekman's Basic Emotion Theory

### The Universality Thesis

Paul Ekman's foundational claim (1960s-1980s): a small set of emotions is **universal across cultures**, sharing:
1. Distinctive cross-cultural facial expressions
2. Unique physiological signatures
3. Automatic, rapid onset
4. Brief duration
5. Unbidden occurrence

**The six basic emotions** (Ekman & Friesen 1969, extended):
| Emotion | Core trigger | Adaptive function |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Anger | Goal blockage | Remove obstacle |
| Disgust | Contamination | Avoid toxin/social violation |
| Fear | Threat | Escape harm |
| Happiness | Goal achievement | Approach/sustain |
| Sadness | Irrecoverable loss | Withdrawal/attachment signal |
| Surprise | Unexpected event | Reorient attention |

A seventh — **contempt** — was added later as the only unilateral facial expression.

### The Cross-Cultural Evidence

Ekman and Friesen (1969) showed photographs of facial expressions to people in the US, Brazil, Japan, Chile, and Argentina. Agreement on emotion labeling was high (70-90%). Crucially, they reached the Fore people of Papua New Guinea, a pre-literate culture with minimal Western media contact, and found similar recognition — the signature claim for biological universality.

**The Facial Action Coding System (FACS)**, developed with Friesen (1978), provided an objective, muscle-action-based taxonomy of facial movements. FACS enables both scoring of naturalistically filmed faces and "reading" of micro-expressions (brief, involuntary expressions that leak concealed emotion).

### The Evolutionary Rationale

Ekman drew on Darwin's *Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals* (1872): facial expressions evolved as communicative signaling systems, homologous with expressions in non-human primates. Emotion expressions are biologically prepared responses, not learned conventions.

### Critiques and Defenses

**Critical replications**: Maria Gendron and colleagues (2014) found that Himba (Namibian) participants did not spontaneously label Ekman photographs with the same discrete categories when given an open-ended labeling task (vs. a forced-choice task). Forced choice artificially inflates apparent universality.

**Dunn & Lindquist (2012)** meta-analysis: emotion recognition from faces is well above chance but far below the universality Ekman's original framing implied; accuracy depends on context, culture, and task structure.

**Defense**: Ekman acknowledges cultural display rules — learned suppression or exaggeration of expressions — while maintaining that underlying emotional programs are universal. The debate continues.

## Russell's Circumplex Model

### Two-Dimensional Affect Space

James Russell (1980) proposed that **all affective states can be located in a two-dimensional space**:

- **Valence (pleasantness)**: negative ←→ positive
- **Arousal (activation)**: low arousal ←→ high arousal

Emotions are arranged in a circular (circumplex) structure:

```
                High Arousal
                    |
         Afraid     |    Excited
    Angry           |           Happy
Sad    —————————————+————————————    Content
    Depressed       |           Relaxed
         Bored      |    Calm
                    |
                Low Arousal
```

**Core affect** is the neurophysiological state that underlies all emotions — a continuously varying position in valence-arousal space. Discrete emotion *categories* are conceptual overlays applied to core affect by the cognitive system.

### Evidence for the Two Dimensions

1. **Self-report studies**: Multidimensional scaling of emotion terms consistently recovers two primary dimensions.
2. **Physiological correlates**: Arousal correlates with skin conductance, heart rate; valence correlates with asymmetric frontal EEG activity (left-frontal approach, right-frontal withdrawal).
3. **Cross-linguistic stability**: The two-dimension structure holds across many languages, though specific category boundaries vary.

### What the Circumplex Lacks

The circumplex accounts for hedonic tone and energization, but misses **action readiness** (what the organism is prepared to do). Several theorists have added a third dimension:

- **Dominance** (control vs submission) — yielding a three-dimensional PAD (Pleasure-Arousal-Dominance) space
- **Approach-avoidance** — linking to motivational systems

## Barrett's Constructed Emotion Theory

### Core Claim

Lisa Feldman Barrett (*How Emotions Are Made*, 2017; earlier: Barrett 2006): **emotions are not universal, biological entities read from the face or body**. Instead, they are **mental constructions** — predictions generated by the brain when it categorizes core affective states using **emotion concepts** learned from culture and language.

> "Your brain predicts your emotions; it doesn't passively detect them."

### The Process

1. **Interoceptive signals**: The body continuously generates visceral and metabolic signals (heartbeat, gut state, muscle tension, etc.).
2. **Allostatic regulation**: The brain's predictive model tries to regulate the body's resource budget by predicting what those signals mean.
3. **Conceptual categorization**: The brain applies emotion concepts (learned cultural categories) to make sense of undifferentiated arousal/valence states. The same racing heart might be categorized as *fear*, *excitement*, or *love* depending on context.
4. **The constructed emotion**: What you experience as "anger" or "sadness" is the brain's best categorical prediction, not a readout of a dedicated neural circuit.

### Key Experimental Evidence

**Misattribution of arousal (classic)**: Schachter & Singer (1962) showed aroused participants interpreted their state as anger or euphoria depending on environmental cues — suggesting emotion categories are context-dependent attributions to undifferentiated arousal.

**Conceptual act model**: Barrett's own work shows that **emotional granularity** (precision of emotion concepts) predicts emotion regulation outcomes — people with rich emotion vocabularies regulate more effectively, suggesting concepts are generative, not merely descriptive.

**Brain imaging**: Barrett argues there is no dedicated "fear center" or "anger area" in the brain. The amygdala responds to biological relevance (salience, uncertainty), not to specific emotions. The same regions activate across many different emotions.

### The Role of Language

For Barrett, language does not merely name pre-existing emotions — it **constitutes** them. Communities who lack a word for a given emotion construct it less or not at all. This is a strong Whorfian claim about emotion (though Barrett is careful to say concept, not word, matters — non-linguistic concept systems could also construct emotions).

## Comparing the Three Frameworks

| Dimension | Ekman | Russell | Barrett |
|-----------|-------|---------|---------|
| Basic units | Discrete categories (6-7) | Continuous dimensions (2) | Constructed categories from core affect |
| Universality | Yes — biologically fixed | Dimensions universal; categories variable | Concepts vary cross-culturally |
| Facial expression | Reliable diagnostic | Weak read of valence/arousal | Contextual act, not readout |
| Neural substrate | Dedicated circuits per emotion | Undifferentiated arousal/valence systems | Prediction + interoception + conceptual systems |
| Role of culture | Display rules only | Minor — dimensions universal | Central — concepts are culturally acquired |
| Relationship to cognition | Post-emotional appraisal possible | Emotion = core affect + categorization | Emotion = cognitive construction |

## Cross-Species and AI Welfare Implications

### Cross-Species Generalization

**Ekman's view**: Basic emotions are homologous in mammals. Fear, anger, and play behaviours in non-human animals are interpreted as the same emotion categories.

**Barrett's challenge**: Attributing discrete emotion categories to animals requires that they have the same conceptual apparatus. She is more cautious — non-human animals may have core affect without constructed emotions. This has major implications for animal welfare research.

**Panksepp** (addressed in affective-neuroscience file): his seven primary emotional systems provide a middle position — subcortical circuits generate basic affective states in all mammals, regardless of whether conceptual elaboration follows.

### AI Implications

| Framework | Implication for AI emotion |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Ekman | Hard — AI lacks the biological circuits; facial expression readout is behaviorally surface-level |
| Russell | Tractable — a system tracking valence-arousal-like states (reward signal + autonomic analog) could have something like core affect |
| Barrett | Depends on whether AI systems form genuine emotion concepts informed by interoceptive-like states; sophisticated LLMs may simulate conceptual categorization without the interoceptive grounding |

Whether AI systems can genuinely experience emotion hinges on the still-contested question of which framework is correct. See `../../philosophy/ethics/moral-status-ai-welfare.md` for the welfare implications.

## Current Consensus (Such As It Is)

Most emotion researchers in 2026 hold a position that integrates elements of all three:

1. **Some emotional responses are biologically prepared** (consistent with Ekman) — the startle reflex, fear conditioning, some facial expressions.
2. **Continuous dimensions of core affect are real** (consistent with Russell) — and underlie all emotional episodes.
3. **Cultural concepts shape what gets constructed from those states** (consistent with Barrett) — explaining cross-cultural variation in emotion categories.

The most productive research programs now study **how** conceptual categories interact with affective states, rather than whether emotion is "real" or "constructed."
