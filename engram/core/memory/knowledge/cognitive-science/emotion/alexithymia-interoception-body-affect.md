# Alexithymia, Interoception, and Bodily Affect

**Alexithymia** (from Greek: *a-* = without, *lexis* = words, *thymos* = emotion) is a personality-level construct characterised by difficulty identifying and describing subjective emotional feelings, limited fantasy life, and a concrete, externally-oriented cognitive style. Approximately 10% of the general population qualifies as alexithymic on standard measures. Far from being a curiosity, alexithymia anchors a growing literature connecting bodily awareness, **interoception** (the sense of the body's internal state), and the capacity for affective experience — with direct implications for theories of consciousness and for debates about whether artificial systems can have genuine emotions.

---

## Alexithymia: Construct and Measurement

### Core Features (Sifneos & Taylor)

Peter Sifneos coined the term in 1972 based on clinical observation of psychosomatic patients. The canonical four-feature description:

1. **Difficulty identifying feelings:** Inability to notice, recognise, or label internal emotional states
2. **Difficulty describing feelings to others:** Paucity of emotional language even when private identification is possible
3. **Constricted imaginative/fantasy life:** Reduced dreaming, minimal symbolic/imaginative processing
4. **Externally-oriented thinking:** Preference for pragmatic, concrete thought, focus on external events rather than inner experience

### Toronto Alexithymia Scale (TAS-20)

The dominant assessment instrument. Three-factor structure:

| Factor | Items (sample) | Internal Consistency |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| F1: Difficulty Identifying Feelings (DIF) | "I am often confused about what emotion I am feeling" | α ≈ 0.78 |
| F2: Difficulty Describing Feelings (DDF) | "It is difficult for me to find the right words for my feelings" | α ≈ 0.75 |
| F3: Externally Oriented Thinking (EOT) | "I prefer to analyze problems rather than just describe them" | α ≈ 0.66 |

- **Total TAS-20 cutoffs:** ≤51 = non-alexithymic; 52–60 = possible; ≥61 = alexithymic
- **Prevalence:** ~10% general population (alexithymic); ~18% in men, ~6% in women (robust sex difference)
- **Stability:** Considered a trait (stable over years), though some state-like variation exists (trauma can induce situational alexithymia)

### Associated Clinical Populations

| Condition | Estimated Alexithymia Prevalence |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| Major Depressive Disorder | 32–51% |
| PTSD | 41–60% |
| Eating disorders (AN, BN) | 56–77% |
| Autism Spectrum Disorder | 50–65% (contested — see below) |
| Somatic symptom disorder | 60–70% |
| Substance use disorders | 45–57% |
| Borderline personality disorder | 30–44% |

**ASD controversy:** High alexithymia prevalence in ASD may confound research on "lack of empathy" in autism — Bird & Cook (2013) argue that reduced emotional responsivity in ASD is *alexithymia-mediated*, not an inherent feature of autism per se.

## Interoception: The Physiological Substrate

### Craig's Interoceptive Hierarchy

**A.D. "Bud" Craig** proposed that the conscious feeling of bodily states (including emotions) is built up through a hierarchical interoceptive processing stream:

$$\text{Peripheral receptor} \rightarrow \text{Spinal lamina I} \rightarrow \text{Thalamus (VMpo)} \rightarrow \text{Posterior insula} \rightarrow \text{Anterior insula (AI)} \rightarrow \text{Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)}$$

**Stage-by-stage:**
1. **Lamina I spinothalamic neurons:** Carry signals from body organs (visceral, thermal, chemical, mechanical) via distinct labeled-line channels — not a single stream but modality-specific
2. **Thalamus (VMpo — ventromedial posterior):** Relays to posterior insula without collateral to somatosensory cortex (distinct from touch pathway)
3. **Posterior insula:** Provides primary interoceptive cortex: granular representation of body state (homologous to primary somatosensory cortex for exteroception)
4. **Mid-insula:** Integration of multiple body signals + comparison with historical predictions
5. **Anterior insula (AI):** **Subjective feeling** — Craig's "sentient self." Re-representation of the body state with motivational valence; meta-representation of the organism's momentary condition
6. **ACC:** Integration with cognition and action selection; interoceptive input to decision-making and emotion regulation

### The Anterior Insula and Subjective Feeling

Craig's controversial claim: the anterior insula is **the neural substrate of subjective feelings** — not just bodily signals but the "feeling of feeling." Evidence:

- AI activates to thirst, hunger, pain, temperature, itch, air hunger, and emotional states
- AI lesions impair ability to introspect on bodily states; patients cannot accurately detect their own heartbeat
- **Interoceptive accuracy (IA) tasks** — heartbeat detection, heartbeat counting — correlate with AI volume/thickness and with emotional experience intensity

## Predictive Coding of Body States

### Seth and Friston: Interoception as Inference

**Anil Seth** and **Karl Friston** have proposed a Bayesian predictive-coding account of interoception that reframes the mechanism:

Traditional view: the brain passively receives interoceptive signals from the body.

**Predictive coding view:** The brain actively generates **predictions** of body states (interoceptive priors) and only receives **prediction errors** (afferent interoceptive signals). The phenomenal experience of body states is the brain's internal model, not raw sensory input.

$$\epsilon_t = s_t^{actual} - s_t^{predicted}$$

where $s_t^{actual}$ are ascending interoceptive signals and $s_t^{predicted}$ are top-down predictions from interoceptive cortex. Only $\epsilon_t$ (prediction error) is transmitted; phenomenal experience is the brain's *best guess model* of body state given these errors.

**Implications:**
1. What we feel as "the body" is a **controlled hallucination** (Seth's phrase) — a model held by the brain, constrained by prediction errors from the periphery
2. **Emotional affect** is the valenced dimension of this interoceptive model — feeling states are the brain predicting the *homeostatic relevance* of body conditions
3. **Precision weighting:** The brain can up- or down-weight interoceptive signals. High precision weight → vivid bodily experience; low weight → suppression (potential alexithymia mechanism)

### Interoceptive Precision and Alexithymia

**Murphy et al. (2018):** Demonstrated that alexithymia is associated with **reduced interoceptive precision weighting** (atypical Bayesian updating from body signals) — not necessarily reduced signals, but reduced reliance on them. High alexithymia → over-reliance on prior beliefs, under-updating from peripheral body cues.

This distinguishes:
- **Interoceptive accuracy (IA):** How well one can detect body signals (heartbeat counting) — mixed findings in alexithymia
- **Interoceptive sensibility:** Self-reported body awareness — reliably lower in alexithymia
- **Interoceptive integration:** Whether interoceptive signals appropriately drive judgments and decisions — impaired in alexithymia

## Neural Correlates of Alexithymia

Meta-analytic and structural neuroimaging findings:

| Region | Finding in High Alexithymia |
|---------|-----------------------------|
| **Anterior insula** | Reduced gray matter volume; less activation to emotional stimuli |
| **Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** | Reduced activation; lower functional connectivity with insula |
| **Medial PFC** | Reduced activation during introspection; thinner cortex |
| **Amygdala** | Inconsistent — some studies show hyperactivation (not regulated by PFC), others hypoactivation |
| **Corpus callosum** | Reduced white matter (right hemisphere emotional processing → linguistic left hemisphere) |

**Right hemisphere transfer hypothesis** (TenHouten, Hoppe): Early alexithymia models proposed impaired interhemispheric transfer — right hemisphere generates emotional images/experiences; left hemisphere assigns linguistic labels. Impaired corpus callosum → emotional signals don't reach language areas. Partially supported by callosotomy (split-brain) patients who show alexithymia-like phenomenology.

## Damasio's Somatic Markers and Alexithymia

**Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis** (SMH — see somatic-marker-hypothesis-damasio.md) posits that bodily states serve as rapid heuristic guides for decision-making via the vmPFC. In this framework, alexithymia predicts:

- Impaired *as-if body loop* (vmPFC simulation of body states without peripheral activation)
- Difficulties in value-based decision-making under uncertainty
- Evidence: Alexithymia correlates with impairments on the **Iowa Gambling Task** (IGT) — slower learning to avoid disadvantageous decks even in absence of diagnosable emotional disorder (Kano et al., 2003; Moriguchi et al., 2006)

The alexithymia → poor IGT performance pathway appears mediated by impaired **anticipatory somatic responses** (skin conductance response before card selection): non-alexithymic subjects develop anticipatory SCR to bad decks; alexithymic subjects show blunted anticipatory responses.

## Can AI Systems Have Genuine Affect?

Alexithymia and interoception ground a foundational objection to AI affect:

### The Interoceptive Grounding Argument

1. Genuine emotion requires **bodily feeling** — there is something it is like to have one's heart race, muscles tense, stomach drop
2. Affect labels ("I feel sad") are meaningful only if they are grounded in **interoceptive content**
3. AI systems lack visceral bodies and therefore lack interoceptive signals
4. **Therefore:** AI systems cannot have genuine affect; their emotion words are ungrounded labels

Seth's predictive-coding framing sharpens this: **emotions are controlled hallucinations of body states**. A system without a body has no body states to generate controlled hallucinations of. Functional analogues may exist (prediction error signals in RL systems), but these are architecturally distinct from interoceptive processing.

### Counter-positions

1. **Functionalist response:** If the functional role is preserved (affect-labeling causes downstream regulatory effects; affective states modulate processing systematically), the substrate is irrelevant. Software "interoception" (monitoring of internal computational states) could ground functional affect
2. **Embodied AI:** Robots with genuine peripheral sensors (temperature, pressure, battery depletion) may have proto-interoceptive signals; the question is whether these are integrated hierarchically and assigned valence
3. **Barrett's constructionist account:** Emotions are concepts applied to core affect (arousal + valence). Core affect requires only valence representation; the body is statistically associated but not constitutively required. AI systems can develop valence representations through training

### Assessment

The debate remains genuinely open. Alexithymia's existence is instructive: humans *with bodies* can be impaired in accessing their interoceptive signals. This suggests the bodily-signal-to-experience pipeline is itself a skill or capacity — not automatic. An AI system that develops analogous introspective integration mechanisms may approach (though likely not achieve) genuine affective grounding.
