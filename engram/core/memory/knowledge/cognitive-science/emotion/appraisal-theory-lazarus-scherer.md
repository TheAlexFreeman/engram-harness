# Appraisal Theory: Lazarus and Scherer

**Appraisal theory** holds that emotions are generated not by stimuli directly, but by the subject's evaluation — appraisal — of what those stimuli mean for their goals, values, and coping resources. The same event produces different emotions in different people, or in the same person at different times, because their appraisals differ. Appraisal is the interface between cognition and emotion generation.

The two dominant appraisal frameworks are **Lazarus's cognitive-motivational-relational (CMR) theory** and **Scherer's component process model (CPM)**.

---

## Lazarus's Cognitive-Motivational-Relational Theory

### The Two-Stage Appraisal Process

Richard Lazarus (1966, 1991) proposed that emotion generation requires two sequential appraisal operations:

**Primary appraisal**: Is this event relevant to my well-being?
- *Stake* — does it concern something I care about?
- *Goal relevance* — does it bear on current goals?
- *Goal congruence/incongruence* — does it aid or obstruct goal achievement?

**Secondary appraisal**: What can I do about it?
- *Accountability* — who is to blame or credit?
- *Coping potential* — can I manage this situation?
- *Future expectancy* — will things improve or worsen?

These are called "secondary" not because they matter less, but because they logically follow the initial relevance assessment. In practice, both operations happen rapidly and often in parallel.

### Core Relational Themes

Lazarus proposed that each discrete emotion corresponds to a **core relational theme** — the characteristic appraisal profile that reliably generates it:

| Emotion | Core Relational Theme |
|---------|----------------------|
| Anger | Demeaning offense against me or mine |
| Anxiety | Uncertain, existential threat |
| Fear | Concrete, imminent danger |
| Sadness | Irrevocable loss |
| Guilt | Transgression of a moral imperative |
| Shame | Failure to live up to an ego ideal |
| Envy | Desire for what someone else has |
| Jealousy | Resentment over a third party threatening a relationship |
| Pride | Enhancement of ego-identity by credit for something valued |
| Love | Attachment to something or someone that is part of my identity |
| Happiness | Reasonable progress toward goal achievement |
| Hope | Fearing the worst but yearning for better |
| Relief | Noxious condition has been lifted |
| Compassion | Being moved by another's suffering and wanting to help |

### The Stress and Coping Framework

Lazarus integrated appraisal theory with a broader **stress-coping model**:

1. **Person-environment transaction**: Emotion begins with a specific transaction, not an abstract situation.
2. **Appraisal of stakes and coping potential** generates emotional quality and intensity.
3. **Coping** — problem-focused (change the situation) vs emotion-focused (regulate the emotional response) — alters subsequent transactions.
4. **Reappraisal**: After coping, the person re-evaluates the situation, which generates new emotion (or diminishes the old one).

This creates a **feedback loop**: appraisal → emotion → coping → reappraisal → new emotion...

### Controversies in Lazarus's Account

**Does appraisal have to be cognitive?** Lazarus insisted that some form of "meaning" assessment is necessary — even if rapid and unconscious. Zajonc's (1984) counter-argument: **preferences without inference** — affective reactions precede cognitive evaluation. Lazarus replied that Zajonc's "affect" is mere arousal, not emotion, and that even rapid emotional reactions require minimal meaning evaluation. This debate shaped the field.

**Sequencing**: Are primary and secondary appraisals genuinely sequential, or parallel? Most current accounts treat them as parallel processes with feedforward and feedback connections.

## Scherer's Component Process Model

### Overview

Klaus Scherer (1984, 2001, 2009) proposed a more differentiated, process-based appraisal account. Emotions are not static states but **episodes** — sequences of synchronized changes across multiple organismic subsystems:

| Component | System | What changes |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Cognitive | Central nervous system | Appraisal evaluations |
| Neurophysiological | Autonomic/somatic NS | Bodily arousal |
| Motivational | Motivational system | Action tendencies |
| Motor expression | Facial/vocal/postural | Expression |
| Subjective feeling | Monitoring system | Conscious experience |

The components are coupled through **appraisal checks** which drive coordinated changes in all subsystems.

### The Five Sequential Stimulus Evaluation Checks (SECs)

Scherer's model specifies **five appraisal dimensions** evaluated in a fixed sequential order:

#### 1. Novelty Check
- Is the stimulus sudden? Unfamiliar? Change in current situation?
- Output drives the **orienting reflex** (attention, arousal)
- Generates: surprise (high novelty), boredom (low novelty)

#### 2. Intrinsic Pleasantness Check
- Is the stimulus pleasant or unpleasant in itself, independent of goals?
- Grounded in evolutionary conditioning (bitter = unpleasant, sweet = pleasant)
- Generates: basic appetitive/aversive responses; grounds disgust and basic attraction

#### 3. Goal Relevance and Conduciveness Check
- Does this event bear on current concerns?
  - *Relevance*: Is it related to a goal at all?
  - *Conduciveness*: Does it help me achieve goals (positive emotion) or obstruct them (negative emotion)?
- Generates: the valence dimension; distinguishes joy from sadness, fear from anger

#### 4. Coping Potential Check
- Can I deal with this? Do I have the resources?
  - *Cause* (internal/external): Am I responsible?
  - *Control* (can I control the event?)
  - *Power* (do I have the ability to cope?)
  - *Adjustment* potential
- Coping potential high + cause internal → pride, confidence
- Coping potential low + cause external → sadness, fear → anger if blocked
- Distinguishes **fear** (low control, low power) from **anger** (blocked control)

#### 5. Norm Compatibility Check
- Is this event/action compatible with social norms and my personal values?
  - External norm compatibility (social standards)
  - Internal norm compatibility (personal standards)
- Incompatibility with external norms → shame, embarrassment
- Incompatibility with internal norms → guilt

### The Sequential Architecture's Predictions

Scherer's sequential architecture makes specific timing predictions:
- **Novelty** and **pleasantness** checks happen earliest (~100-200ms)
- **Goal relevance** follows (~200-400ms)
- **Coping potential** and **norm compatibility** checks are later (~400-1000ms)

This accords with the finding that some emotional reactions (startle, rapid fear conditioning) precede full cognitive processing, while complex moral emotions (guilt, shame) take longer to unfold.

### Discreteness Versus Continuity

Unlike Ekman's discrete basic emotions, Scherer argues that **emotions are not natural kinds**. There are many possible appraisal profiles, and the emotion space is **continuous**. Discrete emotion labels are folk psychological categories that carve approximate regions in a continuous space — useful shortcuts but not reflecting distinct underlying types.

## Comparing Lazarus and Scherer

| Feature | Lazarus CMR | Scherer CPM |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Appraisal dimensions | Primary/secondary (2-level) | Five sequential checks |
| Processing order | Primary before secondary | Strictly sequential SECs |
| Role of the body | Integral but not specified in detail | Full component integration |
| Emotion categories | Core relational themes (discrete emotions) | Continuous appraisal profiles |
| Evolutionary grounding | Moderate | Strong (pleasantness check is evolutionary) |
| Empirical operationalization | Qualitative; difficult to measure | Specific predictions about timing and physiological signatures |

## The Appraisal-Emotion Interface: Open Questions

### Are Appraisals Sufficient?

Appraisal accounts typically claim that the same appraisal profile generates the same emotion regardless of prior history, physiology, or personality. Critics note that:
- Chronically anxious individuals make threat appraisals more readily (trait effects)
- Physiological arousal state at the time of appraisal affects emotional outcome (Schachter & Singer)
- Culture shapes which appraisal dimensions are attended to

### Unconscious Appraisal

Most appraisal theorists now accept that appraisals can be **unconscious and automatic** — not necessarily deliberate evaluations. This is essential for explaining the rapid onset of emotions. LeDoux's "low road" fear system (see affective-neuroscience file) can be understood as an unconscious, subcortical novelty + threat appraisal bypassing cortical evaluation.

### Connection to Emotion Regulation

Understanding appraisal is crucial for emotion regulation (Gross's process model, next file): **cognitive reappraisal** is literally a change in appraisal dimensions (reinterpreting a threatening event as a challenge changes coping-potential assessment). Reappraisal works because emotions are driven by appraisals, not directly by events.

### Connection to Metacognition

The monitoring-and-control architecture in Scherer's CPM has structural parallels to Nelson & Narens' metacognitive framework (see `../metacognition/metacognitive-monitoring-control.md`): the monitoring subsystem tracks the state of all components, enabling self-observation of emotional state. This is the basis for emotional *meta-awareness*.
