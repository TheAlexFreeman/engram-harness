# Representational Change Theory, the Einstellung Effect, and RR as the Transformative Mechanism

## The Need for a Mechanism

The Gestaltists correctly identified that insight requires restructuring — a reorganization of the problem's mental representation. Behavioral research confirmed this: warmth curves, incubation effects, and the systematic pattern of insight-problem failures all point to frame change as the core cognitive event. Neural research established the time-course and regional correlates: ACC conflict detection → right-ATL semantic broadening → gamma burst → Aha.

But what, specifically, is the *cognitive mechanism* by which frame changes happen? What operation does the mind perform that takes it from the wrong representation to the right one? Stellan Ohlsson's **Representational Change Theory** (RCT, 1992) is the most systematic cognitive-level answer.

---

## Ohlsson's Representational Change Theory (1992)

Ohlsson proposed that insight occurs when the solver's **current problem representation** is replaced by a new, more adequate one. He identified three mechanisms by which this replacement can occur:

### 1. Constraint Relaxation
**Mechanism**: The solver is implicitly imposing a constraint on solutions that is not actually required by the problem. Relaxing that constraint expands the solution space to include the correct answer.

**Classic case**: The nine-dot problem. Solvers implicitly constrain their solutions to stay within the square boundary formed by the 3×3 array of dots — a constraint the problem statement never imposes. Relaxing the "stay within the square" constraint immediately makes the solution available.

**RR analysis**: The implicit constraint is a relevance judgment: features outside the "box" are treated as not-relevant to the solution. Constraint relaxation = making those features relevant again.

**Experimental evidence**: Telling subjects that "the solution extends beyond the square" substantially increases solution rates — even without providing the actual solution. Knowing which constraint to relax is sufficient to unlock the frame.

**The meta-constraint problem**: Constraint relaxation requires knowing *which* constraint to relax. This is itself a relevance judgment — which of the many implicit constraints is the one blocking progress? Ohlsson's theory does not fully answer this; RR theory addresses it via the opponent-processing mechanism: divergent broadening loosens *multiple* constraints simultaneously, allowing exploration of constraint-relaxed sub-spaces, with convergent processing then finding whether any loosening leads to a productive path.

### 2. Re-encoding
**Mechanism**: The solver redescribes the features of the problem elements, attributing different properties to them. The physical elements remain the same; their representation changes.

**Classic case**: Duncker's candle problem. The box is initially encoded as a *container* (encoded under the "box-as-object-with-contents" schema). Insight = re-encoding the box as a *surface* or *platform*. The physical box is unchanged; its representation changes.

**Additional example**: The two-string problem (Maier, 1931). Two strings hang from the ceiling, too far apart to hold both simultaneously. One solution: tie something heavy (e.g., pliers) to one string and swing it as a pendulum, grab the other string, then catch the swinging one. Many subjects fail because they encode the pliers as a *gripping tool*, not as a *weight for a pendulum* (not as an object that can swing). Re-encoding pliers as "heavy pendulum weight" makes the solution available.

**RR analysis**: Re-encoding is a relevance reassignment at the feature level — which properties of the object are relevant to the current task situation (affordance switch).

### 3. Elaboration
**Mechanism**: The solver retrieves and activates knowledge that was not initially considered relevant to the problem, expanding the active representation to include new elements.

**Classic case**: When analogical transfer aids insight (e.g., the radiation problem solved after reading the fortress story), the solver has elaborated the active problem representation to include the analogy's relational structure, which then makes the convergent-rays solution available.

**RR analysis**: Elaboration is the reverse of constraint imposition — instead of narrowing the relevance field, it expands it by importing previously backgrounded knowledge as newly relevant.

## The Einstellung Effect: When Past Success Blocks Future Insight

Karl Luchins's **water-jar experiments** (1942) demonstrate one of the most replicated phenomena in problem-solving: **Einstellung** ("set" or "mental rut").

**Paradigm**: Subjects are given a series of water-jar problems: Given jars of sizes A, B, and C liters, how do you measure exactly X liters? The first five problems all have the same complex solution: B − A − 2C. After five such problems, subjects are given a problem that *also* has this solution but *also* has a much simpler solution (A − C). The majority of subjects apply the complex formula — missing the simpler solution. More strikingly: when given a problem that the complex formula *cannot* solve but the simple approach can, subjects declare the problem unsolvable.

**The RR failure**: Prior successful use of the B−A−2C solution establishes a tightly convergent relevance frame in which:
- The "relevant moves" are filling/pouring from specific jars in a formulaic sequence
- Simpler relationships between jar sizes are not attended to because they were never relevant to the previous problems

The Einstellung effect is a **relevance frame perseveration**: the agent's convergent system has over-learned its current frame, and this over-learning actively suppresses the divergent processing that would reveal simpler solutions.

**Cross-domain Einstellung**: The effect is not limited to water-jar problems. Expert chess players shown positions with elegant solutions they have never encountered show Einstellung: they find valid-but-suboptimal familiar moves and often miss the elegant solution, precisely because their expert relevance systems are tuned to familiar patterns.

This is paradoxical: *expertise* — typically an asset — becomes a liability for problems that require frame revision. The more deeply the expert's relevance system is calibrated to the domain's standard structures, the harder frame revision becomes when those structures are not applicable.

## Analogical Transfer as RR Flexibility

Gick and Holyoak's (1980, 1983) research on analogical transfer is the most systematic study of cross-domain relevance import:

**The radiation problem** (Duncker): A surgeon wants to destroy a tumor using radiation. Rays strong enough to destroy the tumor will also destroy surrounding tissue. How can the tumor be destroyed without harming surrounding tissue? Solution: Use multiple convergent low-intensity rays, each safe individually, that add up to destructive intensity at the tumor.

**The fortress story**: A general wants to capture a fortress via a road mined to destroy any large force. Solution: divide the army into small groups and send each via a different route converging on the fortress simultaneously.

**Key result**: Only ~10% of subjects solve the radiation problem without the fortress story. After reading the fortress story, ~30% solve it. After reading the fortress story *and being told to use it as a hint*, ~75% solve it.

**What the analogy provides**: The fortress story provides a *relevance template* — a mapping from structural roles in the solved problem (general → army → divided roads → fortress) to structural roles in the unsolved problem (surgeon → rays → multiple angles → tumor). The analogy is not about shared surface features; no armies or fortresses are in the radiation problem. It is about shared *relational* relevance structure: the key relation is *convergence of weak-but-additive agents*.

**RR interpretation**: Analogical transfer is the paradigm case of RR flexibility — importing relevance structure from one domain (where it is explicit and solved) into another (where it is implicit and blocked). This is exactly what Gestalt "productive thinking" requires: not applying a formula, but recognizing that this problem's structure *relevantly resembles* that problem's structure.

## Vervaeke's Synthesis: Insight as RR-Mediated Landscape Jump

Synthesizing Gestalt observations, Ohlsson's mechanisms, Einstellung research, and analogical transfer:

**Insight is what happens when the opponent-processing system oscillates from an over-converged state (wrong frame, impasse) into a sufficiently divergent state (multiple constraints loosened, remote associates activated, analogical structures imported) that a new, adequate frame emerges and becomes the new dominant attractor in the relevance landscape.**

The three mechanisms — constraint relaxation, re-encoding, elaboration — are the *content of* the frame change; the opponent-processing dynamic is the *cause of* the transition. Neither account is complete without the other:
- Ohlsson's mechanisms describe what structural operations transform the old frame into the new one
- RR theory describes why these operations occur when they do, and why they succeed or fail

**Insight therapy and mindfulness**: Interventions that reduce rigidity — mindfulness practice (which trains defusion from automatic thought patterns), flow-inducing skill engagement (which loosens deliberate control), even mild positive affect (which broadens the scope of attentional engagement, Per Fredrickson's broaden-and-build theory) — have been shown to improve insight problem-solving performance. This is strong evidence that the operative mechanism is RR: anything that recalibrates the convergent/divergent balance toward greater divergence in a structurally rich problem context improves the probability of frame change.
