# The Four Kinds of Knowing and RR's Role in Each

## Why Epistemology Needs More Than Propositions

Western analytic epistemology has focused predominantly on **propositional knowledge** — knowing-that (justified true belief, or its successors). This narrowing obscures the richness of how minds actually engage with the world. Gilbert Ryle's distinction between knowing-how and knowing-that was a first corrective. Vervaeke extends this considerably, proposing that there are (at least) four qualitatively distinct modes of knowing, each involving a different relationship between agent and world, and each engaging relevance realization differently.

The four kinds are not a hierarchy (higher kinds don't subsume lower ones) but rather *dimensions* of cognitive engagement that operate simultaneously. A skilled practitioner deploys all four in integrated fashion; confusion between them is a frequent source of cognitive and pedagogical error.

---

## 1. Procedural Knowing: Knowing-How

**What it is**: Embodied, enacted skill — the capacity to perform an activity competently. Riding a bicycle, speaking a native language fluently, catching a ball, improvising in jazz. Procedural knowledge is stored in motor programs and sensorimotor loops; it is largely unavailable to conscious report ("I just know how to do it") and cannot be fully captured in explicit rules.

**The implicit relevance structure**: Procedural knowing involves finely calibrated relevance selection built into the body's action repertoire. The accomplished cyclist knows — in the body, not in the mind — which micro-adjustments in balance are relevant to maintaining trajectory at speed; which are noise to be ignored. This relevance is not computed explicitly; it is *enacted* through practice-shaped neural connectivity.

**RR in procedural knowing — calibration through practice**: Procedural skills are acquired by progressively refining which sensorimotor signals are relevant to the action's success. Early in learning, novices attend to many irrelevant cues and miss relevant ones (a novice driver consciously monitors hand position, gear, mirror, speed simultaneously — overload). Expert performance involves *background-ing* the irrelevant and *fore-grounding* the relevant until the whole skill runs as a smooth, integrated system operating below deliberate attention.

**Catastrophic RR failure in procedural knowing — "choking"**: When expert procedural performance is disrupted by the injection of deliberate attention (i.e., when System 2 intrudes on System 1), performance catastrophically degrades. The golfer who thinks explicitly about their swing mechanics putts worse. The pianist who consciously tracks finger movements stumbles. This is a RR failure: explicit attention brings declarative relevance frames that interfere with the implicit procedural relevance already built into the skill. The lesson: **procedural knowing has its own relevance structure that is not transparently accessible to declarative attention**.

## 2. Declarative Knowing: Knowing-That

**What it is**: Propositional, explicitly representable knowledge — that Paris is the capital of France, that $E = mc^2$, that the Franco-Prussian War preceded World War I. Declarative knowledge is transferable through language without hands-on experience; it can be acquired through testimony and stored symbolically.

**The dominant mode of academic knowledge**: Schools, textbooks, and formal education primarily transmit declarative knowledge. This creates a systematic blind spot: students can acquire vast declarative knowledge while remaining incompetent in the relevant procedural, perspectival, and participatory dimensions.

**RR in declarative knowing — belief relevance management**: The space of possible declarative beliefs is infinite. The agent cannot hold all possible beliefs, cannot update all beliefs in response to every new piece of evidence, and cannot evaluate all logical consequences of current beliefs. Relevance realization governs *which of the infinite available beliefs are worth forming, updating, and reasoning from* given the agent's current goals, situation, and epistemic state.

**Confirmation bias as a RR failure in declarative knowing**: The tendency to seek, interpret, and remember evidence that confirms current beliefs is a relevance distortion: evidence that would bear on a hypothesis is differentially *treated as relevant* based on whether it supports or undermines the hypothesis. This is not a reasoning error per se — it is a relevance-assignment error that corrupts the evidence base before reasoning begins. (See `rr-rationality-theoretical-practical-ecological.md`.)

**The tacit background**: Polanyi's insight — "we can know more than we can tell" — applies here. Declarative knowing is supported by a vast tacit background of procedural and perspectival knowing that is never made explicit. The ability to use the word "chair" declaratively draws on vast procedural experience with chairs and perspectival familiarity with chair contexts. Declarative knowledge alone is radically underdetermined by its explicit content.

## 3. Perspectival Knowing: Knowing-From

**What it is**: First-person, situationally embedded knowledge — Nagel's "what it is like" — knowing what the situation is like from the inside. It is the knowing that constitutes *being in a situation*, not merely knowing facts about it. Examples: knowing what it is like to be in a jazz improvisation (not just knowing the theory of jazz); knowing what it is like to be a parent (not just knowing the developmental psychology literature); knowing what it is like to be in a forest at night.

**Perspectival knowing is not reducible to declarative knowing**: One can know all the propositional facts about jazz improvisation without knowing how it feels to improvise. The phenomenological "inside view" is not a derivative of the outside-view description. This is why lived experience of a domain produces understanding that purely textual acquisition does not — the *perspectival* dimension is missing from text.

**RR in perspectival knowing — the structure of salience in a situation**: From within a situation, certain features *stand out* — they are salient, pressing, or charged — while others recede into background. This is not arbitrary: it reflects the agent's embodied history, emotional state, goals, and the affordance structure of the environment. A chess player sees a chess position differently from a novice — certain configurations appear threatening, urgent, or promising in a way that organizes the entire field. This *perspectival salience* is a form of RR operating at the level of situation-perception, below explicit propositional reasoning.

**Heidegger's ready-to-hand vs. present-at-hand**: When tools are functioning normally, they are "ready-to-hand" — transparent to awareness, constituting the medium of the activity rather than objects of attention. When a tool breaks or is missing, it becomes "present-at-hand" — dragged into explicit attention. This transition (readiness ↔ presence) is a RR shift: what was relevant as background now becomes relevant as foreground, and vice versa. Perspectival knowing is largely about the smooth operation of the ready-to-hand; declarative knowing is provoked by breakdowns into the present-at-hand. (See `knowledge/philosophy/phenomenology/heidegger-being-in-world.md`.)

**LLM perspectival limitation**: LLMs acquire massive declarative knowledge through text but have no perspectival access to the situations that knowledge describes. This is not merely a biological-embodiment gap — it is an RR gap. The model's relevance to any given topic is calibrated to *distributional patterns in text*, not to the perspectival salience structure of actual engagement with the subject matter. This produces characteristic errors: technically accurate descriptions that miss what actually matters in the situation.

## 4. Participatory Knowing: Knowing-By-Being-Transformed

**What it is**: The most fundamental and least discussed kind. Participatory knowing is the knowing that constitutes the agent's *being in a relationship* — where the knowing is not separable from the agent's identity. The agent does not just learn facts about X; engaging with X *transforms the agent* — changes who they are, what they care about, what they find relevant.

**Examples**:
- The religious novice's transformation through years of contemplative practice: they do not merely learn facts about contemplative experience; the practice reshapes their attention, emotion, perception, and values
- The profound relationship (mentor, deep friendship, transformative love): knowing the person is inseparable from being the kind of person who knows them
- Genuine philosophical dialogue (in the Socratic sense): not transmitting information but transforming the interlocutor's capacity for inquiry

**Participatory knowing and character**: What one participates in shapes one's relevance landscape at the deepest level — what one notices, cares about, is moved by. This is why Aristotle places such emphasis on habituation in moral education: repeated participation in courageous actions trains one to *perceive* the situation as one calling for courage before any explicit deliberation. The relevance structure is built into character.

**Transformative experience (L.A. Paul)**: Some experiences — having a child, undergoing profound grief, converting to or leaving a faith — cannot be fully evaluated from the outside because they change the evaluator's relevance landscape. The pre-transformation self cannot know what the post-transformation self will find relevant. This is a limit on rational pre-commitment: the agent cannot deliberate in advance with accurate priors about how much they will care about what the transformed self cares about. Wisdom includes making well-founded decisions at these junctures despite (not eliminating) this epistemic gap.

**RR in participatory knowing — the deepest level**: Participatory knowing is not something the agent *does* with RR; it is something that *reshapes* the agent's RR capacity itself. Wisdom — in Vervaeke's synthesis — is fundamentally a participatory achievement: not acquiring the right beliefs or following the right procedures, but becoming the kind of agent for whom the right things are relevant. This is why wisdom cannot be transmitted by instruction alone; it requires sustained transformative encounter with wisdom-modeling situations, persons, and practices.

## Integration: The Four Kinds in Concert

A fully skilled practitioner in any domain integrates all four:
- **Procedural**: Performs the right actions fluently without deliberate attention to mechanics
- **Declarative**: Holds accurate propositional beliefs about the domain's structure, history, and principles
- **Perspectival**: Perceives the situation from the inside with calibrated salience — sees what matters immediately
- **Participatory**: Has been transformed by engagement with the domain so that its concerns are genuinely their concerns

Most educational systems develop declarative knowing at the expense of the other three. Most skill-training develops procedural knowing without the perspectival and participatory dimensions that would generalize it. Wisdom development requires all four in integration.

**The RR failure of declarative-only education**: The student who has declarative knowledge but not perspectival or participatory engagement *treats all topics as equally relevant* in the abstract, and thus cannot exercise the situated judgment that distinguishes expert from novice performance. They know *that* certain considerations matter without knowing *how much* they matter in this particular situation — a RR failure.
