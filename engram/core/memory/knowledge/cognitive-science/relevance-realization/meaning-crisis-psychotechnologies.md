# The Meaning Crisis and Psychotechnologies

## The Meaning Crisis as Civilizational-Scale RR Failure

The **meaning crisis** — Vervaeke's diagnosis of a broad, deepening sense among contemporary Westerners that life lacks significance, purpose, or genuine contact with reality — is not a personal failing, a mental illness, or a cultural preference. It is, in Vervaeke's analysis, a **civilizational-scale failure of relevance realization**: the shared frameworks and practices that once calibrated individual and collective RR have broken down, and no adequate replacement has emerged.

Evidence for the crisis: rising rates of anxiety, depression, addiction, and suicide in high-income countries despite historically high material standards of living; the proliferation of conspiracy theories, cults, and extreme ideologies (symptoms of desperate RR-outsourcing to authoritarian relevance-specifiers); widespread meaning-vacuum that is not reducible to economic precarity or social isolation.

**Why this is an RR phenomenon**: Meaning — the sense that something matters, that life has coherent direction, that one is engaged in something significant — is the phenomenological signature of *RR working well*. When one's relevance landscape is well-calibrated, the world presents itself as charged with significance: situations *call for* responses, relationships *matter*, time is directional rather than homogeneous. When RR fails at a systemic level — when no reliable framework is available for calibrating what matters — the world presents as flat, indifferent, and motivationally inert.

## Historical RR-Supporting Structures: The Axial Age

Karl Jaspers identified the **Axial Age** (approximately 800–200 BCE) as a period during which multiple civilizations independently developed transformative spiritual/philosophical traditions: Socratic philosophy in Greece, Buddhism and the Upanishadic tradition in India, Confucianism and Taoism in China, the Hebrew prophetic tradition. These traditions share structural features:

**Each provided what Vervaeke calls "psychotechnologies"** — systematic practices and techniques for cultivating mind in ways that transform cognition:
- **Socratic dialogue**: Not information transfer but systematic self-examination (*elenchus*) that disrupts comfortable relevance frames and forces confrontation with genuine ignorance. The Socratic interlocutor does not give the answer; they create the conditions under which the student's RR system is forced to reorganize.
- **Mindfulness/meditation**: Present-moment attentional training that trains the observer to notice the automatic relevance assignments of conditioned mind without immediately being controlled by them. The practitioner develops *meta-awareness* of their RR process — a capacity to see "this is being treated as relevant" that is distinct from "this is relevant".
- **Confucian ritual**: Embodied social practices that calibrate the participant's spontaneous relevance perception to the community's cultivated moral salience structure
- **Hebrew prophetic tradition**: Persistent reorientation toward long-term covenant fidelity over short-term advantage, sustained divine presence over local convenience — a temporal and relational relevance recalibration

**RR function of Axial Age traditions**: Each tradition, in different idiom, addressed the fundamental question: how can individuals and communities avoid the systematic RR distortions that lead to self-deception, tribalism, hedonism, and eventually nihilism? Each provided practices — not just doctrines — that trained relevance perception at the level of character, not just rule-following.

## The Platonic/Neoplatonic Synthesis

Plato's philosophy is, among other things, an extended meditation on RR failure: most people are trapped in the cave (an over-convergent relevance frame — the shadows are what matter, the prisoners believe), and the philosophical education is the painful, disorienting process of frame-expansion unto the encounter with the sun (the Form of the Good — the source of all intelligibility and relevance).

**The theory of Forms as a RR theory**: Forms are the structural relations that make it possible to recognize *what matters* in any particular domain. The Form of Justice makes particular just acts recognizable as just; the Form of the Beautiful makes particular beautiful things recognizable as beautiful. Without access to the Forms, relevance perception is locked to surface features — mere opinion (doxa) rather than knowledge (episteme).

**Neoplatonism (Plotinus)**: The philosophical/contemplative tradition that ran from Plato through Plotinus (3rd c. CE) and influenced early Christian theology treated the philosophical life as a *participatory ascent* — a transformation of the self that brings one progressively into alignment with the highest sources of being and intelligibility. This is not accumulating information about the Good; it is becoming more *like* the Good — a participatory transformation that recalibrates the deepest level of one's relevance landscape.

**The Neoplatonic synthesis**: By the late classical period, Platonic philosophy, Stoic psychology, Aristotelian logic, and various religious traditions had been partially integrated into a synthesis that provided a coherent, multi-layered psychotechnology stack for addressing the full range of RR challenges — from perceptual to moral to existential. Many early Christians (Augustine prominently) absorbed this synthesis into Christian theology.

## The Modern Collapse of Shared RR Frameworks

Vervaeke identifies three interlocking historical developments that dismantled the shared relevance frameworks without providing adequate replacements:

### The Scientific Revolution (16th–17th centuries)
The mechanistic worldview of Galileo, Descartes, and Newton displaced Aristotelian natural philosophy. In Aristotelian physics, the world is structured by *final causes* — every natural process is oriented toward its natural end, its appropriate form. This is a relevance-laden ontology: the world itself has a structure that tells you what things are *for* and thus what matters about them.

The Newtonian world is structured by efficient causes — mass, force, motion — which are *relevance-neutral*. A stone falling is not *seeking* the earth; it is subject to a force. Stars are not *guiding* human life; they are massive balls of plasma in gravitational orbits. The world, described in purely mechanistic terms, presents itself as without inherent significance. It must be *assigned* significance from outside — but the mechanistic worldview provides no resources for making that assignment and no shared framework for evaluating it.

### The Reformation and Fragmentation of Religious Authority (16th century)
The Protestant Reformation, whatever its spiritual motives, also fragmented the shared authority structure that had provided a broadly stable relevance framework across Western Europe. When individuals were told that they must read the Bible directly rather than through authoritative interpretation, they gained religious autonomy but also bore relevance-burden that the pre-Reformation social structure had distributed: what does this text mean? What does God want of me here? How do I know I am saved?

The proliferation of Protestant denominations, sects, and interpretive communities that followed is not a spiritual enrichment (though it can be that too) but also a fragmentation of shared relevance structures into incompatible private ones. The resulting pluralism makes shared action on shared relevance assessments progressively harder.

### Cartesian Dualism and the Disenchantment of the World
Descartes' separation of mind (res cogitans) from matter (res extensa) had the effect of concentrating all relevance inside the individual mind. The external world, as purely material and mechanistic, has no inherent relevance — relevance is projected onto it by individual minds pursuing individual desires. This "disenchantment" (Weber's term for the loss of the world's sacred charge) is the ontological dimension of the meaning crisis: the world is experienced as a warehouse of neutral resources to be used for subjectively chosen ends, rather than a cosmos structured by significance.

**The loss of participatory knowing**: The Cartesian framework eliminates participatory knowing by making the world purely external and the self purely internal. There can be no *transformation of the self through encounter with reality* if reality is a neutral mechanism and the self is sealed off from it. Wisdom in the participatory sense becomes literally unintelligible within a Cartesian ontology.

## What Cannot Be Fully Recovered and What Can

**What is lost**: The shared, broadly accepted relevance frameworks that once structured Western culture — the Neoplatonic-Christian synthesis, the shared cosmological narrative — cannot simply be reinstated. The historical disruptions that dismantled them are permanent; the intellectual criticisms of supernatural claims are not dissolved by cultural nostalgia. A return to pre-Reformation or pre-Scientific-Revolution frameworks is neither possible nor desirable.

**What can be recovered**: Vervaeke argues that the *psychotechnologies* embedded in these traditions — the practices that actually calibrate RR — are not necessarily dependent on their historical metaphysical housing:

- **Mindfulness practice** (Buddhist origin) can function as a relevance-calibration technology completely independently of Buddhist metaphysics; its effects on attention, executive function, and metacognition are documented neurologically and clinically
- **Socratic dialogue** (Greek origin) can function as a philosophical practice for disrupting over-convergent frames and cultivating genuine epistemic humility without commitment to the theory of Forms
- **Flow practice** (not tradition-specific; identified by Csikszentmihalyi) involves deep engagement with a skill challenge at the edge of ability — a naturalistic state in which RR is optimized, intrinsic motivation is high, and the self-world distinction temporarily dissolves with productive effect
- **Contemplative depth** (varied traditions): sustained practices that develop the reflective component of wisdom — the ability to step outside one's automatic relevance assignments and observe them — without necessarily requiring the specific metaphysical commitments of the tradition in which the practice developed

**The open question**: Whether these practices can be effectively transmitted and sustained without the *community, ritual, narrative, and commitment-structure* that historical traditions provided is genuinely uncertain. Secular mindfulness programs show effects but may not transmit the full depth of the practice. Philosophical dialogue as therapeutic practice (associated with Pierre Hadot's concept of "philosophy as a way of life") is promising but not yet institutionalized.

## The Meaning Crisis in the Digital Age

Contemporary conditions that aggravate the RR failure:

- **Algorithmic relevance engineering**: Social media platforms optimize for engagement (a proxy for relevance) using behavioral addiction loops — dopaminergic feedback for likes, outrage-maximizing content curation. This trains users' RR systems toward content that activates strong immediate response (outrage, envy, fear) rather than content that actually matters for their lives. Algorithmic relevance is anti-wisdom: it calibrates toward short-term emotional activation, not long-term flourishing.
- **Information overload**: The volume of available information vastly exceeds any individual's capacity to attend to and evaluate it. Without robust personal and communal practices for sorting signal from noise, individuals' RR systems are overwhelmed, and they either over-converge (tribal filter bubbles) or become nihilistically indifferent.
- **Cult and conspiracy dynamics**: Groups that promise comprehensive relevance frameworks — "we will tell you what is really going on, who the real enemies are, what you were made to do" — exploit the meaning-vacuum by offering outsourced RR to an authoritative structure. The appeal is genuine: a comprehensive relevance framework, even a false one, is experientially much better than no relevance framework at all.
