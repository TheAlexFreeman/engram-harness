# Relevance Realization and Rationality: Theoretical, Practical, and Ecological

## The Rationality Landscape

"Rationality" is contested across multiple research traditions. Cognitive psychologists (Kahneman, Tversky) identify systematic departures from normative standards — heuristics and biases — as rationality failures. Ecological psychologists (Gigerenzer) argue that the same heuristics are rational relative to their natural environments. Philosophers of practical reason (Aristotle, Kant) distinguish theoretical from practical rationality and argue that neither reduces to the other.

Relevance Realization offers a unifying frame that makes sense of each position without simply siding with any. The core claim: **rationality fails and succeeds at the level of relevance assignment, and neither the heuristics-and-biases nor the ecological-rationality tradition adequately addresses this level**.

## Theoretical Rationality and Relevance Distortion

### What Theoretical Rationality Requires
Theoretical rationality — roughly, the capacity to form beliefs and revise them in response to evidence in accordance with the norms of logic and probability theory — requires:
1. Correctly identifying which evidence is relevant to which hypothesis
2. Appropriately weighting that evidence (avoiding over- or under-updating)
3. Avoiding distortions in evidence selection and interpretation

All three operations are RR operations. Before probability theory can be applied, the agent must have correctly *framed* the problem — identified which features of the situation constitute evidence, and for what.

### Confirmation Bias as Relevance Misallocation
Confirmation bias — the tendency to seek, interpret, weight, and remember information that confirms current beliefs preferentially — is one of the most replicable findings in cognitive psychology (Wason, 1960; Nickerson, 1998 review). It appears in:
- **Evidence search**: Asking questions whose affirmative answer would confirm rather than disconfirm the hypothesis
- **Evidence interpretation**: Reading ambiguous data as confirming
- **Evidence memory**: Better recall of confirming than disconfirming instances

**RR analysis**: Confirmation bias is not primarily a reasoning error — it is a *relevance assignment error* that operates prior to reasoning. The agent treats evidence as relevant based on whether it aligns with the current hypothesis (the current convergent frame). Disconfirming evidence is assigned lower relevance and thus receives less attentional processing, less memory encoding, and less weight in belief updating. The reasoning that follows this pre-screened evidence base may be perfectly Bayesian and still produce systematically wrong conclusions.

**Design implication**: Correcting confirmation bias requires recalibrating relevance assignment, not just improving reasoning rules. Teaching formal logic or probability theory to confirmation-biased reasoners does not reliably reduce the bias — they apply the logic within a confirmation-distorted evidence set.

### Myside Bias and Motivated Reasoning
Stanovich's **myside bias** is the tendency to generate arguments, evidence, and considerations on "my side" of an issue at higher rates than on the other side, even when instructed to be balanced. Unlike confirmation bias, myside bias does not require believing a hypothesis to be true — it occurs whenever one is emotionally or identity-invested in a conclusion.

**RR analysis**: Myside bias is relevance distortion in the direction of identity-relevant considerations. Considerations that threaten one's team, tribe, or self-concept are *assigned lower relevance* before being evaluated. This is not hypocrisy (deliberately suppressing known considerations) but a genuine automatic relevance distortion — the threatened considerations simply feel less significant, less worth considering, less pressing.

**Persistence under g**: Myside bias does not decrease with general intelligence. If anything, more cognitively sophisticated people generate *more* compelling arguments for their side — sophistication enhances within-frame performance, not frame-evaluation.

### The Base-Rate Neglect Problem
When a problem description includes both a statistically informative base rate and a vivid narrative cue, most people treat the narrative cue as more relevant than the base rate (Kahneman and Tversky, 1973 — the Linda problem; the taxicab problem). The narrative provides a rich, specific, causally coherent story; the base rate is abstract and impersonal.

**Representativeness heuristic**: Probability is judged by resemblance to a prototype, not by statistical frequency. "Linda is a bank teller and feminist" *seems more probable* than "Linda is a bank teller" because Linda's description more closely resembles the prototype of a feminist-bank-teller than the prototype of a generic bank teller — even though any conjunction is necessarily less probable than either conjunct alone.

**RR analysis**: The representativeness heuristic is a relevance substitution: the *prototypical resemblance* feature is treated as relevant to probability judgment instead of the *frequency or base-rate* feature. This is an appropriate heuristic in many ecological contexts (typical instances are more frequent than atypical ones), but it fails in problems where the base-rate information is formally binding. The agent's relevance frame activates prototype-resemblance as the operative relevance criterion and deactivates probabilistic/frequency considerations.

## The Kahneman–Gigerenzer Debate Through an RR Lens

### Kahneman's Position: Heuristics as Rationality Failures
Kahneman and Tversky's decades of research document systematic violations of probability theory and expected utility theory by human reasoners. These violations are:
- **Systematic** (not random): the same errors recur across individuals and context types
- **Large** (not noise): errors can be enormous (100× miscalibration)
- **Motivated**: errors favor intuitive, emotionally available representations over abstract statistical ones
- Conclusion: System 1 heuristics are rationality liabilities in formal reasoning contexts

### Gigerenzer's Position: Heuristics as Ecologically Rational
Gigerenzer argues that the same heuristics are *ecologically rational* — they perform well in the environments in which they evolved and for which they are calibrated:
- The **recognition heuristic**: "If you recognize one of two options and not the other, choose the recognized one." Performs well wherever recognition frequency tracks objective quality (e.g., brand recognition tracks product success; city name-recognition tracks city size) — which is often true in natural environments.
- **Take-the-best**: Choose the option that wins on the single most diagnostic cue. Often matches or beats complex multiple-regression models in real-world prediction tasks with limited data.
- Conclusion: Heuristics are rational-in-context; the irrationality finding is an artifact of laboratory conditions that misrepresent the heuristic's ecological domain.

### Why the Debate Cannot Be Resolved from Within Either Camp
Both positions take the relevance frame as fixed:
- Kahneman measures heuristics against a formal statistical/logical standard and finds them deficient
- Gigerenzer measures heuristics against their evolved ecological niche and finds them adequate

Neither asks: **does the agent correctly identify which situations call for which type of reasoning?**

The prior question — which Vervaeke's RR theory makes explicit — is: does the agent's relevance frame correctly pick out the features of the current environment that determine whether the heuristic is appropriate? A recognition-heuristic user who applies it in a domain where recognition frequency *does not* track quality (e.g., recognizing a company's name via fraudulent advertising) is exhibiting RR failure at the frame-selection level. The heuristic itself is not the problem; the agent's failure to identify that this environment's relevance structure does not support the heuristic is the problem.

**RR as the third position**: Rationality = deploying the right cognitive process for the right situation, where "rightness" is determined by whether the agent's relevance frame accurately picks out the features that make the process appropriate. This is not reducible to formal standards (Kahneman) or ecological fit in the abstract (Gigerenzer) — it requires the agent to correctly assess, in this particular context, which type of problem this is and which frame is appropriate.

## Practical Rationality and Phronesis

### The Structure of Practical Rationality
Practical rationality governs action, not belief. A rational action is one that is appropriately responsive to reasons for acting — means-end reasoning that correctly tracks what actions will achieve one's goals, given one's situation and values.

Standard philosophical accounts (Humean instrumentalism: reason is the slave of passion; Kantian deontology: reason prescribes universal maxims) both abstract away from the *particular situation*. They provide algorithms for reasoning once the features of the situation are fixed. They do not explain how the rational agent *identifies which features of the situation are relevant* to the practical question.

### Aristotle's Phronesis: The Perception of the Salient
Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* identifies **phronesis** (practical wisdom, practical reason) as the intellectual virtue that governs moral action. Its core operation is **aesthesis** — a kind of perception of the moral situation that identifies its *practically salient* features:
- Which considerations bear on what is required here?
- What kind of situation is this, ethically speaking?
- Which general principles apply, and which must be weighed differently given this particular context?

**The skilled perceiver**: The person of phronesis perceives situations differently from the morally uneducated person. They notice features that others miss (the suffering that is easy to ignore; the competing interests that are easy to discount; the long-term consequences that are easy to discount in favor of short-term gains). This perceptual sensitivity is *trained* — not taught via rules, but developed through moral education, habituation, and experience. It is a form of RR calibration.

**Why rules cannot replace phronesis**: Rules operate at the level of general features ("do not lie", "maximize welfare"). Real situations are particular, context-specific, and often involve competing relevant considerations that no rule can fully specify how to weigh. The morally educated person navigates this not by having better rules but by having *better situated perception* — they correctly identify which features of this situation are the ones that matter, and at what weight.

**RR connection**: Phronesis is morally situated relevance realization. The perception of the salient is the practical analog of the cognitive insight that reorganizes a problem's relevance landscape — except that here the "problem" is a moral situation, and the "solution" is a fitting response to what that situation actually calls for.

## Relevance Realization as the Basis for Both Rationality Forms

The unified claim:
- **Theoretical rationality** fails when relevance frames distort evidence selection, evidence weighting, or problem-type identification before formal reasoning begins
- **Practical rationality** fails when the agent cannot perceive the morally or practically salient features of the concrete situation — no phronesis
- Both are RR failures, but at different levels and in different domains

The practical consequence: improving rationality requires not just teaching formal inference rules (for theoretical rationality) or moral principles (for practical rationality), but recalibrating relevance frames — which features are worth attending to, which problems call for which type of process, which aspects of the moral situation need to be foregrounded.

This is precisely what the virtue ethics tradition (Aristotle, Confucius) proposes through moral education: not rule-transmission, but character formation — the gradual recalibration of what the agent notices, cares about, and finds salient. And it is what Vervaeke's account of wisdom extends: wisdom is the deep optimization of this relevance-calibration process across a life.
