# Feeling of Knowing and the Tip-of-the-Tongue State

## The Tip-of-the-Tongue Phenomenon

The **Tip-of-the-Tongue (TOT)** state is one of the most phenomenologically striking experiences in everyday cognition: the sense of *almost knowing* something — a word, a name, a fact — that cannot currently be retrieved, accompanied by certainty that it *is* known and is retrievable with more effort.

**Characteristics of the TOT state:**
- Strong impression that the target is in memory (high Feeling of Knowing)
- Inability to retrieve the full target at the moment
- Partial information accessible: first letter, approximate number of syllables, phonological or semantic neighbors ("not Hitchcock, it's another director who worked in black and white...")
- Tip-of-the-tongue states are more common for proper names, technical terms, and words with distinctive phonological shapes
- They resolve (pop into mind) more often than chance would predict — demonstrating that retrieval was genuinely imminent, not imagined

**Brown and McNeill (1966)** conducted the foundational experimental study: subjects heard dictionary definitions of rare words and were asked to recall the target word. When they experienced a TOT, they were asked to report partial information about the target. Key finding: subjects could report the first letter, number of syllables, and phonologically similar words significantly above chance — demonstrating that meaningful partial information coexists with retrieval failure.

---

## Feeling of Knowing (FOK)

**Feeling of Knowing** is the metacognitive sense that a target is stored in memory and could be recognized even when it cannot be recalled. FOK judgments are made during recall failure ("I can't remember, but I feel I'd recognize it if I saw it") and predict subsequent recognition performance significantly above chance.

**Measuring FOK:**
1. Present a question or cue
2. Subject fails recall
3. Subject rates FOK: "How likely are you to recognize the correct answer if given options?"
4. Administer a recognition test
5. Correlate FOK ratings with recognition performance (Nelson's gamma)

FOK-recognition performance correlations are consistently positive (gamma ~0.3–0.6 across studies), establishing that FOK is a genuine metacognitive signal, not random noise.

## Theories of FOK: What Generates the Feeling?

### Trace Access Account
The "feeling" reflects direct access to the memory trace itself — a signal from its activation strength. The trace is there (hence the feeling) but cannot generate a complete retrieval output.

**Problem:** If the trace can be accessed sufficiently to generate a strong FOK, why doesn't that access yield the target? The trace-access account implies a paradox: the FOK signal comes from being "in the neighborhood" of the target without it being retrievable.

### Koriat's Accessibility Model (1993)
Asher Koriat proposed that FOK is not a direct readout of trace strength but is **inferentially derived from the accessibility of related information**.

The model:
- When a cue is processed, it retrieves a range of associated information — partial information, related items, phonological neighbors.
- The amount and fluency of this retrieved associated information is used as the **signal** for the FOK judgment: "Much is coming to mind → the target is probably stored and retrievable."
- FOK is a **feeling generated by aggregate accessibility**, not a direct readout.

**Evidence:** Koriat (1993) showed experimentally that providing subjects with more associated partial information (without providing the target) increased their FOK for the target — they felt they knew it better when more related information was accessible, independently of whether the target was actually in memory.

**Implications:** FOK can be high for false memories — when lots of related information comes to mind, FOK rises, even if what comes to mind is incorrect or misassociated. This is the mechanism by which high FOK can accompany retrieval of a wrong answer with full confidence.

---

## Mismatch Between FOK and Actual Recall

A central empirical finding is that **high FOK does not guarantee subsequent correct recall**:

1. **TOT states with wrong primes:** When a tip-of-the-tongue state is accompanied by a "blocker" (a similar-sounding wrong word that keeps coming to mind), the blocker's high accessibility inflates FOK while simultaneously interfering with correct retrieval.

2. **False memories:** Subjects who produce a compelling false memory often show high FOK for the false answer — the accessibility of the reconstructed narrative drives FOK up even when no trace of the true event exists.

3. **Expert overconfidence in domains with high accessibility:** Experts have abundant related information available, producing high FOK, even when their specific claim is wrong. The expert's rich associative network inflates FOK in a way that does not track claim accuracy.

4. **Illusion of knowing from rereading:** Rereading a passage makes the passage content highly accessible, inflating JOL/FOK for that content — even though rereading produces minimal actual learning benefit (see `metacognitive-control-learning.md`).

## Agent Implications

The FOK-accessibility relationship has a specific and important analog for LLMs:

**Fluent output as high FOK:** When the model generates a fluent, detailed, internally consistent response to a query — much related information comes to mind, associations flow easily — this is the functional analog of high accessibility and high FOK. The model "feels" (in a functional/output sense) confident.

**But accessibility ≠ accuracy:** Just as human FOK based on accessibility can be high for false memories, model fluency based on distributional co-occurrence can be high for inaccurate claims:
- Topics heavily represented in training data generate fluent, confident-sounding outputs — regardless of accuracy.
- False information that frequently co-occurs with true claims (conspiracy theories, plausible-sounding confabulations) is distributionally "accessible" and generates fluent, high-apparent-FOK outputs.
- The model's "confidence" (as expressed in the fluency and assertiveness of its output) tracks training-data accessibility, not claim accuracy.

**Design response:** The trust system's `source: agent-generated` flag + human review requirements are a system-level response to this FOK miscalibration. They impose external verification precisely because internal signals (fluency, accessibility) are not reliable accuracy indicators. Designing prompts that explicitly ask the model to distinguish "I know this precisely" from "I'm generating this from training patterns" is a prompt-level intervention targeting the same failure mode.

**Source monitoring extension:** FOK mismatch is closely related to source monitoring errors — the agent may have high FOK for a claim whose "source" is training-data pattern rather than a specific reliable document. See `source-monitoring-reality-monitoring.md`.
