# Social Construction of Scientific Knowledge: The Strong Programme and Its Aftermath

## Overview

The **sociology of scientific knowledge** (SSK) is the research program, developed primarily at Edinburgh in the 1970s and 1980s, that applies sociological analysis to the *content* of scientific knowledge — not just to the social organization of science (Merton's project) but to the actual theories, experiments, and facts that science produces. The "strong programme" (David Bloor, Barry Barnes) is its foundational manifesto. The "science wars" of the 1990s represent its confrontation with the scientific community and postmodern philosophy. The upshot — that knowledge is socially *produced* (true and uncontroversial) while denying that it is socially *constituted* all the way down (contested) — is the synthetic position that most working historians and philosophers of science occupy today.

## The Strong Programme (Edinburgh School)

David Bloor's *Knowledge and Social Imagery* (1976) stated the strong programme's four tenets:

1. **Causality:** SSK must explain beliefs by identifying the causes that produce them — social, psychological, and material conditions, not just "following the evidence"
2. **Impartiality:** The same types of causes must explain both true beliefs and false beliefs, both beliefs that turned out to be correct and those that did not
3. **Symmetry:** Sociological analysis does not privilege "rational" or "scientific" beliefs as needing different explanation from "irrational" or "superstitious" ones
4. **Reflexivity:** The programme must apply to itself — the sociology of knowledge is itself socially produced

The symmetry tenet is the crucial one. Standard history and philosophy of science (Merton included) treated true science as explained by "it corresponded to reality" while false beliefs (phlogiston, caloric, ether) require social or psychological explanation (ideology, self-interest, cognitive error). The strong programme insists on symmetry: the same kinds of social causes explain the acceptance of phlogiston *and* the acceptance of oxygen. The difference in outcome cannot be traced purely to a difference in epistemological rationality; social negotiation, authority, and contingency are always involved.

This does not mean all theories are equally true! It means the *social process* of acceptance is symmetrically social in both cases. "Oxygen" won not just because Lavoisier's evidence was better (though it was) but because of how Lavoisier assembled his network: his prestigious position, his access to the Académie, his rhetorical skills, his measurement devices.

## Harry Collins: Replication and the Experimenters' Regress

Harry Collins extended SSK through detailed studies of scientific controversy. His concept of the **experimenters' regress** (from *The Golem: What You Should Know About Science*, 1993) captures a deep problem in empirical science:

- To know whether an experiment was done correctly, you need to know if it produced the right result
- But to know if it produced the right result, you need to know if the experiment was done correctly
- The regress is broken not by purely logical means but by **negotiated closure** — the scientific community converges through social processes (reputations, theoretical priors, politics) on which experiments to trust

## The Science Wars

In the 1990s, the strong programme and allied approaches in cultural studies of science provoked fierce reaction from scientists and philosophers. The high point of the conflict was **Alan Sokal's hoax** (1996): Sokal submitted a deliberate nonsense paper, dense with fashionable postmodern jargon and scientific errors, to the journal *Social Text.* It was accepted and published. Sokal then revealed the hoax, arguing it demonstrated that the social constructivist approach in science studies had lost contact with scientific reality.

The Sokal affair was intellectually illuminating in its limitations:
- It showed that *some* work in science studies used jargon without substance
- It did not engage with the actual empirical and philosophical arguments of the strong programme or ANT
- The best SSK work (Collins, Shapin, Latour) never claimed that facts are simply invented or that truth doesn't matter
- The conflict was partly a category error: Sokal attacked a cultural-studies fringe while SSK practitioners had quite different commitments

What the science wars revealed: *the relationship between the social study of science and science itself is politically loaded.* When scientists feel that their authority is challenged, they respond politically as well as epistemically.

## The Synthesis: What Was Established

By the early 2000s, most historians and philosophers of science converged on a nuanced position:

1. **Knowledge is socially produced:** The process by which claims become accepted as facts involves community negotiation, authority, trust in instruments, rhetorical persuasion, and institutional gatekeeping — not just a pure correspondence with nature. This is uncontroversial among historians of science.

2. **Social production does not mean arbitrary construction:** The claim that "facts are socially constructed" does not mean they are false or invented. Constructed facts can accurately describe real phenomena; the construction is about the *process*, not the *product*.

3. **Different domains, different degrees of social mediation:** The social component in the construction of mathematical proofs (peer review, notation conventions, community acceptance) is different from the social component in the construction of political-economic claims. SSK is most illuminating in contested, pre-paradigmatic, or crisis-state science; it is less illuminating for well-established normal science.

4. **Reflexivity is genuine but manageable:** The strong programme must apply to itself, but this does not collapse into paralysis. It means doing the sociology of knowledge with awareness that it is itself socially situated.

## The Connection to Cultural Evolution

The cultural evolution tradition and SSK are asking related but distinct questions:

| | Cultural Evolution | Sociology of Scientific Knowledge |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit of analysis** | Ideas, beliefs, practices in a population | Scientific claims, facts, paradigms |
| **Central question** | Which ideas spread and why? | How do claims become accepted as facts? |
| **Key mechanisms** | Transmission biases, selection, drift | Negotiation, network-building, inscription, closure |
| **Relationship to truth** | Fitness vs. truth (fitness wins in short run) | Socially constructed vs. corresponding to reality |
| **Policy implication** | Design institutions that select for truth-apt beliefs | Design science to maintain CUDOS norms and open review |

Both frameworks converge on the insight that **ideas don't spread just because they're true.** Social mechanisms determine which ideas become taken for granted, which are invisible, and which are contested.

## The Idea-Fitness-vs-Truth Connection

The cultural evolution file `knowledge/social-science/cultural-evolution/idea-fitness-vs-truth.md` asks: why do maladaptive beliefs persist? SSK provides one answer specific to scientific communities: the social machinery of science (peer review, citation, prestige) can become captured by ideas that are socially fit (supported by well-funded networks, prestigious institutions) but not epistemically superior. The replication crisis is a case study: publication bias, p-hacking, and prestige effects made certain theories "facts" that were socially robust but empirically fragile.

## The AI-Specific Application

AI development exhibits strong-programme dynamics:
- The **deep learning paradigm** was constructed through the enrollment of a specific community (primarily Hinton, LeCun, Bengio's networks), funding structures (NIPS/NeurIPS, Google Brain, FAIR), and computational infrastructure (GPU clusters)
- Competing approaches (probabilistic graphical models, kernel methods, symbolic AI) did not simply fail for internal reasons; they were outcompeted in a network-building contest
- The current paradigm encodes choices — scale as the primary variable, gradient descent as the optimization method, benchmark performance as the evaluation criterion — that are only beginning to be questioned

Opening the black box of the deep learning paradigm is a cultural and institutional project as much as a scientific one.
