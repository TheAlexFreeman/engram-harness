# Merton: Scientific Norms and the Sociology of Science

## Overview

Robert K. Merton (1910–2003) was the founding figure of the sociology of science as a legitimate academic field. His central contribution was identifying the **institutional norms** of science — the shared values and imperatives that constitute science as a distinct social institution and, when functioning properly, make it an unusually reliable truth-producing system. Merton's framework is both descriptive (what norms do scientists actually follow?) and functional (what norms would a knowledge-producing institution need in order to work reliably?). The contrast with purely individual-based epistemology is sharp: for Merton, science's power to produce knowledge is an *institutional* achievement, not just a property of individual rationality.

## The CUDOS Norms

Merton identified four normative imperatives in "Science and Technology in a Democratic Order" (1942, later reprinted as "The Normative Structure of Science"):

### 1. Communalism (Communism)

Scientific knowledge is **public property.** The results of research must be communicated and shared with the scientific community; they cannot be privately held. This norm flows from the collective character of science — knowledge builds on prior work, and the community cannot advance if results are hoarded.

Implications: priority disputes (who discovered it first) are common and important because the only legitimate form of "property" in science is priority of discovery. Once results are communicated (by publication), they become the common property of the community.

This norm is structurally opposed to commercial secrecy. When science is conducted under patent conditions or corporate interests that require withholding results, the communalism norm is violated, and the institution functions less well as a truth-tracking system.

### 2. Universalism

Claims to truth are evaluated by **impersonal, pre-established criteria** — internal consistency, agreement with prior knowledge, compatibility with evidence — not by the personal or social attributes of the claimant. A claim should be accepted or rejected based on its scientific merit, not on the nationality, race, gender, religion, or class of the person making it.

This is an anti-discrimination norm in epistemological disguise. Its opposite in practice was "German physics" or "proletarian science" — ideological programs that evaluated scientific claims by the identity of the claimant (Jewish physics is bourgeois physics, therefore wrong) rather than by evidence.

Universalism connects to Mannheim's free-floating intellectual: if knowledge is to transcend particular social positions, the evaluation of knowledge claims must be universalistic — whoever you are, your results are evaluated the same way.

### 3. Disinterestedness

Scientists are expected to behave as if they have no personal stake in the outcome of any particular investigation. This does not mean scientists have no interests; it means the institutional structure creates incentives that approximate disinterested inquiry.

The mechanism: scientific findings are subject to public scrutiny and criticism. Fraud, wishful thinking, and self-serving results will be detected by competitors who have every incentive to find errors. The community therefore acts as a check on individual motivated reasoning, even if individual scientists are not themselves disinterested.

This is a **structural** claim, not a psychological claim. It does not depend on scientists being unusually virtuous; it depends on the institution organizing incentives so that disinterested inquiry is rewarded.

### 4. Organized Skepticism

Knowledge claims must be subjected to **systematic, impartial scrutiny** before being incorporated into the body of scientific knowledge. No authority — religious, political, or scientific — is above critical examination.

Organized skepticism is institutionalized in peer review, replication requirements, and the perpetual openness of scientific results to refutation. It is the operational mechanism by which universalism and disinterestedness are enforced.

Organized skepticism is in structural tension with other institutions: religion, politics, and commercial enterprise all have authority structures that require deference to certain claims. Science's insistence on scrutinizing everything (including foundational assumptions) makes it institutionally distinctive.

### The CUDOS Acronym

The four norms are sometimes remembered as CUDOS:
- **C**ommunalism
- **U**niversalism
- **D**isinterestedness
- **O**rganized **S**kepticism

Some presentations add a fifth: **Originality** (CUDOS becomes CUDOS+O or the variation is noted separately). Science rewards novel contributions; merely confirming what is already known does not generate prestige.

## The Matthew Effect

Beyond the CUDOS norms, Merton's most empirically influential concept is the **Matthew Effect** (1968), named from Matthew 25:29: "For to every one who has will more be given, and he will have abundance; but from him who has not, even what he has will be taken away."

In science, the Matthew Effect refers to the phenomenon that **credit, recognition, and resources accumulate disproportionately to those who already have prestige.** A discovery by a famous scientist is attributed to them, remembered under their name, and given more weight — even when the actual contribution came primarily from students or collaborators.

### Manifestations

- Papers by high-prestige authors are more likely to be published, cited, and credited
- When the same discovery is made independently, priority is usually attributed to the more famous researcher (Merton's own concept of "obliteration by incorporation")
- Grant funding follows track record more than promise
- The first publication of a concept gets cited forever; corrections and refinements by less-famous scientists are forgotten

### Implications

The Matthew Effect is a deviation from universalism: the evaluation of claims is systematically contaminated by the social authority of the claimant. Merton saw this as a tension within science between the universalistic ideal and the prestige-stratified reality.

From a cultural evolution perspective, the Matthew Effect is a prestige bias operating on scientific ideas: ideas associated with high-prestige researchers spread more readily than their epistemic quality alone would warrant. ## The Normative vs. Descriptive Question

A persistent debate: Do scientists actually follow the CUDOS norms? Empirical sociology of science has repeatedly shown deviations:

- **Priority disputes** reveal that scientists are intensely interested in personal credit (violating disinterestedness in practice though perhaps reinforcing it structurally)
- **Peer review** shows systematic bias toward prestige, novelty, and positive results
- **Fraud and data manipulation** occur and are not always detected promptly
- **The replication crisis** (psychology, nutrition science, medicine) shows that organized skepticism failed to maintain quality in many fields

Two responses:
1. **Mertonian functionalism:** The norms are ideals — what science *should* do to work well. Deviations are pathologies, detected and corrected over time (the long-run truth-tracking claim).
2. **SSK critique (Bloor, Barnes, Collins):** The norms are ideological self-presentation, not the real mechanism of science. What actually happens in labs is negotiation, power, and rhetoric — the CUDOS norms are a legitimating myth.

The truth is likely between: the CUDOS norms shape behavior in systematic ways even when violated, because they define what counts as a legitimate accusation and a legitimate response.

## Counter-Norms

Ian Mitroff (1974) studied NASA scientists and identified a set of **counter-norms** that co-exist with the Mertonian norms in practice:

| CUDOS Norm | Counter-norm |
|------------|-------------|
| Communalism | Secrecy (protecting priority until publication) |
| Universalism | Particularism (judging by the source) |
| Disinterestedness | Self-interestedness in one's own findings |
| Organized skepticism | Organized dogmatism (protecting accepted theory) |

Counter-norms reveal that the norms are not simply followed or violated — they are in active tension within scientific practice.

## Connection to Cultural Evolution and AI

Merton's framework matters for understanding why scientific knowledge is unusually reliable despite being socially produced:
- Communalism ensures redundant independent checking
- Universalism limits the prestige bias in claim-evaluation
- Organized skepticism institutionalizes error correction

On the AI side: LLMs trained on scientific literature absorb the outputs of this institution, including its biases (Matthew Effect, replication failures) and its strengths (accumulated verified knowledge). The quality of the cultural substrate matters.
