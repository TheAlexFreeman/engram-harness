# Latour: Actor-Network Theory

## Overview

Bruno Latour (1947–2022), together with Michel Callon and John Law, developed **Actor-Network Theory** (ANT) in the 1980s and 1990s through laboratory ethnography and philosophical reflection. ANT's central move is to refuse the separation between the social and the technical, the human and the nonhuman. Instead, all entities — people, organizations, machines, documents, instruments, animals, concepts — can be actors in networks, and the task of the analyst is to **follow the actors** as they enroll one another, form alliances, and stabilize or destabilize configurations. ANT is less a theory than a method and sensibility: a commitment to not explaining anything in advance and to treating the social as something that must be performed and held together rather than a background context.

## Core Concepts

### Translation and Enrollment

The central mechanism in ANT is **translation**: the process by which one actor redefines the interests and identities of other actors to align with their own project. To translate is to speak for others, to represent them, to make their interests equivalent to yours.

The stages of translation (from Callon's 1986 scallop paper):

1. **Problematization:** An actor defines a problem in such a way that other actors *need* to pass through them to achieve their goals. ("You want sustainable scallops? Then you need to work through us, the researchers who understand the larvae.") This establishes an **obligatory passage point**.

2. **Interessement:** Active work to lock other actors into the proposed roles. Devices of interessement (contracts, machines, arguments, alliances) hold other actors in place.

3. **Enrollment:** Interest becomes commitment — actors accept the roles defined for them by the translation. The network grows.

4. **Mobilization:** The enrolled actors become representatives of larger groups; a few scientists can now claim to speak for the scallops, the fishermen, the industry.

A paradigm, in ANT terms, is a highly stabilized translation: a configuration in which the interests of a large network of actors (researchers, funders, instruments, textbooks, journals) are locked together around a common program. Disrupting a paradigm requires disrupting the entire network.

### Black-Boxing

When a network stabilizes, its internal complexity becomes invisible — it becomes a **black box.** A scientific fact is black-boxed when it no longer needs to be defended; it is simply used as a resource in further work. A technology is black-boxed when you can use it without understanding how it works.

Black-boxing is both an achievement and a risk:
- **Achievement:** It allows cognitive efficiency; you don't need to re-derive the second law of thermodynamics every time you use it
- **Risk:** The black box encloses contestable choices, values, and assumptions that are no longer visible. Opening the black box requires a controversy or crisis

The history of science, in ANT, is a history of black-boxing and occasional black-box opening. Normal science (Kuhn) operates within black boxes; scientific revolutions pry open the boxes that have been shut too long.

### The Symmetry Principle

ANT's most radical methodological commitment: **explain the successful and the unsuccessful, the accepted and the rejected, by the same kinds of causes.** Do not explain scientific success by "it corresponded to reality" and failure by "it was caught up in social interests." Both require the same kinds of alliance-building, inscription, and network stabilization.

This symmetry principle extends further: **extend the same kind of analysis to human and nonhuman actors.** Microbes (Pasteur and the anthrax bacillus), machines (the Diesel engine), and texts (scientific papers) are all treated as actors that can have interests, form alliances, and reshape networks.

This is where ANT becomes philosophically provocative. Latour is not claiming that microbes have intentions; he is claiming that the *analyst* should not import assumptions about what kinds of things can act. If a microbe reliably transforms a network (by causing disease, selecting for immune responses, collapsing population structures), then the microbe is an actor in the relevant network.

### Inscription and Immutable Mobiles

A key mechanism for stabilizing scientific facts across space and time is the **inscription** — a trace or record that can be transported without changing its content. Graphs, papers, measurements, and images are inscriptions. They are **immutable mobiles**: movable (they can travel) and stable (they look the same when they arrive).

Scientific papers are powerful inscription devices: they translate complex, messy laboratory activities into stable written claims that can circulate globally and be cited in new networks. The journal article is an ANT actor that does enormous work in stabilizing facts.

Latour's *Science in Action* (1987) traces how a claim becomes a fact by following it forward in time (as it gets cited, replicated, enrolled in further work) and by "opening" it during production (showing the negotiations, choices, and contingencies in the laboratory). The same claim looks different depending on whether you observe it before or after stabilization.

### The Laboratory and Power

Latour and Woolgar's *Laboratory Life: The Social Construction of Scientific Facts* (1979/1986) was the first major laboratory ethnography from an ANT perspective. They treated the Salk Institute laboratory as if it were an anthropological field site — observing scientists, reading lab notes, interviewing, and tracing the conversion of materials and activities into inscriptions.

Their central claim: the laboratory is a machine for creating inscriptions that make claims credible. The social and material infrastructure of the lab (equipment, funding, personnel hierarchies, publication norms) shapes what claims can be made and stabilized.

This is not a claim that facts are made up; it is a claim that fact-making requires enormous work that goes invisible when the fact is stabilized. "Nature" does not speak for itself — it must be enrolled through instrumentation, measurement, inscription, and persuasion.

## ANT and Kuhn

ANT extends Kuhn in a specific direction:

- Kuhn showed that paradigm change is not purely rational and that community sociology matters
- ANT asks: what *exactly* holds a paradigm together? The answer: a heterogeneous network of humans and nonhumans (instruments, texts, institutions, funding, training programs) that must all be re-enrolled for a paradigm shift to occur
- ANT also refuses Kuhn's distinction between "science" and "society" — the network crosses that boundary

## ANT and AI Systems

ANT is particularly valuable for analyzing AI as a sociotechnical actor:

- AI systems are nonhuman actors that reshape networks: they replace some human activities, create new dependencies, and enroll new actors (data providers, compute suppliers, regulatory bodies)
- LLMs as inscription devices: they are immutable mobiles of enormous scope — absorbing inscriptions from the entire corpus of human writing and producing new inscriptions at scale
- AI deployment always creates new actor-networks: the human-AI assemblage is neither purely human nor purely machine
- The "alignment" problem in AI safety is, in ANT terms, a translation problem: how do you enroll an AI system into a network as a reliable ally rather than an adversary?

## Critiques

1. **Flattening of power:** By treating all actors symmetrically, ANT risks obscuring how power differences make some actors more capable of translation than others. Post-colonial critics and feminist STS scholars (Donna Haraway) have argued that symmetry can mask oppression.

2. **Agency without intention:** Extending agency to nonhumans is philosophically provocative but analytically slippery. What does it mean for a machine to enroll other actors?

3. **Descriptive, not prescriptive:** ANT is a method for *following* activity, not a normative theory for evaluating it. It offers no criteria for distinguishing good science from bad science, just more or less stable networks.

4. **Relativism concerns:** Like the SSK strong programme, ANT was accused of relativism — if facts are network effects, are all networks equally valid? Latour spent much of his later career (in *Reassembling the Social*, 2005) clarifying that ANT is not relativist but relationist.
