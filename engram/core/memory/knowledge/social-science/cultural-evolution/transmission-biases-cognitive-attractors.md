# Transmission Biases and Cognitive Attractors

## Overview

This file examines two complementary approaches to explaining why some cultural variants spread and persist while others don't: Boyd & Richerson's **transmission biases** (formalized in Dual Inheritance Theory) and Dan Sperber's **cognitive attractors** (formalized in the epidemiology of representations). Both address the same question — what determines cultural content — but from different theoretical starting points, and their tension is one of the most productive debates in cultural evolution research.

## Boyd & Richerson: Transmission Biases (Detailed)

Boyd and Richerson's framework (covered in the companion DIT file) identifies four bias types. Here we focus on the **content biases** — intrinsic properties of ideas that make them more or less transmissible — and their interaction with model-based biases.

### Content Biases in Detail

Content biases arise because human cognition is not a blank slate. Evolved cognitive architecture makes some ideas easier to process, remember, and transmit:

#### Minimally Counterintuitive (MCI) Concepts

Pascal Boyer (2001) and Justin Barrett (2004) showed that **minimally counterintuitive concepts** have a transmission advantage. An MCI concept is one that violates exactly one core ontological expectation:

- A rock that listens (physical object + mental property violation)
- A person who can walk through walls (agent + physical property violation)
- A tree that remembers (plant + cognitive property violation)

MCI concepts are memorable because:
1. They mostly fit intuitive ontological categories → easy to represent
2. They violate exactly one expectation → surprising, attention-grabbing
3. The violation is small enough to process → not overwhelming

**Maximally counterintuitive** concepts (a tree that thinks, remembers, walks, and is invisible) are harder to process and remember. **Intuitive** concepts (a tree that grows) are not surprising enough to be preferentially transmitted. MCI concepts hit a cognitive sweet spot.

**Empirical evidence:** Barrett & Nyhof (2001) showed that participants who read stories containing a mix of intuitive, MCI, and maximally counterintuitive items recalled MCI items best after delays. Norenzayan et al. (2006) replicated this and showed the effect applies to cultural persistence — folklore and religious narratives worldwide are enriched for MCI concepts.

#### Emotional Content

Content that triggers emotional responses — threat, disgust, humor, social status changes — is preferentially transmitted:

- **Threat and disgust:** Heath, Bell, & Sternberg (2001) showed that urban legends that evoke stronger disgust are shared more widely
- **Social information:** Gossip about norm violations, status changes, and mate competition is transmitted at higher rates than neutral social information (Mesoudi, Whiten, & Dunbar 2006)
- **Narrative structure:** Stories with clear goal-directed agents, obstacles, and resolutions are transmitted with higher fidelity than stories without narrative structure (Mesoudi & Whiten 2008)

#### Relevance and Problem-Solving

Ideas that help solve current problems are preferentially adopted. This is obvious but important: cultural transmission is not solely driven by cognitive fit or emotional impact. **Functional relevance** — "does this help me now?" — is a powerful filter.

### Model-Based Biases: Prestige and Its Distortions

Prestige bias, though rational in ancestral contexts (copy the best hunter), produces systematic distortions in modern information environments:

#### The Prestige-Domain Transfer Problem

When you copy a prestigious individual on the dimension that earned their prestige, you get useful information. But prestige bias causes **domain transfer** — copying the prestigious individual on *unrelated* dimensions:

- A successful physicist's opinions on politics
- A wealthy businessperson's views on medicine
- A famous actor's dietary recommendations

This is not a corruption of prestige bias but its *normal operation* — ancestral environments were small-scale and multidimensional enough that a good hunter was plausibly a good advisor on many topics. The modern information environment, with extreme specialization and celebrity culture, makes domain transfer systematically misleading.

#### CRISPy Content (Credibility-Enhancing Displays)

Henrich (2009) introduced the concept of **CREDs** (Credibility-Enhancing Displays): behaviors by a model that are costly and consistent with the model's professed beliefs. CREDs increase prestige and thus increase copying:

- A teacher who practices what they preach (costs of compliance → credibility)
- A religious leader who fasts (costly signal → "they really believe this")
- A CEO who uses their own company's products

CREDs solve the cheap-talk problem: anyone can *say* their beliefs, but costly behavior consistent with those beliefs is harder to fake. However, CREDs can also be exploited: deliberate costly signaling to gain prestige and influence.

## Sperber: The Epidemiology of Representations

### The Alternative Framework

Dan Sperber (1996, *Explaining Culture*) proposed a fundamentally different approach to cultural evolution. Rather than treating culture as a system of replicators undergoing Darwinian selection, Sperber modeled cultural spread as **epidemiology** — the spread of representations through a population, analogous to the spread of disease.

### Key Distinction: Replication vs. Reconstruction

Sperber's central argument against memetics: **cultural transmission does not involve high-fidelity copying.** When I tell you a story, you don't receive a copy of my mental representation. Instead:

1. I produce a **public representation** (speech, writing, gesture) based on my mental representation
2. You perceive the public representation
3. You **construct** a new mental representation that is (usually) similar to mine — but not identical

Each transmission event involves two transformations:
- Mental → public (production)
- Public → mental (comprehension)

Both transformations are lossy and constructive. The hearer's mental representation is **influenced by** but not **copied from** the speaker's mental representation. This means cultural transmission is fundamentally different from genetic replication — it is *reconstruction*, not *replication*.

### Cognitive Attractors

If cultural transmission is reconstructive rather than replicative, what explains cultural stability? Why don't ideas degrade to noise across a few transmissions (the telephone game problem)?

Sperber's answer: **cognitive attractors**. Human cognitive architecture contains biases and predispositions that pull reconstructed ideas toward certain forms, regardless of the exact input.

#### The Attractor Concept

An attractor in Sperber's sense is a region in the space of possible representations toward which transmitted ideas tend to converge. It is *not* a specific idea that gets copied; it is a **basin of attraction** created by cognitive biases.

Formal analogy: imagine rolling balls across a landscape with valleys and hills. Each ball (a transmitted idea) doesn't follow the exact trajectory of the previous ball. But many balls end up in the same valleys because the landscape (cognitive architecture) channels them there.

#### Examples

**Color categorization:** Languages differ in their color terms, but there are cross-cultural regularities. Berlin & Kay (1969) showed that color term systems across languages tend to converge on similar focal colors. This is an attractor effect: the human visual system creates perceptual peaks at certain wavelengths, and color terms are reconstructed toward these peaks across transmissions.

**Folktale structure:** Propp (1928) identified recurring structural elements in folktales across cultures. Sperber would explain this not as high-fidelity copying of a proto-folktale, but as cognitive attractors — narrative structures that fit the human capacity for goal-directed agent modeling, causal reasoning, and moral evaluation.

**Numerical cognition:** Dehaene (1997) showed that humans have an innate approximate number sense that creates attractors for certain numerical representations (small integers, round numbers, certain ratios). Cultural number systems converge toward these attractors.

### Attractors vs. Replicators

| Dimension | Replicator model (Dawkins/DIT) | Attractor model (Sperber) |
|-----------|-------------------------------|-------------------------|
| Transmission | Copying (high-fidelity replication) | Reconstruction (constructive process) |
| Stability source | Fidelity of copying | Convergence toward attractors |
| Variation source | Copying errors (mutations) | Reconstruction variance |
| Selection | Differential replication of variants | Differential attraction — some forms are more likely reconstruction targets |
| Unit | Meme (discrete, countable) | No privileged unit — representations are continuous |
| Formalism | Population genetics models | Epidemiological models |

### Sperber's Challenge to DIT

Sperber's challenge to Boyd and Richerson is precise: if cultural transmission is fundamentally reconstructive, then the population genetics models that DIT uses (which assume discrete, heritable units with high-fidelity transmission) are inappropriate. The mathematics of epidemiology (which models transformation, not replication) is the correct framework.

### Boyd & Richerson's Response

Boyd and Richerson (and Henrich) have responded in several ways:

1. **Sufficient fidelity, not perfect fidelity.** Cultural evolution doesn't require gene-level fidelity. It requires that the signal-to-noise ratio be high enough for cumulative selection to operate. Human teaching, institutional transmission (books, laws), and conformity bias provide this.

2. **Attractors and biases are complementary, not competing.** An "attractor" in Sperber's sense is equivalent to a "content bias" in DIT terms. Both describe the tendency of human cognition to process and reconstruct ideas in certain directions. The difference is formalism, not substance.

3. **Cumulative cultural evolution is the proof.** If cultural transmission were as noisy as Sperber suggests, cumulative culture (increasingly complex technology, institutions, knowledge) would be impossible — each generation would reconstruct from scratch. The existence of cumulative culture proves that fidelity is sufficient.

## Synthesis: What Each Framework Contributes

### Sperber Is Right About:
- Transmission is constructive, not purely replicative
- Cognitive architecture shapes the *direction* of cultural change, not just its *rate*
- The "unit" problem in memetics is real — there is no discrete, countable cultural atom
- Attractors explain cultural universals better than selection alone

### Boyd & Richerson Are Right About:
- Cultural evolution is genuinely Darwinian (variation + selection + inheritance), not merely epidemiological
- Cumulative culture requires some level of fidelity, even if imperfect
- Institutional/technological mechanisms (writing, teaching, law) provide the fidelity that cognitive reconstruction alone cannot
- Mathematical population models generate testable predictions that verbal attractor models do not

### The Productive Integration

Henrich & Boyd (2002) proposed a synthesis: use Sperber's attractors to model the *micro-dynamics* of individual transmission events, and Boyd & Richerson's population models to model the *macro-dynamics* of cultural change across populations. The attractor landscape determines the direction of guided variation; the population dynamics determine which variants ultimately dominate.

## Implications for the Engram System

### 1. The System Has Both Biases and Attractors

Engram's knowledge pipeline exhibits both:
- **Biases:** Prestige (from successful sessions), frequency (access counts), content (well-structured files), conformity (citing popular existing knowledge)
- **Attractors:** The agent's base model creates cognitive attractors — it will reconstruct stored knowledge toward forms that fit its training distribution, its current task context, and its session prompt. This means retrieved knowledge is always reconstructed, not merely copied.

### 2. Attractor Dynamics Explain Summarization Drift

When the agent summarizes a knowledge file, it reconstructs the content toward its cognitive attractors (its training distribution, the current session context). Over multiple summarization cycles (file → summary → new file influenced by summary), the content can drift toward these attractors and away from the original. This is exactly Sperber's reconstruction-based drift.

**Mitigation:** Preserve originals (git history), limit summarization depth, periodically compare summaries to source files for drift detection.

### 3. Content Bias Predicts Which Knowledge Files Will Dominate

Files that are:
- Well-structured (narrative clarity, clear headers, tables)
- Emotionally/intellectually engaging (surprising findings, practical applications)
- MCI-adjacent (connecting familiar concepts in unexpected ways)

...will be more influential in context (more likely to be attended to, more likely to influence output) than files that are dense, abstract, or poorly organized — regardless of accuracy. This is a content bias that the curation system should track and potentially correct for.
