# Boyd & Richerson: Dual Inheritance Theory

## Overview

Robert Boyd and Peter Richerson developed **Dual Inheritance Theory** (DIT), also called **gene-culture coevolution theory**, across three foundational books: *Culture and the Evolutionary Process* (1985), *Not by Genes Alone* (2005), and *The Origin and Evolution of Cultures* (2005, collected essays). DIT is the most mathematically rigorous and empirically productive framework for studying cultural evolution, and the one taken most seriously by the broader scientific community.

## The Core Framework

### Two Inheritance Systems

DIT models culture as a **second inheritance system** operating alongside genetic inheritance. The key propositions:

1. **Culture is information** acquired from other individuals through social learning (teaching, imitation, observation). It is stored in brains (and artifacts), transmitted across generations, and subject to evolutionary dynamics.

2. **Cultural inheritance is distinct from genetic inheritance.** Culture has its own transmission rules, its own population dynamics, and its own evolutionary forces. You cannot reduce cultural evolution to genetic evolution — but you also cannot ignore their interaction.

3. **Gene-culture coevolution.** The two inheritance systems influence each other:
   - Genes shape the psychology that governs cultural learning (biases, cognitive architecture)
   - Culture shapes the selective environment that governs genetic evolution (agriculture → lactose tolerance; cooking → smaller jaws; norm enforcement → prosocial gene variants)
   - The feedback loop can drive rapid evolutionary change in both systems simultaneously

### Mathematical Population Thinking

Unlike memetics (which tends toward verbal theorizing), DIT is built on **mathematical population genetics models** adapted for cultural transmission. Boyd and Richerson use formal models to derive predictions about:

- When social learning will be favored over individual learning
- Which transmission biases will evolve
- How cultural group selection can operate even when individual selection opposes it
- How conformity and prestige bias interact to produce cultural stability and change

This mathematical grounding is what distinguishes DIT from the more speculative memetics tradition.

## When Is Social Learning Adaptive?

### The Rogers Paradox

Alan Rogers (1988) proved a counterintuitive result: in a simple model, **social learners (who copy others) do no better than individual learners (who learn from scratch).** This is because as social learners increase in frequency, the information they copy becomes increasingly outdated (since nobody is updating it through costly individual learning). Social learners free-ride on individual learners, but if everyone becomes a social learner, there's nothing worth copying.

### Boyd and Richerson's Resolution

Boyd and Richerson showed that the paradox dissolves when social learners are **selective** about *what* and *from whom* they copy. Adaptive social learning requires transmission biases — rules about when to copies and whose behavior to copy. This makes bias the central mechanism in cultural evolution.

## Transmission Biases

Boyd and Richerson identified several classes of transmission bias:

### 1. Content Bias (Direct Bias)

Some ideas are easier to learn, remember, or accept because of their intrinsic content. These spread not because of who holds them but because of what they are.

- **Cognitive naturalness:** Ideas that fit innate cognitive architecture spread more easily. Folk physics (objects fall), folk biology (species are natural kinds), folk psychology (agents have beliefs and desires) are easy to learn because they match evolved cognitive modules.
- **Emotional salience:** Threat-related content, gossip, and sexually relevant information spread preferentially because they engage evolved attention systems.
- **Simplicity/compressibility:** Simpler ideas spread faster than complex ones, all else equal.

Content bias explains why some incorrect ideas persist (they fit cognitive biases) and why some correct ideas resist adoption (they violate cognitive naturalness — e.g., quantum mechanics, natural selection, statistical reasoning).

### 2. Frequency-Dependent Bias (Conformist Bias)

Copy the majority. When uncertain, adopt the behavior or belief of the majority in your local population.

**Why conformity evolves:** Boyd and Richerson (1985) showed mathematically that conformist bias is adaptive when:
- Individual learning is costly or unreliable
- The environment changes slowly relative to generation time
- Spatial variation exceeds temporal variation (the locally common behavior is likely the locally adaptive one)

**Effects:**
- Stabilizes cultural variants within groups (reduces within-group variation)
- Maintains differences between groups (different groups converge on different norms)
- Creates cultural inertia (resistant to innovation, slow to change)
- Can maintain maladaptive traditions (if the majority does it, individuals conform even against their own information)

**Empirical evidence:** Experimental studies (Efferson et al. 2008; Morgan et al. 2012) confirm conformist-biased social learning in humans, and that its strength increases with uncertainty.

### 3. Model-Based Bias (Prestige Bias, Success Bias)

Copy successful or prestigious individuals. When uncertain about *what* to do, identify *who* is doing well and copy them.

Two variants:
- **Success bias:** Copy the behavior of individuals who are observably successful at the relevant task. Requires ability to assess success directly.
- **Prestige bias:** Copy individuals who are accorded *prestige* (deference, attention, voluntary following) by others. Prestige is a proxy for success used when direct assessment is difficult.

**Why this matters:**
- Prestige bias enables cultural transmission of complex skills that take years to evaluate (medicine, engineering, hunting strategy) by using social attention as a shortcut.
- Prestige bias creates **prestige cascades**: prestigious individuals are copied on *many dimensions*, not just the dimension that made them prestigious. A successful hunter's food-sharing norms, ritual practices, and child-rearing advice are all copied, even though prestige was earned through hunting skill. This creates "package deal" transmission.
- In modern contexts: celebrity endorsement, influencer culture, and the authority heuristic are all expressions of prestige-biased transmission detached from its original adaptive context.

### 4. Guided Variation

Individuals don't just copy; they **modify** what they copy based on individual learning, insight, or trial-and-error. This injects new variation into the cultural system — the cultural analog of mutation, but directed (toward perceived improvement) rather than random.

Guided variation is crucial because it means cultural evolution is *Lamarckian* — acquired improvements can be transmitted. This is one of the fundamental differences between cultural and genetic evolution, and it's what makes cultural evolution so much faster.

## Gene-Culture Coevolution: Worked Examples

### Lactose Tolerance

The canonical case of gene-culture coevolution:
1. ~10,000 years ago: dairying cultures domesticate cattle (cultural innovation)
2. Milk-drinking provides caloric advantage, but adults are lactose intolerant (genetic constraint)
3. Mutations for adult lactase persistence arise (genetic variation)
4. In dairying populations, these mutations are strongly selected (~0.05–0.1 selection coefficient — extremely strong)
5. Today: populations with long dairying history have 80–90% lactase persistence; non-dairying populations have 0–20%

**Key point:** The genetic change (lactase persistence) was driven by a cultural change (dairying). Culture changed the selective environment for genes. This is gene-culture coevolution: neither system alone explains the outcome.

### Norm Psychology

Boyd and Richerson, along with Joe Henrich, argue that human norm psychology — the capacity to identify, internalize, and enforce social norms — is itself a product of gene-culture coevolution:

1. Cultural group selection favors groups with prosocial norms (cooperation, reciprocity, punishment of defectors)
2. Within these norm-governed groups, genetic evolution favors individuals who can rapidly learn, internalize, and comply with local norms (norm-learners outcompete norm-violators)
3. This creates a genetically evolved "norm psychology" — a cognitive architecture specialized for detecting norms, feeling guilt at violation, and punishing violators
4. The norm psychology then enables yet more complex norms, further strengthening cultural group selection

The result: humans are uniquely *prepared* for norm-governed cultural learning. We don't just learn skills — we learn *rules*, and we feel strong emotions (guilt, shame, outrage) when rules are violated. This is genetically evolved, but the specific content of the norms is culturally transmitted.

## Implications for the Engram System

### 1. The System Has Transmission Biases

The Engram curation pipeline embodies specific transmission biases:
- **Content bias:** Files that are well-written, well-structured, and connect to existing knowledge are more likely to be promoted (structural quality as analog of cognitive naturalness)
- **Frequency bias:** Files that are accessed often gain higher trust metrics (a form of popularity-weighted selection)
- **Prestige bias:** Files associated with successful sessions (sessions the user values) gain prestige by association — the session is the "prestigious model"

Understanding these as transmission biases (rather than as neutral quality filters) is important: biases can systematically distort the knowledge base in predictable ways.

### 2. Guided Variation Is the Primary Innovation Mechanism

When the agent loads knowledge files and produces new content influenced by them, this is guided variation — not random mutation. The agent reconstructs and modifies ideas in the direction of perceived improvement. This is both the system's greatest strength (rapid, directed learning) and a vulnerability (systematic biases in "improvement" can compound across sessions).

### 3. Conformity Bias Can Create Epistemic Monoculture

If the system preferentially loads and builds on frequently-accessed files, it creates a conformity dynamic: popular ideas become even more popular, while minority ideas starve. This can eliminate useful diversity and create epistemic monoculture — a single dominant perspective that is never challenged because alternative views are never loaded.

Mitigation: intentional injection of diverse/contrarian knowledge files, access-frequency-independent retrieval, and periodic review of under-accessed files.

### 4. Gene-Culture Coevolution as Model for Agent-System Coevolution

The agent's "genetics" (its base model weights, fixed architecture) coevolve with its "culture" (the knowledge base, curation policies, session history):
- The knowledge base shapes what the agent can do (culture shapes cognition)
- The agent's capabilities shape what knowledge gets produced (cognition shapes culture)
- This creates feedback loops that can amplify biases, extend capabilities, or produce brittleness

DIT's mathematical framework for modeling these feedback loops is directly applicable to agent-memory system design.
