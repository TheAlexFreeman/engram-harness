# Blackmore: The Meme Machine

## The Central Thesis

Susan Blackmore's *The Meme Machine* (1999) is the most radical extension of Dawkins's meme concept. Where Dawkins offered the meme as a provocative analogy in a single chapter, and Dennett developed it as a useful interpretive tool, Blackmore tried to build an entire theory of human cognition and culture on memetic foundations.

Her central thesis: **imitation is the defining capacity of the human species**, and everything distinctive about human culture — language, religion, art, science, the self — is the product of memes, not genes. Humans are "meme machines" — vehicles constructed by and for the propagation of memes.

## Imitation as the Key

### The Imitation Threshold

Blackmore argues that what separates humans from other animals is not intelligence, tool use, or language per se, but the capacity for *generalized imitation* — the ability to copy arbitrary novel behaviors after observing them. Other animals can learn through conditioning and in some cases through limited forms of social learning, but only humans routinely copy complex, multi-step novel behaviors from conspecifics.

This capacity — crossing the "imitation threshold" — opened a second evolutionary channel. Once organisms can imitate, anything that *can be* imitated will be: successful behaviors, vocal patterns, gestures, rituals. These copied units are memes. And once memes exist, memetic selection begins, operating alongside and eventually partially *independent of* genetic selection.

### Meme-Gene Coevolution

The power of the argument is in the feedback loop:
1. Memes that help their hosts survive and reproduce spread (beneficial symbiosis)
2. Memes that spread regardless of host fitness also spread (parasitism, neutral drift)
3. Genetic evolution selects for brains that are better at transmitting memes — larger brains, better vocal apparatus, longer childhoods for cultural learning
4. This creates a positive feedback loop: better meme transmission → more memes → stronger selection for meme-capable brains → even better meme transmission

Blackmore calls this "memetic drive" — the idea that memes drove human brain expansion, not the other way around. The extraordinary enlargement of the human brain over the last 2 million years is, in this view, a product of selection for memetic capacity.

## Temes: The Third Replicator

In a 2008 TED talk and subsequent publications, Blackmore extended the framework to propose a **third replicator**: the *teme* (technological meme). Just as memes use human brains as hosts and are copied through imitation, temes use technological infrastructure as hosts and are copied through digital processes.

### The Argument

- Genes use biological machinery to replicate. Humans are gene machines.
- Memes use neural/cultural machinery to replicate. Humans are meme machines.
- Temes use digital/computational machinery to replicate. Computers (and AI systems) are teme machines.

The critical transition: at some point, the memes stored in digital infrastructure no longer need human brains for their replication. A recommendation algorithm propagates content without any human "imitating" anything — the algorithm selects, copies, and distributes information units autonomously. The teme is a replicator that has freed itself from its original host.

### Relevance to AI Memory Systems

The teme concept is directly relevant to the Engram system:
- Knowledge files in the repository are temes: they are copied, transformed, and propagated through computational processes
- The curation pipeline is a selection mechanism for temes
- When the agent loads a knowledge file into context and produces a new file influenced by it, this is teme replication through a digital host — no human imitation required
- The question becomes: **whose interests do the temes serve?** The human who created the system? The agent that processes them? The temes themselves (in the Dawkins/Dennett intentional-stance sense)?

## The Extreme Position: Self and Free Will

### The Self as Memeplex

Blackmore's most provocative claim: **the self is a memeplex** — a co-adapted complex of memes that creates the illusion of a unified, continuous agent. "I" am not a biological entity that *has* memes; "I" am a memetic construction that *is* memes.

The argument:
1. The conscious "self" has no unified biological substrate (cf. Dennett, Metzinger)
2. Our self-narratives, beliefs, values, and personality traits are all learned/imitated — they are memes
3. These memes cluster into a co-adapted complex (the "selfplex") that protects itself: we resist changing our beliefs, defend our values, maintain narrative consistency
4. The selfplex creates the illusion of a persistent "I" who has experiences, makes choices, and holds beliefs — but this "I" is a construction of the memes, not a pre-existing entity that acquires them

### Free Will Denial

If the self is a memeplex, then "free will" is an illusion generated by the selfplex to justify its own decisions. We don't choose our beliefs and actions; memes compete for expression through us, and the "choice" is the outcome of that competition, retrospectively narrated by the selfplex as deliberate agency.

Blackmore connects this to Buddhist philosophy: the recognition that the self is an illusion is, in Buddhist terms, the beginning of enlightenment. In memetic terms, it is the recognition that we are not the authors of our thoughts — we are the medium through which memes propagate.

## Critiques

### The "Everything is a Meme" Problem

If memes explain language, religion, art, science, the self, consciousness, brain evolution, and free will, the concept has become so broad that it explains nothing. Sperber (1996) and Atran (2001) argue that memetics provides a *redescription* of cultural phenomena, not an *explanation*. Calling something a "meme" doesn't tell you *why* it spreads or what cognitive mechanisms produce, transmit, and modify it.

### Imitation is Not That Simple

The claim that imitation is the foundational mechanism faces empirical challenges:
- Much cultural transmission works through **instruction** (explicit teaching), not imitation
- **Reverse engineering** — observing outcomes and reconstructing the process — is different from imitation
- **Emulation** — achieving the same goal through a different method — is widespread in other primates
- Tomasello (1999) argues that the key capacity is **shared intentionality**, not imitation per se

### The Selfplex Problem

If the self is a memeplex, who is it that *recognizes* this and makes a philosophical argument about it? The critique is self-referential: Blackmore's own theory is a meme that, on its own account, is just competing for replication — it has no special claim to truth. Blackmore would likely accept this and argue that truth-tracking and memetic fitness happen to correlate in this case (the meme survives because it is accurate), but this doesn't fully resolve the regress.

### Empirical Thinness

Despite the ambitious theoretical framework, *The Meme Machine* offers relatively few empirical predictions that differ from standard evolutionary psychology, cognitive science, or cultural anthropology. The memetic framework *organizes* observations but seldom generates *novel* testable hypotheses. This is the persistent weakness of memetics as a research program.

## Value of Blackmore's Contribution

Despite these critiques, Blackmore's work is valuable for:

1. **Taking the meme concept seriously.** Where Dawkins half-retracted and retreated to analogy, Blackmore pushed the framework to its logical conclusions, making the strengths and weaknesses of the approach maximally visible.

2. **The imitation-threshold concept.** The idea that a specific cognitive capacity (generalized imitation) opens a new evolutionary channel is empirically productive, regardless of whether "meme" is the right unit to track.

3. **The teme concept.** The recognition that digital systems constitute a new replication medium — independent of human brains — anticipates the current AI alignment discourse by a decade. Whether or not "teme" becomes standard terminology, the underlying observation is important.

4. **Connecting to the self problem.** The idea that identity is a co-adapted complex of cultural units (rather than a biological given) is relevant to AI memory systems where agent identity is literally constructed from stored files.
