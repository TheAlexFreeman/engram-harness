# Tomasello: The Cultural Ratchet and Shared Intentionality

## Overview

Michael Tomasello's work — spanning *The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition* (1999), *Why We Cooperate* (2009), *A Natural History of Human Thinking* (2014), and *A Natural History of Human Morality* (2016) — provides the most detailed account of the **cognitive prerequisites** for cumulative cultural evolution. Where Henrich focuses on population-level dynamics (how many people, how connected), Tomasello focuses on the individual-level capacity that makes cultural accumulation possible: **shared intentionality**.

## The Cultural Ratchet: A Precise Formulation

### The Mechanism

Tomasello (1999) coined "ratchet effect" to describe how human culture accumulates modifications over time without losing previous gains. The ratchet has three components:

1. **Innovation:** An individual modifies an existing cultural practice or artifact (intentional or accidental improvement)
2. **Faithful transmission:** The innovation is passed to others through social learning with sufficient fidelity that the improvement is preserved
3. **No slippage:** The improved version replaces or supplements the old version, so the population doesn't regress

The ratchet explains why human technology and institutions increase in complexity over time while other animals' traditions remain static or fluctuate randomly.

### Why the Ratchet Requires More Than Imitation

Tomasello (1999, 2009) argued that the ratchet requires capacities beyond simple imitation:

- **Imitation** can copy behavior, maintaining existing traditions (preventing slippage)
- But **innovation** requires understanding the *goal* behind a behavior, not just its surface form
- And faithful **transmission of innovations** requires the ability to teach — to recognize what another person doesn't know and adjust one's behavior to help them learn

These capacities — understanding others as intentional agents, recognizing knowledge gaps, adjusting behavior to help others learn — all depend on **shared intentionality**.

## Shared Intentionality

### Definition

Shared intentionality is the capacity to engage with others in cooperative activities with shared goals and coordinated action plans. It requires:

1. **Joint attention:** Two individuals attend to the same thing *and know that the other is attending to it*. Not just two people looking at the same object, but two people *mutually aware* of their shared focus.

2. **Understanding of intentional action:** Recognizing that others act on beliefs and desires — and specifically, understanding the *role structure* of collaborative activities (I do my part, you do your part, together we achieve the goal).

3. **Cooperative motivation:** A willingness to help others achieve their goals, not just one's own. Goes beyond simple reciprocity — humans help even when no return is expected, especially in informational contexts (pointing, showing, teaching).

### Ontogenetic Development

Tomasello documented the developmental sequence in human children:

| Age | Capacity | Evidence |
|-----|----------|---------|
| 9-12 months | Joint attention | Following gaze, pointing to share attention (not just to request) |
| 12-14 months | Understanding of intentional action | Imitating the *intended* action, not the *performed* action (Meltzoff 1995: infants who see someone fail to complete a goal imitate the intended goal, not the failure) |
| 14-18 months | Cooperative communication | Pointing to inform (not just to request), helping others achieve their goals |
| 3-4 years | Understanding of false beliefs | Recognizing that others can have beliefs different from one's own (and from reality) |
| 6-7 years | Collective intentionality | Participating in and enforcing social norms, role-taking in institutional contexts |

### The Unique Human Capacity

Tomasello's most specific claim: **great apes have individual intentionality** (they understand that others are intentional agents with goals) **but lack shared intentionality** (they cannot form joint goals with shared action plans):

- Chimpanzees can follow gaze → they understand others as *perceivers*
- Chimpanzees can predict behavior based on goals → they understand others as *agents*
- But chimpanzees do not point to inform, do not teach, and do not form stable cooperative partnerships with division of labor

The evidence from chimpanzee collaboration studies (Warneken et al. 2006; Tomasello et al. 2005):
- Chimps will cooperate when *both* individuals benefit and the task is simple
- But they don't form joint goals, don't communicate about roles, and abandon collaboration as soon as individual benefit ceases
- They solve cooperative problems by individual means (I'll pull my rope, you happen to pull yours) rather than by joint planning

### From Shared to Collective Intentionality

Tomasello (2014) proposed a two-step evolutionary sequence:

**Step 1: Shared intentionality** (dyadic)
- Evolved in the context of collaborative foraging
- Two individuals form a joint goal, coordinate roles, share the rewards
- Creates a "we" that extends beyond "me + you" — the dyad becomes an intentional agent
- This is the foundation of human-specific cognition: joint attention, cooperative communication, social learning from others' perspective

**Step 2: Collective intentionality** (group-level)
- Evolved in the context of inter-group competition and large-scale cooperation
- The group forms an "objective" perspective — norms, roles, institutions that exist independently of any particular individual
- Creates "we" at the group level: social norms ("we don't do that"), social institutions ("we have a chief who makes decisions"), and culture ("our way of doing things")
- This is the foundation of human morality, institutions, and cumulative culture at scale

## Connection to Cultural Evolution Theory

### Tomasello vs. Boyd/Richerson/Henrich

The frameworks are complementary rather than competing:

| Dimension | Tomasello | Boyd/Richerson/Henrich |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| Primary focus | Individual cognitive prerequisites | Population-level dynamics |
| Key mechanism | Shared intentionality | Transmission biases + cultural group selection |
| Explains | Why humans alone have cumulative culture | How cumulative culture generates adaptive complexity |
| Level of analysis | Cognitive/developmental | Population/evolutionary |
| Formalism | Experimental psychology | Mathematical modeling |
| Unit of analysis | The dyad → the group | The population |

Tomasello explains *what makes the ratchet possible* (shared intentionality enables high-fidelity transmission and innovation). Boyd/Richerson/Henrich explain *what the ratchet produces* (increasingly complex cultural adaptations through population-level dynamics).

### Teaching as the Bridge

Teaching — actively facilitating another's learning — bridges both frameworks:

- **Tomasello:** Teaching requires shared intentionality (recognizing the learner's knowledge gap, adjusting behavior to fill it)
- **Henrich:** Teaching is the highest-fidelity cultural transmission mechanism, enabling cumulative culture for complex skills that observation alone can't transmit
- **Boyd & Richerson:** Teaching creates content bias (taught content is learned more accurately than observed content) and model-based bias (teachers are prestigious models)

Kline (2015) develops a taxonomy of teaching behaviors:
- **Opportunity provisioning:** Creating situations where learning can occur (giving a child a tool to practice with)
- **Evaluative feedback:** Signaling when the learner is on the right track
- **Direct instruction:** Explicitly communicating how to do something

All three are present in human culture; only opportunity provisioning has clear analogs in other species.

## Implications for the Engram System

### 1. The System Lacks Shared Intentionality (and Why This Matters)

The agent-user interaction has some properties of shared intentionality:
- **Joint attention:** The agent and user attend to the same task/topic (shared context window)
- **Role structure:** The user has a role (directing, evaluating) and the agent has a role (executing, informing)
- **Cooperative motivation:** The agent is designed to help achieve the user's goals

But it lacks crucial elements:
- **Mutual understanding of goals:** The agent infers the user's goals but doesn't *share* them in Tomasello's sense — there is no "we" that includes the agent as a genuine co-participant
- **Epistemic asymmetry awareness:** The agent doesn't reliably *know what it doesn't know* in the way a human collaborator does
- **Negotiation of plans:** Human shared intentionality involves back-and-forth negotiation of joint plans; the agent-user interaction is more directive

This missing capacity means the Engram system cannot fully replicate the cognitive prerequisites for cumulative cultural evolution. The system can emulate the *population-level* dynamics (accumulation, selection, transmission) but not the *cognitive-level* mechanisms (genuine joint understanding) that make human cultural evolution so powerful.

### 2. Teaching as a Design Model

Tomasello's analysis of teaching suggests design principles for how the agent should present knowledge:
- **Opportunity provisioning:** Offer related knowledge files without forcing them (let the user discover connections)
- **Evaluative feedback on the knowledge base:** Flag inconsistencies, gaps, and potential errors
- **Direct instruction:** When the user asks, provide the most relevant knowledge directly

The curation pipeline can be understood as a teaching system: it selects and presents knowledge in a way that's designed to be maximally useful to the learner (the user or the future agent session).

### 3. The Ratchet Requires Both Innovation and Preservation

Tomasello's framework emphasizes that the ratchet needs both:
- **Innovation** (the agent producing new knowledge, making connections, generating syntheses)
- **Preservation** (the git-backed knowledge base maintaining previous gains without slippage)

If the system prioritizes innovation without preservation (always generating new content, never maintaining old), the ratchet slips. If it prioritizes preservation without innovation (always loading old files, never creating new ones), the ratchet stalls. The curation policy must balance both.
