# The Internet Meme as Meta-Format

## The Problem of Two "Memes"

The word *meme* now carries two distinct meanings that are rarely kept apart:

1. **Dawkins's meme (1976):** A unit of cultural transmission that replicates, varies, and undergoes selection — the cultural analog of the gene. Abstract, theoretical, applies to any cultural idea (tunes, fashions, beliefs, practices).

2. **The internet meme (c. 2007–present):** A specific participatory format for digital humor — typically a template (image, video, verbal pattern) that circulates online and is remixed by many users. Concrete, observable, applies to a specific class of digital artifacts.

These two usages share vocabulary and some structural logic, but they are **not the same thing**. Conflating them produces confusion in both directions: it makes internet memes look like evidence for memetics theory when they are not, and it makes the theoretical concept look trivially obvious when the real work lies elsewhere.

Dawkins himself acknowledged the divergence. In a 2013 Wired interview, he described the internet meme as a "hijacking" of his original concept:

> "An internet meme is a hijacking of the original idea. Instead of mutating by random change and spreading by a form of Darwinian selection, internet memes are altered deliberately by human creativity."

The key distinction: Dawkins's memes are subject to *quasi-Darwinian* selection (random variation, differential fitness, passive spread). Internet memes are *intentionally* transformed by human participants who are *aware they are remixing* an existing template. The mechanism is participatory creativity, not random mutation.

This file documents the internet meme as a cultural meta-format — its structure, typology, spread dynamics, and significance — while keeping the relation to Dawkins's concept clear.

## Shifman's Definition: The Scholarly Anchor

Limor Shifman's *Memes in Digital Culture* (MIT Press, 2014) is the foundational academic treatment. Her definition has become standard:

> An internet meme is **(a) a group of digital items sharing common characteristics of content, form, and/or stance, which (b) were created with awareness of each other, and (c) were circulated, imitated, and transformed via the Internet by many users.**

Several features of this definition are worth unpacking:

### 1. A Group, Not a Single Item

The meme is not any individual image or video — it is the **family of related items** that share a template. "Distracted Boyfriend" is not a single photograph; it is the photograph *plus all the remixed versions* in which the boyfriend, girlfriend, and new attraction have been relabeled with different concepts. The meme is the template abstracted from its instances.

This is a crucial structural insight: the internet meme is a **type**, and individual instances are **tokens** of that type. The type (the template) is what spreads and accumulates cultural meaning; tokens are evidence of the type's vitality.

### 2. Three Memetic Dimensions

Shifman identifies three axes along which meme instances vary:

| Dimension | What it captures | Example (Distracted Boyfriend) |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| **Content** | What is depicted — the semantic proposition | New labels for the three figures |
| **Form** | How it looks/sounds — visual/audio structure | Always uses the same photograph; same framing |
| **Stance** | Attitude toward the subject and toward the audience | Irony, self-deprecation, social commentary |

Different meme types fix different dimensions and allow variation in others. The image macro fixes form and stance, varies content. The snowclone fixes a verbal construction (stance/form), varies content. Understanding which dimensions are fixed and which are free is key to understanding why a meme works.

### 3. Created with Awareness

This is the feature that most sharply separates internet memes from Dawkins's memes. When a user creates a new instance of the "One Does Not Simply" meme, they *know what they are doing*. The creative act is *intertextual* — its meaning depends on the reader recognizing the template and its history. A meme instance that looks like "One Does Not Simply" but is created by someone who has never seen the template is not an instance of that meme — it is a coincidental visual resemblance.

The awareness criterion makes internet memes a fundamentally **social and semiotic** phenomenon, not just a transmission phenomenon. They operate within communities of shared reference.

## The Meme as Meta-Format

The most precise way to characterize the internet meme structurally is as a **meta-format**: a format for generating formats.

### What Is a Format?

A format is a set of constraints on content production: what kinds of elements to include, how to arrange them, what style to use, what audience relationship to adopt. A haiku is a format (5-7-5 syllable constraint). A newspaper op-ed is a format (lead argument, evidence, counterargument, conclusion). A format is not a single text — it is a template for producing texts.

### The Meta-Format Structure

An internet meme is a meta-format because it is itself a template for generating instances. The template has:

- **Fixed slots:** Elements that must remain constant across instances to preserve template identity (the base image, the verbal frame structure, the visual layout)
- **Variable slots:** Elements that users fill in to generate new instances (the text labels, the specific referent of the verbal template, the object in an exploitable image)
- **Encoded frame:** The base image or verbal pattern activates a cognitive frame — a structured representation of a situation, relationship, or scenario
- **Encoded stance:** The template carries an implied attitude — irony, absurdism, complaint, solidarity, mockery — that instances inherit or subvert

The humor (or rhetorical force) of an internet meme instance typically arises from one of three operations:
1. **Frame resonance:** The variable-slot content fits surprisingly well into the encoded frame, producing recognition humor ("yes, this is exactly what this situation feels like")
2. **Frame clash:** The variable-slot content violates the encoded frame in a productive way, producing incongruity humor
3. **Stance transfer:** The encoded stance (e.g., mock-epic gravity) is applied to trivial or absurd variable-slot content, producing bathos

### The Meta-Format and Collective Creativity

The meta-format structure is what enables *participatory creativity* at scale. Because the template absorbs the cognitive overhead of format creation (layout, stance, frame), participants can focus entirely on the variable-slot content — the creative act of finding the right referent for this template in this context. This dramatically lowers the barrier to entry for humor production.

This is McLuhan's logic applied to a new medium: the format is the medium, and the medium constrains and enables the message. The meme format is not neutral — it carries affordances (what kinds of meaning it is easy to make with it) and constraints (what kinds of meaning it makes difficult or impossible).

## Cognitive Linguistics of Memes

### Construction Grammar: Snowclones as Constructions

Barbara Dancygier and Lieven Vandelanotte (2017, *Cognitive Linguistics*; 2022, *The Language of Memes*, Cambridge University Press) provide the most rigorous linguistic analysis of internet memes, applying construction grammar and conceptual blending theory.

Their core insight: **internet memes are constructions in the technical sense of construction grammar** — pairings of form and meaning that are stored as units and applied productively. Just as the ditransitive construction ("X gave Y Z") is a grammatical form paired with a meaning (transfer of possession), the "One Does Not Simply [verb phrase]" snowclone is a construction: a specific verbal form paired with a meaning (mock-epic assertion of difficulty/impossibility), derived from Boromir's line in *The Lord of the Rings*.

Properties of meme-constructions:
- **Schematic:** They have variable slots (like argument slots in syntactic constructions)
- **Conventionalized:** Their meaning exceeds what would be derivable from the parts alone (the "One Does Not Simply" construction means more than its literal content)
- **Intertextually grounded:** Their meaning depends on recognizing the source (the Boromir scene) and the history of the construction's use

### Conceptual Blending: Image + Caption

The image macro meme is a paradigm case of conceptual blending (Fauconnier & Turner). The base image and the caption constitute two input spaces; the meme instance is the blend:

- **Input 1:** The base image, which activates a rich frame (the situation depicted in the image, the implied narrative, the character's apparent motivations/emotions)
- **Input 2:** The caption text, which describes a situation, claim, or reaction
- **Generic space:** The structural skeleton shared by both inputs (typically: agent, situation, response/evaluation)
- **Blended space:** An emergent meaning that is not in either input alone — the humor or insight arises from the way the two inputs map onto each other in the blend

Example: *Distracted Boyfriend*. Input 1: a stock photo of a man in a relationship ogling another woman (frame: divided attention, betrayal of commitment). Input 2: labels replacing the three figures with concepts (e.g., boyfriend = "me", girlfriend = "sleep", new woman = "one more episode"). The blend: the frame of romantic betrayal/divided attention is *projected onto* the conceptual relationship, producing the insight that my relationship to sleep is like a committed partner I keep betraying. The humor is in the apt but incongruous mapping.

### Frame Semantics: The Base Image as Frame Activator

In Fillmore's frame semantics and Sweetser's extensions, words and images activate *frames* — structured representations of event types, relationships, and scenarios that include default values for participants, relations, and outcomes. The base image of an internet meme is a powerful frame activator precisely *because* it is an image: images activate frames holistically, with all their relational structure intact, faster than verbal descriptions.

This is why the meme's humor is often instantaneous: the base image pre-loads the entire frame into the reader's working comprehension, and the caption need only specify how the variable slots map onto the current referent. The cognitive load of meaning construction is distributed across the fixed and variable components.

## Virality and Transmission Dynamics

### Why Memes Spread

Applying the transmission bias framework from Boyd, Richerson, and Sperber:

| Transmission mechanism | How internet memes exploit it |
|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Content bias (MCI)** | Meme instances are minimally counterintuitive: familiar template (intuitive) + unexpected content (violates one expectation) |
| **Content bias (emotional)** | Templates encode emotionally resonant frames (disgust, frustration, social anxiety, absurdism) |
| **Prestige bias** | Meme formats used by respected or funny accounts are copied more; "dank" memes signal insider knowledge |
| **Conformity bias** | Users adopt formats that are already widely used — template recognition requires exposure |
| **Relevance** | A meme that maps precisely onto a current shared cultural moment spreads rapidly because it is useful for expressing what many people are feeling |

The meta-format structure itself is a transmission advantage: because the template is fixed and the variable slots are what users create, instances are easy to produce and recognize. High **fecundity** (low production cost), moderate **fidelity** (the template is preserved even as content varies), and variable **longevity** (a meme format can persist for years; a specific instance is ephemeral).

### Meme Life Cycles

Internet memes follow observable life cycles:
1. **Emergence:** A novel format appears, often from a single viral instance
2. **Standardization:** The format's constraints are established through repeated use
3. **Peak virality:** The format is applied across domains — wide intertextual spread
4. **Saturation:** Overuse depletes surprise; instances become predictable
5. **Ironic meta-use:** The saturated format is used ironically, making the saturation itself the joke
6. **Archival / niche persistence:** The format retreats to communities that maintain it as in-group reference

This cycle mirrors Kuhn's paradigm cycle in a compressed timeframe: emergence of a new exemplar → normalization → exhaustion → ironic or post-paradigmatic use. The "dank meme" aesthetic (see below) lives at stage 5-6.

### Platform Effects

Different platforms produce different meme ecologies:
- **Reddit** (2005–): subreddit-based meme communities; rapid template turnover; upvote dynamics select for recognition + novelty
- **Tumblr** (2007–): long-form meme chains; absurdist mutation; text-post-as-meme format
- **Twitter/X** (2006–): compression → quotations and screenshots as meme carriers; viral dynamics intensified by retweet
- **4chan/8chan** (2003–): high-speed template generation; anonymity enables extremes; "irony poisoning" aesthetic
- **Instagram** (2010–): visual primacy; carousel meme format; image macro dominance
- **TikTok** (2016–): audio-visual meme templates; sound/gesture constructions; accelerated lifecycle

Each platform's affordances (content length, media type, social graph structure, recommendation algorithm) shapes which meme formats thrive and how templates mutate.

## The Semantic Shift: From Theory to Vernacular

### How 'Meme' Came to Mean 'Internet Meme'

The word *meme* entered internet usage early — the usenet community used it loosely in the late 1990s and early 2000s for any viral internet content (jokes, chain emails, flash animations). The modern template-based image macro sense solidified around 2007–2009 with the rise of LOLcats ("I Can Has Cheezburger?", 2007), Advice Animals, and early Reddit meme communities.

By 2012–2013, "meme" in ordinary conversation overwhelmingly meant the internet phenomenon, not the Dawkins concept. This is itself a striking instance of cultural evolution: the academic term was appropriated by a community, transformed to fit a new purpose, and rebroadcast at scale. The original academic meaning is now the *secondary* sense for most speakers.

This semantic shift is not purely a corruption. The internet usage identifies something real: a class of cultural artifacts with genuine structural regularities — the template logic, the participatory creativity, the intertextual humor. It found a word (meme) for a phenomenon that previously lacked one. The problem is that the word already had a technical meaning, and the two meanings interfere.

### Implications for Memetics Research

The semantic takeover has had real effects on the academic study of memetics:
1. **Terminology pollution:** Research on cultural transmission must now distinguish "Dawkinsian meme" from "internet meme" in ways that Dawkins did not anticipate and that journals handle inconsistently.
2. **Empirical distraction:** Internet memes are *extremely* easy to study (abundant data, clear instances) and *superficially* seem to confirm memetics theory. This can produce the illusion that the hard theoretical problems (unit problem, fidelity problem) are solved when they are not.
3. **Productive convergence:** Conversely, the study of internet meme spread *does* provide unusually clean data on cultural transmission dynamics: how templates propagate, how variants are generated, how some formats achieve longevity and others don't. This is relevant to the transmission-bias and attractor frameworks.

## Dank Memes and Post-Irony

### The Aesthetic of Ironic Exhaustion

The "dank meme" aesthetic (emerging c. 2015–2016, primarily on Reddit, 4chan, and later Instagram/TikTok) represents a distinctive cultural moment: **post-ironic meme humor**. Dank memes are characterized by:

- **Meta-referentiality:** The meme is explicitly *about* being a meme — it references its own format, its own spread, the conventions of meme culture
- **Irony stacking:** Multiple layers of irony, often recursive — irony about irony, sincere expression *through* heavy irony
- **Surrealism and non-sequitur:** Humor that deliberately violates frame coherence, producing absurdist meaning through the *absence* of standard meme logic
- **Anti-polish:** Deliberately low-quality production (blurry images, impact font deliberately misused, deep-fried image filters) as a signal of ironic distance from mainstream internet culture

From the cognitive linguistics perspective, dank memes exploit the very constructional knowledge that standard memes rely on: the reader's familiarity with the template is activated and then *violated* in ways that produce humor through frustrated expectation. They are constructions whose meaning is *the deconstruction of the construction*.

From the cultural evolution perspective, dank meme aesthetics represent the ironic/meta-use phase of a meme life cycle, generalized to an entire aesthetic regime.

## Memes as Political Instruments

### Political Memetics

Internet memes became significant political tools in the 2010s, particularly in the context of:
- **2016 US election:** Pepe the Frog's appropriation; "Great Meme War" narrative; meme-driven political communities on Reddit and 4chan
- **Brexit campaign:** Viral image macros carrying political messaging
- **Global protest movements:** Memes as coordination tools and solidarity signals (Hong Kong protests, BLM, Arab Spring)

The political meme exploits the same meta-format logic as humor memes, but deploys the stance for persuasion rather than amusement. The template's encoded stance (e.g., heroic, absurd, contemptuous) is transferred to the political referent. The participatory structure creates **distributed political messaging**: no single source controls the message; many participants produce instances, adapting the template to local contexts.

Ryan Milner's *The World Made Meme* (MIT Press, 2016) analyzes this dimension: memes as a logic of cultural participation that is simultaneously democratic (anyone can create) and prone to weaponization (bad-faith actors can exploit participatory structures for coordinated disinformation).

## Synthesis: The Internet Meme in Theoretical Perspective

| Dimension | Dawkins's meme | Internet meme |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| **Unit** | Abstract cultural unit (idea, tune, practice) | Template + family of instances |
| **Transmission** | Quasi-Darwinian: random variation, selection | Participatory: intentional remix, intertextual awareness |
| **Fidelity** | Variable; theoretically requires high fidelity | Template is preserved; content varies — high formal fidelity, variable semantic fidelity |
| **Mechanism** | Brain-to-brain imitation | Platform-mediated, algorithm-assisted distribution |
| **Intentionality** | Passive (spreads through host brains) | Active (human creators are conscious agents) |
| **Humorous function** | Incidental (some memes happen to be jokes) | Constitutive (the humor/rhetorical function is the format's primary purpose) |
| **Academic home** | Evolutionary biology, cultural evolution | Communication studies, internet studies, cognitive linguistics |

### What Makes the Internet Meme Distinctive

The internet meme is not just a fast-spreading cultural unit (that's Dawkins). It is specifically:

1. **A template-based participatory format** — the unit is the template, not the instance
2. **Intertextually dependent** — meaning depends on recognizing the template and its genealogy
3. **Humorous or rhetorical by design** — the format encodes stance; instances deploy that stance
4. **Platform-mediated** — spread dynamics are shaped by algorithmic amplification, not just cognitive transmission
5. **Self-aware** — meme creators and consumers know they are operating within a meme culture; meta-irony is constitutive

### The Engram Connection

The Engram knowledge base is itself a meme transmission system in Dawkins's sense (files circulate, vary, and are selected across sessions). It is *not* an internet meme system — there is no template-filling humor. But the meta-format analysis is relevant to the design of knowledge files:

- **Template knowledge:** When a knowledge file is well-formatted (fixed structure: overview, theory, critique, implications), future knowledge files are generated by filling the template. The good template is a replication advantage.
- **Stance encoding:** Files that encode a clear analytical stance (critical, synthetic, comparative) are more influential in context than stance-neutral data files — they carry a content bias.
- **Intertextual awareness:** Files that know their relationship to other files (via `related:` frontmatter) participate in a network of meaning that isolated files do not.
