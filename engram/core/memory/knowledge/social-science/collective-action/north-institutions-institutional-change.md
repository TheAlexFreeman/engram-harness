# North: Institutions, Institutional Change, and Economic Performance

## Overview

Douglass North (1920–2015) received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1993 for his foundational work in institutional economics. His key contribution: **institutions — the rules of the game in a society — determine economic performance more than any other factor.** Technology, factor endowments, and individual effort all matter, but they operate within an institutional framework that either enables or stifles their productive combination. *Institutions, Institutional Change, and Economic Performance* (1990) is the concise synthesis of this research program, and *Structure and Change in Economic History* (1981) provides historical depth. North connects cultural evolution (norms as informal institutions) to economic history (why some societies prosper and others don't) and to the organizational theory of collective action.

## What Are Institutions?

North's definition:**Institutions are the humanly devised constraints that structure political, economic, and social interaction.** They consist of:

1. **Formal institutions:** Written rules — constitutions, statutes, property rights regimes, contracts, regulatory frameworks. These are explicit, visible, and enforced by official mechanisms (courts, police, regulatory agencies).

2. **Informal institutions:** Unwritten codes of conduct, norms of behavior, conventions, and self-imposed standards. Examples: business customs, social norms around honesty and reciprocity, cultural attitudes toward work, corruption, or trust.

The critical distinction from **organizations:** institutions are the *rules of the game*; organizations are the *players.* A firm, a political party, a labor union, and a university are organizations that operate within institutional constraints. Confusing institutions with organizations (as casual use of "institution" often does) muddles the analysis.

### Enforcement

Institutions constrain behavior through three mechanisms:
1. **External, formal enforcement:** Courts, bureaucracies, police
2. **External, informal enforcement:** Social norms, ostracism, reputation
3. **Internal, self-enforced:** Internalized values, conscience

Most institutions rely on a mix of all three. Formal rules without informal backup are often ineffective; informal norms without any formal backstop may be insufficient for complex economic interactions.

## Transaction Costs

North imported the concept of **transaction costs** from Ronald Coase. In a world of zero transaction costs, contracting is costless, information is perfect, and efficient bargains are always struck (Coase theorem). But transaction costs are never zero:

- **Measurement costs:** Determining the relevant attributes of what is being exchanged
- **Contracting costs:** Writing, monitoring, and enforcing agreements
- **Information costs:** Learning about counterparties, alternatives, and market conditions

Institutions reduce transaction costs by creating shared frameworks for measurement, enforcement, and expectation-formation. A reliable property rights regime reduces the cost of every transaction involving property. A stable currency reduces the cost of every price-denominated exchange. A functioning court system makes contracts enforceable at lower cost.

Conversely, bad institutions *raise* transaction costs: corruption forces bribe payments into every business deal; insecure property rights deter investment; weak courts prevent complex long-term contracting.

## Path Dependence

North's most important and controversial concept: **institutional path dependence.** The current set of institutions in a society is heavily constrained by its history. Initial institutional choices create network effects, vested interests, and cognitive frameworks that make alternative arrangements increasingly costly to adopt — even if the alternatives are more efficient.

### Mechanisms of Path Dependence

1. **Increasing returns:** Institutions, like technologies, become more valuable as more actors coordinate around them. QWERTY, the common law, the US dollar — their value comes partly from the fact that everyone else uses them.

2. **Organization lock-in:** Organizations that develop under a given institutional framework have invested in specialized assets (human capital, physical capital, political relationships) suited to that framework. They will resist institutional change that devalues those assets.

3. **Mental models:** Informal institutions shape how people conceptualize the economic and political world. These mental models change slowly. People raised in a culture of low trust, corruption, or rent-seeking internalize these frameworks and reproduce them even when formally the rules change.

The practical implication: **you cannot simply import efficient institutions from one context and drop them into another.** The failure of many post-Soviet and post-colonial institutional transplants (democracy, property rights, rule of law) is partly explicable by path dependence: formal rules changed but informal institutions (norms, mental models, organizational interests) did not.

### History Matters

Path dependence means that small differences in initial conditions can produce large differences in long-run outcomes. The branch points where institutions diverge — colonial experiences, constitutional choices, property rights reforms — matter enormously for what is subsequently possible

## Formal and Informal Institutions

North's framework integrates cultural evolution directly into economic analysis:

- **Formal institutions** (laws, constitutions) can change rapidly — a new government can rewrite the rules overnight
- **Informal institutions** (norms, conventions, mental models) change slowly — governed by cultural transmission dynamics, intergenerational learning, and gradual reinterpretation

The mismatch between formal and informal institutions is a central source of institutional dysfunction. A newly enacted anti-corruption law means little if informal norms continue to treat corruption as normal and expected. Formal democratic procedures mean little if informal norms of clientelism, patronage, and factional loyalty are deeply rooted.

This connects directly to cultural evolution: norms are transmitted through the same biases (conformist, prestige, authority) as other cultural elements. Changing norms requires sustained intervention in the cultural transmission process, not just formal rule changes.

## Adaptive Efficiency

North's normative concept: **adaptive efficiency** — the ability of an institutional framework to adjust to new conditions, new technologies, and new challenges. This is different from allocative efficiency (extracting maximum output from current inputs).

Adaptive efficiency requires:
- Secure property rights (to create incentives for long-run investment)
- Enforcement of contracts (to enable complex long-term arrangements)
- Low barriers to entry and exit (to allow organizational experimentation)
- Tolerance for failure (to permit learning from unsuccessful experiments)
- Rule-bound governance (to limit arbitrary government extraction)

North argued that Western developed economies flourished partly because their institutional frameworks happened to evolve toward adaptive efficiency over centuries of institutional competition and selection (though he acknowledged the process was messy and luck-dependent).

## Application to AI Development

North's framework illuminates the institutional dimensions of AI development:

1. **Property rights in AI:** Who owns AI-generated content, training data, and model weights? Current intellectual property regimes are formal institutions designed for industrial-era products; they are imperfectly adapted to AI. Transaction costs of IP disputes in AI are high.

2. **Path dependence in AI governance:** Regulatory frameworks being adopted now (EU AI Act, EO 14110 in the US, China's AI regulations) will create path-dependent constraints on AI development for decades. Initial choices about liability, disclosure requirements, and permitted applications create vested interests and cognitive frameworks that will be very difficult to revise.

3. **Informal institutions in AI labs:** Lab culture (norms around publication, safety, hiring, whistleblowing) is an informal institution that shapes behavior more than formal policies in many cases. Cultural change in AI labs requires engaging these informal norms directly.

4. **Mental models:** The current mental model of "AI alignment" as primarily a technical problem (rather than also an institutional problem) shapes what solutions are pursued. North would predict that changing this mental model requires sustained institutional effort, not just argument.
