# Compression, Intelligence, and What LLMs Compress

## Lede

The thesis that "compression is intelligence" has a long history in algorithmic information theory and has found renewed empirical support in the scaling era of LLMs. The observation is simple: a model that predicts the next token well must have learned regularities in the corpus; learning regularities is compression; therefore better language modeling = better compression = more intelligence (in some operationally meaningful sense). This file extends and critiques that thesis, connecting to the through-lines of capability, interpretability, and epistemic questions about what current AI systems actually represent. See also: `knowledge/_unverified/philosophy/compression-intelligence-ait.md` for the information-theoretic foundations.

---

## Next-Token Prediction as Minimum Description Length

**What the loss function minimizes:**

Standard language model training minimizes cross-entropy loss:
$$\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \log p_\theta(x_t | x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1})$$

This is formally equivalent to minimizing the expected code length when encoding the sequence using the model's probability distribution as a code. The connection to compression is not metaphorical: the model assigns probability $p$ to a token; if you Huffman-code that token under the model, the code length is $-\log_2 p$ bits.

**Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle:** The best hypothesis for data is the one that minimizes (description length of hypothesis) + (description length of data given hypothesis). A very compressed model description (hypothesis) that still predicts data well is preferred over a model that requires much more data-specific encoding.

**Applied to LLMs:** Model weights (billions of parameters) constitute the description of the hypothesis. The hypothesis is the probability distribution over token sequences. The training objective minimizes description length of the data given the model — i.e., the cross-entropy. The regularization / capacity constraints loosely correspond to penalizing overly large hypothesis descriptions.

**The empirical consequence:** Models that achieve lower perplexity on held-out text are better compressors of that text and tend to perform better on downstream tasks. The Chinchilla scaling laws (Hoffmann et al. 2022) confirmed: the perplexity frontier is highly correlated with capability across tasks — improving the compression of text generalizes to improving task performance.

## What a Language Model Compresses

**Syntax:** Grammatical regularities are highly compressible — sentences follow rules, and a model that has learned those rules needs far less code to predict grammatically legal next tokens than one that hasn't. Syntax compression is essentially complete in modern transformers (they do not make grammatical errors on standard constructions).

**Factual co-occurrence:** Facts encoded in text ("Paris is the capital of France," "the Battle of Hastings occurred in 1066") are recurring patterns. A model that stores them compresses text that mentions them — instead of treating their presence as surprising, it predicts them efficiently.

**Style and register:** Different authors, genres, and domains have characteristic patterns. A model that has learned these patterns compresses domain-specific text efficiently. This is why models transfer well to domains well-represented in training.

**World regularities (partially):** Physical regularities — the sun rises in the east, objects fall downward — are implied by vast amounts of text. A model that has encoded these regularities can predict corresponding text efficiently. This is the indirect route by which language models learn partial world models.

## What Cannot Be Compressed (or Is Poorly Compressed)

**Genuinely random or low-regularity sequences:** Truly random text cannot be compressed by any model. For LLMs, content that is generated by processes orthogonal to training data distribution — novel scientific results unpublished during training, personal ephemera, adversarially constructed sequences — cannot be compressed and produces high perplexity.

**Counterfactuals and interventions:** Descriptions of what would have happened under different conditions are sparsely represented in natural text (which describes what did happen). A model that achieves excellent compression on observed-world text may compress counterfactual text poorly — the patterns required are fundamentally different.

**Embodied knowledge:** Sensorimotor regularities — how it feels to catch a ball, what happens when you lean too far forward — are encoded in text only through descriptions. The model compresses the descriptions, not the represented reality. This creates the systematic gap identified in the grounding literature.

**Long-horizon logical structure:** A mathematical proof may consist of tokens where each depends on the entire preceding proof — the probability of a step is near-zero without the full context. Current transformer architectures handle this partially via long-context attention, but the compression of long-horizon logical dependencies remains significantly less efficient than compression of local syntactic patterns.

## The Compression-Quality Spectrum

**A well-trained LLM is a lossy compressor.** The weights are not a lossless representation of the training corpus (which at current scales is petabytes; model weights are tens of gigabytes). The compression is lossy: specific facts from the training data may be only approximately retained, or merged with similar facts (producing confabulation), or forgotten entirely.

**Lossy compression introduces bias:** The compression retains what is statistically regular and discards what is irregular (rare, unusual, idiosyncratic). This is important:
- Common facts are highly reliably retrieved
- Rare facts (one occurrence in training data) are poorly retained
- Frequent patterns that are factually wrong (incorrect claims that appear regularly in text) may be retained and reproduced

**The compression-confabulation connection:** When queried on a topic where the model's "compressed representation" is incomplete, the model fills in using patterns from adjacent compressed representations — it generates a syntactically and topically appropriate sequence, but one that may not correspond to any specific training fact. This is confabulation: the model is interpolating in compressed representation space.

## Hypothetical: Decompressing a Language Model

**Gedanken experiment:** If you could "decompress" a language model's weights — unpack the learned representation back into explicit propositions — what would you find?

**Likely first-level contents:**
- Syntactic rules (extremely high-fidelity)
- Common factual patterns (high fidelity for common facts, decreasing for rare)
- Domain-specific vocabulary and register (high fidelity)
- Patterns of argument structure and rhetorical convention

**Probably absent:**
- The specific examples that provided evidence for the above (training data is not stored; the distilled patterns are)
- Calibrated uncertainty over contested factual claims
- A causally accurate world model (correlations, not causal structures)
- Subjective phenomenology

**The consequence for interpretability:** The model's "knowledge" is distributed representations, not discrete propositions. Decompression would yield something more like a probability distribution over text-space than a database of facts. This is why interpretability methods (probing, activation patching) are necessary to interrogate model knowledge — there is no explicit representational format to read off.

## The Intelligence-Compression Claim: Limits

**The strong version of the thesis:** More general compression capability equals more general intelligence.

**Challenge 1 — The domain specificity of compression:** A model trained on English text is an excellent compressor of English text but a poor compressor of Binary Spiking Neural data or high-frequency financial tick data. Compression ability generalizes only within the distributional family of training data. General intelligence would require compression ability that generalizes across very different distributional families — which current LLMs do not demonstrate.

**Challenge 2 — The difference between predicting and understanding:** A model can predict the next token of a mathematical proof without understanding the mathematical structure. It achieves compression by learning the statistical patterns of proof-text — what sequences of symbols tend to follow others — without the functional equivalent of manipulating mathematical objects.

**Challenge 3 — The Hutter Prize and limits:** The Hutter Prize rewards compression of a slice of Wikipedia. Progress on this prize (achieved by combining LLM-based predictors with arithmetic coding) suggests that LLMs are good text compressors, but Wikipedia compression is a narrow-distribution task, not general intelligence.

**The moderate version (more defensible):** Within the distributional family of the training data, compressing that data requires extracting and encoding the regularities of that family. Extracting and encoding regularities is a form of model-building that has predictive value for tasks within the same family. This is not general intelligence but it is genuine generalization — and it scales enormously with training compute and data.

## Open Questions

- **The compression spectrum across capability domains:** Can we measure whether domains with higher first-principles regularity (mathematics, logic, formal systems) show better compression-capability correlation than domains with lower regularity (social reasoning, aesthetics, political opinion)?
- **Is the perplexity-capability correlation causal?** The correlation between held-out perplexity and downstream task performance is robust empirically, but the causal mechanism is debated. Does perplexity reduction cause capability improvements, or are both driven by a third factor (capacity to model the data distribution)?
- **What compression regime produces best transfer?** Models can compress training data by memorizing it (zero compression benefit) or by extracting universal patterns (high compression benefit). The actual compression regime of LLMs seems to be somewhere in between ("grokking" literature suggests transition points). Understanding when generalizing compression occurs vs. overfitting has major implications for training recipes.
