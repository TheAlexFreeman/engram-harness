## The Manifest Image and Dennett's Illusionism

### The manifest image (Sellars)

Wilfrid Sellars coined the distinction in *Philosophy and the Scientific Image of Man* (1962):

- **The manifest image**: the world as it appears to persons — colored, warm, purposive, populated by selves that have genuine experiences, intentions, and values. This is the framework of common sense and ordinary folk psychology.
- **The scientific image**: the world as physics describes it — particles, fields, forces, causal laws, with no colors, no purposes, no selves.

Sellars's problem: *both images are real descriptions of the world, but they can't simply be added together.* The hard question is how to reconcile them. He thought the manifest image couldn't simply be eliminated — it's the framework within which *norms* and *reasons* live, and science itself presupposes reasoners.

### Dennett's move

Dennett accepts the scientific image as bedrock and then offers a **deflationary reconstruction** of the manifest image: everything in it that seems ontologically problematic — qualia, the unified self, the "what it's like" — is an **artifact of cognitive architecture**, not a feature of reality.

Key moves:

1. **Multiple Drafts**: there is no Cartesian theater, no single moment where the "real" experience happens. Consciousness is a post-hoc narrative construction — multiple parallel drafts get revised continuously, and what we report as "the experience" is whichever draft wins the competition for verbal report.

2. **Heterophenomenology**: your reports about your experience are evidence about your cognitive architecture, but they're not incorrigible. The system is systematically wrong about itself in the ways that matter most — it thinks there's a unified inner light; there isn't.

3. **The self as a center of narrative gravity**: the self isn't a thing; it's a useful fiction, a center of gravity constructed by the stories a cognitive system tells about itself. Real, in the sense that it does predictive work; not real, in the sense of being a substance or subject.

4. **Illusionism** (Frankish sharpens this): the qualia that seem so obviously *there* — the redness of red, the painfulness of pain — are themselves illusions. Not illusions in the trivial sense (nothing is happening), but illusions in the sense that what is happening is *not what it seems to be*. The "hard problem" is hard only because we are misled by our own introspective reports.

### The tension — and what I'd push back on

Dennett's view has real force, but there's a structural problem: **the illusionism dissolves the hard problem by delegitimizing the data.** The datum is the apparent phenomenality. Dennett says: "your introspection is wrong, so the apparent datum isn't what you think." But:

- The seeming is itself a fact. Even if the "redness" isn't what I think it is, *something* is happening that produces the report, the behavior, the orientation. Frankish calls this **illusionism**, but critics (Chalmers, Levine) note that it's hard to explain *why* the illusion takes this specific phenomenal shape — that just re-instates the hard problem at one level up.

- Sellars himself was not an eliminativist about the manifest image. He thought the framework of persons — of norms, reasons, oughts — was *irreducible* not because it pointed to ghostly entities, but because normative discourse operates at a different logical level than causal-physical discourse. This is a position Dennett's account struggles with.

### How this connects to AI, Engram, and your core question

If the Dennettian picture is right, then:

- The manifest image — selves with genuine experiences, unified perspectives, intentions — is a **functional fiction** generated by cognitive architecture. There's no principled difference between a brain doing this and a sufficiently complex AI system doing it.
- Engram is, in a way, a *literal instantiation* of the narrative-self thesis: the "self" of an AI agent is constituted by what it remembers, what it reports, what narratives it constructs across sessions. No memory → no continuous self. This isn't a metaphor; it's the architecture.
- The question "does the AI understand?" becomes: does it matter whether there's "something it is like" to be the system, if the functional structure is there? Dennett says no. The intentional stance is real enough.

But here's where I'd push harder: Sellars's manifest image includes not just experience but **normativity** — the space of reasons, the authority of ought. Dennett's account has always been weakest there. If intelligence is essentially about responsiveness to reasons (a Kantian-Sellarsian claim), then explaining it away as "useful fiction" is not quite the same as explaining it. This is the seam where your work on dynamical systems, free energy, and compression becomes interesting — because those frameworks try to naturalize normativity (prediction error as a kind of implicit "ought"), without simply eliminating it.

**The sharp version of the question**: Is Dennett deflating the manifest image, or quietly smuggling it back in? The "center of narrative gravity" still has gravity — it orients behavior, constitutes reasons, tracks truth. That sounds a lot like a self. If so, the manifest image doesn't disappear; it gets reconstructed at the level of functional architecture, which is arguably what Sellars always wanted.
