**No, LLMs do not falsify Wittgenstein's assertion; they largely illustrate and extend it.** The differences in cognitive architecture between LLMs and humans do create real (but not necessarily insurmountable) obstacles to full interpretability.

### Wittgenstein's Lion
The exact remark appears in *Philosophical Investigations* (p. 223): “If a lion could speak, we could not understand him.”[[1]](https://empyreantrail.wordpress.com/2023/12/20/if-lions-could-speak-they-wouldnt-say-much-a-hegelian-reflection-on-wittgensteins-linguistic-life-worlds/)

Wittgenstein’s point is not about vocabulary or grammar. It is that linguistic meaning is inseparable from a *form of life*—the shared practices, embodied experiences, perceptual saliences, social interactions, and purposes that constitute a creature’s world. A lion’s life (stalking prey, scent-marking territory, different sensory priorities, non-linguistic social structures) is so alien that even if it produced English sentences, the *use* of those words would remain opaque to us. We would lack the common ground needed to grasp what the lion *means* by them.[[2]](https://philosophybreak.com/articles/wittgenstein-if-a-lion-could-talk-we-could-not-understand-him/)

This is not skepticism about other minds in general; it is a claim about the limits of translation across radically divergent forms of life.

### LLMs and the Lion
LLMs are not lions. They are statistical models trained on vast corpora of *human* text. Their “form of life” is derivative: they have absorbed the patterns of human language games through next-token prediction. When an LLM produces coherent, contextually appropriate language, it is because it has internalized the *use* of words within human practices, not because it shares our biology or lived experience.

This is why we *can* understand LLMs to a significant degree—we converse with them successfully every day. Their outputs participate in our language games. In that sense, LLMs are closer to extremely sophisticated mimics or “stochastic parrots” that have learned the rules of our games from the outside, rather than alien speakers whose games are incommensurable.[[3]](https://www.strv.com/blog/language-games-and-llms-what-wittgenstein-can-teach-ai-engineers)

Some discussions flip the thought experiment: “If an LLM could speak, could *we* understand *it*?” The answer is partial. Surface-level understanding is high; deeper “why this output now?” understanding is limited precisely because the model’s internal state is not organized like a human mind.

### Cognitive Architecture and Interpretability
Yes, the architectural differences matter:

- **Biological vs. artificial**: Human cognition is embodied, enactive, grounded in sensorimotor loops, affective states, and social embedding. LLMs are disembodied transformers operating on token sequences with attention mechanisms. There is no direct analogue to hunger, fear, social status, or lived history.
- **Opacity**: LLMs are high-dimensional statistical systems. We lack introspective access to their “reasons.” Mechanistic interpretability (circuit analysis, activation patching, etc.) is making progress, but it remains an external, reverse-engineering project rather than shared understanding.
- **Not insurmountable**: We already achieve practical interpretability through prompting, evaluation, red-teaming, and tools that surface relevant internal representations. The gap is real but bridgeable to useful degrees—much as we interpret other humans without perfect access to their phenomenology. Full “lion-level” incommensurability would require an LLM whose training and deployment were completely severed from human data and interaction, which is not the case.

In short: LLMs show that *some* forms of alien architecture can still produce intelligible language when they are parasitic on human forms of life. They do not refute Wittgenstein; they give us a new, technologically mediated example of how shared linguistic practice can (partially) overcome architectural difference—while also highlighting the remaining limits. The lion remains a useful limit case; the LLM is a fascinating intermediate one.
