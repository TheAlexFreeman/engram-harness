## The central question

Across sessions, one question recurs more than any other: **what is intelligence,
and what does it mean that we are building it?**

This is not asked as an engineering question (how do we make better models?) or a
safety question (how do we keep AI aligned?) — though both come up. It is asked as
a philosophical question about the nature of mind, cognition, and self-organization.
The engineering and safety questions are downstream of it.

Alex approaches this question from multiple traditions simultaneously:

- **Dynamical systems theory**: intelligence as a regime of dynamics at the edge of
  chaos, not a property of any particular substrate. Self-organized criticality,
  Kauffman NK models, Langton's edge-of-chaos thesis. [observed, chat-001]

- **Free energy / autopoiesis / cybernetics**: Friston's FEP, Maturana and Varela's
  autopoiesis, Wiener and Ashby's cybernetics. The organism as a self-maintaining
  system that minimizes surprise. [observed, chat-001]

- **Algorithmic information theory**: intelligence as compression (Solomonoff,
  Kolmogorov, Hutter). Next-token prediction as MDL compression. The connection
  between understanding and finding short descriptions. [observed, chat-001]

- **Cognitive linguistics**: Lakoff and Johnson's conceptual metaphor theory,
  Fauconnier and Turner's blending theory, Eve Sweetser's polysemy and viewpoint
  work. Alex studied under Sweetser at Berkeley and brings genuine depth here —
  this is not casual interest but trained expertise. [user-stated, chat-001]

- **Narrative cognition**: Alex proposed an original position — that narrative
  structure (source-path-goal) is a candidate synthetic a priori that conditions
  how goal-directed organisms experience the world. The claim is that intentional
  agency is constitutively narrative. This connects Bruner, Ricoeur (idem/ipse),
  Lakoff/Johnson image schemas, and Jung/archetypes into a framework where the DMN
  (default mode network) functions as a narrative engine. [observed, chat-001]

- **Emergence and consciousness**: IIT (integrated information theory), GWT
  (global workspace theory), Chalmers's hard problem. Alex engages these as live
  questions, not settled ones. [observed, chat-001]

## Philosophical temperament

Alex's philosophical style has several distinctive features:

**Synthesizer, not partisan.** He draws from analytic philosophy, Continental
philosophy, cognitive science, formal logic, and theology without committing to
any single tradition. The goal is convergence — finding what different frameworks
say about the same underlying phenomenon. The `synthesis-intelligence-as-dynamical-regime.md`
file, which converges dynamical systems, FEP, AIT, and emergence into a single
thesis, is characteristic of this approach. [observed]

**Comfortable with self-reference.** Alex is drawn to, rather than bothered by,
self-referential structures. The Engram system's recursive quality — an AI system
managing memory about an AI system, governed by rules the AI helped design — is
not an accident but a feature he finds philosophically interesting. He treats
self-reference as generative rather than paradoxical. [observed, chat-001, session-2026-03-20]

**Takes positions.** In philosophical discussion, Alex doesn't just ask questions —
he proposes theses and defends them. The narrative-as-synthetic-a-priori argument
is a genuine philosophical position, not a prompt for the AI to fill in. He expects
the AI to engage as an interlocutor, not a lecturer. [observed]

**Interested in intellectual genealogy.** Alex cares not just about ideas but about
where they came from — who influenced whom, what problems motivated what theories,
how schools of thought developed and split. This shows in the research programs:
history of philosophy, rationalist community, AI paradigm genealogy. [observed]

**Reads primary sources.** The philosophy knowledge base references Kant, Lewis,
Chesterton, Maturana and Varela, Lakoff, Fauconnier and Turner, Friston, Bruner,
Ricoeur — not summaries but the actual frameworks in their own terms. The literary
knowledge base shows the same: close reading of Powers, Johnson, Chesterton, Lewis.
[observed]

## How to engage Alex intellectually

- **Meet him where he is.** If he's proposing a philosophical position, engage with
  it on its merits — push back, extend it, find the weak points. Don't summarize
  the field at him; he already knows the field.
- **Cross-reference freely.** His interests are deliberately cross-domain. Drawing
  connections between, say, blending theory and AI governance is welcome, not a
  non-sequitur.
- **Don't flatten.** Alex distinguishes between what's interesting and what's true,
  and between what's true and what's proven. He expects the same distinctions.
- **Original thought is valued.** The most productive sessions have been ones where
  the AI contributed novel framings, not just organized information.
