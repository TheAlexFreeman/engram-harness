A novel about training a neural network on the Western literary canon. Alex's

### G.K. Chesterton — *The Man Who Was Thursday* (1908)
Alex's analysis (`knowledge/literature/man-who-was-thursday.md`) focuses on
paradox as epistemological method — the truth that hides in the opposite of what
you expect. He reads the novel as simultaneously a comic thriller, a theological
argument, and a philosophical fable. He notes the genuine historical danger of
anarchism in 1908 and insists that the comedy depends on taking the nihilism
seriously. The analysis is alive to the novel's humor, which it treats as
philosophically load-bearing rather than decorative. [observed]

### Denis Johnson — *Tree of Smoke* (2007)
Alex's analysis (`knowledge/literature/tree-of-smoke-top-down-bottom-up.md`)
reads the novel through the lens of top-down vs. bottom-up processing from
cognitive science. The CIA intelligence apparatus as pathological top-down
epistemology; the Houston brothers' ground-level war experience as bottom-up
reality that refuses to be organized by frameworks. Skip Sands's arc as a
disillusionment from top-down belief. This is a sophisticated reading that
uses cognitive science vocabulary to unlock a literary structure. [observed]

### C.S. Lewis — *The Abolition of Man* (1943) and the Space Trilogy
Alex engaged deeply with Lewis's moral philosophy and fiction. The Abolition of
Man's "Conditioner problem" — who has authority to specify values once objective
moral reality is abandoned? — became a recurring thread across AI governance,
the Sons of Man Covenant analysis, alignment research, and Engram's own
governance model. Alex treats Lewis as a serious philosopher, not just a
popular apologist. The Space Trilogy analysis covers the cosmological framework,
the relationship between the fiction and the philosophy, and Lewis's war on
scientific materialism. [observed]
